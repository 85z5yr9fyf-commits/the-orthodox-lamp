The Orthodox Lamp — Christological Theology

The Chalcedonian Schism & the Ecumenical Councils

In 451 AD a single council split the Christian East in two — a division that has endured for over 1,500 years. Understanding why the Oriental Orthodox rejected Chalcedon, and why different Christian traditions count 3, 7, or 21 ecumenical councils, is essential to understanding ancient Christianity itself.

Part I

The Chalcedonian Schism — What Happened, Why It Happened, and Why It Still Matters

Before Chalcedon — The Road That Led There

▼

To understand why Chalcedon caused a permanent rupture, you must first understand the theological conflict that preceded it — a battle fought over how to speak about the person of Jesus Christ, and two rival theological schools that read the same Scriptures in fundamentally different ways.

325 AD — First Council of Nicaea

Foundation of Orthodox Christology

Condemned Arianism. Defined the Son as "of the same substance" (ὁμοούσιος, homoousios) as the Father — fully divine, not a lesser being. All three major Christian traditions today accept Nicaea I. This is the bedrock on which all subsequent Christology is built.

381 AD — First Council of Constantinople

Completing the Nicene Creed

Condemned Macedonianism (denial of the Holy Spirit's divinity) and Apollinarianism (which claimed Christ had a divine mind but a human body — effectively denying his full humanity). Expanded the Nicene Creed into its final form. All three traditions accept this council.

The Two Schools — Alexandria vs. Antioch

Background Tension

Alexandria emphasized the unity of Christ's person — the divine Word taking human nature so completely that the result is one unified subject. The technical term is the "Word-flesh" (Logos-sarx) framework. Cyril of Alexandria is its greatest representative.

Antioch emphasized the completeness of Christ's two natures — divine and human — each remaining distinct and fully intact in the one person. The technical term is the "Word-man" (Logos-anthropos) framework. Theodore of Mopsuestia and Nestorius are its representatives.

428 AD — Nestorius Becomes Archbishop of Constantinople

The Spark

Nestorius, an Antiochene theologian, refused to call Mary "Theotokos" (God-bearer) — preferring "Christotokos" (Christ-bearer). His logic: Mary bore the human Jesus, not the divine Word. To Cyril of Alexandria and to popular piety throughout the East, this seemed to split Christ in two — a divine person and a human person loosely united. The controversy exploded immediately.

431 AD — Council of Ephesus

The Third Ecumenical Council

Condemned Nestorianism. Defined Mary as Theotokos — affirming the unity of Christ's person against any division that would effectively create two sons, two Christs. Cyril's Twelve Anathemas against Nestorius's theology were accepted as the standard of orthodoxy. All three major traditions accept Ephesus.

449 AD — The "Robber Council" of Ephesus

The Crisis Before Chalcedon

Dioscorus of Alexandria, Cyril's successor, convened a council at Ephesus that condemned Flavian, Patriarch of Constantinople, and rehabilitated Eutyches — a monk who had gone too far in the Alexandrian direction, teaching that Christ's humanity was absorbed into his divinity (Eutychianism/Monophysitism). Pope Leo I refused to recognize it, calling it the "Latrocinium" (Robber Council). The stage was set for a showdown.

451 AD — The Council of Chalcedon Is Convened

Emperor Marcian and Empress Pulcheria Call the Council

Emperor Marcian, newly crowned after the death of Theodosius II (who had supported Dioscorus), convened a new council at Chalcedon near Constantinople. Over 500 bishops attended — the largest council in the ancient Church's history. The theological and political pressures of an empire hung on its outcome.

Cyril's Formula — The Foundation of Oriental Orthodox Christology

▼

St. Cyril of Alexandria (376–444 AD) is the towering theological giant of the pre-Chalcedonian era — accepted as a Father and Doctor by Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, and Oriental Orthodox alike. His Christological formula is the cornerstone of what the OO tradition defends.

Cyril's Formula — The Alexandrian Christology

"μία φύσις τοῦ Θεοῦ Λόγου σεσαρκωμένη"
"One nature of God the Word incarnate" — Cyril of Alexandria, borrowing (unknowingly) from Apollinarius but filling it with orthodox content

For Cyril, "one nature" (μία φύσις, mia physis) does not mean Christ has no human element — that would be Eutychianism, which Cyril himself condemned. It means there is one subject, one person, one acting agent who is simultaneously and completely divine and human. The unity is so complete, so hypostatic (personal), that you cannot divide "the divine Christ" from "the human Jesus" without destroying Christ.

Cyril's Second Letter to Nestorius — Accepted at Ephesus 431
"We do not say that the nature of the Word was changed and became flesh, or that it was transformed into a whole man consisting of soul and body; but rather that the Word, having personally united to himself flesh animated by a rational soul, inexplicably and incomprehensibly became man."
Cyril is explicitly guarding against any reading that absorbs or destroys the human nature. His "one nature" preserves complete humanity — but insists it is united hypostatically (personally) with the divine, forming one subject: Jesus Christ.
Oriental OrthodoxAccepted at Ephesus 431
The Oriental Orthodox use the term Miaphysitism (from Greek "mia" — one, unified) to describe their position. This is deliberately distinct from Monophysitism ("only one nature, the human absorbed"), which Cyril himself condemned. The OO insist that Christ's one nature is a union of divine and human — not a replacement of one by the other.

Leo's Tome — The Western Challenge

▼

Pope Leo I of Rome (440–461 AD) entered the Christological controversy with his famous Tome — a letter to Flavian, Patriarch of Constantinople, written in 449 before the Council of Chalcedon was called. It would become the defining document of the Chalcedonian settlement.

Leo's Tome — The Western/Antiochene Formula

"Each form does the acts which belong to it in communion with the other; namely, the Word performing what belongs to the Word, and the flesh carrying out what belongs to the flesh."
Leo I, Epistola ad Flavianum (The Tome of Leo), §4

Leo's language alarmed Alexandrian theologians. The phrase "each form does the acts which belong to it" seemed to describe two distinct subjects of action within Christ — the divine Word doing divine things, the flesh doing human things — with Christ as the meeting point. To Alexandrian ears, this sounded dangerously close to Nestorianism's two-sons error.

When Dioscorus heard the Tome read at the Robber Council of 449, he refused to allow it to be accepted. His opposition was theological: Leo's framework did not use Cyril's language, appeared to divide Christ's actions between two natures as if they were two acting subjects, and seemed to undermine the hypostatic unity that Ephesus had defended.

The crucial difference in vocabulary: Cyril used "physis" (nature) and "hypostasis" (person) interchangeably in his earlier writing, following the older Alexandrian usage. Leo and the Western tradition used a different framework: una persona, duae naturae — one person, two natures. When the Chalcedonian definition adopted Leo's framework using the word "two natures" (δύο φύσεις, dyo physeis), the Alexandrian tradition heard it as exactly what Cyril had spent his life opposing.
Dioscorus of Alexandria
c. 449 AD — At the Robber Council
"I accept 'of two natures' but not 'in two natures.' The difference is this: 'of two' means they came together in union; 'in two' means they remain separate after the union."
Reported in the Acts of Chalcedon — This is the precise theological fault line of the schism
Pope Leo I
449 AD — The Tome
"For each form does the acts which belong to it in communion with the other; namely, the Word performing what belongs to the Word, and the flesh carrying out what belongs to the flesh… one of these shines out in miracles, the other succumbs to injuries."

The Council of Chalcedon, 451 AD — What Actually Happened

▼

The Council of Chalcedon convened in October 451 at the Church of St. Euphemia, across the Bosporus from Constantinople. Over 500 bishops attended — overwhelmingly Eastern, with only a handful of Western representatives. The atmosphere was highly charged: political pressure from the new emperor, theological passions running deep, and decades of controversy coming to a head.

The council proceeded in several sessions spanning three weeks. Key actions:

  • Dioscorus of Alexandria was deposed and exiled — not primarily for doctrine but for procedural abuses at the 449 Robber Council (physical intimidation, irregular proceedings)
  • The theological rehabilitation of Flavian of Constantinople (who had died from injuries suffered at the Robber Council)
  • Condemnation of both Nestorius (too much division) and Eutyches (too much fusion)
  • Leo's Tome was read and acclaimed: "Peter has spoken through Leo"
  • A new Christological definition was composed
Political dimension: The Emperor Marcian and his court had strong reasons to want a council that rehabilitated Constantinople's patriarch (Flavian) at Alexandria's expense, restored Roman prestige after Leo's Tome had been snubbed, and ended the theological chaos. The council was not held in a political vacuum — and the OO tradition argues its conclusions bore the marks of imperial management as much as theological discernment.
On the deposition of Dioscorus: Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholics both accept Chalcedon and the deposition of Dioscorus. The OO do not recognize Dioscorus's deposition as legitimate — they regard him as a confessor for the Cyrillian faith, exiled for theological reasons dressed up as procedural ones. He died in exile in 454 AD and is venerated as a saint in the Coptic and Syriac churches.

On first reading, the Oriental Orthodox agree with all four adverbs — they oppose division and separation as firmly as confusion and change. The controversy is not about the adverbs themselves, but about the framework that contains them: "in two natures" (ἐν δύο φύσεσιν).

Why the Oriental Orthodox Rejected Chalcedon

▼

The OO rejection of Chalcedon is not a refusal of orthodoxy — it is a refusal of a specific theological framework the OO believe departs from Cyril's approved language and opens the door to Nestorian thinking. The rejection operates on several levels.

1. "In two natures" contradicts Cyril's approved formula

▼

Cyril's formula — "one nature of God the Word incarnate" — was the basis on which Nestorius was condemned at Ephesus. The Council of Ephesus accepted Cyril's letters as the standard of orthodoxy. Chalcedon adopted Leo's formula "in two natures" — language Cyril had explicitly criticized as Nestorian in tendency. To the OO, replacing Cyril's approved language with language he condemned is not a clarification but a reversal.

The OO ask: if "one nature" was good enough to condemn Nestorius, why was it suddenly inadequate 20 years later? The answer, they argue, is political — imperial desire to rehabilitate Antiochene theology and create a formula acceptable to the West — not theological necessity.

2. "Two natures after the union" sounds Nestorian

▼

The specific phrase "in two natures" — as opposed to "of two natures" (which the OO would accept, describing the elements that entered the union) — implies that after the Incarnation, Christ continues to exist in a state of two natures. For the Alexandrian tradition, this means the union is not complete: you can still mentally (and possibly practically) distinguish "the divine acting" from "the human acting," which is exactly the division Cyril warned against.

Dioscorus put it precisely: "of two natures" (ἐκ δύο φύσεων) describes what came together; "in two natures" (ἐν δύο φύσεσιν) implies they remain alongside each other after the union. The OO insist the former is orthodox; the latter is Nestorianizing.

3. Leo's Tome uses language Cyril rejected

▼

The Tome's phrase "each form does the acts which belong to it" (agit enim utraque forma cum alterius communione quod proprium est) divides Christ's activities between his two natures as if they were two independent subjects taking turns. Christ gets hungry as man; Christ raises Lazarus as God. The OO say: this is the logic of Nestorius — a moral union of a divine person and human person, not a true hypostatic (personal) union in one subject.

Cyril explicitly wrote against this kind of division of operations. For Cyril, Christ's actions are always the actions of the one Word incarnate — you cannot slice them between "divine actions" and "human actions" as if two agents were cooperating.

4. The council's political circumstances undermine its authority

▼

The OO note that Chalcedon was heavily shaped by imperial politics. Emperor Marcian had political reasons to favor Constantinople over Alexandria. The majority of attending bishops had previously signed Cyril's condemnation of Nestorius at Ephesus — yet at Chalcedon they accepted language far more sympathetic to Antiochene theology, largely under imperial pressure. Dioscorus was deposed not by theological verdict but on procedural charges, in a council whose acts show bishops being pressured and intimidated by imperial commissioners.

A council whose conclusions were shaped more by imperial management than theological consensus — the OO argue — cannot claim the authority of an ecumenical council guided by the Spirit.

The OO position in one sentence: Chalcedon introduced Nestorian-leaning language ("in two natures," "each nature acting according to its own properties") that Cyril of Alexandria had condemned, under imperial political pressure, in a council whose procedures were irregular — and therefore it cannot be received as an authoritative ecumenical definition of the Apostolic faith.

The Aftermath — A Church Divided

▼

The Chalcedonian definition did not settle the controversy — it deepened it. The Christian East fractured along theological, geographical, and increasingly ethnic lines in the decades after 451 AD.

451–460 AD — Immediate Rejection

Egypt, Syria, Armenia

Egypt almost entirely rejected Chalcedon. When the Chalcedonian patriarch Proterius was installed in Alexandria, the population revolted — Proterius was murdered by a mob in 457 AD. The Coptic faithful and their bishops rejected the Council as Nestorian. Syria (especially the Syriac-speaking population) and Armenia similarly refused the definition.

482 AD — The Henotikon of Zeno

Imperial Attempt at Compromise

Emperor Zeno issued the Henotikon (Union Formula) — a theological compromise that deliberately avoided the language of both Chalcedon and its opponents, affirming Cyril's language but not explicitly condemning Chalcedon. It temporarily satisfied many Miaphysites but was rejected by Rome, causing the Acacian Schism (484–519 AD) between Rome and Constantinople.

519 AD — Chalcedon Reimposed

Justin I Ends the Acacian Schism

Emperor Justin I reunited Constantinople with Rome by fully accepting Chalcedon. Anti-Chalcedonian bishops in Syria and Egypt were exiled. The division between Chalcedonian and Non-Chalcedonian Christians hardened into a permanent institutional separation.

553 AD — Second Council of Constantinople

Attempted Reconciliation

Emperor Justinian convened the Fifth Ecumenical Council in a bid to win back the Miaphysites by condemning the "Three Chapters" (writings sympathetic to Nestorianism). This partially succeeded theologically but came too late — the institutional separation was already complete. The OO do not recognize this council.

7th Century — Arab Conquests Seal the Division

Political Separation Becomes Permanent

The Arab Muslim conquests of Egypt (641 AD), Syria (636 AD), and Armenia separated the Oriental Orthodox churches from Byzantine imperial rule. The political-religious pressure from Constantinople ceased. The OO churches continued under Islamic rule, developing independently — the Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, and Ethiopian traditions hardening into distinct churches with their own patriarchs, liturgies, and theological identities.

The Oriental Orthodox Churches — Heirs of the Rejection

▼

Six churches today constitute the Oriental Orthodox communion — united by their rejection of Chalcedon, their Miaphysite Christology, and their acceptance of only three ecumenical councils. Together they represent approximately 60–80 million Christians worldwide.

Coptic Orthodox Church
Egypt — Pope of Alexandria
The most ancient African church, traditionally founded by St. Mark the Evangelist. Largest OO church, centered in Egypt with diaspora worldwide. Liturgy in Coptic and Arabic.

One of the oldest Christian nations (4th century). "Tewahedo" means "united" — a direct reference to Miaphysite Christology. ~40–50 million members.

The first nation to adopt Christianity as state religion (301 AD). The Armenian church was not present at Chalcedon and rejected its findings at the Synod of Dvin (506 AD). Catholicos of All Armenians based in Etchmiadzin.

Traced to the Apostle Thomas's mission to India (52 AD). Joined the Oriental Orthodox communion through contact with the Syriac tradition. Based in Kerala, South India.

Modern Dialogue — Are We Really Saying Different Things?

▼

One of the most remarkable developments in 20th century ecumenism is the growing consensus that the Chalcedonian and Oriental Orthodox traditions may be confessing the same faith in different theological vocabularies — that 1,500 years of separation may have been, at least in part, a catastrophic failure of translation.

Oriental Orthodox

"One nature (mia physis) of God the Word incarnate — united without confusion, without change, without mixture, without separation."

The "one nature" is a fully divine-human reality. Not one nature at the expense of the other. The union is complete and personal.

Eastern Orthodox / Chalcedonian

"One person (hypostasis) in two natures — without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."

The "two natures" are both fully real but united in one person. Not two Christs — one subject who is fully divine and fully human.

Roman Catholic

"One divine person (the eternal Son) subsisting in two natures — the divine nature of the Trinity and a human nature assumed in the Incarnation."

Developed through Aquinas's application of Chalcedonian terminology. Fully human, fully divine, one person.

The Joint Commission established between Eastern Orthodox and Oriental Orthodox churches produced the following agreed statement in 1989:

First Agreed Statement — Anba Bishoy Monastery, Egypt, 1989
"We have inherited from our fathers in Christ the one apostolic faith and tradition, though as Churches we have been separated from each other for centuries. As two families of Orthodox Churches long out of communion with each other we now pray and trust in God to restore that communion on the basis of the common apostolic faith of the undivided church of the first centuries."
Both sides agreed: the OO are not Monophysites (which would deny Christ's full humanity); the Chalcedonians are not Nestorians (which would divide Christ into two persons). The actual theological content confessed by both sides is the same — the differences are terminological and historical, not doctrinal.
OO AgreedEO Agreed1989

Full communion between Oriental Orthodox and Eastern Orthodox has not yet been achieved — practical, canonical, and some remaining theological questions persist. But the recognition that the Chalcedonian Schism was largely a terminological tragedy is one of the most hopeful developments in Christianity in centuries.

Part II

The Ecumenical Councils — Why Oriental Orthodox Count 3, Eastern Orthodox Count 7, and Catholics Count 21

What Makes a Council "Ecumenical"?

▼

The word "ecumenical" comes from the Greek οἰκουμένη (oikoumene) — the inhabited world. An ecumenical council is, in principle, a gathering of the whole Church — all her bishops from all her regions — to define doctrine or address major questions. But in practice, the criteria for what counts as genuinely ecumenical have been contested for centuries.

3
Oriental Orthodox
Nicaea I, Constantinople I, Ephesus — the pre-Chalcedonian councils received by all
7
Eastern Orthodox
The 3 above plus Chalcedon, Constantinople II, Constantinople III, Nicaea II
21
Roman Catholic
The 7 above plus 14 more — from Lateran I (1123) through Vatican II (1965)

The divergence in the count reflects the divergence in ecclesiology. The criteria most commonly cited for a genuinely ecumenical council are:

  • Universality — bishops from across the whole Church, not just one region
  • Apostolicity — representation from the ancient Apostolic sees
  • Orthodox content — conformity to the rule of faith and prior conciliar definitions
  • Reception — accepted over time by the faithful, not merely imposed by imperial force
  • Doctrinal definition — actually settling a matter of faith or morals, not merely disciplinary

Each tradition applies these criteria differently — and where they disagree about a council's validity is precisely where they disagree about theology and ecclesiology.

From the OO perspective, these councils rest on a foundation (Chalcedon) the OO do not accept.

Why the Oriental Orthodox Stop at Three — The Theological Case

▼

The OO acceptance of only three ecumenical councils is not arbitrary conservatism — it is a principled theological position rooted in the criteria for genuine conciliar authority.

1. Councils 4–7 rest on Chalcedon, which the OO reject

▼

The Second Council of Constantinople (553) tried to clarify Chalcedon. The Third Council of Constantinople (681) condemned Monothelitism — a heresy that arose directly from the Chalcedonian controversy. The Second Council of Nicaea (787) addressed Iconoclasm, which arose in the Byzantine empire shaped by Chalcedonian theology. All four presuppose the Chalcedonian framework the OO reject. Accepting them would require first accepting Chalcedon — which is precisely what the OO cannot do.

2. The three councils define the complete Apostolic faith on essential doctrines

▼

Nicaea I defined the full divinity of the Son. Constantinople I defined the full divinity of the Spirit and completed the Trinitarian faith. Ephesus defined the unity of Christ's person and the Incarnation. The essential matters of salvation — who God is (Trinity), who Christ is (the Incarnate Word), how we are saved (through the God-man) — are completely and sufficiently addressed by these three. The OO ask: what essential doctrine of salvation requires Chalcedon that was not already covered by Ephesus?

3. Later councils (Lateran, Trent, Vatican) were purely Western gatherings

▼

The 14 Roman Catholic councils after 1054 were held after the Great Schism had already separated East and West. The Oriental Orthodox, Eastern Orthodox, and all Eastern churches were entirely absent from Lateran I through Vatican II. An ecumenical council, by definition, represents the whole Church (oikoumene). A gathering of Western bishops and the Pope, with no Eastern representation whatsoever, cannot claim ecumenical authority no matter how significant it is for the Western church.

4. Many later councils defined novel doctrines not rooted in Apostolic tradition

▼

The Fourth Lateran Council's definition of transubstantiation uses Aristotelian philosophy (substance/accidents) foreign to the Patristic era. The Council of Trent defined purgatory, indulgences, and merit in ways the East regards as departures. Vatican I defined papal infallibility — unknown to the early Church. Vatican II introduced religious liberty in ways that contradict earlier Catholic conciliar and papal teaching. For the OO, these are not recoveries of ancient truth — they are genuine innovations, and no council has the authority to bind the Church to innovations beyond the Apostolic deposit.

The OO criterion is the Vincentian Canon: "What has been believed everywhere, always, and by all." Applying this standard, the three pre-Chalcedonian councils represent the fullest expression of universal Christian consensus. Everything after Chalcedon represents divergence from that consensus — not development of it.

What the Post-Ephesus Councils Decided — and the OO Assessment

▼

For the sake of clarity, here is what each of the major post-Ephesus councils defined and the OO evaluation:

Chalcedon (451) — "Two Natures"

OO Verdict: Rejected as Nestorian-leaning innovation

Replaced Cyril's approved "one nature" language with "two natures" — framing the OO regard as departing from Ephesus. The OO accept the four adverbs in principle but reject the "in two natures" framework.

Constantinople II (553) — Three Chapters

OO Verdict: Not recognized — presupposes Chalcedon

Condemned Nestorian-sympathetic writings to appease the Miaphysites — but without rejecting Chalcedon itself. Too late and insufficient to heal the schism.

Constantinople III (680–681) — Monothelitism

OO Verdict: Not recognized — the issue arose from Chalcedon

Defined Christ has two wills. The OO affirm Christ has two wills but note that Monothelitism was a distinctly post-Chalcedonian controversy generated by the Chalcedonian framework itself. Also condemned Honorius I as a heretic — embarrassing for the Roman Catholic doctrine of papal infallibility.

Nicaea II (787) — Iconoclasm

OO Verdict: Not recognized as ecumenical — the OO venerate icons independently

Defined the veneration of icons as orthodox. The OO tradition had always venerated icons — the Iconoclastic controversy was a Byzantine imperial crisis the OO were not part of, since they were under Islamic rule by then. The OO affirm the content (icon veneration is legitimate) but do not need this council to justify it.

Trent (1545–1563) — Counter-Reformation

OO Verdict: A local Western council, not ecumenical

Important for defining Western Catholic theology against Protestantism but entirely absent of Eastern input. Definitions on justification, Scripture/Tradition, and transubstantiation reflect Latin scholastic categories foreign to the patristic East.

Vatican I (1870) — Papal Supremacy and Infallibility

OO Verdict: Innovation — unknown to the early Church

Defined dogmas the OO regard as departures from the Apostolic conciliar model. The first clear dogmatic departure from what East and West had previously shared.

Vatican II (1962–1965) — Pastoral Renewal

OO Verdict: Some positive developments, but not an ecumenical council

The OO welcomed some Vatican II trajectories — especially its opening toward ecumenism and the lifting of mutual excommunications with Constantinople (1965). But the council's authority is not recognized as ecumenical, and some of its definitions (especially on religious liberty vis-à-vis earlier Catholic teaching) raise questions the OO leave to Rome's own theologians to resolve.

Common Objections Answered

▼

"Aren't the Oriental Orthodox just Monophysites — believing Christ has only a divine nature?"

▼

This is the most common and most damaging misunderstanding. Monophysitism — strictly speaking — is the Eutychian teaching that Christ's humanity was absorbed into or swallowed by his divinity after the Incarnation, leaving only one (divine) nature. Cyril of Alexandria himself condemned this. The Oriental Orthodox have always condemned it.

The OO position is Miaphysitism: Christ has one united nature (mia physis) that is fully divine AND fully human, without confusion, without change. The humanity is not swallowed — it is perfectly united. The difference from Chalcedon is about which philosophical framework best expresses this unity, not about whether Christ is fully human. The Joint Agreed Statements of 1989–1990 between Eastern Orthodox and Oriental Orthodox explicitly confirmed that the OO are not Monophysites in the condemned sense.

"If the OO and EO confess the same faith, why aren't they in communion yet?"

▼

The 1989–1990 agreed statements established that both sides confess the same Christological faith in different terminologies. But full communion requires more than a theological agreement paper — it requires canonical decisions at the highest level of each church, reception by the faithful, resolution of practical questions (how to handle mixed marriages, how to integrate the churches' separate canons), and the formal lifting of anathemas that accumulated over 1,500 years of separation.

Progress has been genuine but slow. Some local OO-EO communities already practice a degree of eucharistic hospitality informally. Official full communion has not been achieved, partly because the political and institutional weight of 15 centuries of separation cannot be dissolved overnight, however genuine the theological convergence.

"Chalcedon was accepted by the vast majority of bishops — doesn't the OO rejection put them outside the Church?"

▼

Numerical majority alone does not determine ecumenical validity — if it did, Arianism would have been orthodox, since the majority of Eastern bishops supported it at various points in the 4th century. Athanasius stood "against the world" (contra mundum) for the Nicene faith against majority pressure. The OO regard their situation similarly: a faithful minority holding Cyril's approved Ephesine Christology against a council they believe was shaped as much by imperial politics as by the Spirit.

Furthermore, the majority that accepted Chalcedon was not as overwhelming as often presented — Egypt, a major portion of Syria, and Armenia (the first Christian nation) all rejected it immediately. The initial Chalcedonian majority was heavily concentrated in areas closer to Constantinople and Roman imperial influence.

"Why do Catholics count post-schism Western councils as ecumenical?"

▼

Roman Catholic ecclesiology grounds ecumenical authority in the Pope — a council is ecumenical when convened and confirmed by the Pope. Since the Pope is understood to hold universal jurisdiction, a council he convenes and confirms is universal in authority regardless of Eastern attendance. The Eastern absence is, from this view, a deficiency in the Eastern churches (who are in schism), not in the council itself.

The OO (and EO) reject this logic entirely. A council cannot be ecumenical — representing the whole inhabited Church — if the overwhelming majority of Eastern Christianity is absent. The definition of "ecumenical" is not "whatever the Pope calls and confirms" — it is "what the whole Church gathers to define." Post-schism Western councils are, for the OO, regionally authoritative for the Latin church — but they have no claim over those who were never represented at them.

Frequently Asked Questions

▼

Are the Oriental Orthodox and Eastern Orthodox the same thing?

▼

No — these are two distinct families of churches that are often confused. Eastern Orthodox (Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox, Serbian Orthodox, etc.) accepted the Council of Chalcedon (451) and recognize seven ecumenical councils. Oriental Orthodox (Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara) rejected Chalcedon and recognize three. Both are episcopal, liturgical, sacramental churches with Apostolic succession, but they have been in separation since 451 AD. Recent dialogue has revealed profound theological convergence, but full communion has not been restored.

What do the OO believe about Christ's human nature?

▼

The OO believe Christ is fully and perfectly human — a complete human being with a rational soul, a human body, human emotions, human experiences, human limitations (fatigue, hunger, sorrow). The Incarnation was not the appearance of humanity but its full assumption. Christ was born, grew, suffered, died, and rose — all as a real human being. The OO reject any suggestion that the humanity was illusory, partial, or absorbed into the divinity. "Without confusion, without change" — the humanity remains fully itself in the union.

Do the OO have any councils after Ephesus that they recognize?

▼

Yes — the OO churches have held their own synods and councils that carry authority within the OO communion. For example, the Armenian church held the Synod of Dvin (506 AD) which formally rejected Chalcedon. The Coptic church has held synods addressing internal matters of discipline, liturgy, and doctrine. These are not claimed to be "ecumenical" in the universal sense — they are authoritative for the OO communion and consistent with the three ecumenical councils the OO recognize.

Why is Cyril of Alexandria so important to the OO?

▼

Cyril of Alexandria (376–444 AD) is the theological father of Oriental Orthodox Christology. His letters against Nestorius were formally accepted as the standard of Christological orthodoxy at the Council of Ephesus (431) — which all three major traditions recognize. His formula "one nature of God the Word incarnate" is the bedrock of OO Christology. When Chalcedon replaced Cyril's approved formula with Leo's Tome and "two natures" language, the OO saw it as a betrayal of the council that had just defined the faith using Cyril's own words. He is venerated as a pillar of the faith, a confessor, and a Doctor of the Church across the OO tradition.

Is the Chalcedonian Schism the same as the Great Schism of 1054?

▼

No — these are two separate schisms involving different parties. The Chalcedonian Schism (451 AD) separated the Oriental Orthodox (Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, Ethiopian) from the Chalcedonian churches (which became Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism). The Great Schism of 1054 was the split between Eastern Orthodoxy (Constantinople) and Roman Catholicism (Rome) over papal authority, the Filioque, and other issues. The Oriental Orthodox were already separated from both sides before the 1054 Schism occurred — though they share much more in common with Eastern Orthodoxy than with Rome.

Glossary of Key Terms

▼
Miaphysitism
from Greek μία (mia) — one/unified
The OO Christological position: Christ has one united nature that is fully divine and fully human without confusion or separation. Distinct from Monophysitism (which denies full humanity).
Dyophysitism
from Greek δύο (dyo) — two
The Chalcedonian position: Christ has two distinct natures (divine and human) united in one person (hypostasis). Accepted by Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholics.
Monophysitism
from Greek μόνος (monos) — only/alone
The Eutychian heresy: Christ has only one nature — his humanity was absorbed into or transformed by his divinity. Condemned by all Christian traditions including the OO. Not the OO position.
Nestorianism
After Nestorius of Constantinople (c. 386–450)
The teaching (attributed to Nestorius) that Christ's divine and human natures are so distinct they constitute two persons or subjects. Condemned at Ephesus (431). The OO regard Chalcedon as tending toward this error.
Hypostasis
ὑπόστασις — underlying reality, person
In Trinitarian theology: one of the three persons of the Trinity. In Christology: the one personal subject of Christ — the eternal Word who assumed human nature. Key term in defining the Incarnation.
Physis
φύσις — nature
The concrete nature or essence of a being. Cyril used physis and hypostasis interchangeably; Chalcedon distinguished them (one hypostasis, two physeis). This terminological difference is the crux of the schism.
Theotokos
Θεοτόκος — God-bearer
Title for Mary defined at Ephesus (431). Means she bore the one who is God — affirming the unity of Christ's person. Nestorius preferred "Christotokos." All OO, EO, and RC accept Theotokos.
Homoousios
ὁμοούσιος — of the same substance
The Nicene term defining the Son as of the same divine substance as the Father. The key word Nicaea I used to defeat Arianism. Accepted universally by all three traditions.
Ecumenical Council
Οἰκουμενικὴ Σύνοδος
A gathering of bishops from the whole Church whose doctrinal definitions are binding on all Christians. The number recognized varies: OO (3), EO (7), RC (21).
Leo's Tome
Epistola ad Flavianum, 449 AD
Pope Leo I's letter to Flavian of Constantinople, defining Christ's two natures in Western theological terms. Accepted at Chalcedon over Dioscorus's objection. The OO regard its "each nature acting according to its own properties" language as Nestorian-leaning.
Tewahedo
ተዋሕዶ — Ge'ez: "being made one"
The name used by Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox churches — literally meaning "united" or "unified." A direct reference to their Miaphysite Christology: Christ's divine and human are made perfectly one.
Vincentian Canon
quod ubique, quod semper, quod ab omnibus
"What has been believed everywhere, always, and by all." The standard of authentic tradition articulated by St. Vincent of Lérins (c. 434). The OO apply it to limit ecumenical councils to those with universal pre-schism acceptance.
