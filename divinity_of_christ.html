THE ORTHODOX LAMP

The Divinity of Christ — Lord, Prophet, or Legend?

"And Thomas answered and said to Him, 'My Lord and my God!'" — John 20:28

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

Nearly everyone honors Jesus of Nazareth. Islam calls Him a great prophet; secular admirers call Him history's finest moral teacher; others say He was a sincere visionary whose followers later deified Him. The one thing all these views share is that they stop short of what He actually claimed and what His Church has confessed from the beginning: that He is God — "true God of true God," as the Creed says.

The options are limited, and they have been laid out honestly since antiquity. A man who said the things Jesus said is either telling the truth (Lord), knowingly lying (deceiver), sincerely deluded (madman), or never said them at all (legend). The comfortable modern compromise — "a good teacher, but not God" — is the one option His own words close off. C. S. Lewis put the trilemma famously, but the logic is far older: the Sanhedrin itself understood Him to claim deity, and condemned Him for it (Mark 14:61–64). This page walks through what He claimed, what His apostles preached, and why each alternative fails.

The Alternatives

Jesus was a prophet (Islam), a great moral teacher (secular admiration), a sincere mystic whose words were inflated by later legend (liberal scholarship), or a lesser, created heavenly being (Arius then, others now). In every version, the worship of Jesus as God is a mistake His followers made about Him.

Oriental Orthodox Teaching

Jesus Christ is the eternal Son and Word of God — distinct in Person from the Father, one with Him in essence — who became man without ceasing to be God. The Church did not promote a man to godhood; she recognized God who had come down to man (John 1:14). This is not a late development but the confession of the apostles who knew Him.

This page concerns whether Christ is God. For how He is God and man in one, see the page on Christology; for how one God is Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, see the page on the Trinity.

II. What the Church Confesses

▼

Precision first, because every alternative trades on a misstatement of the claim.

The Son is true God, not a second god. The Nicene Creed, which the Coptic Church confesses in every Liturgy, calls the Son "Light of Light, true God of true God, begotten not made, of one essence with the Father." There is one God; the Son is not a rival deity or a creature promoted to divine rank, but God's own eternal Word — "and the Word was God" (John 1:1).

The Son is not the Father. The Church has never taught that Jesus is the Father wearing a mask — that error (modalism) was rejected as firmly as Arianism. The Son is eternally distinct in Person from the Father: He prays to the Father, is sent by the Father, returns to the Father. Distinction of Person; unity of essence. So the common challenge "who was Jesus praying to, Himself?" refutes modalism, which the Church also rejects — not the Creed.

"Begotten" is not "made." The Son's begetting is eternal, not an event in time, and has nothing to do with the pagan idea of a god taking a consort — a notion Scripture and the Fathers reject as emphatically as the Quran does. The Creed's very point in saying "begotten, not made" is that the Son is of the Father's own essence, the way light is of fire — not a creature, however exalted.

And He became man without ceasing to be God. "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us" (John 1:14). The eternal Son took true humanity from the Virgin Mary, so that the man Jesus of Nazareth is the Word of God Himself living a human life — hungry, weeping, dying — without His divinity being diminished or His humanity being absorbed.

III. Christ's Own Claims

▼

The objection "Jesus never said the exact words 'I am God'" misunderstands how a first-century Jew would make — and hear — the claim. Within Israel's strict monotheism, the claim is made by doing and saying what belongs to God alone. Jesus did so repeatedly, and His hearers understood Him perfectly: they reached for stones.

John 8:58–59
The Divine Name
"Most assuredly, I say to you, before Abraham was, I AM." Then they took up stones to throw at Him…
Not "before Abraham was, I was" — "I AM," the name God speaks from the burning bush (Exodus 3:14). The grammar is broken on purpose. His hearers did not mishear: the penalty they reached for was the penalty for blasphemy.
Mark 14:61–64
The Trial
Again the high priest asked Him… "Are You the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" Jesus said, "I am. And you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven." Then the high priest tore his clothes and said, "What further need do we have of witnesses? You have heard the blasphemy!"
Jesus answers under oath, at the cost of His life, by combining Psalm 110:1 and Daniel 7:13–14 — the Son of Man who rides the clouds (in the Old Testament, God's own conveyance) and whom "all peoples, nations, and languages should serve." Claiming to be Messiah was not blasphemy; this was. The court that heard Him is the best witness to what He meant.
John 10:30–33
One with the Father
"I and My Father are one." Then the Jews took up stones again to stone Him… "For a good work we do not stone You, but for blasphemy, and because You, being a Man, make Yourself God."
His audience states the claim for us. Jesus' reply (vv. 34–38) does not retract it — He sharpens it: "the Father is in Me, and I in Him."
Mark 2:5–12
Forgiving Sins
"Son, your sins are forgiven you." …"Why does this Man speak blasphemies like this? Who can forgive sins but God alone?"
Anyone can forgive a wrong done to himself. Jesus forgives sins as such — sins against God — on His own authority, and heals the paralytic precisely "that you may know that the Son of Man has power on earth to forgive sins." The scribes' theology was correct: only God can do this. That was the point.
John 20:28–29
Accepting Worship
And Thomas answered and said to Him, "My Lord and my God!" Jesus said to him, "Thomas, because you have seen Me, you have believed. Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed."
A devout Jew addresses Jesus as "my God" — and Jesus blesses the confession instead of correcting it. Contrast every godly man and angel in Scripture: Peter lifts Cornelius up — "Stand up; I myself am also a man" (Acts 10:26) — and the angel twice stops John: "Worship God!" (Revelation 19:10; 22:9). Jesus accepts worship repeatedly (Matthew 14:33; 28:9, 17) and never once deflects it.
Matthew 5:21–22; Mark 2:28
Authority over the Law
"You have heard that it was said to those of old… But I say to you…" / "Therefore the Son of Man is also Lord of the Sabbath."
Every prophet says "thus says the LORD." Jesus says "but I say to you" — amending Torah on His own authority — and claims lordship over the Sabbath, which is God's own institution (Exodus 20:10, "the Sabbath of the LORD your God"). No prophet talks like this; that is why "the people were astonished… for He taught them as one having authority, and not as the scribes" (Matthew 7:28–29).

IV. The Witness of the Apostles

▼

If the divinity of Christ were a legend, it should appear late. Instead it saturates the earliest documents Christianity possesses — the letters of Paul, written within roughly two to three decades of the crucifixion, quoting hymns and confessions older still.

Philippians 2:5–11
The Earliest Hymn
"…Christ Jesus, who, being in the form of God, did not consider it robbery to be equal with God, but made Himself of no reputation, taking the form of a bondservant… Therefore God also has highly exalted Him and given Him the name which is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow…"
Most scholars regard this as a pre-Pauline hymn Paul quotes — worship of Christ as divine within the first Christian generation. Its climax quotes Isaiah 45:23, where every knee bows to the LORD alone, and applies it to Jesus.
John 1:1, 14
The Word
"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God… And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we beheld His glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father."
Both truths in one sentence: "with God" (distinct from the Father) and "was God" (one in essence). The whole Creed is here in seed.
Colossians 1:16–17; 2:9
Creator, Not Creature
"For by Him all things were created that are in heaven and that are on earth, visible and invisible… All things were created through Him and for Him. And He is before all things, and in Him all things consist." / "For in Him dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily."
If all created things were created by Him, He is not one of them. "Firstborn over all creation" (1:15) is a title of rank, as in Psalm 89:27 where David, the youngest son, is appointed "firstborn" — the verses that follow define it: Creator of everything.
Hebrews 1:3, 8
God Addresses the Son as God
"…who being the brightness of His glory and the express image of His person, and upholding all things by the word of His power…" — "But to the Son He says: 'Your throne, O God, is forever and ever.'"
The Father Himself, quoting Psalm 45, addresses the Son as "O God." The chapter's whole argument is that the Son is not an angel — not the highest creature, but the Creator whom angels worship (1:6).
Titus 2:13; Romans 9:5; Revelation 22:13
Called God Outright
"…our great God and Savior Jesus Christ" / "…Christ… who is over all, the eternally blessed God" / "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the Beginning and the End, the First and the Last."
"The First and the Last" is the LORD's own self-description in Isaiah 44:6 — "besides Me there is no God" — and in Revelation it is the risen Jesus speaking (cf. 1:17–18; 2:8).
1 Corinthians 8:6
Christ inside the Shema
"…yet for us there is one God, the Father, of whom are all things, and we for Him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, through whom are all things, and through whom we live."
Paul takes Israel's daily creed — "The LORD our God, the LORD is one" (Deuteronomy 6:4) — and places Jesus inside it: "God" answering to Elohim, "Lord" to the divine name itself. The earliest Christians likewise "call on the name of the Lord" Jesus (1 Corinthians 1:2), language Joel 2:32 reserves for the LORD.

V. The Alternatives Answered

▼

Alternative 1: A prophet, but not God (the claim of Islam)

▼

The Claim

Jesus (Isa) was a great messenger of God — virgin-born, miracle-working, the Messiah — but a man, and to worship him is to compromise God's oneness.

Response

The Church honors the intention — guarding God's oneness — but the category "prophet only" does not fit the evidence. Prophets say "thus says the LORD"; Jesus says "but I say to you." Prophets refuse worship (as do angels — Revelation 19:10); Jesus accepts it. Prophets pray for forgiveness; Jesus grants it. Prophets point away from themselves; Jesus says "come to Me" (Matthew 11:28), "I am the way, the truth, and the life" (John 14:6), and "before Abraham was, I AM." A "prophet" who says such things while not being God would be precisely what prophets must never be — one who leads the people to worship someone other than God. Notably, the Quran itself gives Jesus titles it gives no other prophet: the Messiah, the Word of God and a Spirit from Him (Quran 4:171; 3:45), virgin-born and pure (19:19–21). The question this page presses is simply whether "prophet" can contain the man who said what the earliest records report Him saying.

Alternative 2: A madman — sincerely deluded

▼

The Claim

A man who believes himself God is, by ordinary clinical standards, suffering a grandiose delusion. Jesus was sincere — and wrong.

Response

Grandiose delusion has a recognizable shape: instability, grievance when slighted, collapse under pressure, an inner world that warps everything around it. Set that profile against the man in the Gospels: the parables' piercing realism about human nature; the composure under interrogation and torture ("He answered nothing" — Mark 15:3–5); the concern for others at the point of His own death (Luke 23:34, 43; John 19:26–27); the Sermon on the Mount, which even His critics rank among the sanest moral documents ever spoken. The skeptics of His own day never thought Him mad — the charge at His trial was blasphemy, not insanity, and madmen are dismissed, not crucified as threats. A delusion that produces the most lucid teaching in history is a diagnosis that refutes itself.

Alternative 3: A deceiver — He knew He was not God

▼

The Claim

Jesus, or His inner circle, fabricated the divine claims for influence.

Response

Liars lie for gain. The claim of deity, in first-century Judaea, purchased only poverty, hatred, and a cross — and Jesus walked toward it with eyes open, refusing every exit (John 18:4–8; Matthew 26:53). A deceiver recants on the gallows; He repeated the claim at His trial, when one sentence of retraction would have saved His life. And the deception would then have to be sustained by disciples who got nothing for it but floggings and execution — men do not die for what they know they invented (see the Resurrection page). The hostile Jewish tradition preserved in the Talmud remembers Jesus as one executed for "sorcery" and leading Israel astray — hostile confirmation that He did the wonders and made the claims, disputing only their source.

Alternative 4: A legend — the deification came later

▼

The Claim

The real Jesus claimed nothing divine; decades of retelling among Gentiles inflated a teacher into a god (so, in different forms, Ehrman's "exaltation Christology" and older liberal scholarship).

Response

Legends need time, and the documents leave none. Philippians 2 — Christ "in the form of God," receiving the worship Isaiah reserves for the LORD — is a hymn already traditional when Paul quoted it in the 50s. The confession "Jesus is Lord," with the Aramaic prayer Maranatha, "our Lord, come!" (1 Corinthians 16:22), goes back to the original Aramaic-speaking Jewish community — Gentile legend cannot explain an Aramaic prayer addressed to Jesus. By about AD 112 the pagan governor Pliny reports Christians singing "a hymn to Christ, as to a god" as long-settled custom (see Section VII). And the deifiers would have to be the same devout Jews whose entire identity was refusing to worship any creature — the least likely population on earth to invent a divine man. Something forced the conclusion on them; they said it was the Resurrection.

Alternative 5: A good teacher — and nothing more

▼

The Claim

One can admire Jesus' ethics while setting aside the supernatural claims — keep the Sermon on the Mount, drop the divinity.

Response

This is the one position His own words exclude, because the "teaching" and the claims are not separable strands — the claims are the teaching. "Come to Me, all you who labor"; "he who loses his life for My sake will find it"; "heaven and earth will pass away, but My words will by no means pass away"; "no one comes to the Father except through Me." Remove the self-claims from the Gospels and the famous ethics come away with them — they hang on His authority to make them. As C. S. Lewis argued in Mere Christianity (Paraphrase), a merely human being who said such things would not be a great moral teacher but a lunatic or worse; the one option Jesus did not leave open is patronizing Him as a fine teacher while rejecting His central claim. Good teachers do not accept worship. He did.

Alternative 6: A lesser, created divine being (Arius then, others now)

▼

The Claim

The Son is the first and greatest creature — "a god" (so some render John 1:1), but not the eternal God. "There was when He was not," said Arius; modern groups argue similarly.

Response

Scripture closes this door from both sides. From above: "all things were created through Him," "without Him nothing was made that was made" (John 1:3) — a creature cannot be the maker of all created things, himself included. From below: Israel's God says "besides Me there is no God" (Isaiah 44:6, 8) and "My glory I will not give to another" (Isaiah 42:8) — there is no category of "lesser true god" for the Son to occupy; a being who is worshipped, creates, forgives, and judges either is the one God or is an idol. The Church saw the dilemma clearly at Nicaea (AD 325): if the Son is a creature, then Christians are idolaters every time they pray to Him — and Christians had been praying to Him from the beginning (Acts 7:59; 1 Corinthians 1:2). On "the Father is greater than I" and "the firstborn," see Sections VI and VIII.

VI. Distinct from the Father, One God

▼

The last alternative is the subtlest: granting that Jesus is divine in some sense, but "separate" from the Father — a second, subordinate deity, or simply not the same being at all. Here the Church's confession must be stated with care, because Scripture teaches two things at once and the Creed refuses to drop either.

The Son is truly distinct from the Father. At the Jordan, the Son stands in the water, the Father speaks from heaven, the Spirit descends as a dove (Matthew 3:16–17) — three Persons, plainly not one Person in three costumes. The Son prays to the Father (John 17), is sent by Him, and returns to Him. The Church condemned modalism — the idea that "Father" and "Son" are masks of a single Person — centuries before Nicaea.

But distinct is not separate. "I and My Father are one" (John 10:30); "He who has seen Me has seen the Father" (John 14:9); "all things that the Father has are Mine" (John 16:15); "that all should honor the Son just as they honor the Father" (John 5:23). The Son has everything the Father has — except being the Father. One essence, one will, one worship; two Persons (with the Holy Spirit, three). Not three gods: one God.

"My Father is greater than I" (John 14:28). The Fathers answered this text already in the fourth century, two ways that work together. First, the Son speaks as man: the eternal Word had taken "the form of a bondservant" (Philippians 2:7), and a servant's place is genuinely lower — He who is equal to the Father in His divinity became less than the angels in His humanity (Hebrews 2:9). Second, even within the Godhead the Father is the source — the Son is begotten of the Father — which is an order of origin, not a gradient of deity, just as a son is from his father yet not less human than he. What the verse cannot mean is that the Son is a creature; the same Gospel opens by calling Him God and Creator of all (John 1:1–3).

Why it matters. This is not theological hair-splitting. If the Son is separate from the Father — a lesser god — then the Cross is one being punishing a different, innocent being, and worshipping Jesus is idolatry. Because the Son is one God with the Father, the Cross is God Himself bearing what we could not (2 Corinthians 5:19, "God was in Christ reconciling the world to Himself"), and the worship of Jesus is the worship of the one true God. Salvation itself hangs here, which is why the Church fought the Arian crisis as a battle for the Gospel: only God can save, so if Christ is not God, we are not saved. That is St. Athanasius's whole argument.

VII. The Witness of the Fathers

▼

The confession of Christ's deity is not a fourth-century novelty. It is the unbroken voice of the Church from the generation that knew the apostles — and even pagan observers heard it.

St. Ignatius of Antioch Direct Quote
Epistle to the Ephesians 18 (c. AD 107)
"For our God, Jesus Christ, was, according to the appointment of God, conceived in the womb by Mary, of the seed of David, but by the Holy Ghost."
A disciple of the apostle John, writing on the road to martyrdom, says "our God, Jesus Christ" as naturally as breathing — and addresses his letter to "the Church… at Ephesus" in the name of "Jesus Christ our God." Two centuries before Nicaea.
Pliny the Younger (pagan governor) Direct Quote
Letters 10.96, to the Emperor Trajan (c. AD 112)
"…they were in the habit of meeting on a certain fixed day before it was light, when they sang in alternate verses a hymn to Christ, as to a god…"
A hostile Roman official, interrogating ordinary Christians in Asia Minor, reports the worship of Christ as settled custom — some of those he questioned said they had left the faith as much as twenty years earlier. Outside testimony that Christ was worshipped as God within living memory of the apostles.
St. Melito of Sardis Paraphrase
On Pascha (c. AD 170)
In his great paschal homily Melito presses the paradox of the Cross to its limit: He who hung the earth in place was hung; He who fixed the heavens was fixed to wood; God Himself was slain by the hand of Israel. The shock is deliberate — the Crucified is no mere man.
Second-century liturgical preaching already assumes what the Creed will later define: the one on the Cross is God.
St. Athanasius the Apostolic Direct Quote
On the Incarnation 54 (c. AD 318)
"For He was made man that we might be made God."
The 20th Pope of Alexandria compresses the whole logic of salvation into one line: the exchange only works if the one who became man is truly God. This is why Athanasius — and the Coptic Church behind him — refused every compromise with Arius, even against the whole world (Athanasius contra mundum).
St. Cyril of Alexandria Paraphrase
Letters and treatises against Nestorius (c. AD 430)
Cyril's constant theme: the Word of God did not merely dwell in a man named Jesus — the Word Himself became man, so that Mary is rightly called Theotokos, "Birth-giver of God," and the one who suffered on the Cross is the Word in His own flesh. His watchword, "one incarnate nature of God the Word," remains the banner of the Oriental Orthodox confession.
The deity of Christ is not an abstract attribute but the identity of the person: the baby in the manger and the man on the Cross is God the Word.
Pope Shenouda III Paraphrase
The Divinity of Christ (1989)
The late Coptic patriarch devoted an entire book to this question, arguing from Christ's titles, His acceptance of worship, His miracles done in His own name, His forgiveness of sins, and His "I AM" sayings that the divinity of Christ is the central dogma on which Christianity stands, and answering the objections of Arians old and new verse by verse.
The modern Coptic Church teaching with one voice across eighteen centuries: the confession of Thomas is her own.

VIII. Modern Objections

▼

Objection: "Jesus never said the words 'I am God'"

▼

The Claim

Nowhere in the Gospels does Jesus utter the sentence "I am God"; the doctrine therefore rests on inference, not His own words.

Response

By this standard Jesus also never claimed to be a prophet, a teacher, or the Messiah in the required formula. First-century claims are made in first-century idiom: taking the divine name ("before Abraham was, I AM"), accepting worship, forgiving sins, claiming the Son of Man's universal dominion, calling God's angels and God's kingdom "His" (Matthew 13:41). His audiences — friendly and hostile alike — had no doubt what He meant; the stones and the trial verdict are the proof. Demanding the exact modern sentence is like denying a man claimed to be king because he only said "bow before my throne."

Objection: "The divinity of Jesus was invented at Nicaea"

▼

The Claim

Constantine and the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) turned a human teacher into God by vote — a story popularized by The Da Vinci Code.

Response

Every piece of evidence above predates Nicaea, most of it by more than two centuries: the New Testament documents themselves, Ignatius's "our God, Jesus Christ" (c. 107), Pliny's pagan report of hymns "to Christ, as to a god" (c. 112), Melito's "God was slain" (c. 170). Nicaea did not invent the confession; it defended the existing confession against Arius's novelty, and the question before the council was never whether Christ is divine — both sides granted that Christians worship Him — but how to state His relation to the Father. The bishops who signed, many bearing scars of Diocletian's persecution, were the least likely men alive to redesign the faith for an emperor's convenience.

Objection: "The texts were corrupted to add His divinity"

▼

The Claim

The original Gospels presented a human Jesus; later scribes inserted the divine claims.

Response

The manuscript evidence runs the wrong way for this theory. The earliest copies and papyri already contain John 1:1, John 20:28, and the rest; there is no manuscript stream anywhere preserving a divinity-free Gospel. Where real textual variants exist, scholars can see them precisely because the evidence is so abundant — and none of the passages this page relies on stands on a disputed reading. Moreover the claim is multiply attested across independent authors: Mark, John, Paul, Hebrews, Revelation do not copy one another, yet all present Christ as divine. See the pages on the canon of Scripture and alleged corruption for the fuller case.

Objection: "Only John's late Gospel makes Jesus divine"

▼

The Claim

The "high Christology" belongs to John, written last; the earlier Synoptics know only a human Jesus.

Response

Paul is earlier than every Gospel, and Paul's Christ is "in the form of God" receiving the worship of Isaiah 45 (Philippians 2). And Mark — by this dating the earliest Gospel — opens by applying to Jesus a prophecy about preparing "the way of the LORD" (Mark 1:2–3, quoting Isaiah 40:3), then shows Him forgiving sins as only God can (2:5–7), commanding the sea (4:39–41; cf. Psalm 107:29), walking on the water and saying "It is I (egō eimi, "I AM"); do not be afraid" (6:50; cf. Job 9:8), claiming the Sabbath (2:28), and answering the high priest with Daniel 7 (14:62). The Christology of the Synoptics is high; it is simply shown more than stated. John makes explicit what Mark dramatizes.

IX. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Q: If Jesus is God, who was He praying to — Himself?▼

To the Father. The question assumes the Son is the Father, which the Church has never taught — that is modalism, condemned as a heresy. The Son is a distinct Person who eternally loves, addresses, and is loved by the Father; the Incarnation adds to this that He now prays also as true man on our behalf. Prayer between the Persons is not a problem for the Trinity; it is a window into it (John 17:5, 24).

Q: Doesn't "Son of God" imply God took a wife — which is absurd?▼

It would be absurd, and the Church rejects it as firmly as the Quran does (cf. Quran 6:101). "Begotten" in the Creed is explicitly "begotten, not made… before all ages" — an eternal relation within God, not a biological event. The analogy the Fathers loved is light from fire or radiance from the sun (Hebrews 1:3): the radiance is from the sun, of the same nature, and there was never a sun without its radiance. Scripture also calls the Virgin Birth the occasion of the title (Luke 1:35) — and that conception was expressly "of the Holy Spirit," without any union, as Matthew 1:20 states.

Q: How can Jesus not know "that day and hour" (Mark 13:32) if He is God?▼

The same way He could be hungry, tired, and dead — by truly becoming man. The eternal Word, remaining God, took a real human mind and lived within its creaturely limits, "increasing in wisdom" (Luke 2:52). The Fathers read Mark 13:32 of Christ speaking from His humanity in His role as the one sent; what He possesses as God He did not always exercise as man (cf. Philippians 2:7, He "made Himself of no reputation" — laying aside glory, not deity). A Christ with no real human limits would be God in costume, which is a heresy the Church also rejects.

Q: Was Christ's divinity decided by a close vote at Nicaea?▼

No. The bishops at Nicaea — traditionally numbered 318 — rejected Arianism nearly unanimously; ancient accounts say only two bishops finally refused the Creed. And the council voted on wording, not on whether Christ is divine: His worship was the universal practice both sides started from. The "close vote" is modern fiction.

Q: Why does Paul call Christ "the firstborn of creation" if He isn't a creature?▼

Read the next two verses: "for by Him all things were created… all things were created through Him and for Him" (Colossians 1:16). "Firstborn" (prototokos) in Scripture is a title of rank and inheritance, not of birth order — Psalm 89:27 appoints David, the youngest son of Jesse, as "firstborn, the highest of the kings of the earth." The firstborn over creation is its heir and Lord; verse 16 explains he cannot be inside it.

Q: How should I talk about this with a Muslim friend?▼

With respect, and from common ground: you both confess one God, and you both honor Jesus — that is rare and precious. Then ask, gently, what is to be done with His actual words. Suggest reading the Gospel of Mark together (it takes about ninety minutes): watch what Jesus does — forgives sins, commands the sea, claims the Sabbath, accepts the verdict of blasphemy in silence rather than correct the "misunderstanding." The question "prophet or more?" answers itself from the text. And remember the goal is not to win an argument but to introduce a Person (1 Peter 3:15).
