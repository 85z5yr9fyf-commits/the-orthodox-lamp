THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Judaism & Jesus — Is He the Promised Messiah of Israel?

"For if you believed Moses, you would believe me; for he wrote of me." — John 5:46

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction — Charity and Clarity

▼

Christianity owes an immeasurable debt to the Jewish people. The Scriptures of Israel are our Scriptures; the patriarchs, the prophets, the psalms, and the Law are our inheritance. Our Lord was born a Jew, of the tribe of Judah and the house of David; His mother, His apostles, and the first thousands of His followers were Jews. Any contempt for the Jewish people is a sin against the very root from which the Church grew (Romans 11:18), and the long history of "Christian" antisemitism is a betrayal of Christ, not an expression of Him. This page is written in friendship and respect.

The question between Judaism and Christianity is not whether God chose Israel — He did — but a single, specific question: has the Messiah promised to Israel already come, in Jesus of Nazareth? Faithful Jews say not yet; Christians say He has come, and will come again in glory. This page sets out, from the Hebrew Scriptures themselves, why the Church believes Jesus is the Messiah of whom Moses and the prophets wrote.

Throughout, we state the traditional Jewish reading fairly before responding, and we argue from the Scriptures both faiths hold in common — not by importing the New Testament as proof, but by asking what the prophets foretold.

II. What "Messiah" Means — The Hope of Israel

▼

"Messiah" (Hebrew Mashiach, Greek Christos) means "anointed one." In Israel, kings, priests, and prophets were anointed with oil to mark them as set apart for God's service. Over time, the prophets spoke of a coming Anointed One above all others — a descendant of King David who would reign forever, bring God's justice to the nations, and usher in an age of peace and the knowledge of God.

The Core Expectations

From the Hebrew Scriptures, the traditional hope gathered several threads: the Messiah would be a son of David (2 Samuel 7:12–16), born in Bethlehem (Micah 5:2); He would be a king and deliverer, would gather Israel, draw the nations to the God of Israel (Isaiah 2:2–4; 49:6), and establish everlasting peace and righteousness (Isaiah 9:6–7; 11:1–10).

The Tension Within the Prophets

Yet the same Scriptures also describe an Anointed One who is despised, rejected, and made to suffer for the sins of others (Isaiah 53), who is "cut off" (Daniel 9:26), and who is "pierced" (Zechariah 12:10; Psalm 22). How can the Messiah be both a triumphant king and a rejected sufferer? The Christian answer — that He comes twice, first in humility to atone, then in glory to reign — resolves this tension. As we will see, even some of the rabbis sensed two messianic profiles in the text.

III. The Suffering Servant — Isaiah 53

▼

The fourth "Servant Song" (Isaiah 52:13–53:12) is the single most striking messianic passage in the Hebrew Bible. Written centuries before Christ — and preserved in the Dead Sea Scrolls' great Isaiah scroll (c. 125 BC), so it cannot be a later Christian insertion — it describes a righteous servant who suffers and dies for the sins of others, and is then exalted.

Isaiah 53:3–6
OO
"He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows and acquainted with grief… But he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed… and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all."
A righteous, innocent sufferer bears the sins of others and brings them healing and peace. This is precisely the Christian understanding of the Cross — the Messiah suffering not for His own sin but for ours.
Isaiah 53:8–9
OO
"By oppression and judgment he was taken away… stricken for the transgression of my people? And they made his grave with the wicked and with a rich man in his death, although he had done no violence, and there was no deceit in his mouth."
The Servant dies ("cut off out of the land of the living," v.8), is buried, and is sinless. The detail of being with "the wicked" yet "a rich man in his death" matches the crucifixion between criminals and burial in the tomb of Joseph of Arimathea.
The Traditional Jewish Reading

Following Rashi (11th c.), many later rabbis read the Servant as a personification of the nation of Israel, suffering among the gentiles. On this view Isaiah 53 is not about an individual Messiah at all.

The Older & Christian Reading

The Servant is an innocent individual who suffers for the people — "for the transgression of my people" (v.8). Israel cannot atone for Israel. Notably, several ancient Jewish sources (e.g. the Targum, and the Babylonian Talmud, Sanhedrin 98b) read the passage messianically, before the medieval national reading became standard.

The honest point is this: the national reading is a relatively late development, adopted partly in response to Christian use of the chapter. The passage's own words — an individual, sinless, suffering "for the transgression of my people," then "see his offspring" and be exalted — fit a personal, atoning, then vindicated Messiah far better than a collective nation.

IV. Bethlehem & the Throne of David

▼

The Messiah was to be a son of David, born in David's town. Both are foretold plainly, and both were fulfilled in Jesus — a Galilean whose family nonetheless traces to David and whose birth was in Bethlehem.

Micah 5:2
OO
"But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel, whose coming forth is from of old, from ancient days."
The ruler of Israel comes from the small town of Bethlehem — and his "coming forth is from of old, from ancient days," language hinting at an origin beyond mere human birth. Jewish tradition itself names Bethlehem as the Messiah's birthplace.
2 Samuel 7:12–13, 16
OO
"I will raise up your offspring after you… and I will establish his kingdom… And your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me. Your throne shall be established forever."
God's covenant with David promises an everlasting throne through David's line. The Messiah must therefore be a verifiable son of David — a point that becomes decisive once the genealogical records are considered (Section VIII).

This is more than a coincidence of birthplace. The prophet ties the ruler's origin both to a definite town and to "ancient days," and Scripture binds the Messiah to an unbroken Davidic line. Jesus meets both: born in Bethlehem, of the house and lineage of David.

V. Daniel's Seventy Weeks — The Timing of Messiah

▼

Daniel 9 gives the most precise prophecy of when the Messiah would come — and, strikingly, that He would be "cut off" before the destruction of the city and Temple. That destruction happened in AD 70. The Messiah, therefore, had to come — and be killed — before then.

Daniel 9:25–26
OO
"From the going out of the word to restore and build Jerusalem to the coming of an anointed one, a prince, there shall be seven weeks. Then for sixty-two weeks it shall be built again… And after the sixty-two weeks, an anointed one shall be cut off and shall have nothing. And the people of the prince who is to come shall destroy the city and the sanctuary."
An "anointed one" (Messiah) appears after a measured period from the decree to rebuild Jerusalem (5th c. BC), and is then "cut off" — killed — before the city and sanctuary are destroyed. The Temple fell in AD 70. So the prophecy locates the Messiah's coming and death in the late Second Temple era — exactly the time of Jesus.

The exact arithmetic of the "weeks" (taken as weeks of years) is debated, but the structural point is firm and was felt acutely in antiquity: the Messiah's death is placed before the destruction of the Second Temple. After AD 70, that window closed. This is part of why the talmudic sages themselves recorded a sense that the time for Messiah had already passed (e.g. the discussions in Sanhedrin 97–98), and why some lamented that the appointed times had come and gone.

VI. The Pierced One — Psalm 22 & Zechariah 12

▼

Two further passages describe a figure who is pierced, in language that the Gospels apply to the crucifixion.

Psalm 22:16–18
OO
"A company of evildoers encircles me; they have pierced my hands and feet — I can count all my bones… they divide my garments among them, and for my clothing they cast lots."
A psalm of David describes pierced hands and feet, exposed bones, and garments divided by lot — a vivid picture of crucifixion, a form of execution not practised in David's day. (See the note on the text below.)
Zechariah 12:10
OO
"And I will pour out on the house of David… a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy, so that, when they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child."
God speaks: the people will look on "me" — and yet mourn for "him whom they have pierced," as for an only son. The pierced one is somehow identified with the LORD Himself, and is mourned in repentance. Christians see here both the Cross and the future turning of Israel to her Messiah.

A Note on the Text of Psalm 22:16

This is worth stating honestly. The standard Masoretic Hebrew here reads ka'ari ("like a lion"), while the Septuagint (Greek, 3rd–2nd c. BC) and other ancient versions read a verb meaning "they pierced/dug." A Dead Sea Scroll fragment from Nahal Hever supports a reading closer to "pierced." Both traditions are ancient; we do not hide the variant. But the older Greek translation made by Jewish scholars centuries before Christ already read "pierced," which weighs against the idea that the Christian reading is a late invention.

VII. Two Comings — Suffering, Then Glory

▼

The most common Jewish objection is straightforward: the Messiah was to bring world peace, gather the exiles, and rebuild the Temple — and the world is plainly not at peace. So Jesus cannot be the Messiah. This deserves a serious answer.

The Christian reply is that the Scriptures describe two distinct profiles of the Anointed One that cannot both be fulfilled at once: one suffering, rejected, and "cut off" (Isaiah 53; Daniel 9; Psalm 22), and one reigning in everlasting glory and peace (Isaiah 9; 11; Daniel 7:13–14). The simplest way to honour both sets of texts is that the Messiah comes twice: first in lowliness to atone for sin and open the way to God, and again in glory to judge the world and complete the kingdom. The peace of the messianic age belongs to the second coming, which Christians still await.

Remarkably, the rabbinic tradition itself wrestled with the same tension and produced a "two-Messiah" framework — Mashiach ben Joseph, a suffering and dying Messiah, and Mashiach ben David, the reigning one (see the discussion in Sukkah 52a). The rabbis split the offices between two figures; the Church confesses them united in one Person who comes in two advents. Either way, the suffering-then-glory pattern is not a Christian imposition but is read out of the Hebrew text.

Daniel 7:13–14
OO
"Behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man… And to him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom… an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away."
Alongside the suffering Servant stands this glorious "son of man" who receives an everlasting kingdom. Both belong to the Messiah — and Jesus took "Son of Man" as His own title, pointing to this very text.

VIII. The Sign of the Temple's Fall

▼

Several prophecies require the Messiah to come while the Second Temple still stood and while Davidic descent could still be traced. The destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple in AD 70 — with the loss of the genealogical and priestly records — closed both windows. This is among the strongest reasons to look to the late Second Temple era for the Messiah.

Genesis 49:10
OO
"The scepter shall not depart from Judah, nor the ruler's staff from between his feet, until tribute comes to him; and to him shall be the obedience of the peoples."
Authority would remain with Judah "until" the one comes to whom it belongs — and to whom even the nations will be obedient. With the loss of Jewish self-rule and records after AD 70, the tribal identity of a future claimant became unverifiable. The "until" points to a coming already in the Second Temple period.
Malachi 3:1
OO
"Behold, I send my messenger, and he will prepare the way before me. And the Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple… behold, he is coming, says the LORD of hosts."
"The Lord" comes to "his temple" — which requires the Temple to be standing at His coming. After its destruction in AD 70, this could no longer be fulfilled in the way the prophet describes. So the Lord's coming to the Temple must have occurred before then.

Put together with Daniel's seventy weeks, the picture is consistent: the Messiah's first coming and "cutting off" were to fall in the era of the Second Temple, and the records needed to prove Davidic descent perished with it. A Messiah who arrives only after AD 70 cannot, in principle, demonstrate the lineage and Temple-presence that the prophets require — but Jesus, who came before, can.

IX. The Fathers Speak

▼

From the earliest centuries, Christians argued for Jesus as Israel's Messiah precisely from the Hebrew Scriptures, often in direct dialogue with Jewish teachers. Two witnesses stand out — one Greek, one Syriac (and so especially dear to the Oriental Orthodox tradition).

St. Justin Martyr (c. 100–165)
Dialogue with Trypho the Jew — c. AD 160 · ANF vol. 1 Paraphrase
The prophets foretold two advents of the Christ: one in which He comes in dishonour and suffering, despised and pierced; and a second in which He comes in glory from the clouds of heaven, as Daniel foretold of the Son of Man.
Significance: In the earliest surviving Christian–Jewish dialogue, Justin already answers Trypho's objection by distinguishing the two comings of the Messiah from the Hebrew prophets themselves. The "suffering then glory" solution is second-century, argued from Scripture, not a modern device. (Paraphrase of Justin's argument across Dialogue chs. 32, 49, and 110.)
Aphrahat the Persian Sage (c. 280–345)
Demonstrations 17, "On Christ the Son of God" · Syriac, NPNF Series 2, vol. 13 Paraphrase
From the Law and the Prophets it is shown that the Messiah was to come of the seed of David, to be born in Bethlehem, to suffer and be rejected, and yet to be honoured as Lord — and that in Him the nations would come to worship the God of Israel.
Significance: Aphrahat, a Syriac-speaking father writing in the Persian Empire, frames the whole case from the Hebrew Scriptures in conversation with Jewish neighbours — a model close to the Oriental Orthodox heritage. He shows the messianic argument was being made in the Semitic East as well as the Greek West. (Paraphrase of the themes of Demonstration 17.)
St. Athanasius the Great (c. 296–373)
On the Incarnation, §40 — NPNF Series 2, vol. 4 Paraphrase
When did prophet and king cease in Israel, and Jerusalem and the Temple stand desolate, except from the time that Christ came? For the prophecy held only until His coming.
Significance: Athanasius makes the same historical argument as Section VIII — that the ceasing of the Jewish polity, prophecy, and Temple after the first century is itself a sign that the Messiah of the prophets had already appeared. (Paraphrase of On the Incarnation §§37–40.)

X. Objections & Responses

▼

"The Messiah was to bring world peace and gather the exiles. The world is not at peace, so Jesus is not the Messiah."

▼

Response

This is the most serious objection, and it assumes the Messiah comes only once. But the Hebrew Scriptures describe two incompatible profiles — a suffering, "cut off" Servant (Isaiah 53; Daniel 9) and a glorious everlasting King (Isaiah 9; Daniel 7). They cannot both be true of a single coming. Christians hold that the Messiah comes twice: first to atone (already accomplished), then to reign in the peace of the messianic age (still awaited). Strikingly, the rabbis felt the same tension and proposed two Messiahs — ben Joseph (suffering) and ben David (reigning). The unfulfilled peace is not evidence against Jesus; it is the still-future part of His own work. See Section VII.

"Isaiah 53 is about the nation of Israel suffering among the gentiles, not about a personal Messiah."

▼

Response

The text resists this. The Servant suffers "for the transgression of my people" (53:8) — distinguishing the Servant from the people he suffers for; Israel cannot atone for Israel. The Servant is sinless ("no deceit in his mouth," 53:9), which is not said of the nation. And the national reading is comparatively late, becoming standard with Rashi in the 11th century, partly in response to Christian usage; older Jewish sources (the Targum; Sanhedrin 98b) read it of the Messiah. The plain sense — an innocent individual suffering and dying for others, then exalted — fits a person, not a personified nation.

"Isaiah 7:14 says 'young woman' (almah), not 'virgin' — so the virgin birth rests on a mistranslation."

▼

Response

The Hebrew almah denotes a young woman of marriageable age, and in its biblical uses she is unmarried — hence the assumption of virginity. Decisively, Jewish scholars themselves, in the Septuagint (c. 2nd century BC, long before Christ), translated almah here with the Greek parthenos, "virgin." So the "virgin" reading is pre-Christian and Jewish in origin, not a later Christian twist. And the verse calls the child Immanuel, "God with us" — a name pointing beyond an ordinary birth. Christians read this within the larger pattern of messianic prophecy, not as a lone proof-text.

"Christianity worships three gods (or a man as God), which violates the Shema — 'the LORD is one.'"

▼

Response

Christians wholeheartedly confess the Shema: there is one God (Deuteronomy 6:4). The doctrine of the Trinity is not three gods but one God in three Persons — one indivisible divine essence. The Hebrew Scriptures themselves contain hints of plurality-in-unity within the one God: the plural "let us make man" (Genesis 1:26), the Angel of the LORD who is both distinct from and identified with God, the Word and Spirit of God as agents of His action, and Zechariah 12:10, where the LORD says they will look on "me" yet mourn "him whom they pierced." On Jesus' divinity, that the Messiah is more than merely human is suggested by Micah 5:2 ("from ancient days"), Isaiah 9:6 ("Mighty God"), and Daniel 7's heavenly Son of Man. (See this site's page on the Trinity.)

"Jesus didn't keep or fulfil the Torah, and Christianity abandoned the Law — so He broke the covenant."

▼

Response

Jesus lived as an observant Jew and said He came "not to abolish the Law… but to fulfil" it (Matthew 5:17). The prophets themselves foretold a new covenant written on the heart (Jeremiah 31:31–34) and a day when the nations would worship the God of Israel (Isaiah 49:6; 56:6–7; Zechariah 8:23) — which is exactly what has happened through the Jewish Messiah. The Church holds that the ceremonial and sacrificial Law pointed forward to the Messiah and finds its goal in Him, while the moral law abides. Far from breaking the covenant, the Messiah extends the knowledge of the God of Israel to the whole world, as the prophets said He would.

XI. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Q: Is it antisemitic to say Jesus is the Messiah?▼

No. Believing that the Jewish Messiah has come is not hatred of Jews — it is, in fact, a deeply Jewish hope brought to fulfilment. What is sinful is contempt, persecution, or collective blame, which the Church condemns. We honour the Jewish people as the root onto which we gentiles were grafted (Romans 11), and we hold this conviction in love, not enmity.

Q: Don't Christians just quote the Old Testament out of context?▼

It is a fair caution, and some popular apologetics do overreach. That is why this page leans on passages whose messianic force is recognised even within Jewish tradition (Isaiah 53 in the Targum and Talmud; Bethlehem as the Messiah's birthplace; the two-Messiah idea), and is honest about textual variants (Psalm 22:16). The cumulative case — birthplace, lineage, timing, suffering, vindication — is stronger than any single verse.

Q: Why do most Jews not accept Jesus, if the case is so clear?▼

The reasons are historical and human as much as exegetical: the expectation of a one-time, triumphant deliverer; the trauma of centuries of persecution carried out in Jesus' name, which understandably hardened hearts against Him; and the development of rabbinic Judaism as a distinct tradition after AD 70. None of this settles the question of truth, but it explains why sincere, faithful people read the same Scriptures differently — and why Christians should approach the conversation with humility and repentance for past wrongs.

Q: What is the single strongest line of argument?▼

The convergence of timing and suffering. Daniel 9 and the AD 70 destruction place the Messiah's "cutting off" in the Second Temple era; Genesis 49:10 and Malachi 3:1 require Him to come while Judah's identity and the Temple still stood; and Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, and Zechariah 12 describe Him as pierced and dying for others. A suffering, atoning Messiah who comes before AD 70 fits Jesus precisely — and no later claimant can meet those conditions.
