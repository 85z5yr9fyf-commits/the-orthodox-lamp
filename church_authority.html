The Authorial Power of the Church

Christ did not leave a book — He founded a Church. The Holy Spirit guides not individual readers but the Body of Christ assembled in council, preserving the Faith once delivered to the saints. This is the pillar and bulwark of the truth.

1 Timothy 3:15 — The Church as Pillar and Bulwark of Truth

▼
The Foundational Text
1 Timothy 3:15 — Written c. AD 62–65
"…so that you may know how one ought to behave in the household of God, which is the Church of the living God, the pillar and bulwark of the truth."

στῦλος (stulos) — Pillar

A load-bearing column that holds up the structure above it. The Church upholds truth — truth does not float free of the Church, waiting to be discovered by individual readers.

ἑδραίωμα (hedraiōma) — Bulwark

A foundation or stay that prevents collapse. The Church stabilizes truth against error, heresy, and private misinterpretation. It is the ground of certainty, not Scripture alone.

Notice what Paul does not say. He does not say the Bible is the pillar and bulwark of truth — he says the Church is. This does not pit Scripture against the Church; both are authoritative. But the living organism through which truth is preserved, proclaimed, and defended is the Church, not a text floating independently of its community.

This was the universal understanding of the pre-Reformation church. Patristic writers cited this verse consistently when defending the authority of councils and the episcopate. It was not a Catholic invention — it was the received tradition of East and West.

Acts 20:28 — Paul to the Ephesian Elders
"Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood."
The Holy Spirit appoints overseers (ἐπισκόπους — bishops) to guard the Church. Authority over God's flock is pneumatically conferred, not self-assumed.
Oriental OrthodoxEcclesiology
John 16:13 — Promise of the Spirit
"When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth…"
The "you" is plural — addressed to the Apostles and their successors as a body, not to every individual Christian privately. The Spirit's guidance is ecclesial, not individualistic.
Oriental OrthodoxGreek: ὑμᾶς (hymas) — plural

Matthew 16:18–19 — The Keys of the Kingdom

▼
Matthew 16:18–19
"And I tell you, you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it. I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."
The Church is Christ's own — He builds it, He owns it, and He promises it indestructibility. The keys represent governing authority, entrusted first to the Apostolic college.
Oriental OrthodoxApostolic Authority

The Oriental Orthodox reading of this passage is neither Roman Catholic nor Protestant. We do not read it as granting permanent monarchical supremacy to one bishop (the Pope), nor do we dismiss it as merely Peter's personal confession. We read it ecclesiologically: Christ builds His Church on the Apostolic faith, entrusting governance to the Apostles collectively, carried forward in the episcopate.

The "keys" in Jewish tradition are administrative — they govern access and authority within the household (cf. Isaiah 22:22 — the key of David placed on Eliakim's shoulder as a type). This is an image of genuine governing authority granted to the Church's leadership.

Isaiah 22:22 — OT Type of the Keys
"And I will place on his shoulder the key of the house of David. He shall open, and none shall shut; and he shall shut, and none shall open."
The key of the house of David is a symbol of stewardship and administrative authority. Matthew 16 deliberately echoes this — the Church exercises stewardship over the Kingdom under Christ the King.
TypologyOT Fulfillment
OO Position: The keys were given to Peter as head of the Apostolic college and, through him, to the entire episcopate in Apostolic succession. Authority belongs to the Church as a whole, exercised collegially through her bishops — not concentrated in any single bishop as a jurisdictional monarch.

Matthew 18:15–20 — Binding and Loosing

▼
Matthew 18:17–20
"If he refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church. And if he refuses to listen even to the church, let him be to you as a Gentile and a tax collector. Truly, I say to you, whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven… For where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them."
Binding and loosing authority here is given not just to Peter but to the Church assembled. The Church has genuine judicial authority over her members — including discipline, canon law, and doctrinal definition.
Oriental OrthodoxChurch Discipline

Here binding and loosing extend to the full community of believers under apostolic leadership. The phrase "tell it to the church" (εἰπὲ τῇ ἐκκλησίᾳ) presupposes an actual structured body capable of rendering authoritative judgment — not a vague spiritual community where each member decides for themselves.

This passage was understood from the earliest centuries as establishing the Church's authority to:

  • Excommunicate unrepentant sinners
  • Define dogma in council
  • Pronounce on disputed interpretations of Scripture
  • Regulate liturgical and canonical practice
St. Cyprian of Carthage
c. 250 AD — On the Unity of the Church
"He cannot have God as his Father who does not have the Church as his Mother… Whoever is separated from the Church is joined to an adulteress."
De Catholicae Ecclesiae Unitate, §6
St. Ignatius of Antioch
c. 107 AD — To the Smyrnaeans
"Wherever the bishop appears, there let the congregation be… wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church."
To the Smyrnaeans, §8

Acts 15 — The Jerusalem Council as the Conciliar Model

▼

Acts 15 is the New Testament's own precedent for how doctrinal disputes are resolved. When controversy arose over circumcision and the Law, the Church did not appeal to private interpretation of Scripture. She convened a council.

Acts 15:6, 28
"The apostles and the elders were gathered together to consider this matter… For it has seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us to lay on you no greater burden than these requirements…"
The council's decree is issued with co-authorship: "the Holy Spirit and us." This is the foundational pattern of conciliar authority — the bishops assembled in the Spirit speak with binding authority for the whole Church.
Oriental OrthodoxConciliarity

The phrase "It seemed good to the Holy Spirit and to us" (ἔδοξεν γὰρ τῷ πνεύματι τῷ ἁγίῳ καὶ ἡμῖν) became the template for every subsequent ecumenical council. This is not arrogance — it is faith that the Spirit of truth keeps His promise to guide the Church into all truth.

Key observation: The Jerusalem Council did not resolve the controversy by having each believer read Leviticus privately and reach their own conclusion. It resolved it through the authoritative judgment of the Apostles and elders gathered in the name of Christ. This is the biblical model of church authority.
St. Athanasius of Alexandria
c. 369 AD — Letter to the Bishops of Africa
"The word of the Lord which was given through the ecumenical council at Nicaea abides forever. Whatever the synod determined was not determined by mere human reasoning, but was taught by the Spirit."
De Decretis, §4
St. Vincent of Lérins
c. 434 AD — Commonitory
"What has been believed everywhere, always, and by all — this is truly and properly Catholic. This is what is indicated by the authority of the universal Church herself."
Commonitorium, §II

The Church Defined the Biblical Canon

▼

One of the most decisive demonstrations of the Church's authority is this: the Bible did not produce the Church — the Church produced the Bible's canon. The New Testament canon did not fall from heaven as a table of contents. It was discerned, debated, and defined by the Church.

If sola scriptura — Scripture alone — is the final authority, then on what authority does the Protestant know which books belong in Scripture? The canon itself is a Church decision. To accept the canon is to trust the Church. To reject the Church's authority is to saw off the branch you are sitting on.
Council of Laodicea
c. 363 AD
Among the earliest councils to list approved canonical books for public reading in churches.
First canonical list excluding Revelation
Council of Hippo
393 AD
North African synod affirms the 27-book NT canon and sends its decision to wider churches for confirmation.
Canon ratified with deuterocanon included
Council of Carthage III
397 AD
Reaffirms the canon of Hippo. The canonical list is accepted East and West.
Canon consensus across major sees
Quinisext Council (Trullo)
692 AD
Eastern ecumenical council reaffirms the canonical list for the universal Church. The OO tradition receives this.
Eastern canonical confirmation

The process of canonical discernment used criteria: apostolicity (was it from an apostle or apostolic companion?), catholicity (was it received throughout the churches?), and orthodoxy (did its content accord with the rule of faith?). All three criteria are ecclesial — they presuppose a Church with the authority to judge.

Historical footnote: Martin Luther himself doubted James ("an epistle of straw"), Hebrews, Jude, and Revelation. His personal canon would have been smaller than the Church's. This illustrates exactly where private judgment leads when severed from the Church's authority.

Apostolic Succession — The Unbroken Chain of Authority

▼

The Church's authority is not self-generated by each generation. It is transmitted. The Apostles ordained bishops; those bishops ordained more bishops; and that chain of ordination — each bishop receiving the laying on of hands from another bishop in an unbroken line back to the Apostles — is apostolic succession.

2 Timothy 2:2
"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses, entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
Paul explicitly instructs Timothy to pass on the deposit of faith through a chain of trustworthy transmission: Paul → Timothy → faithful men → others. This is the logic of apostolic succession.
Oriental OrthodoxSuccession of Teaching
Titus 1:5
"This is why I left you in Crete, so that you might put what remained into order, and appoint elders in every town as I directed you."
Apostolic authority to appoint clergy is delegated and structured — bishops serve as Apostolic representatives in each locale.
Oriental OrthodoxEpiscopal Office
Christ

Commissions the Apostles: "As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you." (John 20:21)

The Twelve Apostles

Lay hands on deacons (Acts 6:6), ordain elders (Acts 14:23), consecrate bishops

First Generation Bishops

Timothy, Titus, Clement, Ignatius, Polycarp — ordained by Apostles or their immediate delegates

Patristic Episcopate

The great Fathers — Athanasius, Basil, Chrysostom, Cyril of Alexandria — all in the unbroken chain

Today's OO Bishops

Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara — all in direct succession from the Apostolic age

St. Irenaeus of Lyon
c. 180 AD — Against Heresies
"It is within the power of all… who may wish to see the truth, to contemplate clearly the tradition of the apostles manifested throughout the whole world; and we are in a position to reckon up those who were by the apostles instituted bishops in the churches, and the successors of these men to our own times."
Adversus Haereses, III.3.1
Tertullian
c. 200 AD — Prescription Against Heretics
"Let them produce the origins of their churches; let them unfold the roll of their bishops, running down in succession from the beginning… For this is the manner in which the apostolic churches transmit their registers."
De Praescriptione Haereticorum, §32

The Ecumenical Councils — Conciliar Authority in History

▼

The Oriental Orthodox Church receives the first three Ecumenical Councils as authoritative definitions of the faith. The conciliar process — bishops gathered from across the Church, debating, praying, and defining — is the Spirit-guided mechanism by which the Church authoritatively interprets and guards the Apostolic deposit.

First Council of Nicaea
325 AD
Defined the full divinity of the Son against Arianism. Established the Nicene Creed. 318 bishops gathered from across the Roman Empire.
Condemned Arianism — "of the same substance" (ὁμοούσιος)
First Council of Constantinople
381 AD
Defined the full divinity of the Holy Spirit. Expanded the Nicene Creed. Condemned Macedonianism and Apollinarianism.
Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed finalized
Council of Ephesus
431 AD
Defined Mary as Theotokos (God-bearer), affirming the full unity of Christ's person. Condemned Nestorianism.
Unity of Christ's person — one prosopon

Regarding Chalcedon (451 AD) and the councils after: the Oriental Orthodox reject Chalcedon not because they reject conciliar authority, but because they believe Chalcedon erred in its formulation of Christ's nature — introducing a Nestorian tendency. This itself is an exercise of conciliar discernment, not its abandonment.

The OO principle: Councils are authoritative when they truly speak for the whole Church, confirmed by apostolic sees, received by the faithful (sensus fidelium). A council that errs — as the OO believe Chalcedon did on Christology — can be recognized as such by the broader tradition. Authority is real but not unconditional.

The Church's Interpretive Authority Over Scripture

▼

The Oriental Orthodox tradition does not teach that the Church invented doctrines or stands above Scripture. Rather, the Church is the God-appointed community within which Scripture was written, collected, preserved, and rightly interpreted. Scripture and Tradition are not two separate channels — they are one Apostolic deposit, of which the Church is the keeper.

2 Peter 1:20–21
"Knowing this first of all, that no prophecy of Scripture comes from someone's own interpretation. For no prophecy was ever produced by the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."
If prophecy does not originate in private human will, neither is it properly interpreted by private human will. The same Spirit who inspired it guides its interpretation — through the Church.
Oriental OrthodoxInterpretation
2 Peter 3:16
"…as he does in all his letters when he speaks in them of these matters. There are some things in them that are hard to understand, which the ignorant and unstable twist to their own destruction, as they do the other Scriptures."
Peter explicitly warns that Scripture can be twisted. The existence of this danger implies the necessity of an authoritative interpreter — the Church — to guard against distortion.
Oriental OrthodoxHermeneutics
St. Augustine of Hippo
c. 400 AD — Against the Fundamental Epistle of Manichaeus
"I would not believe in the Gospel if the authority of the Catholic Church did not move me to do so."
Contra Epistulam Manichaei, §5
St. John Chrysostom
c. 390 AD — On the First Epistle to the Corinthians
"Many disputes have arisen, and yet the Church stands. Heresies have beaten against her, yet she has not been overthrown, because she is founded on the Rock."
Homily 3 on 1 Corinthians

The Oriental Orthodox Fathers consistently read Scripture within the "rule of faith" (κανὼν τῆς πίστεως / kanon tes pisteos) — the summary of Apostolic teaching, preserved and taught by the Church. A text interpreted outside this rule is a text misread, however sincerely.

Three Models: OO Conciliarism vs. Sola Scriptura vs. Roman Papalism

▼

Oriental Orthodox

  • Authority is conciliar — bishops gathered in the Spirit
  • Scripture and Tradition as one unified Apostolic deposit
  • Ecumenical councils define and protect the faith
  • No single bishop has universal jurisdiction
  • Reception by the faithful confirms conciliar truth
  • The Church interprets Scripture; Scripture judges proposed councils
  • Apostolic succession guarantees continuity of ministry

Protestant Sola Scriptura

  • Scripture alone is the final authority
  • Individual or community interprets Scripture directly
  • Church traditions are secondary and revisable
  • No binding conciliar authority beyond Scripture
  • Apostolic succession not required or recognized
  • Result: thousands of denominations, each with its own reading
  • Canon accepted without account for who defined it

Roman Catholic Papalism

  • Pope holds supreme and universal jurisdiction
  • Ex cathedra papal definitions are infallible
  • Scripture, Tradition, and Magisterium as three sources
  • Councils derive authority from papal confirmation
  • Apostolic succession through Roman succession emphasized
  • Developed dogmas (Immaculate Conception, Assumption) added
  • OO view: papalism is an innovation beyond the early Church

The OO position occupies a distinct space: genuine authority that is neither individualistic (sola scriptura) nor monarchical (papalism). It is collegial, conciliar, and pneumatic — the bishops together in the Spirit, confirmed by the reception of the faithful, rooted in the Apostolic succession.

Scripture and Church — Not Rivals but Partners

▼

A common misunderstanding is that upholding Church authority means denigrating Scripture. This is a false dilemma. The Oriental Orthodox position is that Scripture is the inspired Word of God — authoritative, sufficient for salvation when rightly understood — but its right understanding requires the community that produced it, preserved it, and is guided by the same Spirit who inspired it.

Ephesians 2:19–20
"So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus himself being the cornerstone."
The Church is built on the Apostles — their persons, their teaching, their authority — not on texts floating free of that community. The Apostles are the foundation; Scripture is their inspired testimony within that foundation.
Oriental OrthodoxEcclesiology
Acts 8:30–31 — The Ethiopian Eunuch
"And Philip ran to him and heard him reading Isaiah the prophet and asked, 'Do you understand what you are reading?' And he said, 'How can I, unless someone guides me?' And he invited Philip to come up and sit with him."
Even a devout man reading Scripture could not understand it without a guide. Philip — an ordained deacon in the Apostolic community — provides the authoritative interpretation. This is the Church's role.
Oriental OrthodoxInterpretation

The Oriental Orthodox do not pit Scripture against Tradition or Tradition against Scripture. Both are expressions of the one Apostolic Faith. Tradition is the living transmission of that faith — in liturgy, in councils, in the writings of the Fathers, in the prayers of the Church. Scripture is its primary written expression. Together, they are guarded by the Church and interpreted by her bishops in council.

Protestant Objections Answered

▼

"The Church has been wrong before — councils have contradicted each other. How can you trust it?"

▼

Local and regional councils have erred. The OO tradition does not claim that every gathering of bishops is infallible. True ecumenical councils — received by the whole Church, conforming to the Apostolic rule of faith — carry the Spirit's guidance. The test is reception: a council that contradicts the faith once delivered is recognized and rejected, as the Oriental Orthodox rejected Chalcedon's Christological formulation.

Crucially, the existence of erring councils does not vindicate sola scriptura. Protestants also have "councils" — denominations, confessions, synods — that contradict each other at least as dramatically as any disputed councils in Church history. The question is not whether human gatherings can err, but what the Spirit-guided mechanism for discernment is. The OO answer: the conciliar process of the episcopate, confirmed by the faithful.

"Acts 17:11 — the Bereans tested Paul against Scripture. Scripture judges everything, including the Church."

▼

The Bereans were commended for checking whether Paul's preaching matched the Old Testament Scriptures they already had. This is good practice — but notice what they were checking: not private impressions, but a received body of scripture interpreted within a community. They were verifying apostolic teaching against authoritative text, not deciding doctrine independently.

More importantly, the Bereans were not deciding the canon of the New Testament, defining Trinitarian doctrine, or settling Christological controversies. For those tasks, the Apostles and their successors — not the private Bible-reading individual — were the appointed instruments. The Berean example does not underwrite the Protestant doctrine of sola scriptura.

"Church authority produced the papacy and all its abuses. Church authority always corrupts."

▼

The Oriental Orthodox agree that the monarchical papacy as it developed in Rome is an innovation beyond the early Church's conciliar model. This is precisely our critique of Rome. But the solution to a corrupt form of authority is not the abolition of authority — it is the restoration of its proper, conciliar, collegial form.

Every human institution can be corrupted. But the answer to a corrupt government is not anarchy; the answer to abuse of church authority is not the radical individualism of sola scriptura, which has produced thousands of contradictory denominations each claiming to read the Bible correctly. The OO commends the ancient conciliar model, not the monarchical model, as the Spirit's intended instrument.

"Matthew 16 is about Peter's confession, not about any office or institution."

▼

Many Protestants argue that the "rock" on which Christ builds His Church is Peter's confession of faith, not Peter himself or any office. This is a defensible reading — even Augustine held it at times — and the Oriental Orthodox do not require a strictly papal reading.

But the keys given in verse 19 cannot be limited to a confession. Keys are administrative instruments. "Whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven" refers to ongoing governance, not a one-time act of faith. The promise of indestructibility (v.18) belongs to the institutional Church, not merely to a correct doctrinal position floating in the air. At minimum, this passage establishes that Christ founded an enduring Church with governing authority — which is precisely the OO claim.

"The early church was decentralized — there was no single authoritative voice."

▼

The early Church was decentralized geographically but united structurally. Each bishop governed his see; the Apostolic sees (Jerusalem, Antioch, Alexandria, Rome, later Constantinople) carried special weight; and doctrinal questions were settled by councils whose decisions were binding on all.

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 107 AD) — within living memory of the Apostles — already describes a fully structured Church with bishops, presbyters, and deacons as essential, not optional. By the time of Irenaeus (c. 180 AD) the logic of apostolic succession and conciliar authority is fully articulated. The "decentralized early church" is a historical myth constructed to validate later Protestant practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

▼

Does the OO Church claim to be infallible?

▼

The Oriental Orthodox Church claims that the Holy Spirit guides the Church into all truth (John 16:13) and that the gates of hell will not prevail against her (Matthew 16:18). This means the Church will not definitively and permanently err in defining the Apostolic faith. This is not the same as claiming that every bishop, synod, or council is automatically infallible. Regional councils can err; the conciliar process, guided by the Spirit and confirmed by reception of the faithful, discerns and corrects such errors.

What is the difference between the OO view and the Roman Catholic view of authority?

▼

Rome locates final authority in the office of the Bishop of Rome, whose ex cathedra definitions are infallible. Councils derive their authority from papal confirmation. The OO locates authority in the episcopate gathered in council — a collegial body, not a monarchy. No single bishop holds universal jurisdiction over the others. The Bishop of Rome is honored as "first among equals" in the early Church (as is the OO Pope of Alexandria in our tradition), but "first among equals" is not supreme governor. The Chalcedonian formula granting Rome primacy of honor was always understood in the East as honorific, not jurisdictional.

How does the OO Church handle new moral or social questions not addressed in Scripture?

▼

The Church applies the Apostolic deposit — Scripture, Tradition, the mind of the Fathers, and the principles discerned by ecumenical councils — to new questions. This is not innovation; it is the living application of a received deposit. The same Spirit who guided the Jerusalem Council to answer the circumcision question guides the Church today. A question unaddressed in Scripture is not therefore unanswerable — the Church, as pillar and bulwark of truth, has the authority and the Spirit-given capacity to discern.

What does Oriental Orthodox authority look like in practice today?

▼

Each of the Oriental Orthodox churches (Coptic, Ethiopian/Eritrean, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara) is governed by its patriarch and Holy Synod. Major questions are addressed by synodal decisions. The churches maintain communion with one another, sharing the same Christological faith. Pan-Oriental Orthodox gatherings address common concerns. While there is no single supreme head, the churches are united in faith, sacraments, and apostolic order — a genuine koinonia (communion) rather than a confederation of independent congregations.

Isn't saying "the Church defines the canon" circular? The Church uses the Bible to prove its authority.

▼

The relationship is not circular but organic. The Apostolic community preceded the New Testament documents — the Church was there before the letters were written. The documents were produced within the community, addressed to the community, and preserved by the community. When the Church later defined the canon, she was not arrogating a foreign authority over an alien book; she was recognizing her own Apostolic testimony. Scripture and Church are not rivals; they are inseparable expressions of the one Apostolic deposit.

The Protestant position, by contrast, has a genuine circularity problem: the Bible is authoritative because it says so (Sola Scriptura justified by 2 Timothy 3:16–17 is circular), and the canon is whatever books the individual believes belong — without any external criterion beyond private judgment.

Glossary of Key Terms

▼
Apostolic Succession
διαδοχή — diadochē
The unbroken chain of episcopal ordination from the Apostles to the present bishops of the Church, guaranteeing continuity of ministry and authority.
Conciliarity
συνοδικότης — synodikotēs
The principle that doctrinal authority is exercised by the bishops assembled in council, not by any single bishop. The Spirit guides the Church through this collegial process.
Canon
κανών — kanōn
Literally "rule" or "measuring rod." The list of books recognized by the Church as divinely inspired Scripture, defined through a conciliar process over several centuries.
Sola Scriptura
Latin: Scripture Alone
The Protestant doctrine that Scripture alone, without Tradition or Church, is the final authority for Christian faith and practice. The OO regards this as an innovation absent from the early Church.
Sensus Fidelium
Latin: Sense of the Faithful
The instinct of the whole faithful body — laity and clergy — by which the truth is recognized and received. Councils are confirmed when received by the people of God.
Rule of Faith
κανὼν τῆς πίστεως — kanōn tēs pisteōs
The summary of Apostolic doctrine — essentially the Creed — used as a hermeneutical guide for interpreting Scripture. Irenaeus and Tertullian both appeal to it against Gnostic misreadings.
Ecumenical Council
Οἰκουμενικὴ Σύνοδος
A gathering of bishops from across the whole Church, whose definitions on faith and morals carry binding authority when received by the universal Church.
Koinonia
κοινωνία — communion
The deep sharing of faith, sacraments, and apostolic life that constitutes the unity of the Church. The OO churches maintain koinonia with one another as the visible expression of ecclesial unity.
