Miaphysite Christology

One incarnate nature of God the Word — the faith of St. Cyril and the Oriental Orthodox

For fifteen centuries the Oriental Orthodox have been accused of a heresy they never held. “Monophysite” — the charge that they merge Christ’s divinity and humanity into one blended nature — describes Eutyches, whom the Oriental Orthodox themselves condemned. Their true confession is Miaphysite: one united nature of the incarnate Word, fully God and fully man, without confusion, change, division, or separation.

What Miaphysitism Is — and Is Not

▶
Monophysitism (Eutyches) — condemned
  • The humanity is absorbed into the divinity — ‘like a drop of honey in the sea’
  • Christ’s human nature ceases to be fully human after the union
  • One simple nature (Greek monos = single, sole)
  • Condemned by the Oriental Orthodox as firmly as by Chalcedon
Miaphysitism (St. Cyril) — Oriental Orthodox
  • Divinity and humanity are united, each remaining whole and complete
  • Christ is fully God and fully man — the manhood is never diminished
  • One composite nature (Greek mia = a united one, not monos)
  • Without confusion, without change — and without division or separation

The whole misunderstanding rests on two Greek words. Monos means single, sole, alone. Mia is the ordinary feminine word for one — the same ‘one’ used of a married couple who become “one flesh,” two whole realities in one union. The Oriental Orthodox have always confessed mia physis, never mono physis. Eutyches, who taught that Christ’s humanity was swallowed up by his divinity, was rejected by them — his own condemned formula shows exactly what Miaphysites do not believe:

Eutyches (condemned, d. c. 456)
“I confess that our Lord was of two natures before the union, but after the union I confess one nature.”
Leo’s Tome to Flavian — Eutyches’ own words, recorded and condemned Direct quote

St. Cyril’s Formula

▶

The theological heart of Oriental Orthodoxy is the Christology of St. Cyril of Alexandria (d. 444), the great champion against Nestorius at the Council of Ephesus (431). Cyril’s celebrated phrase became the touchstone of the tradition:

St. Cyril’s Formula
Greek: mia physis tou Theou Logou sesarkomenē (μία φύσις τού Θεού Λόγου σεσαρκωμένη)
“One incarnate nature of God the Word.” The key word is sesarkomenē — “enfleshed.” The one nature is not divinity alone; it is the divine Word who has taken full humanity into his own single subsistence. The flesh is not deleted; it is what the “one nature” is incarnate in.

Cyril believed the phrase came from St. Athanasius (in fact it descended from an Apollinarian source), but he used it in a strictly orthodox sense, insisting always on the completeness of Christ’s humanity — a rational soul and a real body. He was equally willing to speak of Christ “in two natures” in his reconciliation with the Antiochenes (the Formula of Reunion, 433). For Cyril the concern was never to erase the humanity but to affirm that there is one Christ, one Son, one acting subject — not two persons loosely joined, as Nestorius seemed to teach.

St. Cyril of Alexandria (d. 444)
There is one Lord Jesus Christ — the Word of God united to flesh, not by a mingling or blending of natures, but so that the same one is at once truly God and truly man, the natures that were brought together in a true union being without division.
Third Letter to Nestorius — the union without division or confusion Paraphrase

Chalcedon 451 — The Tragedy of Words

▶

In 451 the Council of Chalcedon defined that Christ is made known in two natures (Greek en dyo physesin), united in one person and hypostasis. To guard against Nestorius it added four adverbs — the union is:

The Chalcedonian Definition
“…made known in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation” (asynchytōs, atreptōs, adiairetōs, achōristōs).

The Oriental Orthodox affirm every one of those four adverbs. Their objection was to the phrase in two natures. To Cyrilline ears, after the union one should speak of the one incarnate nature; to number “two natures” following the union sounded like the Nestorian division they had just condemned at Ephesus. It did not help that Chalcedon leaned on the Latin theology of Pope Leo’s Tome, which spoke of each nature “doing what belongs to it” — language that, read in isolation, seemed to make Christ two operating subjects:

Pope St. Leo the Great (Tome to Flavian, 449)
“For each ‘form’ does the acts which belong to it, in communion with the other; the Word, that is, performing what belongs to the Word, and the flesh carrying out what belongs to the flesh.”
Leo’s Tome to Flavian — the phrase Cyrilline theologians found dangerously divisive Direct quote

Chalcedon deposed St. Dioscorus of Alexandria, Cyril’s successor — the Oriental Orthodox hold, not for heresy, but for refusing to sign the new formula and for his handling of the earlier council. What followed was not, at root, two different faiths, but two vocabularies for one mystery — hardened by imperial politics, language barriers, and mutual anathema into a schism that has lasted to this day.

St. Severus of Antioch

▶

If Cyril is the fountainhead, St. Severus of Antioch (d. 538) is the great systematizer of miaphysite theology after Chalcedon. Against both Nestorius and Eutyches he insisted with precision: in Christ there is one nature out of two — a real composition in which the divinity and the humanity each remain complete, with all their natural properties intact, yet belong to a single incarnate subject. Severus taught that the difference between the natures is preserved in contemplation (the mind can distinguish godhead and manhood) even though they are never separated in reality.

This is why the miaphysite tradition can affirm everything Chalcedon wanted to protect — that Christ is consubstantial with the Father in his divinity and consubstantial with us in his humanity, that he hungered and wept as man and raised the dead as God — while still confessing the one incarnate nature. The properties of both natures are fully present; they are simply not parceled out to two subjects.

Physis, Hypostasis, Ousia — The Semantic Knot

▶

Much of the division comes down to the fact that the same Greek words carried different weights in Alexandria and Constantinople. Untangling them shows how much of the quarrel was linguistic:

Ousia (οὐσία)
Essence or being — what a thing is in common with others of its kind. Both sides agree Christ is one ousia with the Father (divinity) and one with us (humanity).
Hypostasis (ὑπόστασις)
The concrete individual subject — the “who.” Both families confess one hypostasis in Christ: one Son, one person. There is no disagreement here.
Physis (φύσις)
Nature. Here is the knot. For Cyril and Severus, physis often meant a concrete existing reality — close to hypostasis. For Chalcedon it meant the abstract “whatness.” So “one physis” (OO) and “two physes” (Chalcedon) could describe the same Christ.

When an Oriental Orthodox says “one nature,” he means one concrete Christ, one acting subject — exactly what Chalcedon meant by “one hypostasis.” When a Chalcedonian says “two natures,” he means the abiding fullness of godhead and manhood — exactly what the Oriental Orthodox mean by confessing Christ “out of two” and consubstantial with both the Father and us. The modern dialogues concluded that the two families have been confessing one faith in two idioms.

Are We Really Divided?

▶

In the twentieth century, unofficial and then official theological dialogues between the Eastern Orthodox and the Oriental Orthodox worked patiently through the old vocabulary. Their conclusions were striking.

The First Agreed Statement (Anba Bishoy Monastery, Egypt, 1989) declared that both families have always been faithful to the same apostolic Christology of St. Cyril, in different terminology. The Second Agreed Statement (Chambésy, Geneva, 1990) put it plainly: the two families agree in substance on the doctrine of the person of Christ, each confessing the one Lord Jesus Christ, perfect in his divinity and perfect in his humanity; the Orthodox commission recommended lifting the mutual anathemas and condemnations of the past.

The Fruit of Dialogue
Both commissions concluded that the Oriental Orthodox are not Monophysites (Eutychians) and never were, and that the historic condemnation was based on a misreading of terminology rather than a genuine difference in faith. Full sacramental reunion still awaits reception, but the Christological barrier has, in the judgment of the theologians, fallen.

This is the great hope of the age: that a fifteen-hundred-year wound, born as much of Greek and Coptic and Syriac speaking past one another as of any real heresy, may yet be healed — because the one incarnate Word was never actually confessed in two contradictory ways, but adored by one Church in many tongues. The Scriptural Ground

▶

The one incarnate Word is not a philosophical construction but the plain confession of the New Testament: one Christ, in whom the fullness of God dwells bodily.

“And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth.”John 1:14 — not the Word alongside flesh, but the Word become flesh: one subject
“For in him the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily.”Colossians 2:9 — the total godhead and a real body, in one Christ
“Who, though he was in the form of God… emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men.”Philippians 2:6-7 — one ‘he’ who is both in the form of God and in the form of a servant

FAQ

▶
Are the Oriental Orthodox ‘Monophysites’?▶
No — this is the central misunderstanding. Monophysitism is the teaching of Eutyches, that Christ’s humanity was absorbed into his divinity so that only one (divine) nature remained. The Oriental Orthodox condemned Eutyches. Their position is Miaphysite: one united nature (Greek mia) in which both the full divinity and the full humanity are preserved without confusion. The modern EO–OO dialogues formally concluded they are not and never were Monophysites.
Do Miaphysites deny that Christ is fully human?▶
Not at all. St. Cyril and St. Severus insisted that Christ has a rational human soul and a real human body, consubstantial with us in everything except sin. He genuinely hungered, thirsted, wept, suffered, and died as man. The ‘one nature’ is the divine Word enfleshed — the humanity is exactly what he became incarnate in, never something deleted.
Was the split more about words than substance?▶
Largely, yes — that is the conclusion of both Orthodox families’ official commissions. The Greek word physis meant different things in Alexandria and Constantinople. When the Oriental Orthodox said ‘one nature’ they meant one concrete Christ (what Chalcedon called ‘one hypostasis’); when Chalcedon said ‘two natures’ it meant the abiding fullness of godhead and manhood, which the Oriental Orthodox also confess. Imperial politics and mutual anathemas then hardened a terminological difference into schism.
Do Eastern and Oriental Orthodox now agree on Christology?▶
The joint theological commissions (Agreed Statements of 1989 and 1990) concluded that the two families confess one and the same faith in the person of Christ, and recommended lifting the historic mutual condemnations. Full sacramental communion has not yet been formally restored, and reception varies among the churches, but the Christological barrier itself is widely regarded by the theologians of both families as resolved.
Why does the difference still matter if the faith is the same?▶
Because for fifteen centuries the Oriental Orthodox were slandered as heretics for a heresy they rejected, and cut off from communion over it. Recovering the true meaning of Miaphysite Christology vindicates the faith of Cyril, Dioscorus, Severus, and the martyrs, and opens the door — not yet walked all the way through — to the reunion of the ancient churches.

Glossary

▶
Miaphysite
The Oriental Orthodox Christology: one united nature of the incarnate Word (Greek mia physis), in which full divinity and full humanity are preserved without confusion or separation.
Monophysite
The heresy of Eutyches: one sole nature (Greek monos), the humanity being absorbed into the divinity. Condemned by the Oriental Orthodox themselves.
Dyophysite
The Chalcedonian and Eastern Orthodox / Catholic formula: Christ known in two natures after the union, in one person and hypostasis.
Hypostatic union
The union of divinity and humanity in the one person (hypostasis) of the Word. Affirmed by all sides — there is one Son, one subject, one Christ.
Theotokos
‘God-bearer,’ the title of the Virgin Mary defended by St. Cyril at Ephesus (431) — because the one born of her is the incarnate God the Word, not a mere man.
Communicatio idiomatum
The ‘communication of properties’: because there is one subject, what is said of the humanity and of the divinity is said of the one Christ — God was crucified in the flesh; the child in the manger upholds the universe.
