THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Miracles — Answering Hume and the Skeptics

"For with God nothing will be impossible" — Luke 1:37

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

Beneath most modern unbelief lies a single quiet premise: miracles do not happen. Grant that, and every argument on this site can be waved away in advance — the Resurrection must have a natural explanation, whatever the evidence, because resurrections are impossible. The premise rarely gets argued; it functions as the air educated people breathe. When it is argued, the argument is almost always David Hume's, from his 1748 essay "Of Miracles" — perhaps the most influential short essay ever written against the miraculous.

This page takes Hume seriously: states his case at full strength, then shows where it fails — partly through criticisms philosophers, including non-Christians, have pressed for two centuries. The stakes are concrete: if miracles cannot be ruled out in advance, then the evidence for the central Christian claim must actually be weighed, on the page where it is laid out (The Resurrection — Answering the Skeptics).

II. Hume's Argument, Stated Fairly

▼
David Hume — Scottish philosopher Direct Quote
An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding, Section X (1748)

"A miracle is a violation of the laws of nature; and as a firm and unalterable experience has established these laws, the proof against a miracle, from the very nature of the fact, is as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be imagined."

Hume adds the famous maxim: no testimony suffices to establish a miracle unless the testimony's falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact it reports — and, he judges, it never is.

The argument's force is real, so state it honestly. Laws of nature are supported by the most massive, uniform experience humans possess — every funeral confirms that the dead stay dead. Any report of a miracle is supported by something far weaker: human testimony, which is known to err through deception, credulity, legend, and the love of wonder. So when testimony collides with uniform experience, rationality sides with experience — always. Hume seasons this with observations about miracle reports flourishing among "ignorant and barbarous nations," and about rival religions canceling each other's miracles out. The conclusion: a wise man can never believe a miracle on testimony, no matter the witnesses.

III. The Circle in the Argument

▼

The central flaw was spotted early and has never been repaired: the argument assumes what it set out to prove. Hume's engine is "firm and unalterable experience" — but whether experience against miracles is truly unalterable is precisely the question. The experience of the apostles, of the five hundred witnesses Paul cites (1 Corinthians 15:6), of every reporter of every miracle, is part of the human record too. Hume counts only the non-miraculous experience as "experience," which is to say: he assumes all miracle reports are false in order to prove no miracle report can be believed. C. S. Lewis put the point crisply in Miracles: we can know experience against miracles to be uniform only if we already know all the reports are false, and we can know that only by assuming miracles never occur — a circle Paraphrase.

Notice also what the argument would prove if sound — far too much. Any sufficiently rare, unrepeated event becomes unbelievable on testimony: the method that dismisses the Resurrection in advance would, applied consistently, have dismissed reports of meteorites, of ball lightning, of every singular historical surprise, because the reporter's error was always antecedently "more probable." A rule of reasoning that forbids ever learning of the genuinely unprecedented is not rigor; it is a blindfold. Later philosophers — including the agnostic John Earman, whose book title Hume's Abject Failure states his verdict — have argued that Hume's maxim, made mathematically precise, either collapses or proves nothing against well-attested singular events Paraphrase.

IV. What a Law of Nature Is

▼

Hume's definition — a miracle "violates" the laws of nature — smuggles in a picture of laws as inviolable decrees that even God must obey. But laws of nature, as science actually uses them, are descriptions: compact statements of how the natural order runs when nothing outside the described system intervenes. They predict what matter does left to itself; they are silent about what happens if something beyond nature acts.

If I catch a falling apple, I do not violate the law of gravity — I introduce a new factor the law's description did not include, and the law continues to hold perfectly. A miracle, on the Christian account, is exactly that, at the level of the whole system: the Author of nature acting directly within it. Nothing is "broken." Water still ordinarily stays water; the dead still ordinarily stay dead — indeed the miracle is only visible because the regularities hold. The disciples could recognize the Resurrection as God's act precisely because they knew, as well as any modern, that crucified men stay dead. Miracles presuppose the laws; they do not compete with them.

So the real question is not scientific but theological: does anything exist beyond the system? If God exists, miracles are possible — full stop; a Creator who fashioned nature from nothing is not locked out of it. If God does not exist, miracles are impossible. Hume's argument, dressed as a lesson about evidence, is in substance a conclusion about God — assumed, not established. For the case that God exists, see Explanations for God's Existence.

V. Weighing Evidence for the Extraordinary

▼

"Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence," the modern slogan runs. Rightly understood, Christians agree: a resurrection claim needs far better support than a claim that it rained. The slogan goes wrong only when "extraordinary evidence" is defined as "evidence that could never exist" — at which point it is Hume's circle again, restated.

What evidence can rationally support a miracle? The same kinds that support any past event, raised to strength: multiple independent witnesses; testimony against the witnesses' own interests, maintained under persecution to death; public claims checkable by enemies in the place and generation they were made; transformation of the witnesses themselves; and the absence of any natural explanation that fits the agreed facts. These are not hypothetical criteria — they are the actual shape of the case for the Resurrection, laid out in detail on its own page, where the skeptics' alternative explanations (hallucination, theft, legend, swoon) are weighed one by one and found to require more credulity than the miracle.

Background beliefs do their work here, and honestly so: for the convinced atheist, any natural explanation, however strained, beats a miracle; for the theist, a miracle is possible and the evidence may simply win. That is why this page insists on the order of questions: first whether God exists, then whether He has acted. What is not rational is the shortcut — ruling out the miracle in advance and calling the refusal to weigh evidence a weighing of evidence.

VI. Common Objections Examined

▼

"Every religion claims miracles — they cancel out"

▼

The Claim

Hume's own point: rival religions' miracles are equal and opposite testimony, mutually destroying.

Response

Claims do not cancel; evidence is weighed case by case — that two murder trials both feature witnesses does not acquit both defendants. Most religious miracle claims are private, late, or unfalsifiable, and Christianity is not obliged to defend them; some may even be true without threatening Christianity (the question is always what a given event evidences). The Christian wager is specific: one central public miracle, the Resurrection, attested early, by named witnesses, at known cost to themselves, in the city where it could be refuted. Put every religion's best-attested miracle on the table under the same criteria and compare — that is the opposite of special pleading.

"Why are there no miracles today, under cameras?"

▼

The Claim

Miracles flourish exactly where evidence is thin — distant past, remote places — which is what fraud and legend predict.

Response

First, the premise is overstated: claimed healings and answered prayers are reported constantly, including in documented medical literature of unexplained recoveries; the modern world filters them as anecdote, which is its right — but "never investigated" is not "never occurs." Second, Christianity never promised miracles on demand; they cluster, in Scripture itself, around decisive revelatory moments, and Christ rebuked the demand for signs as a substitute for faith (Matthew 12:39). Third, the faith's evidential weight does not rest on contemporary wonders but on a historical event — a deliberate design, one might say, since a public past event can be examined by every generation equally, while a private experience cannot. The absence of miracles in your news feed is not evidence about a tomb in first-century Jerusalem.

"Science has progressively explained miracles away"

▼

The Claim

What looked miraculous — disease, lightning, eclipses — turned out natural; the trend line points to zero.

Response

The trend concerns phenomena once attributed to direct divine action for lack of a mechanism — and Christians should cheerfully concede it; the "God of the gaps" was always bad theology (see Science & Christianity). But the biblical miracles were never gap-filling explanations of recurring phenomena; they are singular acts in history, recognized as extraordinary by their own witnesses precisely against the known regularities. No advance in medicine explains an empty tomb; better embryology will never make a virgin conception ordinary. Science narrows ignorance of mechanisms; it has nothing to say about whether the Author of mechanisms once acted directly. The categories do not meet.

VII. Miracles and the Orthodox Faith

▼

Orthodoxy's relationship to the miraculous differs from both the skeptic's and the enthusiast's. The Church is unembarrassed by miracles — her calendar is full of them, her saints' lives report them, and the Coptic Church in particular remembers wonders in living memory around which her people's devotion gathered. Yet the faith does not rest on wonder-stories, and the Church has always tested claims soberly, knowing that credulity dishonors God as much as unbelief. "Beloved, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits, whether they are of God" (1 John 4:1).

What the faith rests on is the miracle: "if Christ is not risen, then our preaching is empty and your faith is also empty" (1 Corinthians 15:14). Paul stakes everything on one public event — and invites verification, listing living witnesses (15:6). The sacramental life of the Church flows from that event: not a weekly suspension of nature, but the risen Christ giving Himself in the mysteries. And the deepest answer to Hume is finally a Person, not a syllogism: a God who made the world is not a prisoner of it, and "with God nothing will be impossible" (Luke 1:37). The arguments clear the road; the empty tomb stands at the end of it — examine it.

VIII. FAQ

▼
Q: Isn't believing in miracles believing in magic?▼

No — the categories are opposites. Magic, in the ancient and modern sense alike, is technique: manipulating hidden forces to compel outcomes. A miracle is personal: the free act of a personal God, never compelled, never a technology. That is why Scripture forbids sorcery while recording miracles — and why miracles carry meaning (they attest, reveal, redeem) rather than merely astonishing. The Resurrection is not a trick that needs a bigger stage; it is the vindication of the Son of God (Romans 1:4).

Q: Should I believe every miracle story I hear in church?▼

No — and Orthodoxy does not ask it. Test claims with the sobriety the Church herself uses (1 John 4:1); hold loosely what is unverified; let nothing peripheral bear the weight of faith. The hierarchy of confidence is deliberate: the Resurrection is attested as history and holds everything; particular wonders of particular saints edify but do not carry the structure. A faith that believed everything would be as defenseless as one that believed nothing.

Q: If miracles are possible, doesn't that make science pointless — anything could happen?▼

The reverse. Science requires regularity; Christianity supplies a reason for it — a faithful Creator who sustains an ordered cosmos, "who covers the heavens with clouds" in pattern and season (cf. Psalm 147:8). Miracles, on the biblical record, are vanishingly rare, purposeful, and recognizable only against that order. A world of constant divine interruption would indeed make science impossible — and would equally make miracles invisible. The Christian picture — deep regularity, rare signs — is exactly the world science finds, with a signature in the margin.

Q: Where should a skeptical friend start?▼

With the order of questions. Ask whether they reject the Resurrection because the evidence is weak, or because miracles "can't happen" — most discover it is the second, and that they have never examined the first. Then offer the two pages in sequence: the case that God exists (which makes miracles possible), and the case that He raised Jesus (which makes Christianity true). Hume's ghost loses its power the moment the question is allowed to be asked.
