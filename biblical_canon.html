The Biblical Canon

Oriental Orthodox • Eastern Orthodox • Roman Catholic • Protestant • Ethiopian • Syriac
Not all Christians read the same Bible. The Oriental Orthodox canon — rooted in the Greek Septuagint used by every New Testament author — is larger and older than the Protestant Bible, which was trimmed in the 16th century to match a post-Christian Jewish list. This site traces the full history of the biblical canon across traditions, examines each disputed book, surveys the Church Fathers, and answers why the question matters doctrinally.

The Septuagint — The Church's Bible

▶

The Septuagint (LXX) is the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, produced in Alexandria between roughly 285 and 150 BC for Jewish communities who no longer spoke Hebrew. It included the deuterocanonical books — Tobit, Judith, Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, and Baruch — as integral parts of the collection, not appendices.

The New Testament quotes the LXX ~300 times. When NT authors cite “the scriptures,” they are almost always quoting the Septuagint, including passages from books later excluded by Protestants.

The Isaiah 7:14 Problem

This verse is a decisive example of why the LXX matters. The Hebrew text uses almah (young woman). The Septuagint translates this as parthenos (virgin). Matthew 1:23 quotes Isaiah 7:14 specifically using parthenos — the LXX word, not a fresh translation from the Hebrew. Matthew’s Messianic prophecy depends on the Septuagint text. This demonstrates that the New Testament authors treated the LXX as authoritative scripture.

“Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign: behold, the virgin [parthenos] shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Emmanuel.” — Isaiah 7:14 (LXX / Matthew 1:23)

Why the LXX Included More Books

The LXX canon reflected the broader scriptures in use among Diaspora Jewish communities. Books like Wisdom and Sirach were written in Greek, composed in Alexandria and highly regarded. 1 Maccabees was written in Hebrew and translated into Greek. These books circulated as sacred scripture among Greek-speaking Jews for centuries before Christianity and were carried into the Church’s use without controversy.

The Codices

The three great manuscripts of the early Christian Bible — Codex Vaticanus (4th c.), Codex Sinaiticus (4th c.), and Codex Alexandrinus (5th c.) — all contain the deuterocanonical books alongside the protocanonical texts. No ancient Christian Bible manuscripts separate them as “Apocrypha.” That practice began with Luther.

Origen of Alexandria
c. 185–253 AD
“The church did not receive these books as of equal authority with the others, yet they are of benefit to believers.” — Origen acknowledged deuterocanonicals as broadly used scripture while noting scholarly debates over their authority. His own writings frequently cite them as inspired.
Irenaeus of Lyon
c. 130–202 AD
Irenaeus quotes Baruch 3:36–38 as “another prophet” and applies it to Christ, quoting Wisdom 7:26 as divine scripture in his refutation of Gnosticism. He treats these books as authoritative without qualification.

The Jamnia Dispute — A Post-Christian Jewish Decision

▶

Protestants often argue that the Church should follow the “Hebrew canon” — the canon defined by rabbinical Judaism. But this argument rests on a historical reversal: the Hebrew canon in its final closed form was defined after Christianity began, and partly in reaction to it.

Yavneh (Jamnia) c. AD 90

Around AD 90, after the destruction of the Temple, rabbinic gatherings at Yavneh (Jamnia) in Judea took up questions of practice and scripture for a post-Temple world. Note: the older scholarly term “Council of Jamnia” — popularized in the 19th century — has been largely abandoned in modern scholarship. There was no single formal council issuing canon decrees; rather, ongoing rabbinic discussions gradually produced consensus over decades. The functional result of this process was a narrower Jewish canon that excluded Greek-language books, including Wisdom, Sirach, and others already in continuous use in LXX Christianity.

The critical problem: The Christian Church had already been using the Septuagint for 50–60 years before Jamnia. Asking the Church to adopt a Jewish canon finalized after Christ, partly to distinguish Judaism from Christianity, is asking the Church to submit to a post-Christian, non-Christian authority on which books are divinely inspired.

What the Early Church Said

The Councils of Hippo (AD 393) and Carthage (AD 397) — attended by Augustine and confirmed by Rome — explicitly listed the deuterocanonical books as canonical scripture. This was not a new decision but a formal ratification of what had been in continuous use. No major church council in the first millennium excluded these books.

“The canonical scriptures are these: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, Joshua, Judges, Ruth, four books of Kings, two of Chronicles, Job, the Psalter, five books of Solomon, the twelve books of the Prophets, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Tobit, Judith, Esther, Ezra, two books of Maccabees.” — Council of Carthage (AD 397), Canon 24
St. Augustine of Hippo
354–430 AD
“The whole canon of the scriptures, on which we say this discussion should be based, is contained in these books: the five books of Moses...and two books of Maccabees.” (On Christian Doctrine 2.8.13). Augustine explicitly defended the larger Septuagint-based canon and strongly criticized those who would restrict it to the Hebrew list. He noted that the Church’s authority — not rabbinic post-Temple councils — determines the canon.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem
313–386 AD
“Read the divine scriptures, the twenty-two books of the Old Testament...But read also the two books of Esdras, and Tobit, and Judith, and Maccabees.” (Catechetical Lectures 4.35). Cyril distinguished the deuterocanonicals slightly but still directed catechumens to read them as part of their scriptural formation.

The Deuterocanonical Books — Individual Profiles

▶

Each of these books was read as scripture in the early Church, is quoted or alluded to in the New Testament, and was formally declared canonical by councils in both East and West. Here is what each contains and why it matters.

Tobit
OO EO RC Eth.
A narrative about a righteous Jew in exile, his son Tobias, and the angel Raphael who heals blindness and drives away a demon. Rich in teachings on prayer, almsgiving, and marriage. Contains one of the most beautiful wedding prayers in scripture. The angel Raphael is named here (Tob 12:15), one of only three named angels in the entire Bible.
“Prayer with fasting is good, but better than both is almsgiving with righteousness. A little with righteousness is better than wealth with wrongdoing. It is better to give alms than to lay up gold. For almsgiving saves from death and purges away every sin.” — Tobit 12:8–9
Judith
OO EO RC Eth.
A heroic narrative about a pious Jewish widow who saves her city by assassinating the Assyrian general Holofernes. Theologically it emphasizes trust in God over military might, the power of prayer and fasting, and God’s use of the weak to shame the powerful — themes echoed in the New Testament’s Magnificat (Luke 1:52). Many Church Fathers, including Clement of Rome, cited Judith as scripture.
“The Lord is a God who crushes wars; he sets up his camp among his people; he delivered me from the hands of my pursuers.” — Judith 16:2
1 Maccabees
OO EO RC Eth.
A historical account of the Maccabean revolt against Seleucid Greek oppression (175–134 BC), including the desecration and re-dedication of the Temple — the origin of Hanukkah. Essential historical background for understanding the New Testament world. Written in Hebrew (now lost), translated into Greek. Josephus used it as a primary historical source.
“They decreed by public ordinance and vote that the whole nation of the Jews should observe these days every year.” — 1 Maccabees 4:59 (institution of the Feast of Dedication, cf. John 10:22)
2 Maccabees
OO EO RC Eth.
A theological companion to 1 Maccabees, covering the same period from a different angle. Doctrinally the most significant deuterocanonical book: it explicitly teaches bodily resurrection (ch. 7 — the martyrdom of seven brothers), prayer for the dead (ch. 12), and the intercession of the saints (ch. 15, the vision of Onias and Jeremiah interceding). These passages are the direct scriptural basis for Orthodox and Catholic teaching on these doctrines.
“He [Judas] also took up a collection, man by man, to the amount of two thousand drachmas of silver, and sent it to Jerusalem to provide for a sin offering. In doing this he acted very well and honorably, taking account of the resurrection. For if he were not expecting that those who had fallen would rise again, it would have been superfluous and foolish to pray for the dead.” — 2 Maccabees 12:43–44
Wisdom of Solomon
OO EO RC Eth.
Written in Greek in Alexandria, probably 1st century BC, Wisdom presents divine Wisdom as a hypostatic figure who participates in creation. Many scholars see the prologue of John’s Gospel (John 1:1–18) as deliberately engaging Wisdom’s language. The book also contains the most explicit pre-Christian reflection on individual immortality in the Jewish scriptural tradition and a lengthy critique of idolatry that Paul echoes in Romans 1.
“She is a reflection of eternal light, a spotless mirror of the working of God, and an image of his goodness… She reaches mightily from one end of the earth to the other, and she orders all things well.” — Wisdom 7:26; 8:1
Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)
OO EO RC Eth.
The longest deuterocanonical book, written by Jesus ben Sirach of Jerusalem around 180 BC and translated into Greek by his grandson. A comprehensive wisdom text covering ethics, family life, prayer, and the praise of famous men (ch. 44–50). The title “Ecclesiasticus” means “Church book” — a name given by the Latin West because it was so widely used for moral instruction in the early Church. James, Hebrews, and Matthew contain numerous echoes of Sirach.
“Honor your father with your whole heart, and do not forget the birth pangs of your mother. Remember that through your parents you were born; what can you give back to them that equals their gift to you?” — Sirach 7:27–28
Baruch (with Letter of Jeremiah)
OO EO RC Eth.
Attributed to Baruch, the scribe of Jeremiah, and circulated as an appendix to Jeremiah in the Septuagint. Contains a confession of Israel’s sin, a poem on divine Wisdom (chapter 3–4 closely parallels Proverbs 8 and Job 28), and an encouragement to exiles. Irenaeus quotes Baruch 3:36–38 — which speaks of God appearing on earth — as a Christological prophecy, treating it as Jeremiah’s words.
“Afterward she [Wisdom] appeared upon earth and lived among men.” — Baruch 3:38

Additional Books — Eastern & Oriental Orthodox

▶

Beyond the seven deuterocanonicals accepted by Rome, the Eastern and Oriental Orthodox traditions receive several additional texts, though practice varies somewhat by jurisdiction.

1 Esdras
EO OO (Var.) RC (devotional)
A Greek retelling and expansion of material from Ezra, Chronicles, and Nehemiah, plus a unique section — the Contest of the Three Guardsmen (1 Esd 3–4) — which concludes that truth is mightiest of all. The Eastern Orthodox Church treats it as canonical; the Coptic tradition includes it in liturgical use; Rome prints it as an appendix to the Vulgate without canonical status.
“Truth endures and is strong forever, and lives and prevails forever and ever… There is no partiality with her in her judgments.” — 1 Esdras 4:38–39
Prayer of Manasseh
EO OO (Var.) RC (devotional)
A short, beautiful penitential prayer purportedly spoken by King Manasseh of Judah — the most wicked of Judah’s kings (2 Kings 21) who repented in captivity (2 Chronicles 33:12–13). The Chronicles account mentions Manasseh’s prayer but does not record it; this text fills that gap. Widely used in liturgical repentance services. Found in the Didascalia and Apostolic Constitutions, used in Orthodox vespers.
“I have sinned, O Lord, I have sinned, and I acknowledge my transgressions. I earnestly entreat you: forgive me, O Lord, forgive me. Do not destroy me with my transgressions!” — Prayer of Manasseh 12–13
Psalm 151
OO EO RC (devotional)
A short psalm in the first person attributed to David, describing his anointing and victory over Goliath. Found in the Septuagint manuscripts (marked “outside the number” in LXX) and discovered in the Dead Sea Scrolls (11QPs³), confirming its antiquity. Canonical in both Eastern and Oriental Orthodoxy; printed without canonical status in modern Catholic Bibles. A Hebrew version was found at Qumran.
“I was small among my brothers, and youngest in my father’s house; I tended my father’s sheep. My hands made a harp; my fingers fashioned a lyre. And who will tell my Lord?” — Psalm 151:1–2 (LXX)
3 Maccabees
EO OO (Var.) RC: No
Despite its name, 3 Maccabees has nothing to do with the Maccabean revolt. It describes events under Ptolemy IV Philopator of Egypt (3rd century BC): an attempt to force Jews into the cult of Dionysus, miraculous deliverance, and the institution of a feast of thanksgiving. Accepted as canonical in the Eastern Orthodox Church (Greek, Russian, etc.) and included in some Oriental Orthodox traditions. Its Jewish-Egyptian setting resonates particularly with the Coptic tradition.
“You, O King of great power, Almighty God Most High, governing all creation with mercy, look upon the descendants of Abraham, O Father.” — 3 Maccabees 6:2

The Ethiopian Canon — 81 Books

▶

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church preserves the largest biblical canon of any Christian tradition — 81 books in the broad canon (Latiw). This is not theological maximalism but the result of continuous, ancient preservation of texts lost elsewhere. Ethiopia received Christianity in the 4th century through Frumentius, but had Jewish contact through the Beta Israel community for centuries prior, preserving Jewish texts that vanished from other traditions.

The Narrow and Broad Canons

Ethiopian Orthodox scholars distinguish the Tsidk (narrow) canon — which aligns roughly with the Catholic 73 books — and the Latiw (broad) canon which adds texts unique to the Ethiopian tradition. The broad canon is the one typically cited as “81 books.”

1 Enoch (Henok)
Ethiopia Only
One of the most remarkable canonical inclusions: the Book of Enoch, an extensive Jewish apocalyptic text (c. 300–200 BC) preserved in full only in the Ethiopian Ge’ez language. It was widely read in early Christianity — found in the Dead Sea Scrolls, cited by multiple Church Fathers, and explicitly quoted in the canonical New Testament book of Jude (vv. 14–15). Tertullian accepted it as scripture. It was lost to other traditions and only recovered for Western scholarship when James Bruce brought manuscripts from Ethiopia in 1773.
“It was also about these that Enoch, in the seventh generation from Adam, prophesied, saying, ‘Behold, the Lord comes with ten thousands of his holy ones, to execute judgment on all…’” — Jude 14–15 (quoting 1 Enoch 1:9)
Jubilees
Ethiopia Only
Also called “Lesser Genesis,” Jubilees retells the narrative of Genesis and Exodus organized around a 49-year jubilee calendar. It was highly regarded at Qumran (15+ manuscripts found in the Dead Sea Scrolls), was cited by early Christian writers, and has been in continuous canonical use in Ethiopia. Gives expanded accounts of creation, the fallen angels, and patriarchal history. Argues for a solar-based liturgical calendar, which distinguishes it from rabbinic tradition.
“There will be no Satan nor any evil destroyer; for all their days will be days of blessing and healing.” — Jubilees 23:29
1–3 Meqabyan
Ethiopia Only
Despite being called “Maccabees” in some translations, the Meqabyan books are completely distinct from 1–4 Maccabees. They are original Ge’ez compositions dealing with martyrdom, the power of God, and persecution — themes relevant to Ethiopian Christian experience. Not translations of any known Hebrew or Greek text. Unique to the Ethiopian canon and deeply embedded in Ethiopian theological identity.
“God is not found in many words but in the pureness of the heart.” — 1 Meqabyan 4:14
Broader NT Books
Ethiopia Only
The Ethiopian broader NT (Latiw) adds four texts to the 27 shared NT books: Sinodos (Apostolic Canons, church order), Kidan (Book of the Covenant, liturgical law), Qalementos (Clement — a version of the Clementine literature), and Didaskalia (Apostolic teaching). These govern church practice, worship, and ethics. They are treated as having normative authority for church life even if distinguished from the narrative books of the NT.
“Let your prayers, O beloved, be addressed to God who hears prayers, with fasting, almsgiving, and upright life.” — Sinodos (Ethiopian Apostolic Canons)
Tertullian of Carthage
c. 155–220 AD
“Enoch had predicted this very thing, ‘that demons and spirits of evil would turn into idols.’ Enoch has equal standing with Moses since the Lord says of him, ‘Enoch walked with God.’” (On Idolatry 4). Tertullian vigorously defended the canonicity of 1 Enoch, arguing that its preservation in the Flood and its citation in Jude demonstrated its inspiration.
St. Frumentius (Abba Salama)
c. 300–380 AD
The apostle of Ethiopia, consecrated by Athanasius of Alexandria, brought Christianity to Aksum. Under his influence the Aksumite kingdom became Christian under King Ezana. The Ethiopian canon was shaped in this Alexandrian theological environment, inheriting the broad Alexandrian scriptural tradition that included 1 Enoch and Jubilees already valued in the pre-Christian Beta Israel community.

The Syriac Peshitta — An Ancient Variant NT

▶

The Peshitta is the standard Bible of the Syriac-speaking churches (Syriac Orthodox, Maronite, Church of the East, Chaldean). The name means “simple” or “straight.” Its OT is largely translated from Hebrew and predates the LXX-based OT used in Greek churches. Its NT reflects a distinctive textual tradition.

The Original Peshitta NT: 22 Books

The original Peshitta New Testament contained 22 books — the 27 universal books minus 2 Peter, 2–3 John, Jude, and Revelation. These “five missing” books were not in the earliest Syriac canon and entered Syriac use only gradually, with some jurisdictions adding them by the 6th century and others later still. The Peshitta text tradition is one of the most ancient NT witnesses.

Significance: The fact that the original Syriac NT lacked Revelation — the most disputed NT book in both East and West — is important historical evidence that Revelation’s canonicity was genuinely uncertain in the early Church. This undermines the Protestant argument that canon questions have obvious, timeless answers.

The Peshitta OT

The Peshitta OT was largely translated directly from Hebrew, making it a distinct textual tradition from the LXX. It includes many deuterocanonical books (Sirach, Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, 1–2 Maccabees) though the precise canon varies by jurisdiction. Some Syriac communities received additional books through contact with Jewish communities.

Aphrahat the Persian Sage
c. 280–345 AD
The earliest known Syriac writer, Aphrahat’s 23 Demonstrations extensively quote scripture from the Peshitta tradition. His canon — without Revelation — was the standard in the Persian Church. He demonstrates that Syriac Christianity was a fully independent apostolic tradition with its own scriptural formation.
Philoxenus of Mabbug
440–523 AD
A Syriac Oriental Orthodox bishop who commissioned a new Syriac translation (the Philoxenian version) specifically to include the five books missing from the Peshitta NT: 2 Peter, 2–3 John, Jude, and Revelation. This demonstrates both the openness to canonical expansion and the honest acknowledgment that these books were not in the original Syriac tradition.

New Testament Canon Development

▶

The New Testament canon was not handed down complete from the apostles — it developed through the life of the Church over three centuries. This is not a problem for Orthodox Christianity, which understands the Church as the guardian and authenticator of scripture, but it is a problem for Protestant theories of scripture that make the Bible prior to and independent of the Church.

c. AD 50–100
The Writings Appear

Paul’s letters circulate among churches. The Gospels are composed. Revelation is written. These texts have authority from the start, but no “New Testament” as a collection exists.

c. AD 140
Marcion’s Challenge

The heretic Marcion of Sinope publishes his own truncated canon: only a version of Luke and 10 Pauline letters, rejecting the entire Old Testament. His challenge forces the Church to begin articulating criteria for recognizing authentic apostolic scripture.

c. AD 170
Muratorian Fragment

The earliest known list of NT books, from Rome. Lists most of what would become the NT canon but includes the Apocalypse of Peter and the Wisdom of Solomon, and omits Hebrews, 1 Peter, and 2 Peter entirely from its list (they are absent, not questioned). Demonstrates the canon was still in formation.

c. AD 260–340
Eusebius' Categories

Church historian Eusebius of Caesarea classifies NT books into three categories: accepted (Gospels, Acts, Paul’s letters, 1 John, 1 Peter, Revelation “if it seem right”), disputed (James, Jude, 2 Peter, 2–3 John), and spurious (Acts of Paul, Shepherd of Hermas, Apocalypse of Peter, Epistle of Barnabas, Didache). His disputed category includes books that remain in our NT today.

AD 367
Athanasius' 39th Festal Letter

Bishop Athanasius of Alexandria sends his annual Festal Letter listing the 27 books now in every NT. This is the first known complete list matching the modern NT exactly. Athanasius also separately lists OT books and places the deuterocanonicals “outside the canon but appointed for reading” — a pastoral rather than exclusionary decision. His NT list is immediately authoritative across the Alexandrian church.

AD 393 & 397
Councils of Hippo & Carthage

The North African councils, with Augustine present and influential, formally ratify the 27-book NT and a 46-book OT (including deuterocanonicals). These lists are confirmed by Pope Innocent I in 405 and become normative for the whole Western church. The Eastern church arrives at the same NT list through parallel development.

AD 692
Quinisext Council (Trullan)

The Byzantine council ratifies the canon decisions of Laodicea, Carthage, and Athanasius. Notably, it simultaneously endorses multiple slightly different lists — reflecting ongoing diversity — while affirming the core 27-book NT. This is the closest the Eastern church came to a formal conciliar canon decision before the modern era.

AD 1546
Council of Trent

In response to the Protestant Reformation, Trent formally defines the Roman Catholic biblical canon, including all 46 OT books. This is the first dogmatic canon definition in Western Christian history. The Eastern churches never needed such a formal definition because the Protestant challenge did not arise there.

NT Disputed Books — What the Fathers Debated

▶

Several books now in every edition of the New Testament were disputed, doubted, or questioned by major Church Fathers. Far from undermining the canon, this history shows that the canon was discerned carefully by the Church over time — not simply invented or inherited without reflection.

Hebrews Authorship disputed from the beginning▶

The Letter to the Hebrews was disputed not over its content — everyone valued it — but over its authorship. It does not name its author, and stylistic differences from Paul’s other letters led many to doubt Pauline authorship. Origen famously said: “Who wrote the epistle, in truth God knows.” Yet he still used it as scripture. Clement of Rome (c. 96 AD) quotes Hebrews as authoritative, making it one of the earliest-cited NT texts.

Origen of Alexandria
c. 185–253 AD
“The thoughts are those of the apostle, but the style and composition belong to one who recalled the apostolic teachings and, as it were, made short notes of what his master said. If any church holds that this epistle is by Paul, let it be commended for this. For not without reason have the men of old time handed it down as Paul’s. But who wrote the epistle, in truth, God knows.” (apud Eusebius, HE 6.25.11–14)
Revelation The most disputed NT book in the East▶

Revelation (Apocalypse) was the most controversial NT book in the Eastern churches. Cyril of Jerusalem omitted it from his canon list. Gregory Nazianzus listed it as spurious. Eusebius placed it in both his “accepted” and “spurious” categories. The Syriac Peshitta originally excluded it. The Syrian church doubted it for centuries. It was not included in the Eastern lectionary — Orthodox liturgy never reads from Revelation publicly, unlike every other NT book. Despite this, it was eventually accepted as canonical throughout all Orthodox traditions.

St. Cyril of Jerusalem
313–386 AD
“Of the New Testament there are four Gospels only, for the rest have false titles...The Revelation of John is likewise left by many outside our canonical books, but if it be judged proper, it may be received; and this judgment I leave to the Church of each place.” (Catechetical Lectures 4.36)
St. Gregory Nazianzus
329–390 AD
Gregory’s famous poem on the scriptural canon (Carmen 1.12) lists 26 NT books — omitting only Revelation. His list ends: “You have all. If anything is outside these, it is not among the genuine.” Yet Gregory’s judgment on this point was not final: the Church ultimately received the book.
James Luther's “epistle of straw”▶

James was among the “disputed” books in Eusebius’ list. Luther famously called it an “epistle of straw” because its emphasis on works seemed to contradict his doctrine of sola fide (faith alone). He placed James in an appendix to his German New Testament along with Hebrews, Jude, and Revelation — books he considered of lesser authority. While Luther did not finally exclude them, his ranking introduced a Protestant tradition of questioning the universal canonical authority of these books. James has been unproblematically canonical in all Eastern churches.

“You see that a person is justified by works and not by faith alone.” — James 2:24
2 Peter / Jude Catholic Epistles disputed in multiple traditions▶

Both 2 Peter and Jude were widely disputed in antiquity. 2 Peter was doubted by Eusebius, absent from the original Syriac canon, and questioned by Jerome. Jude was problematic because it explicitly quotes 1 Enoch (vv. 14–15) and the Assumption of Moses — non-canonical texts — as authoritative. This created a circular problem: if Jude quotes 1 Enoch as prophecy, does that make 1 Enoch canonical? The Ethiopian Church says yes. For OO and EO, the Jude quotation is treated as citing an early tradition without implying full canonicity for 1 Enoch.

Church Fathers on the Biblical Canon

▶

The consensus of the Church Fathers — especially those whose canon lists survive — shows consistent acceptance of the deuterocanonical books as scripture, despite occasional scholarly notes about their distinct status in some lists.

St. Clement of Rome
c. 35–99 AD
In his letter to the Corinthians (1 Clement, c. 96 AD), Clement quotes Judith as an example of heroic faith: “The blessed Judith, when her city was in danger, asked of the elders that she might go out into the camp of the strangers; and exposing herself to danger, she went forth for the love of her country and her people.” (1 Clement 55.4–5). He treats Judith not as an interesting story but as a normative scriptural example.
St. Polycarp of Smyrna
c. 69–155 AD
Polycarp’s letter to the Philippians quotes Tobit 4:10 and 12:9 (“almsgiving delivers from death”) in a way that integrates it seamlessly with canonical Proverbs: “But almsgiving delivers from death… so also fasting and prayer are good, but better than all is almsgiving.” He shows no awareness that these books might be considered non-canonical.
St. Irenaeus of Lyon
c. 130–202 AD
Irenaeus cites Baruch 3:36–38 as a prophecy by Jeremiah: “And Jeremiah the prophet has pointed out that as many believers as God has prepared for this purpose, to multiply those left upon the earth, should both be under the rule of the saints to minister to this Jerusalem, and that [His] kingdom shall be built up in Jerusalem; and the prophet says: ‘Afterward she appeared on earth and was conversant with men.’” (Against Heresies 4.20.4). He also quotes Wisdom as “the scripture.”
Tertullian of Carthage
c. 155–220 AD
Tertullian cites Wisdom, Baruch, 1 Enoch, and other deuterocanonical texts as authoritative scripture throughout his voluminous writings. He mocks Marcion’s truncated canon and any attempt to separate the Church from its scriptural inheritance: “We must not speak of Christ apart from the scriptures which contain him.” His defense of 1 Enoch as canonical is the most vigorous patristic argument for that book.
Origen of Alexandria
c. 185–253 AD
Origen produced a list of OT books that closely matched the Hebrew canon while acknowledging the deuterocanonicals were read in churches. In practice, his commentaries and homilies cite Sirach, Wisdom, Judith, and Tobit as scripture hundreds of times. His Hexapla — the great six-column Bible — included the LXX with all its books. Origen represents the scholarly tradition of awareness of the Hebrew-Greek distinction without excluding the larger canon.
St. Jerome of Stridon
c. 347–420 AD
Jerome is the Father most cited by Protestants because he expressed preference for the Hebraica Veritas — the Hebrew truth — and noted that Judith, Tobit, Wisdom, and Sirach “are not in the canon.” Yet Jerome’s own practice contradicts his theory: he translated all these books into Latin for the Vulgate, included them in the Bible he produced for the Western church, and in his later commentaries quotes Sirach and Wisdom as scripture without qualification. Augustine sharply criticized his Hebrew-canon preference.
St. Augustine of Hippo
354–430 AD
Augustine was the most forceful patristic defender of the larger canon. Against Jerome, he argued that the Greek-speaking Church’s use of the deuterocanonicals for centuries was itself evidence of their authority: “The whole canon of scriptures, however, in which we say this judgment should be based, is contained in these books: Genesis...two books of Maccabees.” (On Christian Doctrine 2.8). He also defended the LXX as inspired, arguing that the Holy Spirit guided the translation just as he guided the original authors.
St. Athanasius of Alexandria
296–373 AD
Athanasius’ 39th Festal Letter (367 AD) is the first document to list the exact 27 NT books we have today. For the OT he lists 22 books (following Hebrew enumeration) but immediately adds: “But for greater exactness I add this also, writing of necessity; that there are other books besides these not indeed included in the Canon, but appointed by the Fathers to be read by those who newly join us, and who wish for instruction in the word of godliness: the Wisdom of Solomon, the Wisdom of Sirach, Esther, Judith, Tobias...” His distinction between canonical and “for reading” is often misread as exclusion; it was a pedagogical classification for catechumens, not a rejection.
St. Cyril of Alexandria
376–444 AD
Patriarch of Alexandria and the great defender of Theotokos, Cyril regularly cites Wisdom and Sirach as inspired scripture in his Christological writings. His commentary on John draws heavily on Wisdom’s Logos theology. As the leading theologian of the Alexandrian tradition that gave us the Coptic Church, his acceptance of these books is foundational for Oriental Orthodox canon tradition.
St. John Chrysostom
347–407 AD
Chrysostom quotes Sirach throughout his homilies as inspired scripture, often prefacing quotations with “the Holy Spirit says through Sirach” or simply “scripture says.” His use of the book is so extensive and unreflective that modern scholars must note which “Sirach” references come from the canonical Psalms and which from Ecclesiasticus.
Clement of Alexandria
c. 150–215 AD
Clement quotes almost every deuterocanonical book as scripture, often with the formula “the divine scripture says.” He quotes 1 Enoch as inspired prophecy. His Stromata cites Wisdom more than almost any canonical book outside the Psalms and Proverbs. The Alexandrian school Clement founded — which produced Origen, Athanasius, and Cyril — was shaped by this broad canonical inheritance.

How the Protestant Canon Was Formed

▶

The Protestant Old Testament canon was not recovered from early Christianity — it was constructed in the 16th century by adopting the post-Christian Jewish canon against the consensus of 1500 years of Christian biblical use. Understanding how this happened is essential for evaluating Protestant canon arguments.

1517–1520
Luther's Reformation Begins

Martin Luther begins his reform movement. His key doctrines — sola scriptura (scripture alone) and sola fide (faith alone) — place him in conflict with teachings that have scriptural support only in the deuterocanonicals. Prayer for the dead (2 Macc 12) and purgatory (2 Macc 12:43–44) are the most direct conflicts.

1534
Luther's German Bible

Luther publishes a complete German Bible. He places the deuterocanonical books in a separate appendix between the Old and New Testaments, labeled: “Apocrypha: These are books which are not held equal to the Holy Scriptures, and yet are profitable and good to read.” This is the watershed moment: for the first time in the history of Western Christianity, these books are formally demoted from scripture to appendix.

1546
Council of Trent (Catholic Response)

The Roman Catholic Church responds to Luther by formally defining its canon for the first time, including all seven deuterocanonicals as fully canonical. This is the first dogmatic canon definition in Western Christian history.

1611
King James Bible

The original 1611 King James Bible included the Apocrypha between the Testaments — the deuterocanonical books. The KJV without the Apocrypha is a later omission. Many Anglicans continued using these books for devotional reading under Article VI of the Thirty-Nine Articles (1562), which distinguished them from canonical scripture but permitted their use “for example of life and instruction of manners.”

1647
Westminster Confession of Faith

The Westminster Confession formally defines the Protestant canon as 66 books, explicitly stating: “The books commonly called Apocrypha, not being of divine inspiration, are no part of the canon of the Scripture, and therefore are of no authority in the Church of God, nor to be any otherwise approved, or made use of, than other human writings.” This is the final formal Protestant canon definition and the basis for most modern evangelical canon views.

1827
British & Foreign Bible Society

The British and Foreign Bible Society decides to print and distribute Bibles without the Apocrypha. This commercial and missionary decision — not a theological council — is largely responsible for the fact that most Protestant Bibles today do not include the deuterocanonicals even as an appendix. Luther’s and the KJV compilers’ intention to include them (even if demoted) is forgotten.

The decisive question: Luther’s argument was that the deuterocanonicals were not in the Hebrew Bible. But which Hebrew Bible? The Hebrew canon was itself not fixed in pre-Christian Judaism. Different Jewish groups (Pharisees, Essenes, Alexandrian Jews) used different collections. And the decision to follow post-Christian rabbinic Judaism rather than the Septuagint used by Christ and the apostles is itself a theological choice, not a neutral appeal to historical fact.

Doctrinal Implications of the Deuterocanon

▶

The canon question is not merely academic. Removing the deuterocanonical books removes the primary scriptural basis for several important doctrines. This is not a coincidence — Luther’s motivation for questioning the canon was precisely to remove the textual support for teachings he opposed.

Prayer for the Dead

2 Maccabees 12:43–45: Judas Maccabeus collects money to offer sacrifice for the sins of fallen soldiers so they might be freed from sin at the resurrection. This passage is the direct biblical foundation for prayer for the dead and, in Catholic theology, purgatory. Removing 2 Maccabees removes this foundation. Protestant theology correspondingly rejects prayer for the dead.

Resurrection of the Body

2 Maccabees 7 contains the earliest explicit account of individual bodily resurrection in a Jewish text. The seven brothers who are tortured and killed each declare their confidence in bodily resurrection. This text predates the resurrection passages in Daniel 12 in theological explicitness. Hebrews 11:35 alludes directly to these martyrs: “Women received back their dead by resurrection. Some were tortured, refusing to accept release.”

Intercession of Saints

2 Maccabees 15:12–16: Judas sees a vision of the high priest Onias (who had died) and the prophet Jeremiah (long dead) interceding for Israel. Jeremiah is described as “a man who loves the brethren and prays much for the people.” This text directly supports the Orthodox doctrine of the saints’ intercession. Without it, Protestant arguments against intercession of saints lose a major counter-text.

Angelology

Tobit 12:15: The angel Raphael reveals himself as “one of the seven angels who stand ready and enter before the glory of the Lord.” This is the only biblical text explicitly naming Raphael as an archangel and describing the order of seven archangels. Orthodox angelology — which venerates Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, and the other archangels — is grounded in this passage. Revelation 8:2 echoes it.

Wisdom Christology

Wisdom of Solomon 7:26 calls divine Wisdom “a reflection of eternal light, a spotless mirror of the working of God.” Hebrews 1:3 calls Christ “the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature” — almost certainly echoing Wisdom. John 1:1–18’s Logos prologue draws on Wisdom’s personified Wisdom theology. The Christological use of Wisdom provides rich theological grounding that is diminished when Wisdom is removed from the canon.

Almsgiving & Salvation

Tobit 12:9: “Almsgiving delivers from death and purges away every sin.” Sirach 3:30: “As water extinguishes a blazing fire, so almsgiving atones for sin.” These texts ground the Orthodox theology of almsgiving as a spiritual practice with salvific significance — part of the broader Orthodox understanding that salvation involves genuine transformation of life, not merely forensic declaration. Luther’s removal of these texts was consistent with his rejection of works-based soteriology.

Protestant Objections & Oriental Orthodox Responses

▶
Protestant “Jesus and the apostles only quoted from the Hebrew Old Testament, not the Apocrypha.”▶

OO Response This claim is factually incorrect. The NT authors almost always quote the LXX — not the Hebrew text — and the LXX includes the deuterocanonicals. Specific NT allusions and echoes to deuterocanonicals include: Matthew 27:43 echoing Wisdom 2:18–20; Hebrews 11:35–38 alluding to 2 Maccabees 6–7; Romans 1:18–32 extensively paralleling Wisdom 13–14; Hebrews 1:3 echoing Wisdom 7:26; James 1:19 paralleling Sirach 5:11; John 10:22 referencing the Feast of Dedication from 1 Maccabees 4; and Jude 14–15 directly quoting 1 Enoch. The argument that Jesus never cited the Apocrypha ignores the fact that he never explicitly “cited” most canonical books either — allusion and echo are the standard forms of scriptural engagement.

Protestant “The Jews rejected these books — we should follow the Jewish canon.”▶

OO Response The relevant question is: which Jews, and when? Pre-Christian Alexandrian Jews used the LXX and its included books. The Qumran community valued 1 Enoch and Jubilees highly. The post-Christian Pharisaic rabbis at Yavneh (c. AD 90) standardized a narrower canon partly to distinguish Judaism from Christianity. Following a post-Christian, anti-Christian Jewish canon decision makes no theological sense for Christians. Moreover, Christians do not follow Judaism on the question of whether Jesus is Messiah — why would we follow Judaism on which books are scripture?

Protestant “Jerome preferred the Hebrew canon — he is the greatest biblical scholar of the Fathers.”▶

OO Response Jerome’s preference for the Hebrew text is a valid scholarly position about textual transmission — but it is not the same as a theological argument for excluding books from the canon. Jerome himself translated Judith, Tobit, Wisdom, Sirach, and Maccabees for the Vulgate and included them in the Bible that served Western Christianity for a thousand years. He also used them in his letters and commentaries as authoritative scripture. Furthermore, Augustine — Jerome’s contemporary and the more influential theologian in the Western tradition — explicitly rejected Jerome’s Hebrew-canon preference and defended the larger canon. The tradition chose Augustine’s position in the Councils of Hippo and Carthage, not Jerome’s.

Protestant “These books contain errors — Judith's geography is wrong; 2 Maccabees contradicts 1 Maccabees.”▶

OO Response Historical and geographical anomalies in Judith are now understood by most scholars as deliberate — the book signals its own theological character by mixing eras (Nebuchadnezzar reigning over Nineveh) to indicate it is a didactic narrative, not a chronicle. The same hermeneutical charity applied to Genesis, Jonah, and Job — all of which contain features that cannot be read as straightforward historiography — should be applied here. As for 2 Maccabees, the differences from 1 Maccabees reflect different theological purposes and sources, not errors. This argument, applied consistently, would eliminate far more canonical books than it solves.

Protestant “The early Church Fathers sometimes distinguished these books from the canon.”▶

OO Response True — but the patristic evidence is far more complex than Protestant canon apologists typically acknowledge. (1) Fathers who note a distinction (Jerome, Athanasius) still include the books in their Bibles and cite them as scripture in practice. (2) The distinction they draw is typically not “these are false” but “these are for catechumens / for reading / of secondary status” — exactly how many Eastern churches still categorize Psalm 151 or the Prayer of Manasseh. (3) The councils that formally determined the canon — Hippo 393, Carthage 397 — explicitly included the deuterocanonicals over Jerome’s objections. The Fathers who excluded them (Gregory Nazianzus also excluded Revelation, and his list is not cited by Protestants) were outliers, not the tradition.

Frequently Asked Questions

▶
Why does the Oriental Orthodox Bible have more books than the Protestant Bible?▶

Because the Oriental Orthodox tradition preserved the Septuagint (LXX) canon — the Greek Old Testament used by every New Testament author and every major Church Father for the first 1500 years of Christianity. The Protestant canon was reduced in the 16th century when Luther adopted a post-Christian rabbinic Jewish list to remove textual support for doctrines he opposed, particularly prayer for the dead (2 Maccabees 12).

“The whole canon of the scriptures...is contained in these books: Genesis, Exodus...and two books of Maccabees.” — St. Augustine (On Christian Doctrine 2.8.13, c. AD 396)
Did the apostles use the deuterocanonical books?▶

Yes. The New Testament authors quote, echo, and allude to the deuterocanonicals frequently. Jude 14–15 quotes 1 Enoch directly. Hebrews 11:35–38 alludes to the Maccabean martyrs. Romans 1:18–32 parallels Wisdom 13–14 so closely that scholars debate whether Paul is quoting or echoing. Hebrews 1:3 echoes Wisdom 7:26. The LXX — which included these books — was the Bible the apostles used and cited.

Why does the Ethiopian Bible have 81 books?▶

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church received Christianity in the 4th century through Frumentius (consecrated by Athanasius of Alexandria) and had Jewish contact through the Beta Israel community for much longer. This preserved ancient texts — especially 1 Enoch and Jubilees — that were lost to other traditions. 1 Enoch was widely used in early Christianity (cited by Jude, Tertullian, Clement of Alexandria) and survived complete only in Ge’ez. The Ethiopian canon is not expansionist theology but the most complete preservation of early Alexandrian-Jewish-Christian scripture.

Does the Oriental Orthodox Church have an official, definitive canon list?▶

The Oriental Orthodox churches have never issued a single formal canon definition comparable to Trent. The canon is received through tradition: the liturgy, the patristic citations, the ancient manuscripts, and the practice of the churches. The core 73 books (39 + 7 deuterocanonicals + 27 NT) are universally accepted. Additional books like Psalm 151, 1 Esdras, and Prayer of Manasseh are accepted in the Greek-tradition OO churches; the Ethiopian canon extends further to 81. This diversity is not a weakness but reflects the conciliar, distributed nature of Orthodox authority.

What is the difference between “deuterocanonical” and “apocryphal”?▶

“Deuterocanonical” (second canon) is the Catholic and Orthodox term for the books accepted as canonical that are not in the Hebrew Bible — recognizing that they entered the canon’s formal definition slightly later or through different channels than the protocanonical books, but affirming their full scriptural authority. “Apocryphal” means “hidden” and is the Protestant term for these same books — implying they are non-canonical additions. There is also a category of “Pseudepigrapha” (books falsely attributed to biblical figures) that neither Catholics, Orthodox, nor Protestants accept as canonical: texts like the Gospel of Thomas, the Apocalypse of Peter, or the Shepherd of Hermas.

If the canon was disputed for centuries, how can we trust any of it?▶

This question assumes that divine inspiration requires immediate universal recognition, which is not a biblical or patristic principle. The Orthodox answer is that the Church, guided by the Holy Spirit, discerned the canon through its life, worship, and theological reflection over time — exactly as it discerned the doctrines of the Trinity and the nature of Christ. The canon debates demonstrate serious, careful engagement with the scriptures, not casual acceptance. The books that were disputed (Hebrews, Revelation, James) were doubted precisely because they were scrutinized seriously; their eventual acceptance reflects the Church’s Spirit-guided confidence, not arbitrary decision.

Glossary

▶
Septuagint (LXX)
The Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, produced in Alexandria c. 285–150 BC. Used by the New Testament authors and the early Church. Includes the deuterocanonical books.
Deuterocanonical
“Second canon.” The seven books (Tobit, Judith, 1–2 Maccabees, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch) included in Catholic and Orthodox Bibles but not the Protestant Bible. Fully canonical in OO, EO, and RC traditions.
Protocanonical
The 39 Old Testament books accepted by all traditions and corresponding to the Hebrew Bible. Also called the “first canon.”
Apocrypha
Protestant term for the deuterocanonical books, implying non-canonical status. From Greek apokryphos (hidden). Also used technically for books that no tradition accepts as canonical (e.g., Gospel of Thomas).
Peshitta
The standard Syriac Bible, meaning “simple.” The Peshitta OT was translated from Hebrew; the original Peshitta NT contained 22 books (lacking Revelation, 2–3 John, 2 Peter, Jude).
Jamnia / Yavneh
Rabbinic gatherings at Yavneh c. AD 90 where post-Temple Jewish scholars worked toward canon consensus, excluding Greek-language books. The older term “Council of Jamnia” (a 19th-century scholarly construction) is now largely abandoned; the canon settled through gradual rabbinic discussion, not a single formal council. Protestant canon arguments nonetheless depend on following this post-Christian Jewish process.
Canon
From Greek kanon (measuring rod). The list of books recognized as divinely inspired scripture by a given tradition. Different Christian traditions have different canons.
Hebraica Veritas
“Hebrew truth.” Jerome’s principle that the Hebrew text of the OT is more authoritative than the Greek Septuagint. Adopted by Reformers to justify using the shorter Hebrew canon.
Muratorian Fragment
The earliest known list of NT books (c. AD 170, Rome). Lists most of the NT but includes non-canonical texts and lacks several books, demonstrating the canon was still developing.
Sola Scriptura
“Scripture alone.” The Protestant principle that scripture is the only authoritative source of doctrine. Paradoxically, the content of scripture itself depends on tradition and the Church to determine which books are scripture.
1 Enoch (Henok)
An ancient Jewish apocalyptic text quoted in Jude 14–15. Canonical only in the Ethiopian Orthodox Church. Preserved complete only in the Ethiopian Ge’ez language. Cited by Tertullian, Clement, and other early Fathers.
Tewahedo
“Being made one.” The Ge’ez name of the Ethiopian and Eritrean Orthodox churches, referring to the complete union of divinity and humanity in Christ (miaphysitism). The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church holds the largest biblical canon of any Christian tradition.
