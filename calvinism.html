THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Calvinism & TULIP — A Comprehensive Oriental Orthodox Response

"God our Savior, who desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth." — 1 Timothy 2:3–4

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

Calvinism — more precisely, Reformed soteriology as codified at the Synod of Dort (1618–1619) — is summarized by the acronym TULIP: Total Depravity, Unconditional Election, Limited Atonement, Irresistible Grace, and Perseverance of the Saints. Together these five points form a coherent system of monergism: the teaching that God alone acts in salvation, that the human will contributes nothing, and that God has eternally decreed who will be saved and who will be lost, without reference to any foreseen response on their part.

The Oriental Orthodox Church — the family of Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, Ethiopian, Eritrean, and Indian (Malankara) churches — predates this system by over a millennium and a half. Her soteriology was formed not in sixteenth-century Geneva but in apostolic Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem, and articulated by the Fathers: St. Athanasius, St. Cyril of Alexandria, St. John Chrysostom, St. Severus of Antioch, St. Ephrem the Syrian. That tradition teaches synergy (συνέργεια): salvation is entirely God's gift, initiated and sustained by grace alone — yet grace does not crush the human will, but heals it and invites its free cooperation. As St. John Cassian wrote against the predestinarians of his own day, "the grace of God always co-operates with our will for its advantage" (Conferences 13.13).

This page examines each petal of TULIP in depth: the Calvinist claim, the key proof texts, the Oriental Orthodox response from Scripture and the Fathers, and why the difference matters for prayer, repentance, evangelism, and the life of the Church. The aim is not caricature. Calvinism is a serious system built by serious exegetes, and it rightly opposes Pelagianism — the heresy that man can save himself. The Orthodox quarrel is not with grace, but with the claim that grace destroys freedom, that Christ died only for some, and that God created millions of souls with no possibility of salvation.

Calvinist Framework

Monergism: God alone acts. Salvation is decreed before creation for the elect alone; the will is passive in regeneration; grace cannot be resisted by the chosen nor obtained by the reprobate; the truly regenerate cannot finally fall away.

Oriental Orthodox Framework

Synergy: grace initiates, enables, and completes — and the healed will freely cooperates. God desires all to be saved; Christ died for the whole world; grace can be resisted and, through persistent impenitence, lost; salvation is lifelong communion, not a one-time decree.

A Note on Quotations

Every Scripture quotation on this page has been checked against the text. Patristic quotations are given from the standard translations (ANF/NPNF and critical editions); where a citation summarizes a Father's documented teaching rather than quoting his exact words, it is marked Paraphrase. Where a traditional attribution is disputed by scholarship, this is stated plainly — the truth is never served by a false citation.

II. T — Total Depravity

▼

The Calvinist Position

Total depravity teaches that the Fall has corrupted every faculty of human nature — mind, will, and affections — so completely that fallen man is "dead in trespasses and sins," utterly unable to will, desire, or even cooperate with his own salvation. Man is not merely sick; he is a spiritual corpse. Any movement toward God must therefore be entirely God's unilateral work: regeneration precedes faith, and the unregenerate can no more choose God than a dead man can raise himself. The Canons of Dort (III/IV.3) declare that all men are "neither willing nor able to return to God... nor to dispose themselves to reformation."

The Oriental Orthodox Response

The Orthodox confess the devastation of the Fall without hesitation: through Adam came death, corruption (φθορά), darkened understanding, and a powerful inclination toward sin. No one saves himself; no one even begins to turn to God without grace going before. But the Orthodox deny that the image of God in man was destroyed. Scripture continues to speak of fallen man as bearing God's image (Genesis 9:6; James 3:9), and God continues to address fallen man as a being capable of response: "Choose this day whom you will serve" (Joshua 24:15); "Turn yourselves, and live" (Ezekiel 18:32). The Fathers consistently describe fallen humanity as gravely sick, wounded, enslaved to passions — the man left half dead on the road to Jericho — needing the Good Samaritan's healing, not a corpse needing nothing because it can receive nothing.

The deeper difference concerns inherited guilt. Oriental Orthodoxy teaches ancestral sin: from Adam we inherit death, corruption, and the inclination to sin — but not the personal guilt of Adam's act. Each man answers for his own sins (Ezekiel 18:20; Deuteronomy 24:16). The Calvinist doctrine builds on the later Western reading of Romans 5:12 ("in whom all sinned"), which rests on the Latin rendering in quo; the Greek ἐφ' ᾧ πάντες ἥμαρτον is read by the Eastern Fathers as "because all sinned" — death spread to all, and in that mortality all sin personally.

Ephesians 2:1–5
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"And you He made alive, who were dead in trespasses and sins…"
Calvinist use: "dead" means total inability — a corpse cannot believe. OO reading: "dead" is a real but figurative description of separation from God's life, not literal non-existence of the will. The same "dead" prodigal "came to himself" and resolved "I will arise and go to my father" (Luke 15:17–24) — and the father says "this my son was dead, and is alive again." The spiritually dead can still hear and respond to the voice of the Son (John 5:25). Death here is estrangement, healed by grace that invites, not a metaphysical incapacity that excludes invitation.
Genesis 6:5 / Jeremiah 17:9
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"Every intent of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually." / "The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked."
Calvinist use: universal description of every unregenerate heart in every age. OO reading: Genesis 6:5 describes the extreme corruption of the generation of the Flood — yet even then "Noah found grace in the eyes of the LORD" (6:8). Jeremiah laments the deceitfulness of the heart, yet the same book commands "return, you backsliding children" (3:22) — a command emptied of meaning if return is impossible. These texts prove pervasive corruption, which Orthodoxy affirms; they do not prove total inability to respond to grace.
Psalm 51:5
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"Behold, I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin my mother conceived me."
Calvinist use: proof of inherited guilt from conception. OO reading: David's penitential poetry confesses that he was born into a fallen, sin-saturated condition — ancestral corruption — not that a newborn bears the forensic guilt of Adam. The same psalm assumes David can repent, be washed, and have a clean heart created in him (51:7–10) — the very synergy of contrition and grace that Orthodoxy teaches.
Romans 3:10–12
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"There is none righteous, no, not one; there is none who understands; there is none who seeks after God."
Calvinist use: no one ever seeks God; therefore God must regenerate before any seeking. OO reading: Paul is cataloguing Psalm 14's indictment of human wickedness to prove that Jew and Gentile alike are under sin and cannot be justified by works. It is hyperbolic-prophetic idiom: Scripture elsewhere names seekers of God (Psalm 34:10; Acts 10:1–4, where Cornelius's prayers ascend before his conversion; Acts 17:27, where God arranged the nations "so that they should seek the Lord"). No one seeks God unaided — grace always goes first — but prevenient grace makes seeking real, not impossible.
John 15:5
Common Ground
"Without Me you can do nothing."
Both traditions affirm this absolutely. The Orthodox add only what the verse itself presumes: "Abide in Me" is a command addressed to free persons, and the next verse warns "if anyone does not abide in Me, he is cast out as a branch and is withered" (15:6) — a warning St. Cyril of Alexandria reads as a real possibility for believers, which monergism cannot allow.
Deuteronomy 30:19
OO Reading
"I have set before you life and death, blessing and cursing; therefore choose life, that both you and your descendants may live."
God's covenantal address to fallen Israel assumes the capacity, under grace, to choose. Moses adds, "this commandment... is not too mysterious for you, nor is it far off" (30:11). The Fathers cited this verse constantly — Irenaeus, Cyril of Jerusalem, Chrysostom — as the standing refutation of any doctrine that makes God's commands addressed to beings incapable of obeying or refusing.

Patristic Witness

St. Irenaeus of Lyons Direct Quote
Against Heresies IV.37.1 (c. AD 180)
"God made man a free [agent] from the beginning, possessing his own power, even as he does his own soul, to obey the behests of God voluntarily, and not by compulsion of God… For there is no coercion with God, but a good will [towards us] is present with Him continually."
Writing a century after the apostles, Irenaeus treats free will as the apostolic teaching itself — and compulsion as foreign to God's character.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem Direct Quote
Catechetical Lectures 4.18, 4.21 (c. AD 350)
"Know also that the soul is self-governed (αὐτεξούσιος)… The soul has free will: and the devil indeed can suggest, but he has not the power to compel against the will."
Spoken to catechumens as basic, non-negotiable Christian instruction: even after the Fall, even the devil cannot force the soul — much less does God.
St. Athanasius the Apostolic Paraphrase
On the Incarnation §§4–7 (c. AD 318)
Athanasius describes the Fall as humanity turning from contemplation of God toward corruption and non-being — a catastrophic sickness of nature that man could not reverse, which is precisely why the Word became flesh: to renew the image of God in man, "as when a portrait has been effaced, the subject must come and sit again so the likeness can be redrawn."
The image is effaced and must be renewed — not annihilated. The Incarnation heals nature; it does not replace a corpse with a puppet.

Why This Matters

If man is a corpse, preaching, warning, and exhortation are addressed to no one; repentance becomes something done to a man rather than by him under grace. The entire grammar of Scripture — command, plea, warning, lament ("How often I wanted to gather your children together… but you were not willing!" Matthew 23:37) — assumes the wounded yet real freedom that the Orthodox confess and that two centuries of pre-Augustinian Fathers unanimously taught.

III. U — Unconditional Election

▼

The Calvinist Position

Before the foundation of the world, God unconditionally chose certain individuals for salvation — not on the basis of foreseen faith, repentance, or anything in them, but solely by His sovereign good pleasure. The rest of humanity is passed over (or, in Calvin's own stronger formulation, actively reprobated) and left to just condemnation. Calvin called this the decretum horribile — the "dreadful decree" (Institutes III.23.7). Election is therefore the cause of faith, never a response to it.

The Oriental Orthodox Response

Orthodoxy affirms election — Scripture is full of it — but reads it as the Fathers did: christological, corporate, and according to foreknowledge. God elects in Christ; the elect people is the Church; and predestination follows foreknowledge of free response, as Paul's own order states: "whom He foreknew, He also predestined" (Romans 8:29), and Peter's address confirms: "elect according to the foreknowledge of God the Father" (1 Peter 1:2). Divine foreknowledge does not cause what it foresees, any more than a man watching a ship from a cliff causes it to sail. What Orthodoxy rejects is the claim that God, who "desires all men to be saved" (1 Timothy 2:4) and "is not willing that any should perish" (2 Peter 3:9), secretly willed from eternity that most should perish — a teaching that divides God's revealed will from a contrary hidden will and makes the Gospel offer disingenuous.

Romans 8:29–30
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"For whom He foreknew, He also predestined to be conformed to the image of His Son… whom He predestined, these He also called; whom He called, these He also justified…"
Calvinist use: an unbreakable "golden chain" from eternal decree to glorification. OO reading: the chain itself begins with foreknowledge. The Fathers from Origen through Chrysostom and Cyril of Alexandria read this as God predestining on the basis of what He eternally foreknows of each person's free response. The passage describes God's faithful purpose for those who love Him (8:28) — it nowhere says God's foreknowledge is merely His decision, nor that any link is independent of abiding in Christ.
Romans 9:10–21
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"Jacob I have loved, but Esau I have hated… Does not the potter have power over the clay?"
Calvinist use: the classic proof of double predestination of individuals. OO reading: following Chrysostom (Homilies on Romans 16), Paul's subject in Romans 9–11 is the destiny of Israel: why the covenant people largely rejected Messiah while Gentiles entered. "Jacob/Esau" quotes Malachi 1:2–3, where the names denote the nations Israel and Edom; God's "hatred" is the Hebrew idiom of preference in election to historical vocation, not eternal damnation (cf. Luke 14:26). Pharaoh hardened his own heart before God confirmed the hardening (Exodus 8:15, 32). And the potter image, drawn from Jeremiah 18:1–10, is explicitly conditional in its source: "if that nation turns from its evil, I will relent." Romans 9 ends not in fatalism but in chapter 11: hardened Israel can be grafted in again "if they do not continue in unbelief" (11:23).
Ephesians 1:4–5
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and without blame before Him in love."
Calvinist use: unconditional individual election before creation. OO reading: the election is "in Him" — Christ is the Elect One (Isaiah 42:1; Luke 9:35), and all who are united to Him by faith and baptism share His election, as Israel's election was shared by all in Abraham. The choosing is also unto holiness ("that we should be holy"), describing God's purpose for the Church, not a roster excluding most of mankind from the possibility of belonging.
Acts 13:48
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"And as many as had been appointed to eternal life believed."
Calvinist use: appointment precedes and causes belief. OO reading: the participle τεταγμένοι ("appointed/disposed/set in order") describes those Gentiles who — in contrast to the Jews of v.46 who "judged themselves unworthy of everlasting life" — were positioned toward eternal life; many translators and Fathers read it as "as many as were disposed to eternal life believed." Even granting "appointed," Luke does not say the appointment was unconditional or irrespective of God's foreknowledge. A narrative verse cannot bear the weight of an eternal double decree.
1 Timothy 2:3–4
OO Reading
"God our Savior, who desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth."
The verse Calvinism must qualify ("all kinds of men," "the elect among all classes") in order to survive. The Fathers read it plainly: God's salvific will is universal. St. John of Damascus distinguished God's antecedent will (that all be saved) from His consequent will (judgment respecting human refusal) — the refusal originates in man, never in a divine decree of reprobation.
Ezekiel 33:11
OO Reading
"'As I live,' says the Lord GOD, 'I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live. Turn, turn from your evil ways! For why should you die, O house of Israel?'"
God swears by His own life that He does not will the sinner's death. The question "why should you die?" is unanswerable under double predestination — the honest Calvinist answer would be "because God decreed it," which is precisely what God here denies on oath.

Patristic Witness

St. John Chrysostom Direct Quote
Homily on Ephesians 1 (c. AD 395)
"God did not merely choose us, but chose us 'that we should be holy and without blemish.' … He chose us out of His own love — but our virtue also was required; for had holiness not depended also on us, the exhortations would have been superfluous."
Chrysostom — the most prolific patristic exegete of Paul — reads election as inseparable from foreseen and required cooperation.
St. Cyril of Alexandria Paraphrase
Commentary on Romans, on 8:29 (c. AD 430)
Cyril teaches that God's foreknowledge of who would believe precedes, in order of reason, His predestining: God "predestined those whom He foreknew" — knowledge of free response, not arbitrary decree, is the basis of the call.
The chief doctor of the Oriental Orthodox communion, writing two centuries before any TULIP-like system existed in the East.
St. Justin Martyr Direct Quote
First Apology 43 (c. AD 155)
"We have learned from the prophets, and we hold it to be true, that punishments, and chastisements, and good rewards, are rendered according to the merit of each man's actions. Since if it be not so, but all things happen by fate, neither is anything at all in our own power."
The Church's earliest apologist treats deterministic fate — pagan or Christian-flavored — as destructive of all moral reality.

Why This Matters

Under unconditional election, no one can be told "God loves you and Christ died for you" with certainty — the evangelist does not know if it is true of his hearer. Prayer for the conversion of a soul becomes prayer that a decree might exist which already either exists or does not. Orthodoxy can say to every human being without exception: God made you for Himself, Christ died for you, the Spirit calls you — come.

IV. L — Limited Atonement

▼

The Calvinist Position

Christ's atoning death was intended for, and is effective for, the elect alone. Since (it is argued) Christ's death actually accomplishes salvation rather than merely making it possible, and since not all are saved, Christ must not have died for all — otherwise either all would be saved (universalism) or Christ's blood was shed in vain for some. The atonement is "sufficient for all" in value, the Canons of Dort concede (II.3), but "efficient" only for the elect, for whom alone Christ intended it.

The Oriental Orthodox Response

This is the petal with the weakest scriptural support and the latest historical pedigree — even many Reformed theologians ("four-point Calvinists," Amyraldians) abandon it. Scripture's witness is overwhelming and explicit: Christ is "the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29), "the propitiation… not for ours only but also for the whole world" (1 John 2:2), who "gave Himself a ransom for all" (1 Timothy 2:6), who "tasted death for everyone" (Hebrews 2:9), "the Savior of all men, especially of those who believe" (1 Timothy 4:10). In every Oriental Orthodox liturgy the priest confesses Christ's blood "given for us and for many for the salvation of the whole world." The atonement is universal in scope and intention; its fruit is received through faith, baptism, and abiding in Christ. The limitation lies not in the Physician's medicine but in the patient's refusal to drink it.

1 John 2:2
OO Reading
"And He Himself is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the whole world."
John explicitly distinguishes "ours" (believers) from "the whole world" and extends the propitiation to both. The Calvinist gloss — "the world" means "the elect scattered through the world" — inverts the verse's own contrast. When John means believers he says "us"; he then deliberately adds the larger circle.
2 Peter 2:1
OO Reading
"There will be false teachers among you… even denying the Lord who bought them, and bring on themselves swift destruction."
Peter says the Master bought (ἀγοράσαντα) the very heretics who perish. Here is a text where Christ's redemptive purchase extends to people who are finally lost — impossible if the atonement was made only for those who will infallibly be saved.
John 10:11, 15
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"I lay down My life for the sheep."
Calvinist use: Christ dies for the sheep — therefore not for the goats. OO reading: a logical fallacy (negative inference). That Christ dies for the sheep does not state He dies only for them — Paul says Christ "loved me and gave Himself for me" (Galatians 2:20) without excluding everyone else. And in the same discourse Christ says "I have other sheep, which are not of this fold" (10:16) and that He gives His flesh "for the life of the world" (6:51).
Isaiah 53:6
OO Reading
"All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned, every one, to his own way; and the LORD has laid on Him the iniquity of us all."
The "all" who went astray is universal — every Calvinist agrees. The same "all" has its iniquity laid on the Servant. To shrink the second "all" while keeping the first universal is to break the verse's deliberate symmetry.
Hebrews 2:9
OO Reading
"…that He, by the grace of God, might taste death for everyone."
The high-priestly argument of Hebrews rests on Christ's solidarity with the whole of "flesh and blood" humanity (2:14). He tastes death ὑπὲρ παντός — for every one — which is why the warning passages of the same epistle (6:4–6; 10:26–29) treat apostates as those who "counted the blood of the covenant by which they were sanctified a common thing."
Matthew 23:37
OO Reading
"O Jerusalem, Jerusalem… How often I wanted to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, but you were not willing!"
The incarnate Word declares His will to gather those who refused. His saving will was real ("I wanted"); the obstacle was theirs ("you were not willing"). Under limited atonement and irresistible grace, this lament is theater. The Orthodox take the Lord's tears at face value.

Patristic Witness

St. Athanasius the Apostolic Direct Quote
On the Incarnation §9 (c. AD 318)
"For the Word, perceiving that no otherwise could the corruption of men be undone save by death as a necessary condition… takes to Himself a body capable of death, that it, by partaking of the Word Who is above all, might be worthy to die in the stead of all (ἀντὶ πάντων)."
"In the stead of all" — Athanasius repeats the universal scope of the death of Christ throughout the treatise: "the death of all was accomplished in the Lord's body" (§20).
St. Gregory of Nyssa Paraphrase
Catechetical Oration §32 (c. AD 385)
Gregory teaches that Christ's death was offered for the whole of human nature: in assuming our common nature, the Word's saving work touches the entire race, "the lost sheep" being all of humanity carried home on the Shepherd's shoulders.
The Cappadocian reading: the atonement's object is human nature as such — no man is outside its intention.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem Direct Quote
Catechetical Lectures 13.2 (c. AD 350)
"…the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world… He did not suffer for a few, but the world was saved through His suffering."
Wonder, not restriction, is the catechetical instinct of the ancient Church when it contemplates the scope of the Cross.

Why This Matters

At the deathbed, in the confessional, before the unbeliever, the Orthodox priest can say what the consistent Calvinist cannot: Christ shed His blood for you. The Eucharist itself — "given for us... for the salvation of the whole world" — would be unsingable under a limited intention. And the character of God is at stake: a God who creates souls for whom He deliberately provides no remedy is not the God who "so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son" (John 3:16).

V. I — Irresistible Grace

▼

The Calvinist Position

When God extends His effectual call to the elect, that call cannot finally be resisted. The Spirit regenerates the chosen sinner, infallibly producing faith. Grace does not invite or persuade; it accomplishes. The outward Gospel call goes to many, but the inward effectual call is given only to the elect, and it never fails. Conversely, those not given this grace cannot come, no matter what they hear.

The Oriental Orthodox Response

Orthodoxy joyfully confesses that grace is powerful, prevenient, and absolutely necessary — no one comes unless the Father draws him (John 6:44). But Scripture defines the manner of the drawing: "I drew them with gentle cords, with bands of love" (Hosea 11:4) — and Israel still resisted (11:5, 7). Stephen tells the Sanhedrin, "You always resist the Holy Spirit; as your fathers did, so do you" (Acts 7:51). Christ stands at the door and knocks (Revelation 3:20); He does not break it down. The Fathers were unanimous: God draws by persuasion, illumination, and love, in a manner that respects the freedom He Himself created. St. John Chrysostom, preaching on John 6:44 itself, insists the verse "does not take away our free will, but shows that we need help" — for "He draws him that has the will to come."

John 6:44
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"No one can come to Me unless the Father who sent Me draws him."
Calvinist use: the drawing is effectual and given only to the elect. OO reading: the necessity of drawing is affirmed — this is prevenient grace, and Orthodoxy preaches it against every Pelagian. But the same Gospel defines the scope: "And I, if I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all peoples to Myself" (John 12:32) — the same verb, ἑλκύω. If drawing were irresistible, John 12:32 would entail universalism. The drawing is universal and resistible; those who come are those who, drawn, do not refuse: "Everyone who has heard and learned from the Father comes to Me" (6:45).
Acts 7:51
OO Reading
"You stiff-necked and uncircumcised in heart and ears! You always resist the Holy Spirit; as your fathers did, so do you."
An inspired, explicit statement that the Holy Spirit's work upon hearts is resisted — habitually, generationally. The Calvinist reply that only the "outward call" was resisted imports a distinction the text does not make: Stephen says they resist the Spirit Himself.
Acts 16:14 (Lydia)
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"The Lord opened her heart to heed the things spoken by Paul."
Calvinist use: God unilaterally opened her heart — monergistic regeneration. OO reading: Orthodoxy says Amen: God always initiates. But Luke has already described Lydia as one "who worshiped God" — a soul cooperating with prevenient grace before Paul ever spoke. God opens; Lydia heeds. Initiative and response, grace and freedom — synergy in a single verse.
Hosea 11:4–5
OO Reading
"I drew them with gentle cords, with bands of love… He shall not return to the land of Egypt; but the Assyrian shall be his king, because they refused to repent."
God's own description of His drawing: cords of a man, bands of love — and it was refused. The vocabulary of John 6:44 has its Old Testament home here, in resistible covenant love, not in mechanical causation.
Revelation 3:20
OO Reading
"Behold, I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears My voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and dine with him, and he with Me."
Spoken by the risen Christ to a baptized but lukewarm church. The order is His initiative (He comes, He knocks, He speaks), human response (hear, open), then communion (dine together). It is the most compact icon of synergy in Scripture.
Matthew 11:28
OO Reading
"Come to Me, all you who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."
The universal invitation of Christ. An invitation extended to "all" that secretly can be accepted only by a few pre-selected hearers is not an invitation in any honest sense of the word. The Orthodox preach it as it stands.

Patristic Witness

St. John Chrysostom Direct Quote
Homilies on John 46, on John 6:44 (c. AD 390)
"But the Manichaeans spring upon these words, saying that nothing lies in our own power; yet the expression shows that we are masters of our will. … This does not take away our free will, but shows that we greatly need help; and He brings not the unwilling, but draws him that has the will."
Chrysostom answers the deterministic reading of John 6:44 by name, three centuries before Calvin's birth — against the Manichaeans who used it exactly as TULIP later would.
St. Cyril of Alexandria Paraphrase
Commentary on John, Book 4, on 6:44 (c. AD 428)
Cyril explains the Father's drawing as worked through revelation and teaching that wins the soul — a drawing that operates by illumination and persuasion rather than violence, for God "does all things gently and by counsel" with rational creatures.
Alexandria's greatest theologian reads the very proof text of irresistible grace as a description of persuasive, not coercive, grace.
St. John Cassian Direct Quote
Conferences 13.13 (c. AD 426)
"And so these are somehow mixed up and indiscriminately confused… the grace of God always co-operates with our will for its advantage, and in all things assists, protects, and defends it, in such a way as sometimes even to require and look for some efforts of good will from it, that it may not appear to confer its gifts on one who is asleep."
Written against the radical Augustinians of southern Gaul — the fifth-century Church's direct answer to monergism, received and kept by the East.
St. Ephrem the Syrian Paraphrase
Hymns on Paradise & Hymns on Faith (c. AD 360)
Ephrem sings that God places the key of paradise in the hand of human freedom: grace surrounds, woos, and adorns the soul, but the door of the bridal chamber is opened from within. Adam fell by free choice; the second Adam restores the choice and awaits its "yes."
The voice of the Syriac tradition, central to Oriental Orthodox identity — synergy in poetry.

Why This Matters

If grace is irresistible, the unconverted have a perfect excuse: they were never given the only grace that could move them. If grace persuades, every refusal is genuinely ours and every "yes" is genuinely free — which is why the Orthodox tradition can speak of God as the Bridegroom who wins His bride, not a force that overrides her. Love that cannot be declined is not love.

VI. P — Perseverance of the Saints

▼

The Calvinist Position

Those whom God has elected, redeemed, and effectually called will infallibly persevere to the end. True believers may fall into grievous sin, but they can never finally or totally fall from grace; apparent apostates were never truly regenerate ("they went out from us, but they were not of us," 1 John 2:19). This is popularly summarized as "once saved, always saved," though Reformed theologians prefer the stronger formulation that the elect will persevere in faith and holiness.

The Oriental Orthodox Response

Orthodoxy proclaims the full assurance of God's faithfulness: no power in heaven or earth can snatch the believer from Christ's hand (John 10:28; Romans 8:38–39). But Scripture warns — repeatedly, soberly, and to baptized believers — that a man can remove himself: by apostasy, by persistent unrepented sin, by failing to abide in the Vine. Hebrews 6:4–6 describes those "once enlightened" who "have tasted the heavenly gift, and have become partakers of the Holy Spirit" and yet fall away. Paul disciplines his own body "lest, when I have preached to others, I myself should become disqualified" (1 Corinthians 9:27) and tells the Galatians, "you have fallen from grace" (5:4). The Orthodox answer to anxiety is not a doctrine that contradicts these warnings, but the continual remedy of repentance, confession, and the Eucharist. Salvation is a living communion to be guarded — "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you" (Philippians 2:12–13): both halves of the verse, together, are the whole Orthodox doctrine.

John 10:28–29
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"I give them eternal life, and they shall never perish; neither shall anyone snatch them out of My hand."
Calvinist use: the sheep are eternally secure. OO reading: gloriously true — against every external enemy. No one can snatch them. But the promise belongs to the sheep who "hear My voice... and follow Me" (10:27, present tense), and Scripture elsewhere warns that the sheep can stop following. Judas was given by the Father into Christ's hand (John 17:12) and was lost — "the son of perdition" — not snatched, but departing.
Hebrews 6:4–6
OO Reading
"For it is impossible for those who were once enlightened, and have tasted the heavenly gift, and have become partakers of the Holy Spirit… if they fall away, to renew them again to repentance."
The persons described are regenerate by every biblical measure — enlightened (the ancient Church's very word for baptism), tasting the heavenly gift, partaking of the Spirit. The Calvinist must say they were never truly saved; but then the warning warns no one, since by definition it cannot apply to the saved nor matter to the unsaved. The Fathers read it as a real warning to real Christians against the real possibility of apostasy.
Hebrews 10:26–29
OO Reading
"For if we sin willfully after we have received the knowledge of the truth, there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins… of how much worse punishment… will he be thought worthy who has trampled the Son of God underfoot, counted the blood of the covenant by which he was sanctified a common thing?"
"We" — the author includes himself. The apostate was "sanctified" by the blood of the covenant. Spoken of a person now facing "fiery indignation," this is fatal to the claim that the sanctified cannot perish.
2 Peter 2:20–22
OO Reading
"For if, after they have escaped the pollutions of the world through the knowledge of the Lord and Savior Jesus Christ, they are again entangled in them and overcome, the latter end is worse for them than the beginning."
Those who "escaped... through the knowledge of the Lord" — Peter's own definition of conversion (1:3–4) — are again entangled and end worse than they began. The dog returning to its vomit is a proverb about a real dog, not an illusory one.
Romans 8:38–39
Calvinist UseOO Reading
"Neither death nor life, nor angels nor principalities… nor any other created thing, shall be able to separate us from the love of God which is in Christ Jesus our Lord."
Calvinist use: absolute unconditional security. OO reading: Paul's list is a list of external powers — and the Orthodox sing this triumph at every funeral. But the human will is not on the list, because the will is not a third party: Paul has just finished warning these same Romans, "if you live according to the flesh you will die" (8:13) and later, "you stand by faith… otherwise you also will be cut off" (11:20–22). Nothing can separate us from God's love; we can still refuse to live in it.
Ezekiel 18:24
OO Reading
"But when a righteous man turns away from his righteousness and commits iniquity… shall he live? All the righteousness which he has done shall not be remembered; because of the unfaithfulness of which he is guilty… he shall die."
God's own statement of the principle. The same chapter that comforts the penitent wicked (18:21–23) warns the apostate righteous. Both directions are real; Calvinism can preach only one of them.

Patristic Witness

St. John Chrysostom Paraphrase
Homilies on Hebrews 9 (on Heb. 6) (c. AD 400)
Chrysostom treats the warning of Hebrews 6 as addressed to the baptized: there is one baptism and no second cross, therefore the Christian must not presume; yet he immediately guards against despair — repentance remains open to the fallen, for the "impossibility" concerns a second baptism, not the mercy of God toward the penitent.
Real danger without despair: the exact pastoral balance of Orthodoxy — neither "once saved always saved" nor hopelessness.
St. Basil the Great Paraphrase
Moralia & ascetic writings (c. AD 370)
Basil exhorts the baptized to guard the grace received, warning that negligence, willful sin, and abandonment of the commandments grieve the Spirit and forfeit what was given — the lamp must be kept burning with the oil of obedience until the Bridegroom comes.
The monastic tradition's entire structure of vigilance (νῆψις) presumes that grace can be neglected and lost.
St. Polycarp of Smyrna Direct Quote
Epistle to the Philippians 5 (c. AD 110)
"If we please Him in this present world, we shall receive also the future world, according as He has promised to us that He will raise us again from the dead, and that if we live worthily of Him, 'we shall also reign together with Him,' provided only we believe."
A disciple of the Apostle John himself makes the inheritance conditional on faithful living — the apostolic mind, one generation from the apostles.

Why This Matters

Perseverance doctrine produces, by temperament, either anxiety (am I truly elect? was my faith real?) or presumption (I am sealed regardless of how I live). The Orthodox path is different: assurance rests not on introspecting a past conversion but on the present, objective faithfulness of God met in repentance, confession, and the Chalice — "His mercies are new every morning" (Lamentations 3:23). The Christian does not ask "was I saved?" but lives "I was saved, I am being saved, and I hope to be saved" — and abides in the Vine. (See the companion page: Assurance of Salvation.)

VII. Historical Development of Calvinism

▼

TULIP is not ancient. Every element of its codified form postdates the united witness of the early Church by more than a millennium. The timeline below traces how the system arose — and how the Church had already answered its central claims in the fifth century.

1st–3rd centuries

The Apostolic Fathers and apologists — Ignatius, Polycarp, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria — unanimously teach free will healed by grace, universal atonement, and the real possibility of falling. Determinism is the position of their opponents: Gnostics teaching salvation by fixed natures, and pagan fatalists.

c. 397–430

St. Augustine of Hippo, combating Pelagius's denial of the necessity of grace, develops an increasingly strong doctrine of predestination and irresistible operative grace in his late anti-Pelagian works (On the Predestination of the Saints, On the Gift of Perseverance). His anti-Pelagian victory is received by the whole Church; his predestinarian rigorism is not.

418 / 431

The Council of Carthage (418), confirmed in the East, condemns Pelagianism; the Ecumenical Council of Ephesus (431) — a council of the Oriental Orthodox heritage — likewise condemns the Pelagian Celestius. The Church rejects salvation by unaided human effort without ever endorsing determinism.

c. 426–429

St. John Cassian (Conferences, esp. 13) and the monks of Gaul resist the deterministic reading of Augustine, teaching that grace always cooperates with the will. The Second Council of Orange (529) later condemns Pelagianism and semi-Pelagianism while explicitly refusing to affirm predestination to evil — anathematizing those who teach it.

1525

Martin Luther, On the Bondage of the Will, against Erasmus: the human will is a beast ridden either by God or Satan. The Reformation takes up late-Augustinian monergism as its engine.

1536–1559

John Calvin publishes and successively expands the Institutes of the Christian Religion (definitive Latin edition 1559), articulating double predestination: God "determined with himself whatever he wished to happen with regard to every man… eternal life is foreordained for some, eternal damnation for others" (Institutes III.21.5).

1610

The followers of Jacobus Arminius present the Five Articles of Remonstrance, contesting unconditional election, limited atonement, irresistible grace, and unconditional perseverance.

1618–1619

The Synod of Dort condemns the Remonstrants and issues the Canons whose five heads of doctrine are later summarized as TULIP. This is the birth certificate of "five-point Calvinism" — over fifteen centuries after Pentecost.

1646–1647

The Westminster Confession of Faith fixes Reformed predestinarianism for the English-speaking world: "By the decree of God… some men and angels are predestinated unto everlasting life, and others foreordained to everlasting death" (WCF III.3).

All the while…

The Oriental Orthodox churches — separated from the West since 451 and untouched by either medieval Scholasticism or the Reformation — continue teaching what Alexandria, Antioch, and the desert always taught: grace first, freedom real, Christ for all, salvation guarded by repentance unto the end.

VIII. The Oriental Orthodox Doctrine of Salvation

▼

Orthodoxy does not merely critique; it offers the older, fuller positive vision. Salvation is not a courtroom verdict issued once, but healing, adoption, and union with God, mediated through the life of the Church Christ founded.

Salvation as Healing and Union

Christ is the Physician; sin is the sickness; the Church is the hospital. The Word became flesh "that we might become partakers of the divine nature" (2 Peter 1:4). St. Athanasius's famous summary — "He was made man that we might be made god" (On the Incarnation §54) — describes not a change of essence but participation by grace in God's own life. Justification, sanctification, and glorification are one continuous work of the Holy Trinity in the believer, never a legal fiction detached from transformation.

The Holy Mysteries

Grace reaches us through the seven mysteries (sacraments) of the Church: Baptism, the washing of regeneration and new birth (John 3:5; Titus 3:5) — given also to infants, as the Church has done from the beginning; Chrismation (Myron), the seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit; the Eucharist, the true Body and Blood of Christ, "the medicine of immortality" as St. Ignatius of Antioch called it (Ephesians 20), without which "you have no life in you" (John 6:53); Repentance and Confession, the continual restoration of the baptized (1 John 1:9; James 5:16); Unction of the Sick (James 5:14–15); Marriage; and Priesthood. Calvinism, by contrast, reduces the sacraments to signs and seals of a decree already fixed; Orthodoxy receives them as the actual arteries of divine life.

The Ascetic Life

Fasting, prayer, almsgiving, vigil — the disciplines the Lord assumed His disciples would practice ("when you fast," Matthew 6:16) — are not attempts to earn salvation but the cooperation of the healed will with the indwelling Spirit, the athlete's training of 1 Corinthians 9:24–27. The Coptic Church fasts more than two hundred days a year not to purchase heaven, but because the Bridegroom is worth the vigil.

Calvinist Order of Salvation

Eternal decree → effectual call → regeneration → faith → justification (forensic declaration) → sanctification → infallible glorification. The sacraments testify to what the decree fixed; the believer's role is receptive throughout.

Oriental Orthodox Way of Salvation

Universal saving will of God → prevenient grace and the preaching of the Church → free repentance and faith → baptismal regeneration and chrismation → eucharistic communion and continual repentance → synergistic growth in holiness → theosis, persevering by grace unto the end.

IX. Synergy & Theosis

▼

Synergy Is Not Pelagianism

Synergy (συνέργεια, "working together," from 1 Corinthians 3:9 and 2 Corinthians 6:1 — "we then, as workers together with Him") is the patristic name for the mystery Paul states in one breath: "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you both to will and to do for His good pleasure" (Philippians 2:12–13). It must be carefully distinguished from the two errors it stands between. Against Pelagianism: man never initiates his own salvation; every good movement of the will is preceded, enabled, and carried by grace. Against monergism: grace does not annihilate or bypass the will it came to heal; God, who made man free, saves him as free. St. Cyril of Jerusalem told his catechumens that God awaits the sincere resolve of the soul, for "it is God's to grant grace, but yours to receive and guard it" (Catechetical Lectures 1.4).

Theosis: The Goal

The end of salvation is not merely acquittal but theosis — deification by grace: "partakers of the divine nature" (2 Peter 1:4), "transformed into the same image from glory to glory" (2 Corinthians 3:18), "filled with all the fullness of God" (Ephesians 3:19). The Word became what we are to make us what He is — by participation, never by nature. This is why salvation cannot be a one-moment verdict: union is a life, and a life requires the continuing "yes" of the beloved. Forensic justification alone is a doorway mistaken for the house.

Foreknowledge Without Compulsion

God's eternal knowledge embraces all things, including every free choice — yet knowledge is not causation. The Fathers used the analogy of a physician who foreknows the course of an illness without causing it. God "foreknows all things, but does not predetermine all things," as St. John of Damascus summarized the Greek patristic consensus (Exact Exposition II.30): He foreknows what is in our power, and what He foreknows, He does not thereby force. Providence governs all; it governs free creatures as free.

X. The Fathers Against Determinism

▼

A continuous chain of witnesses, East and West, before and after Augustine, spanning the Greek, Syriac, and Coptic traditions. Direct quotations are from the standard English editions (ANF/NPNF and critical translations); summaries of documented teaching are marked.

St. Irenaeus of Lyons Direct Quote
Against Heresies IV.37.4 (c. AD 180)
"If then it were not in our power to do or not to do these things, what reason had the apostle, and much more the Lord Himself, to give us counsel to do some things, and to abstain from others? But because man is possessed of free will from the beginning, and God is possessed of free will, in whose likeness man was created, advice is always given to him to keep fast the good, which thing is done by means of obedience to God."
The disciple of Polycarp, disciple of John: commands presuppose freedom.
St. Clement of Alexandria Paraphrase
Stromata (Miscellanies), Books II & VII (c. AD 200)
Clement, head of Alexandria's catechetical school, teaches throughout the Stromata that faith is a free assent of the soul cooperating with God's drawing, that God's commands address self-determining creatures, and that salvation perfects rather than overrides the rational freedom in which man images God.
The fountainhead of the Alexandrian tradition that formed Athanasius and Cyril — the Coptic Church's own school.
St. Athanasius the Apostolic Direct Quote
Against the Heathen §4 (c. AD 318)
"For God has made man thus (that is, as an embodied spirit), and has willed that he should remain in incorruption; but men… gave themselves up to things corruptible… For the soul is made after the image and likeness of God… the way to God lies not far off, nor outside ourselves, but within us, and it is possible to find it from ourselves."
The 20th Pope of Alexandria locates the Fall in the soul's own free turning — and the return likewise engages the will, renewed by the Incarnate Word.
St. Gregory of Nyssa Direct Quote
Life of Moses II.3 (c. AD 390)
"We are in some manner our own parents, giving birth to ourselves by our own free choice in accordance with whatever we wish to be, whether male or female, moulding ourselves to the teaching of virtue or vice."
Bold Cappadocian language for the dignity of grace-enabled freedom in spiritual formation.
St. Gregory the Theologian (Nazianzen) Paraphrase
Orations on Baptism (esp. Oration 40) (c. AD 381)
Gregory teaches that salvation must be willed as well as given — God does not save the unwilling by force — and exhorts the baptized to guard the gift, since the illumination received can be darkened by a returning life of sin.
The Theologian of the Church: grace crowns consent; baptismal grace is guarded, not unconditional.
St. John Chrysostom Direct Quote
Homilies on Romans 16, on Rom. 9 (c. AD 391)
"Why then are some vessels of wrath, and some of mercy? Of their own free choice. God, however, being very good, shows the same kindness to both. … It is not on the potter that the honour and the dishonour of the things made of the lump depends, but upon the use made by those who employ it; so here also it depends on the free choice."
The golden-mouthed exegete answers the Calvinist reading of Romans 9 eleven centuries before Calvin wrote it.
St. Cyril of Alexandria Paraphrase
Commentary on John, on 15:6 (c. AD 428)
Cyril teaches that the branch genuinely in the Vine can, through its own negligence and refusal to remain, wither and be cast out — the warning of Christ is real, not hypothetical; remaining in Christ engages the believer's continuing will and love.
The pillar of Oriental Orthodox Christology, on the reality of falling away.
St. Ephrem the Syrian Paraphrase
Hymns on Paradise 3 (c. AD 360)
Ephrem sings that God planted the Tree as a contest for Adam's freedom: the command was light, the choice real, the fall free — and the door of Paradise reopens to freedom restored in Christ, for God "does not compel, He who is the Lord of freedom."
The harp of the Spirit — Syriac Orthodoxy's doctor — on freedom before and after the Fall.
The Macarian Homilies Attributed
Fifty Spiritual Homilies, Hom. 15 (4th–5th c.)
The homilies teach that the human will remains a real battlefield after baptism: grace does not bind the will with necessity, but works with the soul that consents, for God honors the freedom He gave even while His grace accomplishes all.
Honest note: these celebrated homilies are traditionally attributed to St. Macarius the Great of Egypt; modern scholarship regards them as the work of an anonymous Syrian author ("Pseudo-Macarius"). Their teaching on synergy is quoted here as a witness to the tradition, with the attribution stated plainly.
St. John Cassian Direct Quote
Conferences 13.12 (c. AD 426)
"And so the grace of God always co-operates with our will for its advantage… These two then, viz., the grace of God and free will, seem opposed to each other, but really are in harmony, and we gather from the system of goodness that we ought to have both alike."
The fifth century's direct, sustained answer to predestinarian monergism — the position the Christian East simply continued to hold.
St. Severus of Antioch Paraphrase
Cathedral Homilies & letters (c. AD 512–538)
Severus, the great miaphysite Patriarch, preaches salvation as the healing of human nature in the one incarnate nature of God the Word: Christ assumed our complete humanity, will included, to restore it — and continually exhorts his flock to repentance, vigilance, and the guarding of grace, presuming throughout that the baptized can fall and be restored.
The authoritative voice of Oriental Orthodox theology after Chalcedon: soteriology of healing and cooperation, not decree.

From Polycarp to Severus, the witness does not waver. The determinist reading of Scripture enters Christian theology as a late-Augustinian development, radicalized at the Reformation. It has no root in the soil of Alexandria, Antioch, Caesarea, or Edessa.

XI. Common Calvinist Objections

▼

Objection: Synergy makes man his own partial savior and robs God of glory

▼

The Claim

If the human will contributes anything — even mere consent — then salvation is partly man's work, man has grounds for boasting, and soli Deo gloria is compromised. Only monergism gives God all the glory.

Response

Consent is not merit. The beggar who opens his hand to receive a king's gift has not thereby earned the gift, contributed to its value, or acquired grounds for boasting — "what do you have that you did not receive?" (1 Corinthians 4:7). The sick man who lets the physician treat him does not share the physician's credit. Orthodoxy ascribes every atom of salvation — the initiative, the means, the power, the completion — to God's grace; the will's "yes" is itself enabled by grace. What the will retains is only the terrible capacity of refusal. Moreover, the glory argument cuts the other way: a God who must program His creatures' love receives less glory, not more, than the God whose love wins the free adoration of beings who could have refused Him. The angels and saints glorify God precisely as free worshipers.

Objection: Romans 9 settles the matter — "it is not of him who wills"

▼

The Claim

"So then it is not of him who wills, nor of him who runs, but of God who shows mercy" (Romans 9:16). Paul anticipates the free-will objection ("why does He still find fault?") and silences it: "who are you to reply against God?" (9:19–20). The objection Orthodox raise is the very objection Paul rebukes.

Response

Read whole, Romans 9–11 is Paul's anguished treatment of Israel's unbelief, and it ends in the opposite of fatalism: Israel was cut off "because of unbelief" (11:20, not decree), can be grafted in again "if they do not continue in unbelief" (11:23), and "God has committed them all to disobedience, that He might have mercy on all" (11:32). Verse 9:16 states that salvation's source is God's mercy, not human striving — which Orthodoxy confesses without reserve; it does not state that mercy is arbitrarily withheld. The rebuke of 9:20 silences the creature who blames God for his own sin, not the one who asks how God's justice works — Paul himself spends chapters 10–11 answering exactly that question, concluding "whoever calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved" (10:13). And the potter text Paul quotes (Jeremiah 18) explicitly makes the vessel's fate conditional on its turning. Chrysostom's homilies remain the classic patristic walkthrough.

Objection: If man can resist grace, then salvation finally depends on man, and no one would ever choose God

▼

The Claim

Given total depravity, no one would ever freely accept the Gospel; and if acceptance is possible, then the decisive difference between the saved and the lost lies in man, not God — which is semi-Pelagianism.

Response

The first premise assumes what it must prove — that fallen man under prevenient grace remains incapable of response. Orthodoxy teaches that grace goes before every soul (John 1:9; 12:32; Titus 2:11, "the grace of God that brings salvation has appeared to all men"), enabling the response it invites. As for the "decisive difference": the difference between the saved and the lost is not a meritorious quality in the saved; it is that the lost refused. Refusal is genuinely man's; acceptance is grace received. An honest analogy: two drowning men are thrown the same rope; one grasps it, one pushes it away. The grasping man does not boast of having saved himself — he thanks the one who threw the rope. The asymmetry is total: salvation is all of God; damnation is all of man. This is precisely the asymmetry Scripture maintains: "O Israel, you have destroyed yourself; but in Me is your help" (Hosea 13:9).

Objection: The early Church was semi-Pelagian until Augustine recovered Paul

▼

The Claim

The pre-Augustinian Fathers, lacking the Pelagian controversy to sharpen them, spoke loosely about free will; Augustine recovered the true Pauline doctrine of sovereign grace, which the Reformers restored.

Response

This concedes the historical point at issue: that for nearly four centuries — the centuries of the martyrs, the apologists, the councils of Nicaea and Constantinople, the canon of Scripture — the entire Church taught what Calvinism calls error. A "recovery" that requires the universal Church to have misread Paul from the apostles' own disciples onward (Polycarp heard John; Irenaeus heard Polycarp) is indistinguishable from innovation. Moreover the Fathers did not speak "loosely": Justin, Irenaeus, and Clement address determinism directly and reject it by name against Gnostics and fatalists. Even Augustine's own legacy is double — the Council of Orange (529), drawing on him, condemned Pelagianism while anathematizing predestination to evil. And the East had its sharpening controversy too: Cassian's Conferences engage the predestinarian position explicitly and reject it. The Oriental Orthodox claim is modest and verifiable: we teach today what the undivided early Church demonstrably taught.

Objection: Foreknowledge-based election is no freer — if God foreknows, the outcome is fixed

▼

The Claim

If God infallibly foreknows that a man will reject Him, that man cannot do otherwise; so conditional election collapses into the same necessity as unconditional election, and the Orthodox gain nothing.

Response

This confuses certainty with causation. God's knowledge is not a force acting on the will; it is His eternal sight of what the creature freely does. As the Fathers put it: foreknowledge no more causes my choice than my memory of yesterday's choice caused it. The man could do otherwise; had he done otherwise, God would eternally have known that instead. God's knowledge tracks the free act — the free act does not bend to the knowledge. The Calvinist decree, by contrast, is explicitly causal: God "foreordained" the outcome and grants or withholds the grace that determines it. The two positions differ exactly where it matters: in one, the reprobate perishes because God decreed it; in the other, because he himself refused a salvation really offered and really possible. Only the second is compatible with "I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked" (Ezekiel 33:11).

XII. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Q: Is Calvinism compatible with the early Church?▼

Not in its TULIP form. The apostolic and patristic Church universally preached repentance to all, practiced baptism as regeneration, and taught cooperation with grace. Cassian's Conference 13 directly engages and rejects predestinarian monergism in the fifth century; Chrysostom's exegesis of Romans 9 and John 6 contradicts unconditional election and irresistible grace. The Synod of Dort postdates the councils shared by the Oriental churches by more than a millennium. Calvinists appeal to the later Augustine — but the Greek, Syriac, and Coptic Fathers consistently taught free will healed by grace.

Q: Do the Oriental Orthodox believe in predestination at all?▼

Yes — in the biblical and patristic sense. Scripture uses the word (Romans 8:29–30; Ephesians 1:5,11), so the Church uses it: God predestines in Christ, on the basis of His eternal foreknowledge of free response, and corporately elects the Church as His people. What the Church rejects is unconditional individual election with its shadow, reprobation — the claim that God created some souls with no possibility of salvation.

Q: What is synergy, in one sentence?▼

Salvation is wholly God's grace, freely received and cooperated with by the will that grace itself has healed — "we are God's fellow workers" (1 Corinthians 3:9), "work out your own salvation… for it is God who works in you" (Philippians 2:12–13). It is neither Pelagianism (man initiates) nor monergism (man is bypassed).

Q: Can grace be resisted?▼

Yes — Scripture says so in as many words: "You always resist the Holy Spirit" (Acts 7:51); "How often I wanted to gather your children… but you were not willing" (Matthew 23:37). Grace is mighty to save all who do not refuse it, and God's drawing (John 6:44) is the persuasion of love (Hosea 11:4), not mechanical force.

Q: Can salvation be lost?▼

A baptized believer can fall from grace through apostasy or persistent unrepented sin — Hebrews 6:4–6, 10:26–29, 2 Peter 2:20–22, and Galatians 5:4 are real warnings to real Christians. But the door of repentance stands open as long as there is breath: confession restores the fallen, and no sincere penitent is ever refused. The Orthodox live between presumption and despair — in hope. See the companion page on Assurance of Salvation.

Q: Does God desire all to be saved?▼

Yes — 1 Timothy 2:4 states it; 2 Peter 3:9 repeats it; Ezekiel 33:11 swears it by God's own life; John 12:32 promises Christ will draw all to Himself. Any system requiring these texts to mean less than they say has placed its system above the Scriptures.

Q: What is the difference between ancestral sin and original sin?▼

Oriental Orthodoxy confesses ancestral sin: from Adam we inherit death, corruption, and a strong inclination to sin — but not the personal guilt of Adam's act (Ezekiel 18:20). The later Western doctrine adds inherited guilt, which total depravity then radicalizes into total inability. Infants are baptized for new birth into life in Christ, not because they stand condemned for Adam's deed.

Q: How should Romans 9 be read?▼

As the Fathers read it: within Romans 9–11 as a whole, where Paul wrestles with Israel's unbelief. Jacob and Esau are the nations Israel and Edom (Malachi 1:2–3); the potter is Jeremiah's explicitly conditional potter (Jeremiah 18:1–10); Israel's fall is "because of unbelief" and reversible "if they do not continue in unbelief" (Romans 11:20–23); and the conclusion is mercy on all (11:32). It teaches God's freedom in the economy of salvation — not double predestination.

Q: What about Augustine — do the Orthodox reject him?▼

No. His stand against Pelagianism, his Confessions, and his Trinitarian work are honored. But his late predestinarian rigorism was never received as the Church's teaching — Cassian and the East answered it in his own generation, and even the Western Council of Orange (529) refused predestination to evil. One Father, however great, is weighed within the consensus of the Fathers; Calvinism builds its system on the point where Augustine stands most alone.

Q: Don't Calvinists and Orthodox at least agree against Pelagianism?▼

Yes — and the agreement is real and worth stating: salvation is by grace; man cannot save himself; God's initiative is first in every conversion. Common ground also includes the Trinity, the full deity of Christ, and the authority of Scripture. The disagreement begins where monergism denies the reality of grace-enabled human freedom, restricts the scope of the Cross, and forecloses the warnings of Scripture.

Q: Why does this matter pastorally?▼

Doctrine becomes pastoral reality. Unconditional election breeds either anxiety ("am I elect?") or presumption ("I cannot fall"). Limited atonement forbids telling a dying man that Christ shed His blood for him. Irresistible grace makes evangelism the announcement of a fixed roster. The Orthodox Gospel can be preached to every person without an asterisk: God made you, Christ died for you, the Spirit calls you, the Church awaits you — come, and having come, remain.
