The Prosperity Gospel

"Health and wealth," seed-faith, and the gospel that has no cross in it

The prosperity gospel — the "health-and-wealth" or "Word of Faith" teaching carried by some of the largest ministries and television channels on earth — promises that God wills every believer to be rich and physically well, and that the right kind of faith, positive words, and financial "seed" will unlock that blessing. It is one of the fastest-growing religious movements in the world, especially among the poor of Africa, Latin America, and Asia. The Orthodox Church regards it not as a harmless emphasis but as a serious distortion: it puts a price on grace, blames the sick and the poor for their suffering, and quietly removes the Cross from the centre of the Christian life. This page sets out what the movement teaches, the verses it leans on, and the ancient gospel it has displaced.

The Heart of the Matter

▶

The prosperity gospel and the historic Christian faith both speak of "blessing," "faith," and "abundant life" — but they mean almost opposite things by them. The dispute is not about whether God is good to His children, which both affirm, but about what God has promised and how the Christian is to live. Where the prosperity teacher sees faith as a force that produces health and money, the Church sees faith as trust in a crucified Lord who calls His people to take up their own cross.

Orthodox
  • The gospel’s promise is forgiveness, the Spirit, sonship, and eternal life
  • Christ calls His followers to deny themselves and carry the cross
  • Wealth is a neutral instrument; the love of it is the danger
  • Suffering and poverty can be holy ground, shared with Christ
  • Almsgiving, contentment, and detachment are the way of the rich
Prosperity gospel
  • God wills every believer to be financially rich and physically healthy
  • Faith is a force; positive confession "calls" blessings into being
  • "Seed-faith" giving (often to the ministry) returns multiplied wealth
  • Sickness and poverty signal sin, a curse, or a lack of faith
  • Healing and riches are guaranteed now, in this present life

The Orthodox objection is therefore not Puritan suspicion of money, nor a claim that God never gives material good. It is that the prosperity gospel mistakes the nature of the gospel itself. It promises in this life what Christ promised in the next, makes the believer’s own words and giving the engine of blessing, and turns the suffering of the faithful — which Scripture calls a gift and a sharing in Christ — into evidence of failure. The sections that follow trace each of these claims to its proof-texts and set against them the words of Christ, the apostles, and the Fathers.

What It Teaches

▶

The prosperity gospel is not a single denomination but a stream of teaching that runs through much of modern Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity, and through a vast network of television and online ministries. It grew out of the early-twentieth-century "New Thought" movement and the "Word of Faith" teaching of figures such as Kenneth Hagin, and is associated today with many of the best-known names in televangelism — Kenneth Copeland, Creflo Dollar, Benny Hinn, Joel Osteen, and others — as well as a great many local pastors in the developing world. Its core ideas can be stated simply:

Faith as a force. Faith is treated almost as a spiritual law or energy. By believing hard enough and refusing all doubt, the believer can activate God’s power and obtain a desired outcome. Faith is measured by results: if the healing or money does not come, the believer’s faith was deficient.

Positive confession ("name it and claim it"). Words are thought to carry creative power. To speak sickness, debt, or failure is to "confess" them into one’s life; to speak health and wealth is to call them into being. The believer is told never to make a "negative confession," even an honest admission of illness.

Seed-faith giving. Money given — very often to the preacher’s own ministry — is described as a "seed" that God is obliged to multiply back to the giver, frequently with a promised "hundredfold return." Giving becomes an investment with an expected financial yield.

Health and wealth as the believer’s birthright. Physical healing and material riches are held to be guaranteed in the atonement of Christ for every Christian here and now. Poverty and sickness are accordingly read as a curse, a demonic attack, or the fruit of unbelief, rather than as trials the faithful may be called to endure.

Stated this way, the system has an obvious appeal: it offers hope, agency, and a concrete promise to people in real need. But each pillar, measured against Scripture and the Church, gives way — and the cost of believing it falls hardest on exactly the poor and sick it claims to help.

The Verses It Misuses

▶

The prosperity gospel rests on a small handful of texts, almost always read apart from their context. Set back into the passages they come from, none of them promises material wealth to the believer.

"Beloved, I pray that all may go well with you and that you may be in good health, as it goes well with your soul."3 John 2 (ESV)

This is the ordinary greeting of an ancient letter — the equivalent of "I hope this finds you well." Far from guaranteeing wealth, it makes the writer’s wish for the body track the health of the soul, which is the real concern of the verse.

"Bring the full tithe into the storehouse… and thereby put me to the test, says the LORD of hosts, if I will not open the windows of heaven for you and pour down for you a blessing until there is no more need."Malachi 3:10 (ESV)

God is rebuking Israel for robbing Him of the tithe owed under the covenant; the "blessing" is covenant faithfulness toward a whole nation, chiefly the rain and harvest of an agricultural people. It is not a personal money-back guarantee triggered by a gift to a ministry.

"Whatever you ask in prayer, believe that you have received it, and it will be yours."Mark 11:24 (ESV)

This is the charter text of "name it and claim it." But Christ Himself prayed in Gethsemane, "not what I will, but what You will" (Mark 14:36), and St. John adds the missing qualifier: we receive what we ask "according to His will" (1 John 5:14). Prayer is not a lever that compels God; it is trust that submits to Him.

"And my God will supply every need of yours according to his riches in glory in Christ Jesus."Philippians 4:19 (ESV)

Paul promises that God will supply needs, not greeds — and he writes it from a prison cell, thanking a poor church for sharing in his hardship, having just said he had "learned in whatever situation I am to be content" (4:11). The verse is a promise of sufficiency to the generous, not of luxury to the acquisitive.

"For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich."2 Corinthians 8:9 (ESV)

Read as a promise of money, this verse collapses: Christ’s "becoming poor" was the Incarnation and the Cross, and the "riches" His poverty wins for us are plainly spiritual — the wealth of salvation. Paul is, moreover, urging the Corinthians to give generously to the poor, the very opposite of accumulating for themselves.

The Gospel of the Cross

▶

Against the promise of guaranteed ease, the New Testament sets the Cross at the very centre of the Christian life — not only as the means of our salvation, but as the pattern of the disciple’s own road.

"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me."Luke 9:23 (ESV)

Suffering for Christ is described not as a sign of failed faith but as a gift granted to the believer:

"For it has been granted to you that for the sake of Christ you should not only believe in him but also suffer for his sake."Philippians 1:29 (ESV)

St. Paul — by any prosperity measure a man of great faith — asked three times for his "thorn in the flesh" to be removed, and God refused, teaching him that weakness, not triumph, is where divine power rests:

"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness… For when I am weak, then I am strong."2 Corinthians 12:9–10 (ESV)

The Lord Himself, the only perfectly faithful man who ever lived, owned nothing:

"Foxes have holes, and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head."Matthew 8:20 (ESV)

And in the Sermon on the Plain He pronounced a blessing on the poor and a woe on the rich that the prosperity gospel exactly reverses:

"Blessed are you who are poor, for yours is the kingdom of God… But woe to you who are rich, for you have received your consolation."Luke 6:20, 24 (ESV)

This is the witness of the whole New Testament: the apostles were "persecuted… hungry and thirsty… poorly dressed and buffeted and homeless" (1 Corinthians 4:11), and "all who desire to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12). A gospel that promises the disciple a life with no cross in it is preaching a Christ the apostles never knew.

Wealth, Poverty & Almsgiving

▶

The Church is not against wealth as such. She does not teach that money is evil, that the rich are damned, or that poverty by itself makes anyone holy. What she rejects is the prosperity gospel’s two great errors: that riches are the sign and reward of faith, and that the gospel exists to make us materially comfortable.

Scripture’s warnings about wealth are sober and constant. To the rich young ruler, who had kept the commandments, Christ said, "Go, sell what you have and give to the poor… How difficult it will be for those who have wealth to enter the kingdom of God!" (Mark 10:21–23). The parable of the rich man and Lazarus (Luke 16) shows a man whose comfort in this life gave way to torment in the next, while the beggar at his gate was carried to Abraham’s side. And St. Paul’s teaching to Timothy is the direct antidote to the seed-faith pitch:

"But godliness with contentment is great gain, for we brought nothing into the world, and we cannot take anything out of the world… But those who desire to be rich fall into temptation, into a snare… For the love of money is a root of all kinds of evils."1 Timothy 6:6–10 (ESV)

The positive command is not to chase riches but to use them rightly. To those who already have wealth, Paul says: do not be haughty, do not trust in it, but "be rich in good works, to be generous and ready to share" (1 Timothy 6:17–18). The biblical attitude to money is stewardship and generosity, never accumulation as a proof of God’s favour.

This is why the Church has always honoured voluntary poverty — the monk who owns nothing, the saint who gives all to the poor — as a high calling, and has measured the rich not by what they keep but by what they give away. The gospel does not promise to make the poor rich; it promises to make the rich generous and the poor blessed, and to seat them together at one table in the Kingdom.

Voices of the Fathers

▶

Long before modern televangelism, the Fathers had already weighed wealth in the balance. Their teaching is remarkably consistent: riches are an instrument, not a reward; the love of money is a deadly root; and what the rich withhold from the poor is not generosity refused but property stolen.

Clement of Alexandria (d. c. 215)
"Riches, then, which benefit also our neighbours, are not to be thrown away… Are you able to make a right use of it? It is subservient to righteousness. Does one make a wrong use of it? It is, on the other hand, a minister of wrong. For its nature is to be subservient, not to rule."
Who Is the Rich Man That Shall Be Saved? §14 (ANF Vol. 2) Direct quote
St. John Chrysostom (d. 407)
"Why do you place your hopes upon what is instantly transferable? For such is wealth!… If you seek riches, seek those that are stable and enduring, and which are the fruit of good works."
Homily 18 on First Timothy (NPNF 1st Series, Vol. 13) Direct quote
St. Polycarp of Smyrna (d. 155)
"'But the love of money is the root of all evils.' Knowing, therefore, that 'as we brought nothing into the world, so we can carry nothing out,' let us arm ourselves with the armour of righteousness."
Epistle to the Philippians §4 (ANF Vol. 1) Direct quote
St. Basil the Great (d. 379)
He taught that hoarding is a form of theft: the bread you store belongs to the hungry, the cloak in your chest to the naked, the silver buried in the ground to the poor. You wrong as many as you might have helped, and did not.
Homily "I Will Tear Down My Barns" / To the Rich — summary of his argument Paraphrase

The contrast with the prosperity gospel could not be sharper. Where the modern preacher makes riches the seal of God’s favour and the reward of faith, the Fathers make them a test and a trust — a thing one is judged by, owed to the poor, and unable to carry past the grave. Clement allows that the rich can be saved, but only by holding wealth as a servant of righteousness; Basil warns that to keep it from the needy is robbery; Polycarp, echoing St. Paul, names the love of money the root of all evils.

Objections & Answers

▶

The prosperity teacher has ready replies. Each contains a half-truth — God is generous, He does heal, giving is blessed — but each then overreaches into a promise Scripture never makes.

"Doesn’t God want to bless His children?"▶

He does — abundantly. But Scripture defines the blessings of the gospel as the forgiveness of sins, the gift of the Holy Spirit, adoption as sons, peace, joy, and eternal life (Ephesians 1:3 speaks of "every spiritual blessing in the heavenly places"). These are guaranteed; a house and a car are not. To love a child is not always to make him rich — often the opposite. God’s blessing frequently comes wrapped in a cross, as it did for His own Son.

"3 John 2 promises prosperity and health."▶

It is the conventional opening greeting of an ancient letter, like writing "I hope this letter finds you in good health." The Greek euodousthai ("go well," "prosper") is a wish, not a covenant, and the verse explicitly ties bodily welfare to the health of the soul — "as it goes well with your soul." Build a doctrine of guaranteed wealth on it and you must also conclude the Apostle John could grant or withhold riches by his good wishes.

"Abraham, Job, and Solomon were rich — wealth is a sign of God’s favour."▶

Some saints were rich; far more were poor — the prophets, John the Baptist, the apostles, and Christ Himself, who "had nowhere to lay his head." Job is the worst possible example for the prosperity case: he lost everything as a righteous man, and his comforters who insisted his ruin proved hidden sin were rebuked by God (Job 42:7). Wealth in the Old Testament is neither proof of holiness nor of guilt. And the pattern of the New Covenant, fulfilled in Christ, is not the full barn but the empty tomb reached through the Cross.

"Sow a financial seed and God will give a hundredfold return."▶

The "hundredfold" of the parable of the sower is a harvest of the word of God bearing fruit in righteousness (Mark 4:20), and the "hundredfold" Christ promises in Mark 10:30 comes "with persecutions" and "in the age to come, eternal life" — hardly a tax-free cash windfall. Seed-faith turns God into a vending machine and prayer into a transaction, and in practice it transfers money from the poor and desperate to wealthy preachers. Genuine Christian giving expects nothing back (Luke 6:35); it is mercy, not investment.

"Healing is in the atonement — 'by his stripes we are healed.'"▶

Isaiah 53:5, quoted in 1 Peter 2:24, is about healing from sin — Peter adds "that we might die to sin and live to righteousness." Christ’s atonement does defeat sickness and death ultimately, but the full healing of the body is the resurrection, not a guarantee for this life. Paul left Trophimus "ill at Miletus" (2 Timothy 4:20), told Timothy to take a little wine for his frequent stomach ailments (1 Timothy 5:23), and bore his own unhealed thorn. The Church earnestly prays for the sick and anoints them in the sacrament of Unction (James 5:14), trusting God for healing — but she never tells the sick that their illness proves a lack of faith.

"Positive confession works — speaking sickness or poverty brings it on you."▶

This makes the believer’s own words, rather than God’s will, the sovereign power in the universe — which is closer to sympathetic magic than to Christian prayer. Scripture commends honesty before God: the Psalms are full of lament, and Christ in Gethsemane spoke openly of His sorrow and prayed "if it be possible, let this cup pass." The faith the Bible asks for is trust in God, not confidence in the creative power of one’s own positive speech.

"So you think poverty and sickness are holy and good?"▶

No. The Church feeds the hungry, heals the sick, and has built hospitals and almshouses for sixteen centuries; she does not glorify suffering for its own sake. The point is narrower and firmer: suffering and poverty are not evidence of God’s displeasure or of failed faith, and they can become holy ground when borne with Christ. The Christian answer to a poor man’s need is to relieve it through almsgiving — not to tell him that his poverty is his own fault for not believing hard enough.

A Different Gospel

▶

It would be easy to treat the prosperity gospel as merely an over-enthusiastic emphasis — too much focus on blessing, perhaps, but harmless. The Church cannot agree, for two reasons.

The first is doctrinal. The prosperity gospel changes the content of the good news. Where the apostles preached "Jesus Christ and him crucified" (1 Corinthians 2:2) and a salvation consummated in the world to come, this teaching offers health and wealth in the present as the substance of what Christ died to give. That is not a difference of emphasis but of object. St. Paul reserved his sharpest words for exactly this — the substitution of another gospel for the true one:

"But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be accursed."Galatians 1:8 (ESV)

The second reason is pastoral, and it is grave. The prosperity gospel does its deepest damage to the very people it courts. The sick are told that if they are not healed, their faith failed — heaping guilt on top of pain. The poor are told to give money they cannot spare, often to preachers who live in luxury, on the promise of a return that does not come; when it fails, they are left poorer and told the fault is theirs. And the believer who has been promised an untroubled life is left with no resources when grief, illness, or loss finally arrives, as it arrives for everyone. A gospel with no place for the Cross has nothing to say beside a deathbed.

The Orthodox answer is the older and the harder one, but it is also the only one that holds. God loves you; He has not promised you wealth or a pain-free life; He has promised to be with you in the suffering, to make His power perfect in your weakness, and to raise you on the last day. That hope does not fail when the money runs out or the body breaks — because it was never resting on them in the first place.

FAQ

▶
Is it sinful to be wealthy or to want financial stability?▶
No. As Clement of Alexandria taught, wealth is an instrument that is "subservient to righteousness" when used well. The sin is not having money but loving it, trusting in it, neglecting the poor, and treating riches as proof of God’s favour. Working honestly, providing for your family, and seeking reasonable security are good; chasing riches as the goal of the Christian life is the snare Paul warns against (1 Timothy 6:9).
Does the Orthodox Church believe God still heals today?▶
Yes, wholeheartedly. The Church has the sacrament of Holy Unction for the sick (James 5:14–15), prays constantly for the ill, and the lives of the saints are full of miraculous healings. What she rejects is the guarantee — the claim that enough faith always produces healing now — and the cruelty of blaming the sick for their illness. Healing is asked for in trust and received as a gift, never demanded as a right.
Should Christians tithe and give generously, then?▶
Absolutely — generously, sacrificially, and joyfully. The difference is the motive. Christian giving is worship of God and mercy to the poor, expecting nothing in return (Luke 6:35); seed-faith giving is an investment expecting a financial yield. The first frees the heart from money; the second binds it more tightly, and too often enriches the preacher at the expense of the giver.
What is the real harm, if it gives poor people hope?▶
The hope it gives is false, and false hope wounds. It extracts money from those who can least afford it, blames the sick and the poor for their own suffering, and replaces the durable hope of the resurrection with a promise this life cannot keep. When the healing or the wealth fails to come — as it usually does — people are left poorer, sicker, guilt-ridden, and often shipwrecked in their faith. A gospel that cannot stand at a graveside is no gospel at all.
Who actually teaches the prosperity gospel?▶
It is a broad stream rather than one church, flowing largely through Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity and the "Word of Faith" movement that traces back to teachers like E. W. Kenyon and Kenneth Hagin. It is carried worldwide by television and online ministries and by countless local pastors, especially across Africa, Latin America, and Asia, where it has grown explosively among the poor. Not everyone who emphasises God’s generosity holds the full system; the concern here is with the specific claims set out above.

Glossary

▶
Prosperity gospel
The teaching that God wills every believer to be financially rich and physically healthy, and that faith, positive speech, and giving will secure it in this life. Also called the "health-and-wealth" gospel.
Word of Faith
The movement, rooted in early-twentieth-century teaching, that treats faith as a creative spiritual force activated by belief and spoken words.
Positive confession
"Name it and claim it": the practice of speaking desired outcomes (health, wealth) as already true, in the belief that words have creative power.
Seed-faith giving
The teaching that money given (often to a ministry) is a "seed" God is obliged to multiply back, frequently with a promised "hundredfold return."
Healing in the atonement
The claim that Christ’s atonement guarantees physical healing for every believer now, drawn from "by his stripes we are healed" (Isaiah 53:5).
Ascesis
From the Greek for an athlete’s training — the Christian discipline of self-denial, fasting, and prayer that the prosperity gospel largely omits.
Theology of the Cross
The biblical understanding that the disciple shares in Christ’s suffering and self-denial; the Cross is the pattern of the Christian life, not only the means of salvation.
Holy Unction
The sacrament of anointing the sick with blessed oil and prayer (James 5:14–15), the Church’s ordered way of seeking God’s healing.
Almsgiving
Giving to the poor as an act of worship and mercy, expecting no return — central to the Orthodox use of wealth.
Mammon
Wealth personified as a rival master. "You cannot serve God and mammon" (Matthew 6:24).
Contentment (autarkeia)
The Greek virtue of sufficiency Paul commends: "godliness with contentment is great gain" (1 Timothy 6:6); having learned to be content in any circumstance (Philippians 4:11).
Stewardship
The teaching that we are not owners but stewards of wealth, accountable to God for how we use it — especially toward the poor.
