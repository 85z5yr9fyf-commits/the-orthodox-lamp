Monasticism & the Desert Fathers

How a young Egyptian who heard one Gospel verse gave the whole Church a school of prayer

Around the year 270, a young Egyptian named Antony walked into a village church, heard the words “Go, sell what you have and give to the poor… and come, follow me,” and walked out to give his life wholly to God in the desert. From that seed grew the monastic movement — first the solitary hermits, then St. Pachomius’s communities, then the great desert cities of Nitria and Scetis — until the Egyptian desert was, in the famous phrase, “made a city” by monks. From Egypt this way of life spread to Palestine, Syria, Asia Minor, and finally the Latin West, shaping the whole of later Christianity. For the Coptic Orthodox, this is not borrowed history: monasticism is Egypt’s own gift to the Church, and it remains the beating heart of Coptic spiritual life today.

The Heart of the Matter

▶

Why would anyone abandon home, marriage, and property to live in a cave or a cell in the desert? To the modern and especially the Protestant ear this can sound like fanaticism, escapism, or a works-righteousness that despises the ordinary Christian life. The Orthodox understanding is quite different: monasticism is not a rejection of the world God made, but a wholehearted answer to Christ’s own call to leave all and follow Him — a life of repentance, unceasing prayer, and love, lived for the sake of the whole Church.

Orthodox
  • A direct response to Christ’s call: “sell all… and follow me”
  • Not a higher class of Christian, but the same Gospel pursued without distraction
  • A life of repentance, prayer, and love — not merit-earning
  • The monks pray for the whole world; they do not flee it in selfishness
  • The training-ground of the saints, theologians, and bishops of the Church
Protestant objection
  • An “unbiblical” invention with no New Testament command
  • Withdrawal abandons the call to be salt and light in the world
  • Vows of celibacy and poverty add to the Gospel of grace
  • Looks like earning salvation by extreme works
  • Marriage and ordinary vocation are the normal Christian path

The Orthodox reply is that monasticism is woven from threads already in the Gospel: Christ’s counsel to the rich young man, His own forty days in the wilderness, His words about those who become “eunuchs for the kingdom,” St. Paul’s praise of the unmarried life, and the prophets who dwelt in the desert before Him. The monk does not claim to be a better Christian; he simply takes one road, freely, that the Lord laid open. This page traces where that road came from, what it is for, and why it lies at the very centre of the Coptic Church.

The Scriptural Roots

▶

Monasticism did not appear from nowhere in the fourth century. Its roots run straight through the Scriptures — in the Lord’s own words and example, and in the apostolic teaching that followed.

The decisive text is Christ’s counsel to the rich young man — the very verse that sent St. Antony to the desert:

"Jesus said to him, 'If you would be perfect, go, sell what you possess and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me.'"Matthew 19:21 (ESV)

The Lord also spoke of those who freely renounce marriage for God’s sake — and called it a gift not given to all, but to be received by those who can:

"…and there are eunuchs who have made themselves eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. Let the one who is able to receive this receive it."Matthew 19:12 (ESV)

St. Paul taught the same: marriage is good and holy, but the unmarried life, freely chosen, frees a person for “undivided” devotion to the Lord:

"The unmarried man is anxious about the things of the Lord, how to please the Lord… I say this… to promote good order and to secure your undivided devotion to the Lord."1 Corinthians 7:32–35 (ESV)

And the pattern of the holy life in the wilderness is older still. Elijah and Elisha and the “sons of the prophets” lived austere lives apart; John the Baptist grew up and preached in the desert, clothed in camel’s hair; and the Lord Himself, before His ministry, went into the wilderness to fast and pray for forty days (Mark 1:12–13). The monks understood themselves as walking this same well-trodden biblical road — not inventing a new spirituality, but taking up an old one with their whole heart.

St. Antony and the Birth of the Desert

▶

St. Antony the Great (c. 251–356) is rightly called the father of monasticism. Born to a prosperous Christian family in middle Egypt, he was orphaned as a young man. Soon after, he entered a church just as the Gospel of the rich young ruler was being read, and took the words as spoken directly to him. He gave away his lands, provided for his sister, and withdrew to live a life of prayer, fasting, and manual labour — first at the edge of his village, then in the tombs, and at last deep in the desert, where he lived for decades in a deserted fort.

His biography — the Life of Antony, written shortly after his death by St. Athanasius, the great defender of Nicaea and Patriarch of Alexandria — became one of the most influential books in Christian history. In it Athanasius describes how Antony’s reputation for holiness drew so many disciples into the wilderness that, in his words, the desert was “made a city” by monks. Antony himself emerged from solitude only to strengthen the Church — to encourage the martyrs during persecution, and to stand with Athanasius against the Arian heresy.

Antony left no written rule and founded no organised order. What he left was something deeper: a living example, and a treasury of sayings, of what it means to wage the inner war against the passions and to seek God with the whole heart. He is the prototype of the anchorite — the solitary hermit — and the spiritual father of every monk who came after.

St. Pachomius and the Common Life

▶

If Antony is the father of the solitary life, St. Pachomius (c. 292–348) is the father of the communal life — what is called cenobitic monasticism, from the Greek koinos bios, “common life.” A former Roman soldier converted by the kindness of Christians who fed him in prison, Pachomius settled at Tabennesi in Upper Egypt around the year 320 and gathered monks not into scattered cells but into an ordered community living under one roof, one discipline, and one rule.

His genius was organisation. Pachomius wrote the first monastic Rule — a daily order of prayer, work, meals, and obedience that allowed large numbers of monks to live together in peace and charity. Each monastery had an abbot; the monks worked with their hands, shared everything in common in imitation of the first Christians of Jerusalem (Acts 2:44–45), and gathered for common prayer and the Eucharist. By the time of his death, a federation of monasteries — the Koinonia — housed thousands of monks and several convents of nuns under the same pattern.

This Pachomian model — the structured, obedient, common life — is the direct ancestor of organised monasticism everywhere, East and West. When St. Benedict later wrote his famous Rule for the monks of the Latin world, he was building on a foundation laid in the sands of Egypt two centuries earlier.

The Desert Cities of Egypt

▶

Between the solitary hermit and the great cenobitic monastery lay a third way — the semi-eremitic life of loosely gathered hermits, living alone in their cells through the week and coming together on the weekend for the Eucharist and a common meal. This was the way of the famous monastic settlements of the northern Egyptian desert.

c. 330 — Nitria & Kellia
St. Amun founded the settlement of Nitria, southeast of Alexandria; as it grew crowded, the monks established “the Cells” (Kellia) deeper in the desert for those seeking greater solitude.
c. 330–390 — Scetis (Wadi al-Natrun)
St. Macarius the Great pioneered the desert of Scetis, the most renowned of all the monastic deserts. So central was he that the whole region came to be called the Desert of Macarius.
4th–5th centuries — A multitude
Tens of thousands of monks filled these deserts. Pilgrims came from across the empire; visitors’ accounts (the Lausiac History, the History of the Monks in Egypt) carried the fame of the Egyptian fathers everywhere.
Today — Wadi al-Natrun
Four ancient monasteries still live and pray in Scetis — among them the Monastery of St. Macarius and the Syrian Monastery (Deir al-Surian) — in unbroken continuity with the fourth century.

It was in these desert cities that the great abbas and ammas (fathers and mothers) lived — Macarius the Great, Macarius of Alexandria, Moses the Black, Poemen, Arsenius, Sisoes, Syncletica, and many more. Their terse, hard-won counsel, passed from disciple to disciple, was eventually gathered into the Sayings of the Desert Fathers — one of the most beloved spiritual books of all time.

The Three Renunciations

▶

Monastic life is shaped by three great renunciations, freely embraced — not as a way to earn heaven, but as a way to clear the heart of everything that competes with the love of God. Each answers to a counsel of Christ Himself.

Poverty
  • Owning nothing of one’s own
  • Frees the heart from the love of money (1 Tim 6:10)
  • “Sell what you have” (Matt 19:21)
Chastity
  • Celibacy for the kingdom’s sake
  • “Undivided devotion to the Lord” (1 Cor 7:35)
  • “Eunuchs for the kingdom” (Matt 19:12)
Obedience
  • Submitting one’s will to a spiritual father
  • Imitating Christ’s own obedience (Phil 2:8)
  • The cure for self-will and pride

The deepest of these, in the desert tradition, is obedience — because the root sin is pride, the assertion of one’s own will against God’s. The monk hands over his self-will to a spiritual father in order to learn humility, the virtue Antony named as the one thing that escapes all the devil’s snares. Poverty and chastity strip away outward attachments; obedience strikes at the inward root. None of these is presented as a burden laid on every believer. They are, in the Lord’s own word, counsels — freely received by “the one who is able” — not commands binding the whole Church.

Wisdom of the Desert

▶

The desert fathers were not theoreticians; they were physicians of the soul, and their wisdom comes in short, piercing sayings — the Apophthegmata Patrum. A few of the most famous:

On humility
"I saw the snares that the enemy spreads out over the world and I said groaning, 'What can get through from such snares?' Then I heard a voice saying to me, 'Humility.'"
St. Antony the Great (Sayings of the Desert Fathers, Antony 7) Direct quote
On the world to come
"A time is coming when men will go mad, and when they see someone who is not mad, they will attack him, saying, 'You are mad, you are not like us.'"
St. Antony the Great (Sayings of the Desert Fathers, Antony 25) Direct quote
On staying in the cell
Abba Moses counselled a brother that the answer to every distraction was simply to remain: “Go, sit in your cell, and your cell will teach you everything.”
Abba Moses the Black (Sayings of the Desert Fathers) Paraphrase
On not judging
When a brother sinned and the others gathered to judge him, Abba Moses came carrying a leaking jug of sand, saying his own sins ran out behind him and he could not see another’s — and the brothers forgave the man.
Abba Moses the Black (Sayings of the Desert Fathers) Paraphrase

The recurring themes are unmistakable: humility above all, ceaseless prayer, guarding the tongue, refusing to judge others, perseverance in one’s cell, and unrelenting honesty about one’s own sin. This is the practical wisdom that formed the saints — and that countless ordinary Christians have drawn on ever since.

How the Desert Filled the World

▶

What began in the Egyptian desert did not stay there. Within a single century the monastic movement had spread across the whole Christian world — and Egypt was acknowledged everywhere as its source.

St. Athanasius’s Life of Antony was translated into Latin and circulated widely in the West. Its effect was immense: St. Augustine, in his Confessions, records how hearing the story of Antony and of others who had abandoned the world struck him to the heart and helped bring about his own conversion. The book lit a fire wherever it was read.

From Egypt the movement passed to Palestine (St. Hilarion, a disciple of Antony), to Syria and Asia Minor (where St. Basil the Great drew up his own monastic rules, still followed in the Byzantine world), to the deserts of Sinai, and at last to the Latin West, where St. John Cassian carried the wisdom of the Egyptian fathers to Gaul and St. Benedict of Nursia distilled it into the Rule that would govern Western monasticism for over a thousand years. Every monastery in Europe, every Benedictine and Cistercian and Carthusian house, traces its lineage ultimately to the sands of Egypt.

This is a point of quiet pride for the Coptic Church, and a historical fact freely granted by historians of every confession: the monastic life that shaped the universities, preserved the learning of antiquity, and produced so many of the saints of East and West was, in its origin, a gift of the Egyptian desert to the whole Church.

Coptic Monasticism Today

▶

In the Coptic Orthodox Church, monasticism is not a museum piece but a living force — arguably more vital today than at any time in centuries. After a period of decline, the twentieth century saw a remarkable monastic revival, led above all by the monk who became Pope Shenouda III and by the great spiritual father Fr. Matthew the Poor (Matta al-Maskeen) at the Monastery of St. Macarius in Scetis.

The ancient monasteries of Wadi al-Natrun, of St. Antony and St. Paul by the Red Sea, and of many other sites are full of monks once more — many of them university-educated professionals, doctors and engineers, who left successful careers for the desert, just as the first fathers left their fields and trades. New monasteries have been founded across Egypt and around the Coptic diaspora, from Europe to the Americas to Australia.

This matters for the whole Church, because in the Coptic tradition — as in all the historic Churches — the bishops are chosen from among the monks. The Coptic Pope himself is always a monk. The monasteries are thus the spiritual lungs of the Church: they form her shepherds, preserve her liturgical and ascetical tradition, and offer a place of pilgrimage, confession, and renewal for the laity. To understand the Coptic Church at all, one must understand that her centre of gravity has always been, and remains, the desert.

The Protestant Objections

▶

The Reformation was largely hostile to monasticism — Luther himself was a former monk — and several standard objections recur. Here is how the Orthodox answer them.

“Monasticism is unbiblical — there’s no command to be a monk.”▶

True — and the Orthodox agree it is not commanded. It is a counsel, freely chosen. But its elements are thoroughly biblical: Christ’s call to sell all and follow Him (Matthew 19:21), His words on celibacy for the kingdom (Matthew 19:12), Paul’s commendation of the unmarried life (1 Corinthians 7), the common life of the first Christians (Acts 2 and 4), and the prophets and Christ Himself praying in the wilderness. The monk does not claim a command was given; he answers a counsel that was.

“Withdrawing to the desert abandons the call to be salt and light.”▶

The monks did not see themselves as abandoning the world but as praying for it. Far from being useless, the desert fathers were sought out by bishops, emperors, and crowds of ordinary people for counsel and prayer; Antony came down to strengthen the martyrs and to fight Arianism. The monastery has always been a lighthouse, not a hiding-place — producing the Church’s saints, bishops, theologians, and a constant stream of intercession for the salvation of the whole world.

“Vows of poverty and celibacy are works-righteousness — earning grace.”▶

The desert fathers were, if anything, the most insistent teachers of humility and of utter dependence on God’s mercy in all of Christian history. Their whole spirituality is one of repentance and grace; Antony names humility — not achievement — as the thing that defeats the devil. The renunciations are not a price paid for salvation but a clearing-away of obstacles to love, undertaken in response to grace already received, never to purchase it.

“Doesn’t this make monks into ‘higher’ or ‘better’ Christians?”▶

No. The Orthodox honour marriage as a holy mystery (sacrament) and the ordinary path of salvation for most Christians; many of the greatest saints were married laypeople. The monk has not chosen a holier Gospel, only a particular road within the one Gospel — a more concentrated, undistracted pursuit of the same union with God to which every Christian is called. The desert fathers themselves often warned that a humble married believer could stand far above a proud monk.

“Wasn’t monasticism a late, medieval development?”▶

No. Organised monasticism is one of the oldest continuous institutions in Christianity, dating to the early fourth century — within living memory of the last great persecutions, and contemporary with the Council of Nicaea (325). St. Antony, St. Pachomius, and St. Macarius all belong to the same century as Athanasius and the Nicene fathers. It is far older than most of the doctrines and practices the Reformers themselves retained.

FAQ

▶
What is the difference between a hermit and a cenobitic monk?▶
A hermit (anchorite, after the pattern of St. Antony) lives alone in solitude. A cenobitic monk (after St. Pachomius) lives in community, sharing prayer, work, and meals under a common rule and an abbot. A third, “semi-eremitic” way — as in old Scetis — has monks living alone through the week but gathering for the Eucharist. All three have existed in Egypt from the beginning.
Did monasticism really begin in Egypt?▶
Yes — this is the consensus of historians across all confessions. While ascetics existed earlier and elsewhere, organised Christian monasticism — both the solitary form under St. Antony and the communal form under St. Pachomius — arose in Egypt in the late third and early fourth centuries and spread from there to the rest of the Christian world.
Are monks “running away” from real life?▶
The fathers would say the opposite: the monk runs toward the hardest reality of all — the naked encounter with God and with one’s own heart, stripped of the distractions and comforts the rest of us use to avoid it. The desert is not an escape from struggle but a deliberate entry into the deepest struggle, the war against the passions, on behalf of oneself and the whole world.
Why do Coptic bishops have to be monks?▶
In the Coptic Church — as in the other historic Churches — the parish clergy may be married, but the bishops are chosen from the celibate monastic clergy. The reason is partly practical (an undistracted shepherd) and partly spiritual: the monastery is regarded as the school in which the deepest pastors and teachers are formed. The Coptic Pope is always a monk.
Can ordinary lay Christians learn from the Desert Fathers?▶
Absolutely — and they always have. The Sayings of the Desert Fathers is read far beyond the monastery, by Christians of every walk of life and even many outside the Church, precisely because its counsel on humility, prayer, anger, forgiveness, and perseverance speaks to everyone. The monk lives these things in an intense and undistracted way, but the virtues themselves belong to the whole Christian life.

Glossary

▶
Monasticism
The way of life of those who renounce the world for God through poverty, chastity, and obedience, devoting themselves to prayer and ascetic struggle.
Anchorite / Hermit
A monk who lives in solitude, after the pattern of St. Antony the Great. From the Greek anachoreo, “to withdraw.”
Cenobitic
The communal monastic life, from the Greek koinos bios (“common life”), founded by St. Pachomius: monks living together under one rule and abbot.
Abba / Amma
“Father” / “Mother” — titles of the elder monks and nuns whose spiritual counsel guided their disciples in the desert.
Apophthegmata Patrum
The “Sayings of the Desert Fathers” — the collected short sayings and stories of the Egyptian monks, a classic of Christian spirituality.
Asceticism
From the Greek askesis, “training.” The spiritual discipline of fasting, vigil, prayer, and self-denial by which the passions are healed and the soul drawn to God.
Scetis
The great monastic desert of Lower Egypt (Wadi al-Natrun), pioneered by St. Macarius the Great; still home to ancient living monasteries.
Koinonia
The “fellowship” or federation of monasteries founded by St. Pachomius, sharing a common rule and life.
Hesychia
“Stillness” or inner silence — the quiet, watchful state of heart the monk seeks, in which unceasing prayer can take root.
The passions
The disordered desires and habits (pride, anger, lust, greed, despondency, and the rest) that the ascetic life seeks to heal, so the soul can love freely.
