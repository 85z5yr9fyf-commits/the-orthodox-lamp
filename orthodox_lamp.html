Oriental Orthodox · Ancient Apostolic Faith

The Oriental Orthodox Churches — including the Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac, and Malankara traditions — represent among the oldest continuously existing Christian communities on earth, tracing their episcopal lineages directly to the Apostles of Christ. This website defends the ancient apostolic faith as it has been preserved, lived, and transmitted in the Oriental Orthodox tradition.

Every bishop in the Oriental Orthodox communion stands in an unbroken chain of ordination stretching back to the Apostles themselves. This is not a matter of institutional pride — it is a theological necessity rooted in the very nature of the Church as the Body of Christ.

1

Apostolic Succession

▼

Historical Overview

Apostolic succession is the unbroken chain of episcopal ordination from the Apostles to the present day. Christ appointed the Twelve, breathed on them and said "Receive the Holy Spirit" (John 20:22), commissioned them to make disciples of all nations (Matthew 28:19), and promised them authority to bind and loose (Matthew 18:18). The Apostles in turn appointed bishops — episkopoi — to carry on their office. This is not a post-Constantinian invention; it is documented in the earliest non-canonical Christian literature, predating any ecumenical council.

The Oriental Orthodox Churches maintain apostolic succession in its most ancient form. The Coptic Church traces its founding to St. Mark the Evangelist (c. AD 42). The Armenian Apostolic Church to Sts. Thaddaeus and Bartholomew. The Syriac Orthodox Church through Antioch, founded by St. Peter. The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church through Philip's baptism of the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts 8) and the mission of St. Frumentius. Each line is documented historically and liturgically.

Scripture: Apostolic Succession

Matthew 28:18–20
"All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them… teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you."
Christ delegates His own authority to the Apostles with a universal and perpetual commission. The phrase "I am with you always, to the end of the age" implies a ministry continuing beyond the lifetimes of the Twelve — demanding successors who carry the same commission.
John 20:21–23
"As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you… Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven."
The sending here mirrors the eternal procession of the Son from the Father — a constitutive, ontological delegation of authority. Forgiving sins requires authorized agents; this office must be transmitted, not independently re-created.
Acts 1:15–26
The Apostles replace Judas with Matthias so that the office of apostleship remains whole: "Let another take his office."
The apostolic college itself establishes the principle of succession — a vacancy in the Twelve is intolerable and must be filled. The Greek word episkopē (office/bishopric) appears here (v.20), directly linking the apostolic office to what later becomes the episcopate.
Acts 6:1–6
The Apostles appoint seven deacons by prayer and the laying on of hands.
The Apostles transmit ministerial authority through a physical rite — laying on of hands. This is the first documented act of ordination in the Church, establishing the pattern the entire tradition follows.
Acts 14:23
"And when they had appointed elders [presbyteroi] for them in every church, with prayer and fasting they committed them to the Lord."
Paul and Barnabas systematically appoint presbyter-bishops in every congregation they plant. This is not spontaneous congregational election; it is apostolic appointment, and the pattern repeats across every Pauline mission.
1 Timothy 4:14
"Do not neglect the gift you have, which was given you by prophecy when the council of elders laid their hands on you."
Timothy's ministry is explicitly traced to the laying on of hands by the presbyterate. The "gift" (charisma) is sacramental — given through an outward rite, not through private spiritual experience. This is a classical description of ordination.
2 Timothy 1:6
"Fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands."
Paul asserts he personally transmitted a sacramental gift to Timothy through ordination. The gift resides "in" Timothy — it is not merely an appointment of function but an ontological bestowal. This directly supports the Oriental Orthodox theology of holy orders.
2 Timothy 2:2
"What you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
Paul explicitly commands a chain of transmission: Paul → Timothy → faithful men → others. Four generations of succession are outlined in a single verse. This is the scriptural DNA of apostolic succession.
Titus 1:5
"This is why I left you in Crete, so that you might put what remained into order, and appoint elders in every town as I directed you."
Titus acts as a delegate of Paul's apostolic authority, appointing bishops on his behalf. This is an early form of the metropolitan-bishop relationship — apostolic authority delegated through persons, not institutions.
Hebrews 5:4
"And no one takes this honor for himself, but only when called by God, just as Aaron was."
The Levitical priesthood required divine appointment through an existing priestly line — not self-appointment. The author of Hebrews applies this principle to Christian ministry, implying that legitimate ministry requires authorization through an established line.
Ephesians 4:11–12
"And he gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the shepherds and teachers, to equip the saints for the work of ministry."
Christ gives specific offices to the Church as structural gifts — not temporary measures but enduring provisions for building up the Body. The permanent gifting of apostles and pastors-teachers implies ongoing succession in these roles.
Romans 10:15
"And how are they to preach unless they are sent?"
Paul insists that legitimate preaching requires being "sent" (apostellō). Authority is not self-generated; it flows downward from Christ through the Apostles to their successors. This is the root logic of apostolic succession.

Church Fathers on Apostolic Succession

St. Clement of Rome
First Epistle to the Corinthians, c. AD 96
"The Apostles received the Gospel for us from the Lord Jesus Christ; Jesus Christ was sent from God. Christ, therefore, is from God, and the Apostles from Christ… Our Apostles knew through our Lord Jesus Christ that there would be strife over the bishop's office. For this reason, therefore, having received complete foreknowledge, they appointed those who have been mentioned, and afterwards added the codicil that if they should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed to their ministry."
Writing within living memory of the Apostles, Clement documents that apostolic succession was a deliberate, foreseen institution — not a later development. This text predates every New Testament canon list and is among the oldest post-apostolic Christian documents.
St. Ignatius of Antioch
Epistle to the Smyrnaeans, c. AD 107–110
"Wherever the bishop appears, there let the congregation be; just as wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church. It is not lawful to baptize or to hold an agape feast without the bishop; but whatever he approves, this is also pleasing to God."
Ignatius, himself a disciple of the Apostles and third bishop of Antioch, insists on the bishop's indispensable sacramental role. For him, the bishop is not an administrative officer but the visible center of ecclesial unity — a theology fundamental to all Oriental Orthodox churches.
St. Ignatius of Antioch
Epistle to the Magnesians, c. AD 107–110
"Do nothing without the bishop and the presbyters."
This brief command recurs across nearly every letter of Ignatius, revealing that episcopal authority was treated as absolute and non-negotiable even before the end of the first century. The Oriental Orthodox insistence on episcopal governance has Ignatian roots.
St. Irenaeus of Lyons
Against Heresies, Book III, ch. 3 (c. AD 180)
"It is within the power of all, therefore, in every Church, who may wish to see the truth, to contemplate clearly the tradition of the Apostles manifested throughout the whole world; and we are in a position to reckon up those who were by the Apostles instituted bishops in the Churches, and to demonstrate the succession of these men to our own times."
Irenaeus, a second-century bishop who traced his lineage through Polycarp to John the Apostle, treats apostolic succession as the definitive test of orthodoxy against Gnostic innovation. His argument is historical and verifiable — an appeal to public episcopal lineages, not private tradition.
Tertullian of Carthage
Prescription Against Heretics, ch. 32 (c. AD 200)
"Let them produce the original records of their churches; let them unfold the roll of their bishops, running down in due succession from the beginning in such a manner that that first bishop of theirs shall be able to show for his ordainer and predecessor some one of the Apostles or of apostolic men."
Even Tertullian — later a Montanist — acknowledged apostolic succession as the standard test of legitimate Christianity. This demonstrates that succession was not a Nicene-era innovation but a universal second-century presupposition.
St. Cyprian of Carthage
On the Unity of the Church, ch. 4 (c. AD 251)
"The episcopate is one, each part of which is held by each one for the whole… The Church is one which is spread abroad far and wide into a multitude by an increase of fruitfulness. As there are many rays of the sun, but one light; and many branches of a tree, but one strength based in its tenacious root… so also the Church, shone over with the light of the Lord, sheds forth her rays over the whole world."
Cyprian articulates the theology of the episcopate as a single, unified office shared among many — exactly the Oriental Orthodox understanding. The bishop does not merely represent the Church; he is the Church's visible unity. Unity broken from the episcopate is unity broken from Christ.
St. Athanasius of Alexandria
Defense of His Flight, ch. 24 (c. AD 357)
PARAPHRASE Athanasius consistently defends the Alexandrian episcopal succession against both Arian usurpers and imperial interference, insisting that episcopal ordination by legitimate orthodox bishops is the only valid source of Church authority. His five exiles were precisely because he refused to accept bishops not ordained in the apostolic line.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem
Catechetical Lectures XVIII (c. AD 350)
"In the Catholic Church herself, too, great care must be taken to hold that which has been believed everywhere, always, by all."
Cyril of Jerusalem's catechesis assumes the episcopal structure as non-negotiable. His detailed instructions on the sacraments are addressed to those who have been received through the bishop's ministry — succession and sacrament are inseparable in his theology.

Theological Analysis

Apostolic succession is not merely a procedural continuity — it is a sacramental and ontological reality. The Oriental Orthodox understanding holds that the grace of the episcopate is not self-generated by congregations or spiritual experience, but transmitted through a physical rite (laying on of hands) by those who have themselves received it. This is a participation in the very mission of Christ, who said "As the Father sent Me, so I send you" (John 20:21).

The chain must be unbroken because every link derives its validity from the prior link, and ultimately from Christ Himself. An ordination performed outside this chain — however sincere — is analogous to water that has left the river: it may still be water, but it no longer flows from the source.

"The Church is not a voluntary association of believers who elect their own leaders. She is the Body of Christ, governed through the Apostles and their successors, by the will and institution of the Lord Himself." — Oriental Orthodox theological consensus
2

Bishops, Priests, and Deacons

▼

The Three-Fold Order

The New Testament and earliest Church documents consistently present a three-fold structure of ministry: bishops (episkopoi), presbyters/priests (presbyteroi), and deacons (diakonoi). While some New Testament texts use "bishop" and "elder" interchangeably in a transitional period, by the close of the first century — as seen in Ignatius — the three-fold order is fully differentiated and treated as divinely established. The Oriental Orthodox tradition has preserved this order unaltered.

Scripture: The Three Orders

Philippians 1:1
"Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, to all the saints in Christ Jesus at Philippi, with the overseers [episkopois] and deacons."
Writing c. AD 62, Paul addresses distinct offices: overseers/bishops and deacons. The epistle's salutation itself acknowledges a structured hierarchy within the congregation — not a flat, undifferentiated community.
1 Timothy 3:1–7
"The saying is trustworthy: If anyone aspires to the office of overseer [episkopē], he desires a noble task."
Paul lists specific qualifications for the bishop — implying a distinct, recognized office with defined requirements. This is not an improvised role but an ordered ministry with apostolic oversight and accountability.
1 Timothy 3:8–13
"Deacons likewise must be dignified, not double-tongued, not addicted to much wine, not greedy for dishonest gain."
Paul devotes equal attention to the deaconate, establishing it as a genuine ministerial office with its own spiritual and moral qualifications — not merely an administrative function. The Oriental Orthodox deaconate continues this apostolic institution.
Acts 20:28
"Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers [episkopous], to care for the church of God."
The Holy Spirit Himself appoints bishops — succession is not merely a human institution but a work of the Spirit acting through the Church. Paul's address to the Ephesian elders is the earliest sermon to ordained clergy on record.
Titus 1:7–9
"For an overseer, as God's steward, must be above reproach… He must hold firm to the trustworthy word as taught."
The bishop is described as "God's steward" — a household manager entrusted with another's property. This metaphor implies derived authority: the bishop governs not in his own name but as representative of the Lord of the household.
James 5:14
"Is anyone among you sick? Let him call for the elders [presbyterous] of the church, and let them pray over him, anointing him with oil."
The presbyterate administers anointing — a sacramental function requiring ordained authority. This passage, from one of the earliest epistles, demonstrates that priestly ministry over the sacraments was an apostolic institution from the beginning.
1 Peter 5:1–4
"So I exhort the elders [presbyterous] among you… shepherd the flock of God that is among you, exercising oversight."
Peter exhorts elders/presbyters as those exercising episkopountes (oversight) — confirming the overlapping terminology while affirming the structured ministry of shepherd-elders over the congregation.
Revelation 4:4
"Around the throne were twenty-four thrones, and seated on the thrones were twenty-four elders, clothed in white garments, with golden crowns on their heads."
The heavenly liturgy depicted in Revelation mirrors the earthly Church's order — elders surrounding the throne of God, clothed in liturgical white, wearing crowns of glory. The earthly priesthood participates in and anticipates this heavenly reality.

Church Fathers on the Three Orders

St. Ignatius of Antioch
Epistle to the Trallians, ch. 3 (c. AD 107)
"In like manner, let all reverence the deacons as an appointment of Jesus Christ, and the bishop as Jesus Christ, who is the Son of the Father, and the presbyters as the sanhedrin of God and assembly of the Apostles. Apart from these, there is no elect Church."
The most explicit early statement of the three-fold order as constitutive of the Church. Ignatius equates the bishop with Christ's own presence, the presbyters with the apostolic college, and the deacons with a divine appointment. The phrasing "apart from these, there is no elect Church" is categorical: the three orders are not optional features but the very structure of ecclesial existence.
St. Clement of Rome
First Epistle to the Corinthians, ch. 42 (c. AD 96)
"The Apostles received the Gospel for us from the Lord Jesus Christ; Jesus Christ was sent from God… They preached from district to district and from city to city, and they appointed their earliest converts… to be bishops and deacons of the future believers."
Clement traces the three-fold order directly to apostolic appointment. The Corinthian church's problem — for which Clement is writing — is precisely the removal of legitimately appointed bishops by a faction. Clement's entire argument rests on the premise that removing validly ordained bishops is an offense against God.
St. Polycarp of Smyrna
Epistle to the Philippians, ch. 5 (c. AD 110)
"The deacons should be blameless before his righteousness, as servants of God and Christ and not of men; not slanderers, not double-tongued, not lovers of money, but temperate in all things, compassionate, careful, walking according to the truth of the Lord, who became the servant of all."
Polycarp — a disciple of John the Apostle — upholds the deaconate as a formal, morally demanding office. His list of qualifications closely mirrors Paul's in 1 Timothy 3, confirming that apostolic teaching on orders was transmitted consistently and faithfully.
St. Cyprian of Carthage
Epistle 3 (c. AD 250)
"Whence you ought to know that the bishop is in the Church and the Church in the bishop, and if anyone is not with the bishop, he is not in the Church."
Cyprian's ecclesiology is entirely episcopal. The bishop is not merely the congregation's representative — the congregation derives its ecclesial existence from the bishop. This is the Oriental Orthodox understanding: outside the apostolic episcopate, there is no Church in the full sacramental sense.

Oriental Orthodox Context

In the Oriental Orthodox tradition, the three orders carry immense theological weight. The Patriarch or Catholicos is the head bishop who represents the fullness of apostolic authority within his tradition. The local bishop governs his diocese as the image of Christ the High Priest. The priest acts in the bishop's name, with delegated authority, administering the sacraments. The deacon assists at the altar and serves the community. None of these orders are self-appointed or congregationally generated — each requires valid ordination by a legitimately consecrated bishop standing in apostolic succession.

3

Ordination and the Laying on of Hands

▼

Historical Overview

Ordination — the formal conferral of holy orders — is accomplished through prayer and the laying on of hands (cheirotonia) by bishops who themselves stand in apostolic succession. This rite is not merely ceremonial; it is a sacrament conferring a permanent and indelible spiritual character upon the recipient. The Oriental Orthodox churches call this keirotonia (from the Coptic and Greek) and treat it as one of the seven sacraments (mysteries). The rite traces directly to the Apostles, as documented in the New Testament.

Scripture: Ordination and Laying on of Hands

Numbers 27:18–23
God commands Moses to lay his hands on Joshua, transferring his authority: "You shall lay your hand on him… and invest him with some of your authority."
The Old Testament establishes the laying on of hands as the divinely ordained means of transmitting spiritual authority and office. The New Testament practice is not an invention — it fulfills the pattern established in the Torah and the prophetic writings.
Acts 6:6
"These they set before the Apostles, and they prayed and laid their hands on them."
The first Christian ordination is recorded here — prayer plus the physical rite of laying on hands. Both elements together constitute ordination; neither alone is sufficient. This apostolic pattern is preserved unchanged in Oriental Orthodox ordination rites.
Acts 13:2–3
"While they were worshiping the Lord and fasting, the Holy Spirit said, 'Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul'… Then after fasting and praying they laid their hands on them and sent them off."
The Holy Spirit initiates the mission, but the Church actualizes it through fasting, prayer, and the laying on of hands — confirming that ordination is both divine and ecclesial. The Spirit works through the appointed human rite, not apart from it.
1 Timothy 5:22
"Do not be hasty in the laying on of hands, nor take part in the sins of others; keep yourself pure."
Paul warns against premature ordination, implying that ordination carries grave spiritual consequences. The laying on of hands is a weighty act — not a formality — and the ordaining bishop shares moral responsibility for the ordained man's conduct.
2 Timothy 1:6
"Fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands."
The charisma is transmitted through the specific physical act of Paul laying hands on Timothy. The gift is permanent ("is in you") — not conditional on continued worthy service, though it must be actively exercised. This is the Oriental Orthodox theology of holy orders as an indelible sacrament.
Hebrews 6:1–2
"…the laying on of hands… the resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment."
The author of Hebrews numbers the laying on of hands among the foundational doctrines of the Christian faith — alongside resurrection and judgment. It is not a peripheral tradition but a constitutive element of basic Christian teaching.
Isaiah 66:21
"And some of them also I will take for priests and for Levites, says the LORD."
God Himself appoints priests — not congregations, not self-appointment. The New Testament fulfills this by showing Christ appointing the Twelve, and the Twelve appointing their successors. Divine initiative is always mediated through appointed human agents.

Church Fathers on Ordination

St. Hippolytus of Rome
Apostolic Tradition, ch. 2–3 (c. AD 215)
"Let the bishop be ordained after he has been chosen by all the people. When he has been named and shall please all, let him, with the presbytery and such bishops as may be present, assemble with the people on a Sunday. While all give their consent, the bishops shall lay their hands upon him, and the presbytery shall stand by in silence."
The Apostolic Tradition is the earliest surviving detailed ordination rite. It shows that by the early third century, episcopal ordination required consent of the people, but the act itself was performed exclusively by bishops through the laying on of hands. Consent and succession work together, not as alternatives.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem
Catechetical Lectures, Lecture 16 (c. AD 350)
"The Holy Spirit works many things in baptism and in ordination; He works everywhere; He works in the presbyters, He works in the deacons, He works in all things."
Cyril explicitly names ordination alongside baptism as a locus of the Holy Spirit's activity — affirming its sacramental character. This is not merely a conferral of function but a genuine work of the Spirit through the Church's appointed rite.
St. John Chrysostom
On the Priesthood, Book III (c. AD 390)
"The work of the priesthood is done on earth, but it is ranked among heavenly ordinances. And this is only right, for no man, no angel, no archangel, no other created power, but the Paraclete himself ordained this succession, and persuaded men still remaining in the flesh to represent the ministry of angels."
Chrysostom elevates the ordained priesthood to an angelic dignity, arguing that it is the Paraclete — the Holy Spirit — who both instituted and perpetuates the succession. The priest stands between heaven and earth, performing a ministry that exceeds the capacities of any natural order.
St. Severus of Antioch
Various Homilies (c. AD 512–518)
PARAPHRASE Severus, the great theologian of the Oriental Orthodox tradition and Patriarch of Antioch, consistently upholds the necessity of episcopal ordination and apostolic succession as the only legitimate form of Church ministry. His extensive correspondence with fellow Oriental Orthodox bishops frequently addresses the invalidity of Chalcedonian ordinations imposed on Oriental Orthodox communities — treating episcopal lineage as a matter of life and death for the Church.
St. Dionysius of Alexandria
Epistles (c. AD 260)
PARAPHRASE Dionysius, the great Alexandrian bishop in direct succession from St. Mark, treats the episcopal office as the pillar of the Church's life. His letters reveal a vigorous consciousness that the See of Alexandria guards apostolic doctrine through its episcopal succession, and that deviation from this succession constitutes a departure from the faith itself.

Liturgical Worship

▼

Overview

Liturgical worship — structured, set-prayer worship offered to God through established forms — is the universal practice of the ancient Church. The Oriental Orthodox churches celebrate among the oldest extant liturgies in Christendom: the Divine Liturgy of St. Basil (used by the Coptic Church), the Liturgy of St. James (Syriac and Armenian tradition), the Liturgy of the Apostles (Ethiopian and Eritrean tradition), and the Qurbana of Addai and Mari. These liturgies, some traceable to the second and third centuries, are living documents of apostolic worship.

Liturgy is not human creativity imposed on worship; it is the Church's participation in the eternal intercession of Christ the High Priest before the Father. The use of incense, set prayers, prostrations, vestments, and a formal liturgical calendar are not human inventions — they are continuous with both Old Testament temple worship and New Testament apostolic practice.

Scripture: Liturgical Worship

Acts 2:42
"They devoted themselves to the Apostles' teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers."
The first Christian community practices structured, regular worship: teaching, fellowship, Eucharist, and "the prayers" — definite article in Greek, implying a known set of prayers rather than spontaneous prayer. Liturgy is the very description of the earliest Church's life.
Acts 3:1
"Now Peter and John were going up to the temple at the hour of prayer, the ninth hour."
The Apostles observe fixed hours of prayer — the ancient practice of the Liturgy of the Hours. Far from abandoning structured prayer, the first Christians fulfilled and extended it. The Oriental Orthodox tradition of the canonical hours traces directly to this apostolic practice.
Revelation 8:3–4
"Another angel came and stood at the altar with a golden censer, and he was given much incense to offer with the prayers of all the saints on the golden altar before the throne."
The heavenly liturgy depicted in Revelation includes incense offered by an angel before God's throne, alongside the prayers of the saints. If incense is used in the worship of heaven itself, its use in earthly liturgy is not superstition — it is participation in heavenly reality (see Psalm 141:2; Malachi 1:11).
Psalm 141:2
"Let my prayer be counted as incense before you, and the lifting up of my hands as the evening sacrifice!"
David explicitly connects incense with prayer and sacrifice — anticipating the liturgical use of incense as a physical symbol of prayer ascending to God. This verse is sung at Vespers in Oriental Orthodox liturgies to this day, connecting ancient Israel's worship with the New Covenant.
Malachi 1:11
"For from the rising of the sun to its setting my name will be great among the nations, and in every place incense will be offered to my name, and a pure offering."
The Fathers — from Justin Martyr onward — read this as a prophecy of the Eucharist offered in every place. But the verse explicitly mentions incense as a component of this universal pure offering, justifying the ongoing liturgical use of incense in Christian worship.
1 Corinthians 11:2
"I commend you because you remember me in everything and maintain the traditions [paradoseis] even as I delivered them to you."
Paul explicitly commends "maintaining the traditions" — not improvising new forms of worship. The Greek paradoseis refers to transmitted liturgical forms, not merely doctrinal summaries. The liturgical tradition is as apostolic as the doctrinal tradition.
Colossians 3:16
"Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly, teaching and admonishing one another in all wisdom, singing psalms and hymns and spiritual songs."
The Colossian community uses psalms, hymns, and spiritual songs — a structured liturgical diet. The Psalter has been the backbone of Oriental Orthodox liturgical prayer for millennia, fulfilling this apostolic instruction in its most complete form.

Church Fathers on Liturgical Worship

St. Justin Martyr
First Apology, ch. 67 (c. AD 150)
"On the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read, as long as time permits; then, when the reader has ceased, the president verbally instructs, and exhorts to the imitation of these good things. Then we all rise together and pray, and… bread and wine and water are brought, and the president in like manner offers prayers and thanksgivings, according to his ability, and the people assent, saying Amen."
Justin Martyr's description of Sunday worship c. AD 150 is clearly a structured liturgy: readings, homily, common prayer, Eucharistic offering, and congregational "Amen." The essential shape of this liturgy is identical to the Oriental Orthodox Divine Liturgy today — a remarkable continuity spanning nearly two millennia.
St. Basil the Great
On the Holy Spirit, ch. 27 (c. AD 375)
"Of the beliefs and practices preserved in the Church, some we possess derived from written teaching; others we have received delivered to us in a mystery by the tradition of the Apostles… Such is the blessing of the water of Baptism, the anointing with oil, the celebration at the Eucharist… all these things, though not mentioned in Scripture, yet are of great force for the mystery of religion, having been received from the tradition of the Apostles."
Basil defends the liturgical traditions — including practices not explicitly described in Scripture — as genuine apostolic tradition. His argument is identical to the Oriental Orthodox apologetic: liturgy transmits apostolic faith, not merely apostolic doctrine.
Egeria (Pilgrim)
Itinerarium Egeriae (c. AD 381–384)
PARAPHRASE Egeria's pilgrimage diary describes the elaborate liturgical life of Jerusalem in the late fourth century: daily morning and evening offices, weekly all-night vigils, the full liturgical calendar with specific commemorations, processions, incense, and a highly structured Eucharistic rite. This liturgy is not a Constantinian innovation; Egeria reports it as the established ancient practice of the Jerusalem church.
St. Dionysius the Areopagite (Pseudo-Dionysius)
The Ecclesiastical Hierarchy (c. 5th–6th cent.)
"Our hierarchy is… conformable to the Divine institutions, and is directed towards the Divine beauty, and united, as far as possible, to it."
The Ecclesiastical Hierarchy, an enormously influential text in the Oriental Orthodox tradition, presents the entire liturgical and sacramental structure of the Church as a participation in and reflection of the heavenly hierarchy. Liturgy is theologically necessary — it is the earthly icon of heavenly worship.
5

The Eucharist and the Priesthood

▼

Overview: Real Presence

The Oriental Orthodox Church holds the Real Presence of Christ in the Eucharist as an absolute and non-negotiable doctrine. The bread and wine become — truly, really, and substantially — the Body and Blood of Christ. This is not a metaphor, a memorial, or a spiritual presence alongside unchanged bread: it is the same Body born of the Virgin, crucified, and risen, now given as food and drink for the life of the world. The theological term "Real Presence" is not sufficient on its own — the Fathers speak of a genuine transformation of the elements, accomplished through the epiclesis (invocation of the Holy Spirit) within the context of the ordained priesthood's ministry.

Scripture: The Eucharist

Matthew 26:26–28
"This IS my body… this IS my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many."
Christ uses the present tense copula — "is," not "represents" or "signifies." At the institution, the language is declarative, not metaphorical. The context of Passover, where the lamb was truly eaten and its blood truly shed, reinforces a literal, not symbolic, meaning.
John 6:51–58
"I am the living bread that came down from heaven… Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life… For my flesh is true food, and my blood is true drink."
When the disciples are scandalized and begin to leave (v.60–66), Christ does not correct their "misunderstanding" and say He spoke metaphorically. He lets them go. The persistence of the discourse in the face of mass departure confirms a literal intent. "True food" and "true drink" are unambiguous realist claims.
1 Corinthians 10:16
"The cup of blessing that we bless, is it not a participation in the blood of Christ? The bread that we break, is it not a participation in the body of Christ?"
Paul's Greek word koinōnia (participation/communion) implies real contact and sharing — not merely a symbolic commemoration. Participation in blood and body requires their genuine presence; one cannot "participate" in an abstraction.
1 Corinthians 11:27–29
"Whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord… For anyone who eats and drinks without discerning the body eats and drinks judgment on himself."
One cannot be "guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord" from consuming mere bread and wine. Paul's warning makes no sense unless the elements are genuinely the Body and Blood. This is among the strongest scriptural arguments for Real Presence.
Luke 24:30–31
"When he was at table with them, he took the bread and blessed and broke it and gave it to them. And their eyes were opened, and they recognized him."
The disciples on the road to Emmaus recognize the risen Christ specifically "in the breaking of the bread" — the Eucharistic terminology of the early Church (Acts 2:42). The Eucharist is presented as the privileged locus of encounter with the risen Lord.
Hebrews 13:10
"We have an altar from which those who serve the tent have no right to eat."
The author of Hebrews tells Christians they have an "altar" — a sacrificial reality. The altar from which they eat implies a genuine, not symbolic, sacrifice and its fruits. This verse has been consistently read by the Fathers as a Eucharistic reference.
Exodus 12:8 / Passover Typology
The Passover lamb was literally eaten; its blood was literally applied to the doorposts.
As Christ is the true Passover Lamb (1 Corinthians 5:7), the typological fulfillment requires real eating of His flesh and real application of His blood. A merely symbolic eating of the antitype is a diminution of, not fulfillment of, the type.

Church Fathers on the Eucharist

St. Ignatius of Antioch
Epistle to the Smyrnaeans, ch. 7 (c. AD 107)
"They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer, because they confess not the Eucharist to be the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ, which suffered for our sins, and which the Father, of His goodness, raised up again."
Ignatius condemns the Docetists for refusing the Eucharist — precisely because they denied the real humanity and real suffering of Christ. His argument only works if the Eucharist genuinely is the flesh of Christ: a symbolic Eucharist would present no problem to Docetists. Real Presence is the premise of Ignatius's refutation.
St. Justin Martyr
First Apology, ch. 66 (c. AD 150)
"For not as common bread and common drink do we receive these; but in like manner as Jesus Christ our Savior, having been made flesh by the Word of God, had both flesh and blood for our salvation, so likewise have we been taught that the food which is blessed by the prayer of His word, and from which our blood and flesh by transmutation are nourished, is the flesh and blood of that Jesus who was made flesh."
Justin explicitly rejects the "common bread and common drink" interpretation and draws a direct parallel between the Incarnation and the Eucharist. The word "transmutation" is particularly significant — Justin describes a genuine change, not a symbolic overlay. This is among the clearest second-century affirmations of Real Presence.
St. Cyril of Jerusalem
Mystagogical Catecheses, Lecture IV (c. AD 350)
"Do not therefore regard the Bread and the Wine as simply that; for they are, according to the Master's declaration, the Body and Blood of Christ… having tasted of His Body and Blood, thou art made of the same body and the same blood with Him."
Cyril's instruction to the newly baptized is the clearest patristic catechesis on the Eucharist. He explicitly contrasts "simply bread and wine" with the Body and Blood — real presence is his starting point, not a conclusion to be argued. His language of becoming "of the same body" with Christ is deification (theosis) through Eucharistic communion.
St. John Chrysostom
Homilies on 1 Corinthians, Homily 24 (c. AD 390)
"How many of you say, I would wish to see His face, His garments, His shoes. Lo! thou seest Him, thou touchest Him, thou eatest Him. And thou desirest to see His clothes, but He gives Himself to thee not to see only, but also to touch and eat and receive within thee."
Chrysostom — whose liturgy is celebrated across the Orthodox world — treats Eucharistic communion as a more intimate contact with Christ than even seeing Him in the flesh. His rhetorical strategy only succeeds if the Eucharist is truly Christ's body — not a memorial of a distant Lord.
St. Athanasius of Alexandria
Fragment (attrib.) / Festal Letters
PARAPHRASE Athanasius, the great defender of the Incarnation, consistently connects Eucharistic theology to Christology: because the Son truly became flesh, the Eucharistic flesh truly is His. His defense of the full humanity of Christ against Arianism has direct implications for Real Presence — a merely symbolic Eucharist would ironically serve the Arian tendency to minimize the reality of the Incarnation.
St. Ephrem the Syrian
Hymns on Faith (c. AD 360)
"In Your bread there is hidden the Spirit who cannot be consumed; in Your wine there dwells Fire that cannot be drunk. Spirit in Your bread, Fire in Your wine — a manifest wonder that our lips have received."
St. Ephrem — one of the greatest theologians of the Syriac tradition, beloved by Oriental Orthodox — expresses the Eucharistic mystery in the language of paradox and wonder. The Spirit hidden in bread, the divine Fire in wine: these are not metaphors for St. Ephrem but participations in the genuine mystery of the Incarnate Word given as food.

The Priest and the Eucharist

In the Oriental Orthodox understanding, the Eucharist requires a validly ordained priest precisely because the priest acts in persona Christi — in the person of Christ, the Great High Priest. When the priest pronounces the words of institution and calls upon the Holy Spirit in the epiclesis, it is Christ who acts through him. This is why ordination and Eucharist are theologically inseparable: without a priest in apostolic succession, there is no valid Eucharist; without the Eucharist, the Church's life is incomplete.

The Oriental Orthodox epiclesis — the solemn invocation of the Holy Spirit over the gifts — is the heart of the Eucharistic prayer. It is not merely a liturgical formality but a genuine cry to the Spirit to transform the bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ.

Common Protestant Objections

▼

Apostolic Succession & Church Authority

Objection: Apostolic succession is nowhere taught in the Bible.▼
Response: Apostolic succession is extensively documented in Scripture. Acts 1:20 records the Apostles replacing Judas using the word episkopē (bishopric). Paul charges Timothy to "entrust to faithful men who will be able to teach others also" (2 Timothy 2:2) — a four-generation chain of succession in one verse. Titus is left to appoint elders in every city (Titus 1:5). The Apostles appoint bishops and deacons in every church they plant (Acts 14:23). Furthermore, the absence of a proof-text for a doctrine does not make it unbiblical — the word "Trinity" does not appear in Scripture either. The early Church — from Clement of Rome (AD 96) to Irenaeus (AD 180) to Tertullian (AD 200) — universally understood succession as apostolic teaching. The burden of proof lies with those who reject a doctrine universally held by the earliest Christians.
Objection: The early Church had no bishops — that structure came later.▼
Response: This claim is historically untenable. Ignatius of Antioch — writing c. AD 107, within living memory of the Apostles — addresses seven churches, each governed by a single bishop (monepiscopate). He writes as though this is an established and non-controversial reality, not a novelty. Clement of Rome (AD 96) defends appointed bishops against those who would remove them. Polycarp of Smyrna, a disciple of John the Apostle, is bishop of Smyrna. The New Testament itself describes the appointment of "overseers" (episkopoi) in Philippi (Philippians 1:1), in every city of Crete (Titus 1:5), and in the churches of Asia Minor (Acts 20:28). The three-fold order is not a second-century development — it is the apostolic institution.
Objection: All believers are priests — we don't need ordained clergy (1 Peter 2:9).▼
Response: The "royal priesthood" of 1 Peter 2:9 refers to the corporate priestly dignity of the Church as the new Israel — exactly as Exodus 19:6 applied to ancient Israel as a "kingdom of priests." Yet Israel also had a distinct Levitical priesthood for specific liturgical and sacramental functions. The two are not in conflict. Similarly, the New Testament affirms the universal priesthood of all believers and the specific ordained priesthood. Revelation 1:6 says Christ "made us a kingdom, priests to his God" — yet Revelation 4:4 still depicts the twenty-four elders (representing the ordained ministry) in distinct roles before the throne. The universal priesthood concerns every Christian's access to God through Christ; the ordained priesthood concerns the specific ministry of Word and Sacrament entrusted to those called and set apart.
Objection: Tradition is man-made and untrustworthy — only Scripture has authority.▼
Response: This objection is self-refuting: the canon of Scripture was itself defined by Tradition — by councils of bishops standing in apostolic succession. No New Testament book announces "I am canonical Scripture." The decision that Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John — but not the Gospel of Thomas — belong in the canon was made by the Church's tradition and conciliar authority. Furthermore, Paul explicitly commands: "Stand firm and hold to the traditions [paradoseis] that you were taught by us, either by our spoken word or by our letter" (2 Thessalonians 2:15). Paul places oral tradition on equal footing with written. The idea that only written Scripture is authoritative is itself a tradition — one invented in the 16th century, without apostolic precedent.

Liturgy and Worship

Objection: Liturgy is vain repetition condemned by Christ (Matthew 6:7).▼
Response: Matthew 6:7 condemns "babbling" (battalogēsēte) — mindless, magical repetition intended to coerce God. This has nothing to do with structured liturgical prayer. In the same chapter, Christ teaches the Lord's Prayer as a model prayer — which Christians have prayed in liturgical worship since the Didache (c. AD 90). Moreover, the Psalter — which Christ prayed and which the New Testament cites more than any other book — is a fixed prayer book. The angels in Revelation cry "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty" (4:8) "day and night without ceasing." If repetitive prayer is vain, heaven itself is guilty. The Oriental Orthodox tradition understands liturgy as the Church joining heaven's unceasing worship — not babbling, but adoration.
Objection: The use of incense is a pagan carryover with no Christian basis.▼
Response: Incense has continuous biblical warrant from the Tabernacle (Exodus 30:7–8) through the Temple (1 Chronicles 23:13) to the heavenly liturgy (Revelation 8:3–4). The Magi brought incense (frankincense) to the infant Christ (Matthew 2:11). Psalm 141:2 — sung at Vespers to this day — says "Let my prayer be counted as incense before you." Malachi 1:11 prophesies that "in every place incense will be offered to my name." The Fathers from the earliest centuries used incense in worship. The claim that it is "pagan" would equally condemn the Temple worship God Himself instituted. The use of incense in Christian liturgy is a fulfillment of Old Testament practice and a participation in heavenly worship.
Objection: The liturgical calendar and fasting rules are human additions to Christianity.▼
Response: The New Testament itself references the practice of fasting (Matthew 6:16–18; Acts 13:2–3; 14:23). The Didache (c. AD 90) already specifies fast days (Wednesdays and Fridays). The observance of Sunday, Easter, and Pentecost are documented in the earliest post-apostolic sources. Paul says "as the Lord has directed" regarding ordering the churches (1 Corinthians 7:17; 11:34) — implying a structured practice. The objection proves too much: applied consistently, it would eliminate Sunday worship itself, which is also not explicitly mandated in the New Testament but universally practiced from the apostolic age.

The Eucharist

Objection: The Eucharist is merely a symbolic memorial — "do this in remembrance of me."▼
Response: The Greek word anamnesis (remembrance) does not mean "merely symbolic recollection." In Hebrew and Greek cultic contexts, anamnesis refers to a re-presentation — making a past event present and effective again. The Passover Seder was not merely a memorial of the Exodus; it was a participation in it (Exodus 13:8; Deuteronomy 16:3). When Christ commands anamnesis of His sacrifice, He commands a genuine re-presentation of Calvary — not a mental exercise. Furthermore, 1 Corinthians 11:27–29 says that eating "unworthily" makes one guilty of the Body and Blood of the Lord — an impossibility if the elements remain mere bread and wine. The universal consensus of the first fifteen centuries of Christianity, across East and West, is Real Presence.
Objection: John 6 is about spiritual eating, not physical eating — Christ says "the flesh profits nothing" (v.63).▼
Response: John 6:63 says "It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh profits nothing." The "flesh" here refers to human fleshly comprehension and effort — not to Christ's flesh, which throughout the discourse He explicitly says gives life and must be eaten. Christ cannot simultaneously say "my flesh is true food" (v.55) and "my flesh profits nothing" (v.63) about the same thing. The contrast is between human carnality and the life-giving Spirit — not between symbolic and literal eating. The disciples who leave at v.66 understand Him literally, and He does not correct them by saying "I was speaking metaphorically." This departure is the most important interpretive datum in the passage.

Frequently Asked Questions

▼
1. What is the Oriental Orthodox Church?+
The Oriental Orthodox Churches are a family of ancient Christian churches that separated from the Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Church following the Council of Chalcedon in AD 451. They include the Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, the Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church, the Armenian Apostolic Church, the Syriac Orthodox Church of Antioch, and the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church of India. Together they represent some of the oldest continuously existing Christian communities, with apostolic lineages tracing to the first century. They hold a Miaphysite Christology, confessing one nature of Christ — fully divine and fully human, united without separation, confusion, or alteration.
2. What is Miaphysitism and how does it relate to Apostolic Succession?+
Miaphysitism is the Christological position that Christ has one united nature — fully divine and fully human — following St. Cyril of Alexandria's formula "one united nature of the incarnate Word." Oriental Orthodox reject the Chalcedonian "two natures" formula not because they deny Christ's humanity, but because they believe the Chalcedonian formula opens the door to Nestorian division. The connection to apostolic succession is direct: the Oriental Orthodox bishops are the successors of the Fathers who defended orthodox Christology (Athanasius, Cyril, Dioscorus). Their succession preserves not only episcopal lineage but doctrinal fidelity to the pre-Chalcedonian consensus.
3. Can apostolic succession be broken and re-started?+
No — this is precisely the Oriental Orthodox concern. Apostolic succession is an unbroken chain; once broken, it cannot be independently reconstituted. A person cannot lay hands on himself and thereby possess apostolic authority. This is why St. Clement of Rome (AD 96) was so alarmed by the removal of Corinthian bishops — the succession there was in danger. If a community's succession is genuinely traced back to Christ's Apostles, it has a legitimate claim. If it originates in the 16th century or later with no verifiable chain to the Apostles, it lacks this claim regardless of how sincerely it holds other Christian doctrines.
4. Do the Oriental Orthodox recognize the sacraments of other churches?+
The Oriental Orthodox recognize the sacraments of other churches with valid apostolic succession, including Roman Catholics, Eastern Orthodox, and other ancient churches, though full communion does not exist between all of these bodies. Sacraments performed in communities without valid apostolic succession are generally not recognized as possessing the same sacramental efficacy, though God's grace cannot be confined by human categories and individuals may receive grace through many means. The principle is that the sacraments require properly ordained ministers — the validity of the sacrament follows the validity of the orders.
5. Why does the Coptic Church claim to trace back to St. Mark?+
The Coptic Orthodox Church holds that St. Mark the Evangelist — author of the Second Gospel and a companion of the Apostle Peter — founded the church of Alexandria around AD 42 and became its first bishop. This is supported by multiple early sources including Eusebius of Caesarea (Ecclesiastical History II.16) and Clement of Alexandria. The subsequent list of Alexandrian bishops from Mark to the present Pope Tawadros II (118th Pope) is carefully preserved. Whether one accepts every detail of this founding narrative, the Coptic Church's antiquity and continuity as an apostolic community from the earliest centuries of Christianity is historically well-attested.
6. What is the difference between the Oriental Orthodox and Eastern Orthodox churches?+
Both families are ancient apostolic churches with valid succession, seven sacraments, liturgical worship, and patristic theology. The primary historical division occurred at the Council of Chalcedon (AD 451) over the precise formulation of Christ's natures. Eastern Orthodox (Chalcedonians) accept Chalcedon's "two natures in one person" formula. Oriental Orthodox (Non-Chalcedonians) hold to Cyril of Alexandria's Miaphysite Christology and reject Chalcedon's formula, viewing it as implicitly Nestorian. In recent decades, ecumenical theological dialogues have produced joint declarations affirming that both families share the same faith in Christ, and that the division may have been as much terminological as substantive.
7. Is the Oriental Orthodox understanding of the Eucharist the same as Roman Catholic transubstantiation?+
Both hold to Real Presence — the genuine transformation of the bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ — but Oriental Orthodox do not use the scholastic Aristotelian terminology of "transubstantiation" (substance/accident). The Oriental Orthodox approach is patristic and doxological rather than philosophical and analytical. The transformation is affirmed as a genuine mystery accomplished by the Holy Spirit (epiclesis), without attempting to explain the metaphysical mechanism in Aristotelian categories. The Coptic, Ethiopian, and Syriac liturgies affirm Real Presence in clear, unambiguous language without the Latin theological framework.
8. What role does Sacred Tradition play in Oriental Orthodox theology?+
Sacred Tradition is not secondary to Scripture in Oriental Orthodox theology — it is the living context within which Scripture was written, collected, and interpreted. The canon of Scripture is itself a product of Tradition. The Fathers, the Councils, the liturgical texts, the creeds, the lives of the saints — all of these are expressions of the one Tradition, which is the Holy Spirit's ongoing presence in the Church. Scripture is the written crystallization of Tradition, not its replacement. The Oriental Orthodox have specifically preserved Tradition through tremendous suffering — under Byzantine imperialism, Persian conquest, Arab invasion, Ottoman rule, and modern persecution — precisely because they believe it to be the deposit of the apostolic faith.
9. Why do Oriental Orthodox use incense, candles, and icons in worship?+
Incense: biblical warrant from Exodus through Revelation; a physical symbol of prayer ascending to God and a participation in heavenly liturgy (Revelation 8:3–4). Candles/Lamps: Christ is the Light of the world (John 8:12); lamps before the altar fulfill the commanded Tabernacle lampstand (Exodus 25:31–40) and represent Christ's presence. Icons: in the Oriental Orthodox tradition (particularly Coptic and Armenian), icons express the truth of the Incarnation — that God became visible in human flesh. To honor an icon is to honor the person depicted, not the material object. The seventh Ecumenical Council (Nicea II, AD 787) established the theology of icon veneration, distinguishing it absolutely from idolatry.
10. What are the seven sacraments in the Oriental Orthodox Church?+
The seven mysteries (sacraments) are: (1) Baptism — rebirth by water and the Spirit (John 3:5); (2) Chrismation/Confirmation — sealing with the Holy Spirit (Acts 8:14–17; 2 Corinthians 1:21–22); (3) The Eucharist — the Body and Blood of Christ (Matthew 26:26–28; John 6); (4) Confession/Repentance — forgiveness of sins through the ordained minister (John 20:23); (5) Holy Orders — ordination to the ministry (1 Timothy 4:14; 2 Timothy 1:6); (6) Holy Matrimony — the sacred covenant of marriage (Ephesians 5:32); (7) Holy Unction — anointing of the sick (James 5:14–15). All seven are rooted in Scripture and attested in the earliest patristic sources.
11. How do Oriental Orthodox respond to the charge that their church is not mentioned in the New Testament?+
The same charge would apply to every church on earth — the New Testament does not name any modern denomination or institution. What the New Testament does describe is the Church with her apostles, bishops, sacraments, and traditions. The Oriental Orthodox Church is precisely the continuation of that Church — not a new institution but the same Church, continuously existing in the same places (Egypt, Ethiopia, Armenia, Syria, India), with the same faith, the same sacraments, the same episcopal succession. The question is not "which church is mentioned in the New Testament?" but "which church has continued in unbroken succession from the New Testament Church?" The answer, for the Oriental Orthodox, is unambiguous.
12. Can women be ordained in the Oriental Orthodox tradition?+
The Oriental Orthodox tradition does not ordain women to the episcopate or presbyterate, following the universal practice of the ancient Church and the teaching of the Fathers. However, women have always held honored and essential roles in the Church: deaconesses (an order documented in the early Church), abbesses, monastic mothers, and lay leaders of great importance. The restriction on ordination to the major orders is not a statement about women's spiritual dignity — women saints are venerated as equals of men, and the Theotokos (Mother of God) is the most exalted of all created beings — but reflects the Church's understanding of the priestly office as a particular form of typological representation.
13. What is the significance of the Coptic Liturgy of St. Basil?+
The Coptic Divine Liturgy of St. Basil is the primary Eucharistic liturgy of the Coptic Orthodox Church. While it shares a name with the Byzantine Liturgy of St. Basil, it is a distinct and older text with elements traceable to pre-Chalcedonian Alexandrian practice. Its Eucharistic prayer is among the most elaborate in Christendom, containing extensive Old Testament typology, New Testament fulfillment, detailed intercessions, and a solemn epiclesis invoking the Holy Spirit over the gifts. The Liturgy is conducted in Coptic — the last living descendant of the language of the Pharaohs — alongside Arabic, preserving a linguistic connection to the earliest Egyptian Christian community.
14. How can I verify that a church has valid apostolic succession?+
The test proposed by Irenaeus (c. AD 180) and Tertullian (c. AD 200) remains applicable: trace the bishop's line of ordination back to the Apostles. For the Oriental Orthodox churches, this lineage is documented in episcopal lists preserved for centuries. The key questions are: (1) Was the bishop ordained by valid bishops? (2) Were those bishops themselves validly ordained in an unbroken chain? (3) Does the community share the apostolic faith? A community founded in the 16th century or later, or one that practiced "spiritual" ordination without the laying on of hands by existing bishops, cannot demonstrate this lineage. The historical record is clear about which communities have maintained the unbroken chain.
15. If someone joins an Oriental Orthodox church from a Protestant background, must they be re-baptized?+
Practice varies by jurisdiction. Many Oriental Orthodox churches require chrismation (and in some cases baptism) for those received from Protestant backgrounds, while recognizing the Trinitarian baptism of some traditions. The concern is not merely the validity of the water rite but the completeness of initiation into the sacramental life of the apostolic Church. Confirmation/Chrismation — the sealing of the Holy Spirit — is administered immediately after baptism in the Oriental Orthodox tradition (including for infants), and someone received from a tradition where it was either omitted or administered separately would typically receive it upon reception into the Church. Specific requirements should be determined with the local bishop or priest.
