THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Apostolic Succession — The Unbroken Chain

A Comprehensive Biblical, Historical, and Patristic Defense

"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also." — 2 Timothy 2:2

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

One of the most contested questions in all of Christian theology is this: How does the authority of Christ and his Apostles continue in the Church today? The Oriental Orthodox answer — shared with Eastern Orthodoxy and Roman Catholicism — is apostolic succession: the unbroken chain of ordained ministers, bishop by bishop, stretching from the original Apostles of Jesus Christ to the bishops of the Church today.

This is not a claim to personal holiness or infallibility. It is a claim about the continuity of a divinely instituted office — the same office that Jesus himself appointed, that the Apostles filled, and that they in turn transmitted to their appointed successors through the laying on of hands and prayer. The grace of the ordained ministry is not self-generated; it is received, transmitted, and received again — an unbroken river of apostolic authority flowing through history.

The Oriental Orthodox churches (Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac Orthodox, Armenian Apostolic, Malankara) each trace their episcopal lineage to specific Apostles: the Coptic Church to St. Mark the Evangelist; the Ethiopian to St. Frumentius (consecrated by the Alexandrian patriarch); the Syriac to SS. Peter and Paul through the Antiochene patriarchate; the Armenian to SS. Bartholomew and Thaddaeus. These are not legends but historical claims supported by early chronicles and patristic testimony.

This site examines apostolic succession from its roots in the Old Testament, through the New Testament institution of the apostolic office, through the testimony of the earliest Church Fathers, to its practical implications for sacramental theology, ecclesiology, and the question of Christian unity today.

II. What Is Apostolic Succession?

▼

Apostolic succession is the doctrine that the authority, mission, and grace of the original Apostles of Jesus Christ has been transmitted to subsequent generations of bishops through an unbroken chain of ordination by the laying on of hands. It operates on two interconnected levels:

1. Succession of office: The bishop today occupies the same office that the original Apostles occupied — not merely a similar one, but the identical divinely instituted office, passed from person to person through the act of ordination. The bishop is the Apostle's successor in a real and specific sense.

2. Succession of faith: The bishop is not only the successor of the Apostles' office but the guardian and transmitter of the Apostles' doctrine — the paradosis (handing-on) of the Faith once delivered to the saints (Jude 3). Apostolic succession is thus both personal (a chain of persons) and doctrinal (a chain of teaching).

These two dimensions are inseparable in the Oriental Orthodox understanding. A bishop who holds apostolic orders but teaches heresy has broken the succession of faith even if he retains the succession of office. Conversely, correct doctrine without the apostolic office is insufficient for the full sacramental life of the Church.

Oriental Orthodox View

Apostolic succession is both the transmission of a divinely instituted office (through the laying on of hands) and the handing on of the apostolic faith. Both are essential. The bishop is the center of the local church's sacramental and doctrinal life — without valid succession, the fullness of the Church's sacramental ministry cannot be exercised.

Protestant View

Most Protestant traditions reject the necessity of apostolic succession. Ordination is understood as recognition of gifts and commissioning for service, not transmission of a distinct grace. Some Anglicans and Lutherans maintain "historic episcopate" for unity or order, but deny it is necessary for the validity of ministry or sacraments. The authority of Scripture is sufficient; the authority of an unbroken episcopal chain is not required.

III. Old Testament Foundations

▼

The principle of divinely transmitted authority through appointed succession is not a New Testament innovation — it is a consistent pattern throughout the entire biblical narrative. God does not leave his people without ordained, appointed leaders whose authority is traceable back to a divine commission.

Scriptural Evidence

Numbers 27:18–23
CTX
"So the LORD said to Moses, 'Take Joshua the son of Nun… and lay your hand on him… You shall invest him with some of your authority, that all the congregation of the people of Israel may obey.'"
Moses lays hands on Joshua, transmitting a portion of his authority. This is the Old Testament prototype of apostolic succession: a divinely ordained leader appoints his successor through a specific ritual gesture (hand-laying), and the congregation is commanded to recognize and obey the successor's authority. The parallel to 2 Timothy 1:6 is exact.
Deuteronomy 31:7–8, 14
CTX
"Then Moses summoned Joshua and said to him in the sight of all Israel, 'Be strong and courageous, for you must go with this people into the land…'"
Moses' formal commissioning of Joshua before all Israel models the public, communal character of ordained succession. Leadership is not self-appointed but publicly transmitted in the presence of the community — the same pattern as episcopal consecration in the Oriental Orthodox tradition.
Exodus 29:1–9
CTX
"Now this is what you shall do to them to consecrate them, that they may serve me as priests… And you shall put the holy garments on Aaron, and anoint him and consecrate him, that he may serve me as priest."
The Aaronic priesthood is divinely instituted, ritually transmitted, and restricted to a specific lineage. This establishes the Old Testament principle: not everyone may officiate at the altar — only those who have received the divinely appointed consecration. The priesthood is transmitted, not self-assumed.
Numbers 3:5–10
CTX
"And the LORD spoke to Moses, saying, 'Bring the tribe of Levi near, and set them before Aaron the priest… But if any outsider comes near, he shall be put to death.'"
The severity of the restriction on priestly ministry — death for unauthorized approaches to the altar — underscores how seriously God takes the appointed transmission of priestly authority. This is not human gatekeeping but divine order for the protection of the community. Korah's rebellion (Numbers 16) illustrates the catastrophic consequences of claiming priestly authority outside the divine appointment.
1 Kings 19:16, 19–21
CTX
"Elisha the son of Shaphat… you shall anoint to be prophet in your place… Elijah passed by him and cast his cloak upon him."
The prophetic succession from Elijah to Elisha is transmitted through a physical act (casting the cloak) — a physical sign of the spiritual transmission of prophetic authority. Elisha then ministers as Elijah's successor, performing greater miracles (2 Kings 2). The prophetic office, like the priestly, is appointed and transmitted — not self-assumed.
Isaiah 22:20–22
CTX
"In that day I will call my servant Eliakim the son of Hilkiah… and I will place on his shoulder the key of the house of David."
The "key of David" — quoted by Christ in Revelation 3:7 and applied to Peter in Matthew 16:19 — is transferred from Shebna to Eliakim. Authority within God's household is appointed and transferred. Christ's citation of this passage in connection with the Petrine office signals the New Testament fulfillment of this Old Testament succession pattern.

IV. Christ Institutes His Ministry

▼

Jesus does not leave his followers as an amorphous community without structure or appointed authority. From the beginning of his public ministry, he deliberately selects, names, trains, and commissions a specific group of Twelve — patterned on the Twelve Tribes of Israel and representing the new Israel of God. This appointment is not incidental but constitutive of his mission.

Scriptural Evidence

Mark 3:13–15
OO
"And he went up on the mountain and called to him those whom he desired, and they came to him. And he appointed twelve (whom he also named apostles) so that they might be with him and he might send them out to preach and have authority to cast out demons."
The deliberate appointment of the Twelve is not spontaneous but purposeful: Christ "called those whom he desired" and "appointed" (Greek: epoiēsen — "made") the Twelve. The office of Apostle is not self-assumed; it is bestowed by Christ. The specific authority to preach and cast out demons is attached to this appointment — not to all disciples generally.
Matthew 16:17–19
OO
"And I tell you, you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church… I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven."
Christ gives Peter a unique authority — the "keys of the kingdom" — that is explicitly connected to the transmission of divine authority ("bound in heaven"). The imagery of keys as administrative authority is drawn from Isaiah 22:22 (see S3). This establishes the episcopal/Petrine office as one that bears genuine divine authority, not merely human leadership.
Matthew 18:18
OO
"Truly, I say to you, whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."
The binding-and-loosing authority extended to the Twelve as a body. This is not advisory or declaratory only — it is effective: what is bound/loosed on earth is correspondingly bound/loosed in heaven. This divine ratification of apostolic authority is the basis for the Oriental Orthodox teaching that the ordained ministry acts in persona Christi — in the person of Christ.
John 17:18
OO
"As you sent me into the world, so I have sent them into the world."
Christ's High-Priestly Prayer explicitly models the apostolic mission on his own divine mission: "as the Father sent me, so I send them." The grammar of succession is here at its most profound: Christ's mission from the Father is transmitted to the Apostles, who will in turn transmit it to their successors. The chain of mission is unbroken and divinely instituted.
John 20:21–23
OO
"Jesus said to them again, 'Peace be with you. As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you.' And when he had said this, he breathed on them and said to them, 'Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them.'"
The Resurrection commissioning is the defining moment of apostolic institution. Christ's breathing of the Spirit on the Apostles echoes God's breath into Adam (Genesis 2:7) — a new creation act. The apostolic ministry is established by a Spirit-imparting gesture, not merely a verbal command. The authority to forgive sins is attached to this Spirit-reception — confirming that the apostolic office is charismatic (Spirit-filled) from its inception.
Matthew 28:18–20
OO
"All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them… teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."
The Great Commission is explicitly universal in scope ("all nations") and temporal extent ("to the end of the age"). The Apostles clearly could not personally fulfill a command spanning the entire Church age — the command is given to the apostolic office, which continues in their successors. "I am with you always" is not merely a promise to the individual Eleven but to the apostolic ministry they inaugurate.
Luke 10:16
OO
"The one who hears you hears me, and the one who rejects you rejects me, and the one who rejects me rejects him who sent me."
Christ identifies himself with his sent ones in an ontological way — to hear/reject the apostolic messenger is to hear/reject Christ himself. This "sacramental" identification of Christ with his ministers is the deepest theological basis for the authority of apostolic succession: the bishop's authority is not merely human or institutional but participatory in the divine authority of Christ.

V. The Apostles Appoint Successors

▼

The Apostles did not regard themselves as an unrepeatable generation whose ministry would die with them. From the earliest chapters of Acts, they appoint, ordain, and commission successors — fulfilling both the practical necessity of expanding ministry and the theological conviction that the apostolic mission was permanent, not temporary.

Scriptural Evidence

Acts 1:20–26
OO
"For it is written in the Book of Psalms… 'Let another take his office.' So one of the men who have accompanied us during all the time that the Lord Jesus went in and out among us… must become with us a witness to his resurrection."
The replacement of Judas is the first act of apostolic succession in the New Testament. Peter cites Psalm 109:8 ("let another take his office") as prophetic warrant for succession — establishing that the apostolic office is an ongoing office to be filled, not an unrepeatable personal role. Matthias is then ordained to the Twelve — the office is filled, not retired.
Acts 6:1–6
OO
"And what they said pleased the whole gathering, and they chose Stephen, a man full of faith and of the Holy Spirit… These they set before the apostles, and they prayed and laid their hands on them."
The appointment of the first deacons through apostolic laying on of hands establishes the diaconate as a distinct order of ordained ministry. The gesture of hand-laying (cheirotonía) plus prayer is the specific, repeatable ritual form of ordination — the same form used in the Oriental Orthodox Church to this day.
Acts 14:23
OO
"And when they had appointed elders for them in every church, with prayer and fasting they committed them to the Lord in whom they had believed."
Paul and Barnabas systematically ordain (Greek: cheirotonēsantes — "appointing by hand") elders in every church they plant. This is not an emergency measure but standard apostolic practice: every new church receives an ordained presbyterate before Paul and Barnabas move on. The apostolic missionary work is not complete until ordained leadership is established.
1 Timothy 4:14
OO
"Do not neglect the gift you have, which was given you by prophecy when the council of elders laid their hands on you."
Timothy's ordination is recalled: a council of elders (presbyterion) laid hands on him, and through this act a specific supernatural "gift" (charisma) was given. The gift is not Timothy's natural ability but a divine grace imparted through the apostolic gesture — confirming that ordination is genuinely sacramental: it actually confers grace, it does not merely recognize existing grace.
2 Timothy 1:6
OO
"For this reason I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands."
Paul specifies that the gift is "in you" — an indwelling divine grace — transmitted "through the laying on of my hands." This language is unambiguous: something real is placed within Timothy through the apostolic gesture. This is the foundational text for the Oriental Orthodox doctrine of sacramental ordination as a genuine transmission of grace.
Titus 1:5
OO
"This is why I left you in Crete, so that you might put what remained into order, and appoint elders in every town as I directed you."
Paul gives Titus a specific mandate: ordain elders throughout Crete. This is not optional — it is the unfinished apostolic work that Titus is explicitly left to complete. The universal establishment of ordained leadership in every local church is presented as a non-negotiable apostolic requirement, not a mere organizational preference.
1 Timothy 5:22
CTX
"Do not be hasty in the laying on of hands, nor take part in the sins of others; keep yourself pure."
Paul's caution against hasty ordination confirms that the laying on of hands is a serious, consequential act with real spiritual implications — not merely a ceremony. The warning against sharing "in the sins of others" through premature ordination reflects the sacramental weight: an unworthy ordination is a genuine spiritual harm, not merely a poor personnel decision.
2 Timothy 2:2
OO
"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
The explicit "four-generation" chain of apostolic succession: Christ → Paul → Timothy → faithful men → others. This verse is the clearest New Testament statement of the principle of apostolic succession as a deliberate, ordained transmission across generations. The chain must go: Christ's teaching, heard by Paul, transmitted to Timothy, who entrusts it to faithful men capable of transmitting it further.

VI. The Threefold Ministry: Bishop, Priest, Deacon

▼

By the time of the Apostolic Fathers (the generation immediately following the Apostles), the three-tiered ministry of bishop (episcopos), priest/elder (presbyteros), and deacon (diakonos) was not a developing experiment but a universal and established reality. The structural differentiation of the ordained ministry is already clearly present in the New Testament and is explicitly defended by the earliest witnesses.

The Three Orders Defined

The Episcopate (Bishop)
OO
Greek: episkopos — "overseer." The bishop is the fullness of the ordained priesthood — the successor of the Apostles, the center of the local church's eucharistic, pastoral, and doctrinal life.
In the Oriental Orthodox theology, the bishop alone may ordain, consecrate the chrism (myron), and is the normal minister of Confirmation. He is the sacramental father of his diocese, the link in the apostolic chain, and the guarantor of communion with the universal Church. Acts 20:28; 1 Tim 3:1–7; Tit 1:7–9.
The Presbyterate (Priest)
OO
Greek: presbyteros — "elder." The priest participates in the bishop's sacerdotal ministry, celebrating the Eucharist, administering Baptism and the other sacraments, and serving as the pastor of a local congregation.
The priest acts in the bishop's name and by his authority — which is why ordination by the bishop is required and why a priest cannot celebrate outside his bishop's jurisdiction without proper release. Titus 1:5; Acts 14:23; 1 Tim 5:17–22; Jas 5:14.
The Diaconate (Deacon)
OO
Greek: diakonos — "servant/minister." The deacon is ordained to assist the bishop and priest in the liturgy, to serve the poor, and to administer various practical and pastoral functions.
The diaconate is not a stepping stone to the priesthood but a distinct and permanent order with its own grace and ministry. Acts 6:1–6 records its institution; 1 Tim 3:8–13 gives its qualifications; Phil 1:1 greets "overseers and deacons" alongside Paul — confirming the threefold ministry as early as Paul's imprisonment.

New Testament Evidence for the Threefold Structure

Philippians 1:1
OO
"Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, to all the saints in Christ Jesus who are at Philippi, with the overseers and deacons."
Paul's earliest datable reference to a two-tier structure (bishops/overseers and deacons) within a local congregation — c. AD 62. By this time, the structural differentiation of ordained ministry is already taken for granted. The Philippians are greeted not as an undifferentiated community but as a body with its leadership explicitly identified.
1 Timothy 3:1–13
OO
"The saying is trustworthy: if anyone aspires to the office of overseer, he desires a noble task… Deacons likewise must be dignified…"
Paul gives separate qualification lists for bishops (vv.1–7) and deacons (vv.8–13) — confirming the two as distinct offices with distinct requirements. The separate treatment implies distinct functions and distinct ordination. By the time of the Pastoral Epistles, the structural threefold ministry is not being developed; it is being regulated.
Acts 20:28
OO
"Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood."
Paul addresses the elders/overseers of Ephesus and attributes their appointment not to human vote or community consensus but to the Holy Spirit: "the Holy Spirit has made you overseers." This is the apostolic theology of ordination: the human act (appointment by apostle) is the instrument of the divine act (appointment by the Spirit).

VII. Witness of the Early Fathers — Overview

▼

The testimony of the early Church Fathers on apostolic succession is not scattered or ambiguous — it is consistent, emphatic, and universal. From the first generation after the Apostles through the 5th century, every major theological tradition (Alexandrian, Antiochene, Roman, Cappadocian) teaches that the validity of the Church's ministry and sacraments depends on continuity with the apostolic ministry through the episcopal office.

c. AD 80–100
The Didache — Instructs communities to appoint bishops and deacons who are worthy, calling them "your honored ones, along with the prophets and teachers" (Did. 15:1). The ordained ministry is treated as the normal, established means of community leadership — the prophets and teachers being the exception, not the rule.
c. AD 96
Clement of Rome (1 Clement) — The most important early post-apostolic document on apostolic succession. Clement explicitly describes the apostles as appointing bishops and deacons, and further arranging for their succession: "other approved men should succeed them in their ministry" (1 Clem 44). Clement intervenes in the Corinthian schism precisely because the unlawful removal of appointed clergy violates apostolic order.
c. AD 107
Ignatius of Antioch — Seven letters written on his way to martyrdom, each insisting with extraordinary urgency on the bishop as the center of the church's life. "Without the bishop, do nothing. Where the bishop is, there let the people be" (Smyrn. 8). The threefold ministry is presented as established, universal, and non-negotiable.
c. AD 155–165
Justin Martyr — Describes the "president" of the Eucharist as presiding over the community's worship and distributing the Eucharist. While Justin does not elaborate on succession theory, his description of the ordered liturgy presupposes established leadership with recognized authority.
c. AD 180
Irenaeus of Lyons — Provides explicit succession lists for the major sees and argues that apostolic succession is the primary criterion for authentic Christianity against the Gnostic heretics. "We are in a position to reckon up those who were by the apostles instituted bishops in the Churches, and to demonstrate the succession of these men to our own times" (AH III.3.1).
c. AD 200
Tertullian — Though he later became a Montanist, in his Catholic period Tertullian argues powerfully for apostolic succession as the criterion of authentic doctrine: "Let the heretics produce some original work of their founder and give us proofs by showing in what succession of bishops these things go back to one of the apostles or to some apostolic man." (Prescription Against Heretics, 32)
c. AD 215
Hippolytus (Apostolic Tradition) — Preserves full ordination rites for bishops, priests, and deacons, each with specific prayers invoking the Holy Spirit for the grace of the office. The bishop's consecration prayer calls for "the Spirit of leadership" that God gave to Moses and transmitted to the elders. The rites assume apostolic succession as the theology underlying every ordination.
c. AD 250–258
Cyprian of Carthage — "The bishop is in the Church and the Church is in the bishop." Cyprian defends the episcopate as constitutive of the Church itself — without the bishop there is no Church, no Eucharist, no valid ministry. His theology of episcopal succession and unity became foundational for all subsequent ecclesiology, East and West.

VIII. Clement of Rome — The First Witness

▼

Clement of Rome's letter to the Corinthians (1 Clement), written c. AD 96, is the most important non-canonical document for the doctrine of apostolic succession. Written during the lifetimes of people who personally knew the Apostles — possibly while the Apostle John was still alive at Ephesus — it describes apostolic succession with remarkable clarity and conviction.

St. Clement of Rome (c. AD 35–99)
1 Clement, Chapter 42 — c. AD 96
"The apostles have preached the Gospel to us from the Lord Jesus Christ; Jesus Christ has done so from God. Christ therefore was sent forth by God, and the apostles by Christ. Both these appointments, then, were made in an orderly way, according to the will of God. Having therefore received their orders, and being fully assured by the resurrection of our Lord Jesus Christ, and established in the word of God, with full assurance of the Holy Ghost, they went forth proclaiming that the kingdom of God was at hand. And thus preaching through countries and cities, they appointed the first-fruits of their labours, having first proved them by the Spirit, to be bishops and deacons of those who should afterwards believe."
Significance: Clement describes a divine chain of authority: God → Christ → Apostles → bishops and deacons. This three-link chain, explicitly stated within a decade of the Apostle John's death, is the earliest non-biblical formulation of apostolic succession. The bishops are appointed by the Apostles as "first-fruits of their labours" — the successors of the apostolic mission, not merely administrators of apostolic communities.
St. Clement of Rome (c. AD 35–99)
1 Clement, Chapter 44 — c. AD 96
"Our apostles also knew, through our Lord Jesus Christ, that there would be strife on account of the office of the episcopate. For this reason, therefore, inasmuch as they had obtained a perfect fore-knowledge of this, they appointed those already mentioned, and afterwards gave instructions, that when these should fall asleep, other approved men should succeed them in their ministry… Those, therefore, who were appointed by them, or afterwards by other eminent men, with the consent of the whole Church, and who have blamelessly served the flock of Christ in a humble, peaceable, and disinterested spirit… we consider to be unjustly thrust out from their ministrations."
Significance: This passage is the most explicit description of apostolic succession in the entire sub-apostolic literature. Clement states: (1) the Apostles foreknew the problem of episcopal succession; (2) they therefore established procedures for it; (3) the procedure is the appointment of "approved men" to succeed them; (4) subsequent appointments by "other eminent men with the consent of the whole Church" continue this succession; (5) removing these legitimately appointed bishops is a grave sin that 1 Clement exists to address. This is apostolic succession fully described, not merely implied.

IX. Ignatius of Antioch — The Martyr Bishop

▼

St. Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–107) wrote seven letters while being transported to Rome for martyrdom — letters addressed to six churches and to Polycarp of Smyrna. These letters are among the most important documents in Church history, and almost every one is saturated with the theology of the bishop as the center of the Church's sacramental and doctrinal life.

St. Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–107)
Letter to the Smyrnaeans, Chapter 8 — c. AD 107
"See that you all follow the bishop, even as Jesus Christ does the Father, and the presbytery as you would the apostles; and reverence the deacons, as being the institution of God. Let no man do anything connected with the Church without the bishop. Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is either under the bishop's presidency or the person to whom he shall entrust it. Wherever the bishop appears, there let the multitude also be; just as, wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church. It is not lawful to baptize or give communion without the consent of the bishop; but whatever he shall approve of, that is also pleasing to God."
Significance: Ignatius makes the bishop the indispensable condition of valid sacramental life: no valid Eucharist, no valid Baptism without the bishop. The bishop's relationship to the Church mirrors Christ's relationship to the Father — not an organizational preference but a theological necessity. "Wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church" is echoed by "wherever the bishop is, there let the people be" — the bishop is the local presence of Christ's apostolic authority.
St. Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–107)
Letter to the Ephesians, Chapter 4–5 — c. AD 107
"It is fitting, O Ephesians, that you should be in perfect harmony with the mind of the bishop, as indeed you are. Your admirable presbytery, worthy of God, is fitted as exactly to the bishop as the strings are to the harp. Therefore in your concord and harmonious love, Jesus Christ is sung. And do ye, man by man, become a choir, that being harmonious in love, and taking up the song of God in unison, ye may with one voice sing to the Father through Jesus Christ, so that He may both hear you, and perceive by your works that ye are indeed the members of His Son."
Significance: Ignatius' famous "harp" metaphor describes the Church as a musical instrument, with the bishop, presbyters, and deacons constituting the structure and the faithful as the strings — the whole instrument making music to God only when properly tuned and in harmony. The threefold ministry is not an extrinsic organizational layer but the intrinsic structure of the Church's worship, without which authentic praise to the Father cannot be rendered.
St. Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–107)
Letter to the Trallians, Chapter 3 — c. AD 107
"In like manner, let all reverence the deacons as an appointment of Jesus Christ, and the bishop as Jesus Christ, who is the Son of the Father, and the presbyters as the sanhedrim of God, and assembly of the apostles. Apart from these, there is no Church."
Significance: "Apart from these, there is no Church." This is Ignatius' most definitive statement on the necessity of the threefold ordained ministry for the existence of the Church. The bishop is identified with Christ; the presbyters with the apostolic assembly; the deacons with the appointment of Jesus Christ. Without these three orders in apostolic succession, the community in question — however devout — is not the Church in the full sense.

X. Irenaeus of Lyons — The Great Defender

▼

St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202) wrote his monumental Against Heresies (Adversus Haereses) to refute the various Gnostic sects that claimed a secret, superior apostolic tradition beyond what the Church publicly taught. His primary argument is apostolic succession: the Gnostics cannot produce a verifiable chain of teachers back to the Apostles, while the Catholic Church can — and Irenaeus provides the lists to prove it.

St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202)
Against Heresies, Book III, Chapter 3 — c. AD 180
"It is within the power of all, therefore, in every Church, who may wish to see the truth, to contemplate clearly the tradition of the apostles manifested throughout the whole world; and we are in a position to reckon up those who were by the apostles instituted bishops in the Churches, and to demonstrate the succession of these men to our own times; those who neither taught nor knew of anything like what these heretics rave about. For if the apostles had known hidden mysteries, which they were in the habit of imparting to 'the perfect' apart and privily from the rest, they would have delivered them especially to those to whom they were also committing the Churches themselves."
Significance: Irenaeus establishes apostolic succession as the primary epistemological criterion for authentic Christianity: truth can be identified by tracing teaching back through the succession of bishops to the Apostles themselves. No hidden, secret tradition can be valid — because the Apostles would have entrusted secret doctrine to the same bishops they entrusted with the churches. If the bishop has no such secret doctrine, neither did the Apostles. This is a decisive argument against every form of esoteric or gnostic Christianity.
St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202)
Against Heresies, Book III, Chapter 3 — c. AD 180
"The blessed apostles, then, having founded and built up the Church, committed into the hands of Linus the office of the episcopate. Of this Linus, Paul makes mention in the Epistles to Timothy. To him succeeded Anacletus; and after him, in the third place from the apostles, Anicetus received the episcopate, of whom Paul also makes mention. Him Soter followed, and after him Eleutherius does now, in the twelfth place from the apostles, hold the inheritance of the episcopate. In this order, and by this succession, the ecclesiastical tradition from the apostles, and the preaching of the truth, have come down to us."
Significance: Irenaeus provides an explicit succession list for the Roman see — from the Apostles Peter and Paul through Linus, Anacletus, Clement, Evaristus, Alexander, Sixtus, Telephorus, Hyginus, Pius, Anicetus, Soter, to the then-current bishop Eleutherius. This is a verifiable historical list, not a mythological claim. Irenaeus provides similar lists for other sees. The succession list is his evidence for authentic Christianity — and it remains the model for all Oriental Orthodox episcopates today.
St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202)
Against Heresies, Book IV, Chapter 26 — c. AD 180
"Wherefore it is incumbent to obey the presbyters who are in the Church — those who, as I have shown, possess the succession from the apostles; those who, together with the succession of the episcopate, have received the certain gift of truth, according to the good pleasure of the Father. But it is also incumbent to hold in suspicion others who depart from the primitive succession, and assemble themselves together in any place whatsoever."
Significance: Irenaeus insists on a double criterion: (1) succession of the episcopate (the unbroken chain of ordination), and (2) the "certain gift of truth" (the apostolic doctrine). Both must be present. A bishop with apostolic succession who teaches heresy, and a teacher with pure doctrine but no apostolic succession — both are suspect. This balanced position is exactly the Oriental Orthodox doctrine: succession of office AND succession of faith.

These succession lists, preserved in ancient chronicles, episcopal catalogs, and church histories, are the historical evidence for the doctrine.

XII. Validity of Orders — The Donatist Question

▼

The most important theological controversy concerning apostolic succession in church history is the Donatist schism (4th–5th centuries). The Donatists — a rigorist North African movement — argued that sacraments performed by traditores (clergy who had handed over the Scriptures during the Diocletianic persecution) were invalid. This position had radical implications for apostolic succession: if a sinful bishop's ordinations were invalid, then entire chains of succession might be broken and all subsequent ordinations void.

The universal Church, through the theological genius of St. Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430), decisively rejected the Donatist position — and the Oriental Orthodox tradition stands fully with Augustine's response.

St. Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430)
On Baptism, Against the Donatists, Book I — c. AD 400
"The sacrament is one thing, the merit of the person administering it is another. The sacrament is holy because of its own origin, not because of the person's holiness. It is administered by good and bad alike; and those who receive it faithfully receive it to their salvation, but those who receive it unfaithfully receive it to their judgment. By this rule, therefore, the person of the minister can neither add to nor take from the power of the sacrament." PARAPHRASE
Significance: Augustine's decisive formulation: the validity of a sacrament depends on Christ's institution and the Church's faith, not on the personal holiness of the minister. A sinful bishop who ordains with proper intention, proper form, and within the Church's communion, performs a valid ordination — regardless of his moral state. This principle is foundational for apostolic succession: sin does not break the chain; schism and heresy may, but personal sin cannot.
St. Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430)
Against the Epistle of Parmenianus — c. AD 400
"Christ baptizes; Christ absolves; Christ offers the sacrifice. The minister is the instrument in the hands of Christ. Just as a clean hand and a dirty hand can hold the same tool, both equally capable of the same work — so the holy and the sinful priest both hold the same instrument: the ordained office given by Christ." PARAPHRASE
Significance: Augustine's "instrument" theology of ordained ministry: the priest is the instrument; Christ is the agent. This completely resolves the Donatist problem — the instrument's cleanliness does not determine the efficacy of the work. An unworthy bishop's ordinations are valid; his personal sin is between him and God. The apostolic chain is not broken by moral failure, only by formal schism from the Church.

The Three Requirements for Valid Ordination

The Oriental Orthodox tradition, drawing on Augustinian theology and the universal patristic consensus, identifies three necessary conditions for a valid ordination:

1. Valid Ordainer
OO
The ordaining bishop must himself be validly ordained — standing within the unbroken apostolic succession.
This is the "chain" requirement: ordination can only convey what it has received. A bishop ordained by a bishop who was ordained by validly ordained bishops transmits genuine apostolic succession. A bishop ordained by a congregation vote, by a council without episcopal hand-laying, or by someone outside the apostolic succession, cannot transmit what he does not possess.
2. Proper Intention
OO
The ordainer must intend to do what the Church does — to ordain a minister of Christ's Church according to apostolic order.
This does not require perfect theological precision. It requires the general intention to perform the sacramental act of the Church, not merely a civil ceremony or a theatrical performance. A bishop who ordains with the conscious intention of conferring the grace of the apostolic ministry fulfills this requirement, regardless of his personal theological sophistication.
3. Proper Form
OO
The ordination must be performed with the essential ritual act: the laying on of hands with prayer for the grace of the specific order being conferred.
This is the apostolic form transmitted from the New Testament (Acts 6:6; 1 Tim 4:14; 2 Tim 1:6): hand-laying plus prayer. A "consecration" performed without hand-laying, or without invoking the Holy Spirit for the grace of the episcopal/presbyteral/diaconal office, lacks the essential form and is not a valid ordination regardless of the ordainer's status.

XIII. Protestant Objections — Comprehensive Responses

▼

Objection 1: The New Testament knows nothing of apostolic succession — it was a later invention

▼

The Claim

The concept of an unbroken chain of bishops tracing back to the Apostles is a 2nd-century Catholic development, not a New Testament teaching. The early churches were pneumatic communities led by charismatic gifts, not hierarchical institutions with successive offices.

Response

This claim cannot be reconciled with the texts. The New Testament itself contains: (1) the replacement of Judas by Matthias, with Peter citing Psalm 109:8 as prophecy that the apostolic office must be filled (Acts 1:20–26); (2) Paul ordaining elders in every city (Acts 14:23; Tit 1:5); (3) Paul transmitting a spiritual gift through hand-laying to Timothy (2 Tim 1:6; 1 Tim 4:14); (4) the explicit four-generation chain of 2 Timothy 2:2; (5) warnings against premature ordination (1 Tim 5:22); (6) detailed qualification lists for bishops and deacons (1 Tim 3; Tit 1). Furthermore, the "charismatic community without hierarchy" model is a modern Romantic construction with no historical support. By AD 96 (1 Clement) and AD 107 (Ignatius), the episcopal succession is already universal, established, and defended — within the lifetimes of apostolic eyewitnesses. Either the Apostles themselves established it, or it somehow emerged universally within 30–50 years of their deaths. The former is overwhelmingly more plausible.

Objection 2: Succession is about true doctrine, not physical hand-laying — the Reformers have the true apostolic teaching

▼

The Claim

What matters is continuity of apostolic doctrine (Scripture), not physical continuity of ordination. The Reformers recovered the true gospel after medieval corruption — they are therefore the true successors of the Apostles in the only sense that matters.

Response

The Oriental Orthodox fully agree that doctrinal continuity is essential to apostolic succession — Irenaeus himself insisted on both succession of office AND succession of doctrine (AH IV.26). But this does not eliminate the necessity of the apostolic office; it adds a second requirement. The problem with the "doctrine only" position is: which doctrine? Luther, Zwingli, Calvin, and the Anabaptists all claimed to recover the true apostolic gospel — and immediately contradicted each other on the Eucharist, Baptism, the church, predestination, and much else. Without the episcopate as a living voice of tradition, every individual's reading of Scripture becomes potentially apostolic — leading to the 30,000+ Protestant denominations currently in existence. This is not the apostolic pattern: the Apostles appointed specific persons to guard and transmit the deposit of faith, not a written text available to everyone's private interpretation.

Objection 3: The Oriental Orthodox succession is historically uncertain — we cannot verify the chain

▼

The Claim

Even if apostolic succession were theologically necessary, the historical records are too fragmentary to verify the actual chains of ordination. Gaps, forgeries, and uncertainties in the early episcopal lists make the claim to unbroken succession historically dubious.

Response

The degree of skepticism demanded here — certainty about every episcopal succession in the first three centuries — is applied to no other historical claim in early Christianity. We do not demand such certainty before accepting that the New Testament canon is the authentic apostolic deposit, or that the Nicene Creed represents the true apostolic faith. The succession lists provided by Irenaeus (c. AD 180), Eusebius of Caesarea (c. AD 313), and subsequent church historians are the best historical evidence available — the same quality as the evidence for nearly everything else we accept about early Christianity. Furthermore, the Oriental Orthodox churches do not claim that every link in the chain is perfectly documented; they claim that the chain has never been deliberately broken, schismatically severed, or abandoned. The collective weight of continuous institutional life, unbroken liturgical tradition, and the convergent testimony of multiple independent succession lines makes the denial of apostolic succession the more historically improbable position.

Objection 4: Scripture teaches a "priesthood of all believers" — there is no special priestly caste in the New Testament

▼

The Claim

1 Peter 2:9 ("a royal priesthood") and the book of Hebrews (Christ as the one High Priest) together eliminate any distinct ordained priesthood in the New Testament. The Levitical priesthood was abolished; Christ is now the sole priest; all believers share equally in the royal priesthood.

Response

The Oriental Orthodox fully affirm the royal priesthood of all believers and Christ as the sole eternal High Priest — these are not in dispute. But the argument proves too much. In the Old Testament, the "kingdom of priests" of Exodus 19:6 (the source of 1 Peter 2:9) coexisted with the Levitical and Aaronic priesthood — the two were not mutually exclusive. The New Testament pattern is the same: the universal priesthood of all believers coexists with the specific ordained priesthood. The ordained priest does not replace or diminish Christ's high priesthood but participates in it — acting as Christ's instrument, not as an independent mediator. The Reformers' argument from 1 Peter 2:9 would equally eliminate the specific ministry of preaching, teaching, and apostolic oversight — since "all believers" could preach as well as baptize. But Paul specifically restricts who may oversee a congregation (1 Tim 3) and who may teach with authority (1 Tim 2:12). The ordered ministry is not a violation of the universal priesthood but its ordained expression.

Objection 5: The Oriental Orthodox rejected the Council of Chalcedon — their succession may be impaired by this schism

▼

The Claim

By rejecting the Council of Chalcedon (AD 451) and breaking communion with the majority of Christendom, the Oriental Orthodox separated themselves from the apostolic succession that the broader Church maintained. Their orders may be valid in form but impaired by schism.

Response

The Oriental Orthodox do not accept the characterization that they "rejected Chalcedon" in a schismatic sense — they understand themselves as the faithful guardians of the Alexandrine Christology of Cyril of Alexandria against what they perceived as a capitulation to Nestorianism. The modern theological dialogues between Oriental Orthodox and Eastern Orthodox (Chalcedonian) theologians, culminating in the Agreed Statements of 1989–1990, have concluded that both traditions confess the same faith in Christ using different terminologies. No valid apostolic succession is contingent on accepting a particular council — councils derive their authority from the Church, not the reverse. Furthermore, even if the Chalcedonian churches regarded the break as a schism, the Oriental Orthodox retained every requirement for valid ordination: validly ordained bishops, proper intention, and proper form. The Catholic Church explicitly recognizes Oriental Orthodox orders as valid apostolic succession, and the Eastern Orthodox theologians have consistently acknowledged the same.

XIV. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Q: If apostolic succession is necessary, what happens to sincere Protestant Christians?▼

The Oriental Orthodox position is that sincere, faithful Christians outside the apostolic succession are not automatically excluded from God's mercy and grace. God is not bound by the sacraments he has appointed — he can and does work beyond the visible boundaries of the Church. What the Church teaches is where God has promised to be present in his fullness, not where God has promised to be absent elsewhere. The consistent patristic principle applies: "The Church knows where God is; the Church does not know where God is not." Sincere Protestant Christians who love Christ, read his Word, and live according to his commands are not beyond God's love — but the Oriental Orthodox believe they lack the fullness of the sacramental life that apostolic succession makes possible.

Q: Do Anglican orders count as valid apostolic succession?▼

This is a contested question. Pope Leo XIII declared Anglican orders "absolutely null and utterly void" in his 1896 encyclical Apostolicae Curae, citing defective intention and defective form in the Edwardian Ordinal (1550, revised 1552). Many Eastern Orthodox and some Oriental Orthodox theologians have expressed similar reservations. The main concerns are: (1) the deliberate Reformation of the Anglican ordination rite to remove explicit sacerdotal and sacrificial language — raising questions about intention; (2) the consecrations of Matthew Parker (1559) and subsequent Anglican bishops have been questioned on the basis of historical evidence (the "Nag's Head Fable" controversy). High-church Anglicans and continuing Anglicans dispute these assessments vigorously and point to Old Catholic bishops participating in some later Anglican consecrations. The question remains unresolved in Oriental Orthodox official theology, with different theologians taking different positions.

Q: Can a woman be ordained in apostolic succession?▼

The Oriental Orthodox churches do not ordain women to the episcopate or presbyterate, maintaining the universal tradition of the pre-Reformation Church. This position is grounded in: (1) the consistent practice of the entire pre-Reformation Church (Eastern and Western) for nearly 2000 years; (2) theological reflection on Christ's deliberate choice of twelve male Apostles despite his countercultural treatment of women; (3) the typological significance of the priest as acting in persona Christi — an argument about the symbolic and sacramental character of the ordained ministry, not about the spiritual equality or dignity of women, which is fully affirmed. The office of deaconess (distinct from the ordained female diaconate) has historical precedent and is being explored for revival in some Oriental Orthodox contexts.

Q: What is "concelebration" and how does it relate to apostolic succession?▼

Concelebration is the liturgical practice by which multiple priests (or bishops) celebrate the Eucharist together, each fully participating in the single celebration rather than one celebrating while others merely attend. It expresses the unity of the ordained ministry around a single Eucharistic offering. In terms of apostolic succession, concelebration is an expression of episcopal communion: bishops concelebrate when they are in full communion with each other's apostolic succession and shared faith. The inability to concelebrate (as between Oriental Orthodox and some other Christian bodies) is not primarily a liturgical preference but a theological statement about the boundaries of full apostolic communion.

Q: What is the difference between "valid" and "licit" ordination?▼

A "valid" ordination is one that actually confers the apostolic grace — the bishop ordained is genuinely a bishop in the apostolic succession, regardless of whether the ordination was properly authorized. A "licit" (lawful) ordination is one that is both valid and conducted with the proper ecclesiastical authorization. An ordination can be valid but illicit: for example, if a bishop consecrates another bishop without the required co-consecrators or without the permission of the relevant patriarch, the new bishop is genuinely ordained (valid) but the consecration was irregular and unauthorized (illicit). This distinction matters for apostolic succession: schismatic groups that retain valid apostolic succession (e.g., some Old Catholic bodies, certain Eastern Catholic churches before reunion) have valid but licit questions hanging over their ministry until full communion is restored.

Q: How does apostolic succession relate to the authority to interpret Scripture?▼

In the Oriental Orthodox tradition, the authorized interpretation of Scripture belongs to the Church — specifically to the bishops in council, as the successors of the Apostles who were commissioned to "teach all nations" everything Christ commanded (Matt 28:20). The Ecumenical Councils — composed of bishops in apostolic succession — are the primary organs of authoritative interpretation. This does not mean Scripture is unclear or that the laity cannot read it; it means that contested interpretations are resolved by the bishops in council, whose authority traces back to the Apostles whom Christ promised to guide into "all truth" by the Spirit (John 16:13). The individual bishop's interpretation is not infallible; the college of bishops in council, guided by the Spirit, speaks with the authority of the apostolic tradition.

Q: Why do the Oriental Orthodox require three bishops to consecrate a new bishop?▼

The requirement of at least two or three bishops for episcopal consecration (the canonical minimum varies by tradition) reflects several principles: (1) the collegial character of the episcopate — no bishop stands alone; each bishop is ordained into the fellowship of the apostolic college, not merely into a personal office; (2) the apostolic precedent — the Apostles acted collectively (Acts 1:15–26; 15:1–29); (3) the safeguard against unilateral or schismatic consecrations — requiring multiple bishops makes it harder for a single schismatic or heretical bishop to perpetuate a separate line of succession. Canon 4 of the Council of Nicaea (AD 325) specifies that a bishop should be consecrated by all the bishops in the province, with a minimum of three — confirming that this collegial requirement is very ancient and ecumenically established.

Q: What is the relationship between apostolic succession and the canon of Scripture?▼

This is one of the most important apologetic points in the apostolic succession debate. The 27-book New Testament canon was not determined by the New Testament itself but by the bishops of the Church — in apostolic succession — meeting in councils and reaching consensus over the 3rd–5th centuries (culminating in the councils of Hippo, AD 393, and Carthage, AD 397). If the same bishops and councils whose authority Protestants reject as non-binding also determined the canon of Scripture that Protestants accept as supremely authoritative, there is an irresolvable inconsistency. You cannot accept the canon on the Church's authority and then reject the Church's tradition on the grounds that it has no authority beyond Scripture. The apostolic tradition and the apostolic canon are inseparable products of the same apostolic succession.

Q: Has apostolic succession been broken at any point in history by persecution?▼

The Oriental Orthodox churches have endured catastrophic persecutions — the Coptic Church under Roman imperial persecution, the Syriac and Armenian churches under Persian, Arab, Mongol, and Ottoman violence, the Ethiopian church under various invasions. In every case, the Church has maintained its episcopal succession, often at great cost. During the worst periods, bishops who were martyred or exiled were replaced by new consecrations — sometimes in secret, sometimes in diaspora. The continuity of the apostolic chain was maintained even when the visible institutional church was nearly destroyed. This resilience is itself testimony to the theological seriousness with which apostolic succession was regarded: bishops went to extraordinary lengths to ensure that the chain was never broken, even when individual links were threatened.

Q: Is apostolic succession the same thing as papal infallibility?▼

No — these are entirely distinct doctrines. Apostolic succession is the transmission of the ordained ministry from bishop to bishop through the laying on of hands, shared by all Orthodox, Catholic, and some Anglican/Lutheran traditions. Papal infallibility is the specific Roman Catholic doctrine (defined at Vatican I, AD 1870) that when the Bishop of Rome speaks ex cathedra on matters of faith and morals, he speaks with the charism of infallibility. The Oriental Orthodox churches fully reject papal infallibility and the jurisdictional primacy of the Roman see. All bishops are equal in apostolic succession — none has jurisdiction over the others outside collegial agreement. The Patriarch of Alexandria, the Catholicos-Patriarch of the Syriac Orthodox Church, and the Catholicos of All Armenians all have identical apostolic succession; no one of them has universal jurisdiction over the others.
