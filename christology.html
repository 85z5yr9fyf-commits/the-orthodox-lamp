Miaphysitism & Dyophysitism

A Patristic Examination of the Nature of Christ

✦ ✦ ✦

The most fundamental question in Christian theology — after the existence of God — is the question of who Jesus Christ is. The answer to this question divides not just Christianity from other religions, but the Oriental Orthodox churches from the Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic traditions to this day.

The debate turns on the precise theological language used to describe the relationship between Christ's divinity and humanity. Is Christ one nature (mia physis) or two natures (dyo physeis)? The answer one gives determines whether one is, in technical terms, a Miaphysite or a Dyophysite.

This examination traces the historical development of Christological doctrine through the great ecumenical councils, presents the primary theological positions and their defenders, examines the key scriptural evidence, and explains why the Oriental Orthodox churches — Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara, and Eritrean — rejected the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE) and have maintained their position for over fifteen centuries.

"Who do people say the Son of Man is?… But what about you? Who do you say I am?" — Matthew 16:13,15
Physis
φύσις — Greek: "nature"
The essential nature or being of something. The central disputed term: does Christ have one physis or two?
Hypostasis
ὑπόστασις — Greek: "subsistence, person"
Individual existence or personal subsistence. Chalcedon uses this to describe Christ's one personal identity despite two natures.
Ousia
οὐσία — Greek: "essence, substance"
The essential being or substance. The Trinity shares one ousia; the Persons are three hypostaseis.
Miaphysitism
μία φύσις — "one nature"
The OO position: Christ has one united nature — divine and human — united without mixture, confusion, separation, or division.
Dyophysitism
δύο φύσεις — "two natures"
The Chalcedonian position: Christ has two complete natures — divine and human — in one Person, without confusion or change, without division or separation.
Theotokos
Θεοτόκος — "God-bearer"
Title for Mary as "Mother of God." Affirmed at Ephesus (431) against Nestorius, who preferred Christotokos ("Christ-bearer").
Nestorius
Archbishop of Constantinople (d. c. 451)
Antiochene theologian whose apparent teaching of two separate persons in Christ was condemned at Ephesus (431).
Eutyches
Archimandrite of Constantinople (d. c. 456)
Archimandrite in Constantinople, of extreme Alexandrian sympathies, who taught that after the incarnation Christ's humanity was absorbed into divinity — true monophysitism. Condemned at Chalcedon.
Monophysitism
μόνη φύσις — "sole nature"
The heresy that Christ had only one nature, the divine, absorbing or replacing the human. Condemned at Chalcedon. OO churches explicitly reject this.
Perichoresis
περιχώρησις — "mutual indwelling"
The mutual interpenetration of the divine and human in Christ — a patristic concept (developed especially by later fathers such as John of Damascus) describing a reality both traditions affirm: union without confusion.

The Alexandrian tradition in Christology, rooted in the theology of Athanasius and Cyril of Alexandria, emphasizes the unity of Christ's person. The starting point is the eternal Logos who took flesh: the subject of the incarnation is always and only the divine Son.

Key features of the Alexandrian approach:

  • Logos-sarx (Word-flesh) framework: The divine Logos is directly united to human flesh (sarx), forming one subject.
  • Emphasis on unity: The union is so intimate that what is said of the human can be attributed to the divine and vice versa (communicatio idiomatum).
  • Theotokos: Because the Logos is the subject of the incarnation, Mary rightly bears the title "Mother of God."
  • Cyril's formula: mia physis tou Theou Logou sesarkōmenē — "one nature of God the Logos incarnate" — preserves Christ's unity while affirming his full humanity.
Cyril of Alexandria
Second Letter to Nestorius (430 CE)
"We do not say that the nature of the Word was changed and became flesh, or that it was converted into a whole man consisting of soul and body; but rather that the Word, having personally united to himself flesh animated by a rational soul, did in an ineffable and inconceivable manner become man."

The Antiochene tradition, associated with Theodore of Mopsuestia and later Nestorius, emphasizes the completeness and integrity of Christ's humanity. The concern is that Alexandrian language threatens to make Christ's humanity unreal or absorbed.

Key features of the Antiochene approach:

  • Logos-anthropos (Word-human) framework: The Logos assumes a complete human being, not merely a body.
  • Emphasis on distinction: The divine and human must be kept distinct to preserve real human development, temptation, and suffering in Christ.
  • Prosopon of union: Antiochenes sometimes speak of one "prosopon" (face/appearance) of union — a unity of personal presentation rather than of nature.
  • Concern about theopaschism: God cannot suffer — so the Logos, strictly speaking, did not die. It was the human nature that died.
Theodore of Mopsuestia
On the Incarnation (c. 390 CE)
"The natures must be distinguished — the divine impassible and eternal, the human capable of growth and suffering. It is the man in whom the Logos dwells who grew in wisdom, who was tempted, who wept at the tomb of Lazarus. We must not confuse what belongs to each nature."
DyophysitePARAPHRASE

The First Ecumenical Council was convened by Emperor Constantine to address the Arian controversy — not the nature of Christ's two natures, but the relationship of the Son to the Father. Arius taught that the Son was the first and greatest creature, subordinate to the Father: "there was a time when he was not."

Nicaea condemned Arianism and affirmed that the Son is homoousios (of the same substance) with the Father. This established the foundation on which all subsequent Christology builds: if the Son is truly divine, the question of how this divine Son became human takes on its full weight.

318 CE
Arius begins teaching subordinationism in Alexandria
325 CE
Council of Nicaea: 318 bishops; condemns Arianism; affirms homoousios
381 CE
Council of Constantinople I: Expands Nicene Creed; affirms full divinity of Holy Spirit

Both Miaphysites and Chalcedonians fully affirm Nicaea. The Nicene Creed — in its expanded 381 form — is the shared foundation of all Orthodox, Catholic, and Oriental Orthodox Christianity.

The Third Ecumenical Council was called primarily to address the teaching of Nestorius, Archbishop of Constantinople. Nestorius opposed the title Theotokos ("God-bearer") for Mary, preferring Christotokos ("Christ-bearer"). His reasoning: Mary bore the man Christ; she did not bear the divine nature.

Cyril of Alexandria, leading the opposition, argued that Nestorius effectively divided Christ into two persons — a divine Logos and a human Jesus — which destroyed the unity of Christ and the soteriological claim that God himself, in the person of the Son, took on human nature to redeem it.

428 CE
Nestorius becomes Archbishop of Constantinople; preaches against Theotokos
429–430 CE
Cyril writes his letters to Nestorius; attaches Twelve Anathemas
431 CE
Council of Ephesus: Nestorius condemned; Theotokos affirmed

Ephesus is accepted by all Orthodox, Catholic, and Oriental Orthodox churches. It is the Council of Chalcedon (451), not Ephesus, that divides them. The OO churches affirm Theotokos fully and condemn Nestorius.

The Fourth Ecumenical Council — accepted by the Eastern Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and most Protestant traditions, but rejected by the Oriental Orthodox — produced the famous "Definition of Chalcedon," which declared that Christ is one Person in two natures:

"We teach… one and the same Christ, Son, Lord, Only-begotten, acknowledged in two natures which undergo no confusion, no change, no division, no separation; at no point was the difference between the natures taken away through the union, but rather the property of both natures is preserved and comes together into a single person and a single subsistent being; he is not parted or divided into two persons, but is one and the same only-begotten Son, God, Word, Lord Jesus Christ."
449 CE
"Robber Synod" of Ephesus: Dioscorus of Alexandria rehabilitates Eutyches; Flavian of Constantinople deposed and beaten (dies from injuries)
450 CE
Emperor Theodosius II dies; new Emperor Marcian and Empress Pulcheria call a new council
451 CE
Council of Chalcedon: ~520 bishops; "two natures" definition adopted; Dioscorus of Alexandria exiled
451–500 CE
Alexandrian, Syriac, Armenian, and Ethiopian churches reject Chalcedon; schism begins

The Oriental Orthodox churches did not reject Chalcedon out of ignorance or political convenience. Their objections were substantive and theological.

1. The Formula Was Novel

Cyril's formula — mia physis tou Theou Logou sesarkōmenē — had been the language of Alexandrian Orthodoxy for decades. The Chalcedonian "two natures" formula introduced new language that Cyril had specifically guarded against. OO theologians argued this amounted to a Nestorian rehabilitation.

2. Dioscorus Was Unjustly Condemned

Dioscorus of Alexandria, the leading OO figure at Chalcedon, was condemned not on doctrinal grounds but on procedural and political ones. The OO churches hold he was an orthodox bishop exiled for opposing imperial policy, not heresy.

3. The Political Dimension

The Council of Chalcedon was called by the Emperor and was deeply political. Canon 28 elevated Constantinople to second place after Rome — a purely jurisdictional decree that Alexandrian and other Eastern churches resented as an imperial power grab.

4. "Two Natures" Opens the Door to Nestorianism

OO theologians argued that saying Christ has "two natures" after the union risks implying two subjects, two agents, or two Christs — exactly the error Cyril had spent his life fighting. The language of "one nature" preserves the Christological unity that is the foundation of soteriology: it is God the Son who died for us, not a human associate of God.

Cyril appended Twelve Anathemas to his Third Letter to Nestorius (430 CE). These define the boundaries of Alexandrian orthodoxy and remain authoritative for the OO churches. Selected anathemas:

Anathema I
"If anyone does not confess that Emmanuel is truly God, and therefore the Holy Virgin is the Mother of God (Theotokos), since she begot according to the flesh the Word of God made flesh — let him be anathema."
Anathema II
"If anyone does not confess that the Word of God the Father was hypostatically united to flesh and is one Christ with his own flesh — the same both God and man — let him be anathema."
Anathema IV
"If anyone distributes between two persons or hypostases the expressions used either in the gospels or the apostolic letters, whether spoken of Christ by the saints or by him about himself — let him be anathema."
Anathema VIII
"If anyone dares to say that the man who was assumed ought to be worshipped together with God the Word, and glorified together with him, and called God as one with another — and does not rather honor Emmanuel with a single veneration and ascribe to him a single doxology, as the Word made flesh — let him be anathema."
Anathema XII
"If anyone does not confess that the Word of God suffered in the flesh and was crucified in the flesh and tasted death in the flesh, and that he is the first-born from the dead as God being life and life-giving — let him be anathema."

The Christological debate is not merely philosophical — it is exegetical. Both sides appeal to the same Scriptures with differing interpretive emphases.

John 1:14
"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth."
🔵 Miaphysite reading: The Logos himself became flesh — one subject, one nature after the union. The incarnation is a real transformation of mode of existence, not a mere association.
🟣 Dyophysite reading: "Became flesh" means took on a human nature. The Logos remains fully divine; the incarnation adds a full human nature. "Became" describes assumption, not transformation.
Philippians 2:6–8
"…who, though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men."
🔵 Miaphysite reading: The self-emptying (kenosis) describes the one Christ taking on the mode of human existence. The subject throughout is the one incarnate Word.
🟣 Dyophysite reading: "Emptied himself" refers to the voluntary limitation of the divine nature's prerogatives as exercised through the human nature. Two natures are required to explain the paradox.
Luke 2:52
"And Jesus increased in wisdom and in stature and in favor with God and man."
🔵 Miaphysite reading: The one Christ genuinely grew in human wisdom — the human dimension is real and complete. Growth belongs to the incarnate mode of the one nature.
🟣 Dyophysite reading: This growth belongs to the human nature alone. The divine nature, being perfect, does not grow. The two-nature framework explains how Christ can grow in one dimension and be omniscient in another.
Hebrews 4:15
"For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin."
🔵 Miaphysite reading: The one incarnate Word truly experienced temptation in the flesh — the human reality is fully preserved within the united nature.
🟣 Dyophysite reading: Temptation belongs specifically to the human nature. A two-nature formula explains how Christ can be genuinely tempted (human) and yet without sin (divine-human synergy).
Both traditions affirm full humanity; the dispute is in the framework of description

Oriental Orthodox (Miaphysitism)

One united nature — divine and human. United without mixture, confusion, separation, or division. Follows Cyril's formula. Rejects Chalcedon. Accepts Nicaea, Constantinople I, Ephesus.

Chalcedonian (Dyophysitism)

Two natures — divine and human — in one Person (hypostasis). No confusion, change, division, or separation. Follows Leo's Tome and the Chalcedonian Definition. Accepts all four councils.

Nestorianism (Condemned)

Two natures and effectively two persons united by moral harmony. Rejects Theotokos. Condemned at Ephesus (431). Survives in the Assyrian Church of the East (which disputes the "Nestorian" label).

Eutychianism / Monophysitism (Condemned)

One nature after the union — the divine absorbs and replaces the human. Christ is not consubstantial with us. Condemned at Chalcedon (451). OO churches explicitly reject this position.

Note: The Oriental Orthodox emphatically distinguish Miaphysitism from Monophysitism. Miaphysitism affirms both natures fully; only the word used to describe their union differs from Chalcedonian language.

Nestorius became Archbishop of Constantinople in 428 CE and almost immediately sparked controversy by opposing the title Theotokos for Mary. His reasoning: Mary was a human woman; she gave birth to a human being. If we say she gave birth to God, we seem to imply that God has a mother, that divinity was born in time, or that Mary predates God — all absurdities.

Cyril's response was decisive: the question is not whether Mary gave birth to the divine nature (she did not — the divine nature is eternal and uncreated), but whether the person born of Mary was God the Son. Because the subject of the incarnation is the eternal Logos, and the Logos is God, Mary rightly bears the title "God-bearer."

"If anyone does not acknowledge that Emmanuel is very God, and that on this account the Holy Virgin is the Mother of God, inasmuch as in the flesh she bore the Word of God made flesh: let him be anathema." — Cyril, Anathema I

The OO position holds firmly to Theotokos. In Coptic liturgy, Mary is called "Theotokos" dozens of times in each Divine Liturgy. The Ethiopic tradition uses Weladite Amlak ("Bearer of God") — a direct Ge'ez rendering of Theotokos.

Eutyches (c. 378–456) was an Archimandrite in Constantinople who, in opposing Nestorianism, went to the opposite extreme. He taught that before the incarnation there were two natures, but after the union there was only one — the divine nature, which absorbed the human. The result was that Christ was not consubstantial with us in his humanity.

Eutyches (reported position)
As recorded at the Synod of Constantinople (448 CE)
"I confess that our Lord was of two natures before the union, but after the union I confess one nature only… I follow the teaching of the holy fathers and especially Blessed Cyril."
MonophysitePARAPHRASE

This is precisely the position the Oriental Orthodox churches reject. Eutyches appealed to Cyril, but Cyril never denied Christ's humanity or consubstantiality with us. The OO formula — one united nature, divine and human — insists that both the divinity and humanity are real, complete, and preserved in the union. This is why "Miaphysitism" is the technically correct term for the OO position, not "Monophysitism."

One of the most persistent mischaracterizations of the Oriental Orthodox churches is the label "Monophysite" — as if the OO tradition teaches that Christ has only one nature, the divine, and his humanity was merely apparent. This is historically and theologically inaccurate.

The OO position, as articulated by Dioscorus, Severus of Antioch, and all subsequent OO theology, teaches:

  • Christ's humanity is real, complete, and consubstantial with ours
  • Christ's divinity is real, complete, and consubstantial with the Father
  • These two are united without mixture, confusion, change, separation, or division
  • The result is one Christ, one Person, one nature — the one nature of God the Logos incarnate

The dispute with Chalcedon is not over whether Christ is fully divine and fully human — both sides affirm this. The dispute is over whether the word "nature" (physis) in the formula "two natures" adequately preserves the unity of Christ or risks implying two subjects.

Severus of Antioch
Letter to Sergius the Grammarian (c. 515 CE)
"We do not dissolve the union by speaking of the properties of divinity and humanity: the fire and the iron that is made red-hot are one — you can speak of the burning and the hardness as distinct, but there is only one red-hot iron. So in Christ: the properties of each nature are preserved, but there is one Christ, the incarnate Logos."

The rejection of Chalcedon by the OO churches created one of the oldest enduring schisms in Christian history — predating by six centuries the 1054 Great Schism between Rome and Constantinople.

The Oriental Orthodox communion today comprises:

  • Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria — Egypt, ~15–20 million faithful
  • Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church — ~40–50 million faithful, the world's largest OO body
  • Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church — became autocephalous in 1994
  • Syriac Orthodox Church of Antioch — Syria, Lebanon, India diaspora
  • Armenian Apostolic Church — Armenia and global diaspora
  • Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church — Kerala, India (~2–2.5 million)

All six churches are in full communion with one another and share the same Christological confession.

One of the most significant developments in twentieth-century ecumenism has been the formal dialogue between the Oriental Orthodox and Eastern Orthodox (Chalcedonian) traditions. These dialogues have produced remarkable agreements that suggest the 1,500-year-old schism may rest more on linguistic differences than substantive doctrinal disagreement.

The OO tradition insists that Christ's humanity is real, complete, and preserved in the union. The word "mia" (one) in Miaphysitism does not describe the elimination of one nature but the real, inseparable union of both. Cyril himself wrote that "two natures come together with one another, without confusion or change, in an indivisible union" (First Letter to Succensus). To call the OO tradition "Monophysite" is as inaccurate as calling Chalcedonians Nestorian.

Christ's hunger, weariness, temptation, suffering, and death are all real because his humanity is real. The OO position does not require a two-nature framework to preserve these realities — it preserves them within the one nature of the incarnate Son.

Both traditions are Orthodox in the sense of holding apostolic faith and order, but they have been in formal schism since 451 CE over Christological terminology.

The rejection was immediate and never reversed. Dioscorus of Alexandria refused to sign the Chalcedonian Definition at the council itself in 451 CE. The Coptic church, the Syriac church (under Severus of Antioch), and the Armenian church rejected Chalcedon within decades of the council. The Ethiopian church rejected it through its dependence on the Coptic Patriarchate. None of these churches have formally accepted Chalcedon at any point in their history.

This is a serious question. The 1989–1990 Agreed Statements suggest that both traditions actually confess the same Christological faith and that the schism rests primarily on terminological differences. Many theologians on both sides accept this conclusion. However, some Chalcedonians argue that the terminological difference carries a real theological weight: the two-nature formula provides a more precise framework for understanding how Christ can be both omniscient and ignorant, both impassible and suffering, both eternal and born in time. The OO response is that these paradoxes are addressed within the one-nature framework by the same logic — the difference is in how the framework is constructed, not in what it is meant to protect.

Christology is the foundation of soteriology — the doctrine of salvation. The OO tradition holds that unless it is truly God the Son who took on human flesh, suffered, died, and rose again, the redemption of humanity is not accomplished. A Christ who is a mere human associated with God (Nestorianism) cannot deify human nature. A Christ whose humanity is illusory or absorbed (Monophysitism/Docetism) did not truly enter and transform the human condition. The OO formula — one united nature, divine and human — is designed precisely to protect these soteriological realities: it is God who died for us, and he truly died as one of us.
