THE ISLAMIC DILEMMA

A Biblical and Historical Examination of the Qur'an's View of the Previous Scriptures

"He has sent down upon you the Book in truth, confirming what was before it." — Qur'an 3:3

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

This site examines one of the most significant challenges in Muslim-Christian dialogue: the Islamic Dilemma regarding the previous scriptures. This is not a polemical attack on Islam or Muslims, but a sincere examination of a logical tension within Islamic teaching that demands honest engagement.

The Qur'an repeatedly and emphatically affirms that the Torah (Tawrat), Psalms (Zabur), and Gospel (Injil) given to Moses, David, and Jesus respectively are divine revelation from God. The Qur'an instructs Christians and Jews to judge by their scriptures and affirms that its own message confirms these earlier revelations.

Yet the Qur'an also contradicts the Bible on major theological and historical points — the nature of Christ (denying his divinity and crucifixion), the Trinity, and the character of key biblical figures. If the Bible is genuinely from God, as the Qur'an affirms, then the Qur'an's contradictions of it are a serious problem. If the Bible has been corrupted and is no longer reliable, then the Qur'an's affirmations of it are empty. This is the Islamic Dilemma.

Note: This site presents the Christian perspective on a contested theological question. It is intended for thoughtful readers engaged in apologetics and interfaith dialogue. All Qur'anic citations are from Sahih International unless otherwise noted.

II. The Dilemma Defined

▼

The Islamic Dilemma can be stated precisely as a logical dilemma with two horns:

Horn 1 — If the Bible is authentic: The Qur'an repeatedly commands people to believe the Torah and Gospel and to judge by them. If these scriptures are genuine and reliable, then Christians and Jews should follow them — and they teach the divinity of Christ, the Trinity (broadly understood), the atoning death of Jesus, and the resurrection. These teachings directly contradict Islamic doctrine. Therefore, if the Bible is authentic, Islam's core theological claims are refuted by the very scriptures the Qur'an endorses.

Horn 2 — If the Bible has been corrupted: If Christians and Jews corrupted their scriptures, then the Qur'an's repeated commands to "judge by the Torah" and "judge by the Gospel" are commands to follow corrupted documents. Furthermore, Muhammad's early followers are told that if they doubt what is revealed to Muhammad, they should ask "those who have been reading the Scripture before you" (Q. 10:94) — but if those scriptures are corrupted, this instruction is worthless. Therefore, if the Bible is corrupted, the Qur'an's commands regarding it become absurd.

Both horns of the dilemma are problematic for Islamic theology. This site examines the Qur'anic evidence for the Bible's authenticity, the historical evidence against the corruption theory, and the theological implications.

III. The Qur'an Affirms the Previous Scriptures

▼

The Qur'an's affirmations of the Torah, Psalms, and Gospel are not peripheral — they are central to its self-presentation as the final confirmation of the Abrahamic revelation.

Qur'an 3:3–4
"He has sent down upon you, [O Muhammad], the Book in truth, confirming what was before it. And He revealed the Torah and the Gospel. Before, as guidance for the people. And He revealed the Qur'an."
The Qur'an describes itself as "confirming what was before it" — the Torah and Gospel. The Arabic word musaddiqan means "affirming the truth of," "confirming as true." The Qur'an does not merely acknowledge the existence of previous scriptures; it declares them to be true divine revelation that it confirms.
Qur'an 5:47
"And let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein. And whoever does not judge by what Allah has revealed — then it is those who are the defiantly disobedient."
This verse commands Christians to judge by the Gospel "which Allah has revealed therein" — present tense. This is addressed to Christians of Muhammad's era (7th century AD), whose Gospel texts were the same texts we have today. If the Gospel were corrupted, this command would be a command to judge by corruption.
Qur'an 5:43–44
"But how do they come to you for judgement while they have the Torah, in which is the judgement of Allah?… Indeed, We sent down the Torah, in which was guidance and light."
The Qur'an refers to the Torah which Jews of Muhammad's day "have" — present tense. It calls this Torah a vehicle of God's judgment and guidance, with divine light in it. This is the Torah as it existed in the 7th century — substantially the same Torah we have today.
Qur'an 10:94
"So if you are in doubt, [O Muhammad], about that which We have revealed to you, then ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you."
Remarkably, the Qur'an instructs Muhammad himself — if he doubts — to consult the People of the Book (Jews and Christians) and their scriptures. This implies that the scriptures in the hands of Jews and Christians in the 7th century were reliable enough to verify prophetic revelation. A corrupted text could not serve this verification function.
Qur'an 6:34
"And there is none to alter the words of Allah."
The Qur'an explicitly states that the words of Allah cannot be altered. Since the Qur'an also describes the Torah and Gospel as "words of Allah" (kalimat Allah), this verse implies that the Torah and Gospel cannot have been corrupted. The Islamic tahrif (corruption) theory contradicts this Qur'anic principle.

IV. What the Bible Actually Teaches

▼

If the Qur'an affirms the Torah and Gospel as divine revelation that should be followed, what do those scriptures actually teach? They teach doctrines that Islam denies.

John 1:1, 14
"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God… And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us."
The Gospel of John — which the Qur'an commands Christians to judge by — opens with the pre-existence, divinity, and incarnation of Jesus Christ. If the Gospel is from God, as the Qur'an affirms, then this teaching is from God. Yet Islam denies the divinity of Christ.
Isaiah 53:3–5
"He was despised and rejected by men… he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities."
The Torah (broadly: Old Testament prophets) predicts the suffering servant's death for the sins of others — the very atonement and crucifixion that Islam denies. If the Torah is from God, as the Qur'an affirms, this prophecy is divine revelation pointing to Christ's atoning death.
Matthew 28:19
"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit."
The Gospel — which the Qur'an commands Christians to follow — contains the Great Commission with an explicitly Trinitarian formula. If the Gospel is authentic, then the Trinity (at minimum, the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as distinct divine persons) is authentic divine revelation. Islam denies the Trinity.
1 Corinthians 15:3–4
"That Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures."
Paul's earliest summary of the Gospel — written c. AD 55, within 25 years of the crucifixion — explicitly states Christ's death, burial, and resurrection. This is the Gospel "in accordance with the Scriptures" — the Old Testament Torah. If these scriptures are from God, the death and resurrection of Christ are from God. Islam denies both.

V. Early Christian Evidence — The Manuscripts

▼

The Islamic corruption (tahrif) theory requires that the Bible was significantly altered before or during Muhammad's time, changing its teaching about Christ's divinity, crucifixion, and resurrection. The manuscript evidence decisively contradicts this theory.

The Manuscript Record: We possess over 5,800 Greek New Testament manuscripts, with the earliest fragments (Papyrus 52 — John 18:31–33, 37–38) dating to c. AD 125 — just 30–90 years after John was written. These manuscripts, found across Egypt, Syria, Greece, and Rome, consistently preserve the texts we have today — including the divinity of Christ, the Trinity, and the crucifixion. There is no manuscript tradition showing an "uncorrupted" original Gospel different from what we have.

The Dead Sea Scrolls: Discovered in 1947, the Dead Sea Scrolls include manuscripts of the Old Testament dating to c. 200 BC – AD 68 — over 600 years before Muhammad. They are substantially identical to the Masoretic Text used today, including the Messianic prophecies (Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, Daniel 9). No corruption of the Old Testament could have occurred after AD 68.

The Geographic Distribution: By the 2nd century, the New Testament texts had spread across the entire Roman Empire and beyond — to North Africa, Persia, India, and Ethiopia. A coordinated corruption of all these geographically dispersed manuscripts would have been logistically impossible, and would have left documentary evidence of the pre-corruption texts. No such evidence exists.

The Patristic Citations: The Church Fathers (Clement, Ignatius, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Origen, Tertullian) cite the New Testament extensively from AD 96 onward. Their citations match our current texts — including the passages affirming Christ's divinity, death, and resurrection. There is no patristic record of a different, uncorrupted version of the Gospels.

VI. Muslim Responses

▼

Muslim Response: The Bible has been corrupted (tahrif)

▼

The mainstream Islamic response is the tahrif (corruption) theory: the original Torah and Gospel were genuine divine revelations, but they were corrupted by Jewish and Christian scribes who changed the texts to remove prophecies of Muhammad, alter the teaching about Jesus, and introduce false doctrines. What we have today is not the original scripture but a corrupted version. Therefore the Qur'an's affirmations apply to the originals, not the corrupted versions we have.

Christian Response: Tahrif contradicts the Qur'an and the manuscript evidence

▼

The tahrif theory faces multiple fatal problems: (1) The Qur'an says "none can alter the words of Allah" (Q. 6:34) — if the Torah and Gospel are God's words, they cannot be altered. (2) The Qur'an commands 7th-century Christians and Jews to judge by the scriptures they currently have — this implies those scriptures are reliable. (3) The manuscript record shows no pre-7th-century version of the Bible different from what we have. (4) The Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the Old Testament's integrity centuries before Muhammad. (5) The geographic distribution of manuscripts across multiple independent regions makes coordinated corruption impossible. The tahrif theory is a theological necessity for Islam but has no historical or textual support.

Muslim Response: The "Gospel" in the Qur'an refers to a lost original Gospel, not the four canonical Gospels

▼

Some Muslim scholars argue that the Injil (Gospel) referred to in the Qur'an was a single, original Gospel given directly to Jesus — different from the four Gospels of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John, which were written by his followers. This original Gospel has been lost or suppressed. The Gospel of Barnabas (a medieval document presenting Jesus as a prophet who predicted Muhammad) is sometimes cited as preserving elements of the original.

Christian Response: The "lost Gospel" theory is historically untenable

▼

The "lost original Gospel" theory has no historical support: (1) There is no early Christian, Jewish, or pagan reference to a single Gospel given directly to Jesus as a written book. Jesus taught orally; the written Gospels were composed by his disciples and eyewitnesses. (2) The Gospel of Barnabas is a medieval forgery — it contains anachronisms referencing feudal Italy (coins, wine barrels, etc.) and quotes from Dante's Inferno. It was unknown before the 15th century. (3) Paul's summary in 1 Corinthians 15:3–4 (c. AD 55) — within the lifetimes of eyewitnesses — already contains the death, burial, and resurrection of Christ. This is not a later addition. (4) The Qur'an's reference to "those who have the Scripture before you" in Muhammad's time clearly means the scriptures in people's hands in the 7th century — the same texts we have today.

VII. The Christian Response — Conclusion

▼

From a Christian perspective, the Islamic Dilemma is not merely an apologetic puzzle — it is an invitation to take the Qur'an's own affirmations seriously and follow them to their logical conclusion.

The Qur'an affirms the Torah and Gospel as divine revelation. The manuscript evidence confirms that the Torah and Gospel we have today are essentially the same documents that existed in Muhammad's time. Those documents teach the divinity of Christ, the Trinity, the atoning death and resurrection of Jesus, and the supremacy of his covenant over all previous covenants.

The Christian conclusion is: the Qur'an, by affirming the previous scriptures, inadvertently endorses the very documents that refute Islam's central theological claims. The dilemma cannot be escaped by the tahrif theory — which contradicts the Qur'an itself and is historically unsupported — nor by the "lost Gospel" theory, which is equally unsupported.

The resolution the Christian tradition offers is straightforward: accept the testimony of the scriptures that both the Bible and the Qur'an regard as divine revelation — and follow them to Jesus Christ, the Son of God, crucified for our sins and raised for our justification.

VIII. FAQ

▼
Q: Is this site anti-Muslim?▼

No. This site engages seriously with Islamic theology and the Qur'an's own claims. It does not attack Muslims as persons or deny the sincerity of Muslim faith. It examines a genuine logical tension within Islamic teaching that Muslim and Christian scholars have discussed for centuries. Honest engagement with theological differences is a mark of respect, not hostility.

Q: Do all Muslims accept the tahrif theory?▼

No. The tahrif theory exists in multiple forms: (1) corruption of meaning (tahrif ma'nawi) — the texts were not changed in wording, but Jews and Christians interpret them incorrectly or hide their true meaning; (2) corruption of text (tahrif lafzi) — the actual wording of the texts was changed. Many classical Muslim scholars accepted only the former (corruption of meaning) because the textual corruption theory contradicts Q. 6:34. The textual corruption theory became more dominant in later medieval Islamic apologetics. The diversity of Muslim opinion on this question reflects its genuine theological difficulty.

Q: Does the Qur'an anywhere explicitly say the Bible was corrupted?▼

The Qur'an accuses some Jews of "distorting words from their places" (Q. 4:46; 5:13, 41) — but this refers to oral misquotation and misrepresentation, not wholesale textual alteration of written scriptures. The Qur'an never explicitly says "the Torah or Gospel texts have been changed." The tahrif of written texts is an inference made by later Muslim scholars, not an explicit Qur'anic teaching. In fact, as noted, Q. 6:34 says Allah's words cannot be altered — which most classical commentators took to mean the Qur'an is protected, not that the Bible was corrupted.
