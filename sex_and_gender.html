Male & Female: Sex, Gender & the Body

“Male and female He created them” • Why the Church affirms the body • Speaking the truth in love
From its very first page, Scripture tells us that to be human is to be made “male and female” in the image of God (Genesis 1:27). The Orthodox Church receives this not as an accident of biology to be edited, nor as a mere social role that shifts with the culture, but as a good and deliberate work of the Creator — part of what it means to be a person made for communion. In our own day this conviction is questioned as never before. This page sets out the Christian vision of sex and the body, examines the modern claim that “gender is a social construct,” and answers the common objections — always aiming to hold together two things the Church never separates: truth and love.

The Heart of the Matter

▶

The whole of the Christian teaching can be stated simply. God created the human person as an embodied soul, and He created that person as male or female. Our being one sex or the other is not a cage laid over a “true self” that is really something else; it is written into the body God knit together (Psalm 139:13) and is part of the goodness He pronounced over His creation.

Modern thought often draws a sharp line between sex (the biological reality of male and female) and gender (an inner sense or a set of social roles), and then argues that gender is essentially a construct — something the culture invents and the individual may choose. The Church can agree that many customs attached to men and women vary from age to age and place to place; what a man or woman wears, does for a living, or is expected to be like differs across cultures. But it firmly denies that the deeper reality of being male or female is merely invented. Sex is a given, received from God, not a role assigned by society or a feeling assigned by the self.

The one-line answer: our maleness and femaleness are gifts of the Creator woven into the body, not costumes we put on or take off. To receive one's own body with thanksgiving is not narrowness — it is the beginning of humility before God, who “made them male and female” and called His work very good. And this truth is never a licence for contempt: every person, whatever they feel or struggle with, bears the image of God and is loved by Him.

“Male and Female He Created Them”

▶

The Christian understanding of the sexes rests on the opening chapter of the Bible. The creation of humanity in the image of God and the creation of humanity as male and female are spoken in a single breath:

“So God created man in His own image; in the image of God He created him; male and female He created them.” Genesis 1:27 (NKJV)

Two truths stand side by side here, and the Church holds both without contradiction. First, men and women share equally and fully in the image of God. Whatever their differences, neither sex is more the image of God than the other; both possess the same dignity, the same rational soul, the same calling to holiness. Second, that shared humanity is expressed in two distinct ways — male and female — and this distinction is God's own design, present “from the beginning,” not a later corruption.

Christ Himself confirms that the male–female distinction is creational and enduring. When questioned about marriage, He did not appeal to shifting custom but reached back to the act of creation:

“Have you not read that He who made them at the beginning ‘made them male and female’?” Matthew 19:4 (NKJV)

Notice what this means. For the Lord, “male and female” is not a temporary arrangement of the old covenant, nor a cultural convention of the ancient Near East; it is how the Creator made the human race, and it remains the foundation of marriage and family. To be male or female is therefore not incidental to who we are — it is part of the answer to the question “what is a human being?”

Sex, Gender & the Modern Distinction

▶

Much of the modern debate turns on a distinction between two words. It is worth stating carefully, because clarity here dissolves a good deal of confusion.

What the terms mean

Sex refers to the biological reality of being male or female — a reality written into the body at the level of chromosomes, anatomy, and reproductive design, and ordered toward the bringing forth of new life. It is not assigned; it is observed. Gender, in its modern usage, has come to mean something different: an inward sense of oneself as a man or woman, or the social roles and expressions a culture attaches to each sex. The now-common claim is that gender is a social construct — that it is produced by culture and can therefore be reshaped, chosen, or detached altogether from the body.

Where the Church can agree — and where it cannot

There is a grain of truth the Church need not deny. Many of the customs a society ties to men and women really are contingent. Colours, clothing styles, occupations, manners, and countless expectations vary from culture to culture and change over time; these are, in that sense, “constructed.” A Christian is free to recognise this and to reject rigid stereotypes that Scripture never taught.

But the modern claim goes far beyond this modest point. It says that the deeper reality of being a man or a woman is itself a construct — that the body is raw material and the “real” self is an inner identity that may contradict it. This the Church cannot accept, for it is finally a claim that the Creator's work is arbitrary and that we author ourselves. Scripture teaches the opposite: we are made, and made male or female, by God.

Why “sex” is the surer word: biological sex is a fact that can be observed and does not depend on anyone's feelings or on the fashions of an age. “Gender” as an inner identity floating free of the body is far harder to define — it tends to be described only in circular terms (a woman is anyone who feels like a woman). The Christian instinct to anchor human identity in the God-given, factual reality of the body rather than in shifting feeling is not stubbornness; it is a refusal to build the human person on sand.

None of this denies that some people experience real and painful distress about their sex — the condition often called gender dysphoria. The Church takes that suffering seriously and responds with compassion (see A Word of Compassion below). But compassion for a person's pain is a different thing from agreeing that the body is false and the feeling is the truth. Love does not require us to call reality by the wrong name.

The Body Is Not a Costume

▶

Underneath the modern debate lies a very old error, and naming it helps. The idea that the “real me” is an inner self trapped in the wrong body is, at root, a form of dualism — the belief that the body is a mere shell, separable from and inferior to the spirit. The early Church fought exactly this error under the names of Gnosticism and Manichaeism, and she condemned it. For the Christian, the body is not a costume the soul happens to wear; it is an essential part of the person, created good, destined to rise again.

The Psalmist marvels that God Himself is the maker of his body, and treats that fact as grounds for praise, not lament:

“For You formed my inward parts; You covered me in my mother's womb. I will praise You, for I am fearfully and wonderfully made.” Psalm 139:13–14 (NKJV)

The whole weight of the Christian story presses in the same direction. God did not save us by rescuing souls out of bodies; He saved us by becoming a body — the Word was made flesh (John 1:14). The Son of God took a real, sexed human body, was born of a woman, and rose again bodily; and He calls our bodies “the temple of the Holy Spirit” (1 Corinthians 6:19). A faith whose centre is the Incarnation and the Resurrection of the body can never treat the body as a disposable outer layer or an obstacle to the true self. To honour the body God gave — including its being male or female — is therefore not vanity or bondage; it is reverence toward the Creator and toward His coming redemption of the whole person, body and soul together.

Made for Communion

▶

Why did God make humanity in two sexes at all? Scripture's answer is that the difference is ordered toward communion and toward life. Man and woman are not two interchangeable copies but two who are made for each other, whose union images something greater than themselves.

“Then the Lord God said, ‘It is not good that man should be alone; I will make him a helper comparable to him.’ … Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and they shall become one flesh.” Genesis 2:18, 24 (NKJV)

The difference between the sexes is not a problem to be overcome but the very ground of the “one flesh” union — and of the family, and of the continuation of the human race. God's first blessing on humanity, “be fruitful and multiply” (Genesis 1:28), presupposes and depends upon the male–female distinction. In the Orthodox vision, the complementarity of man and woman is a good and beautiful thing: equal in dignity, distinct in their gift, joined in a love that brings forth new persons made in God's image.

This is also why the deepest picture of salvation in Scripture is a wedding. The love of husband and wife is made an icon of the love of Christ (the Bridegroom) for His Church (the Bride) — the “great mystery” of which St. Paul speaks (Ephesians 5:31–32). The two sexes, and their union, are woven into the way God has chosen to reveal His own love. To erase the distinction is not liberation; it is to lose one of the richest signs God has given us of Himself.

See also: Marriage & Celibacy for the two honourable callings that flow from this vision, and Women in the Church for the equal dignity and distinct calling of women in the life of the Church.

Answering the Modern Objections

▶

The Christian view is challenged today from several directions. Each objection deserves a clear and charitable answer. Tap any question to expand it.

Objection 1 “Gender is just a social construct — so the categories aren't real” ▶

The argument usually slides between two very different claims. That social roles and expectations attached to the sexes are partly constructed is true, and the Church can grant it. But that being male or female is itself a construct does not follow and is not true. A society may construct many ideas about gravity; gravity itself is not thereby constructed. Male and female are not human inventions but biological realities found in virtually every human culture and, indeed, across the living world — realities ordered toward reproduction that no amount of redefinition changes. Recognising that customs vary is a world away from claiming the underlying reality is fiction.

Objection 2 “St. Paul says in Christ there is 'neither male nor female' (Galatians 3:28)” ▶

This verse is often read as if the Gospel abolished the distinction of the sexes. It does no such thing. Paul is speaking about access to salvation, not about the erasure of the body:

“There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is neither male nor female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” Galatians 3:28 (NKJV)

His point is that Jew and Greek, slave and free, man and woman are equally heirs of the promise, equally baptised into Christ, equally saved by faith — a radical dignity in an age that ranked them. He plainly does not mean that Jews stopped being Jews, or slaves stopped being slaves, the moment they believed; nor that men and women ceased to be men and women. The very same Paul who wrote Galatians 3:28 also grounded his teaching on marriage and the sexes in the creation of “male and female” (Ephesians 5:31; cf. 1 Corinthians 11). Equality of salvation is not sameness of body.

Objection 3 “Intersex conditions prove the male/female binary is false” ▶

Rare disorders of sexual development are real, and the people who live with them deserve compassion and medical care, not to be used as a debating point. But hard cases at the margin do not overturn the norm. That a very small number of people are born with ambiguous or atypical development no more disproves that humanity is male and female than the existence of people born blind disproves that human beings are made to see. These are recognised precisely as conditions — departures from a norm that itself remains real and overwhelmingly universal. The existence of an exception presupposes the rule it departs from; it does not abolish it.

Objection 4 “Holding this view is simply bigotry” ▶

To believe that the body is a gift and that our sex is given by God is not hatred of anyone; it is a conviction about what human beings are, held by nearly every culture in history and taught by the Scriptures. One can hold it — the Church insists one must hold it — together with genuine love, patience, and respect for every person, including those who disagree or who struggle. Disagreement about the nature of the human person is not the same as contempt for persons. Indeed, the Christian claim is the opposite of contempt: it says every human being, male or female, is fearfully and wonderfully made in the image of God (see A Word of Compassion).

Objection 5 “The Church should just get with the times” ▶

The Church has watched a great many “times” come and go across twenty centuries, each certain it had outgrown what came before. Her task is not to keep pace with the spirit of any age but to bear faithful witness to the Word of God, which does not change. On this, as on much else, she stands where she has always stood — not because she is behind the times, but because she answers to a Lord who is above them. Faithfulness sometimes looks like stubbornness from the outside; from the inside it is simply loyalty to the truth she was given.

Objection 6 “If the body is a gift not to be edited, isn't a tattoo wrong — and if so, is surgically removing one wrong too?” ▶

This presses on a real distinction the Church does need to make, and it is worth making carefully. Scripture does address the practice directly: “You shall not make any cuttings in your flesh for the dead, nor tattoo any marks on you: I am the LORD.” Leviticus 19:28 (NKJV) In context this forbids specific pagan mourning-rites — cutting and marking the body as an offering to the dead or to false gods — and the Church has never treated it as a timeless ban on all ink, in the way she treats, say, the moral core of the Ten Commandments. A tattoo is decoration, not redefinition: it changes the surface of the skin while leaving the given reality underneath — this body, this sex, this person — completely untouched. That is why the tradition has generally treated it as a matter of prudence rather than sin as such: is the image itself idolatrous, occult, or degrading? Is it done to conform to the world rather than to Christ, or from vanity, or under compulsion? Those are real pastoral questions, and caution is warranted. But ink on the surface is not an assault on the body's nature the way the objections earlier on this page are.

Removing a tattoo, surgically or otherwise, sits in exactly the same category and raises no separate difficulty: if applying one is a matter of prudence, so is taking one off. A person is free to keep it, regret it, or have it lasered away without any of this touching the deeper claim of this page — that one's sex, unlike a tattoo, is not decoration to be added or removed but the given shape of the body itself.

Objection 7 “Doesn't the same logic condemn surgery to correct a cleft palate, a birth defect, or a disfiguring injury?” ▶

No — and the reason why sharpens the whole argument of this page rather than undermining it. The Church has always honoured the healing arts; physicians such as Ss. Cosmas and Damian are venerated precisely for practicing medicine as an act of Christian charity. The relevant question for any bodily intervention is not “does it change the body?” (surgery, medicine, even a haircut all do that) but “does it restore the body toward its own given wholeness, or does it work against a healthy, given reality?” Reconstructive surgery for a cleft palate, a burn scar, a congenital malformation, or an injury is healing in the fullest sense: it repairs what disease, accident, or the general brokenness of a fallen world has damaged, returning the body toward the integrity it was always meant to have. This is medicine cooperating with the body's own nature, not overriding it.

That is precisely what distinguishes it from surgery aimed at reassigning a healthy body's sex. A cleft palate is a defect measured against the body's own given design — there is no controversy about what a properly formed palate looks like. A healthy body's sex is not a defect at all; there is nothing there to correct. One intervention restores a nature to itself; the other overrides a nature that was never broken to begin with. Healing the wounded and denying the whole are not the same act, however similar the operating table may look.

A Word of Compassion

▶

It would be a betrayal of everything above to state the Church's teaching coldly. Behind the arguments are real people — some of them wrestling with deep and genuine distress about their bodies and their sense of self. The Gospel does not meet such people with a sneer. It meets them, as Christ met everyone, with truth and love held together and never torn apart.

The Church says three things at once, and refuses to drop any of them. First, every person — whatever they feel, however they struggle, whatever they have done — is made in the image of God, loved by Him to the point of the Cross, and welcome to come to Him. Second, that same love speaks the truth: that the body is a good gift, that our sex is given by God, and that lasting peace is found not in remaking ourselves against our nature but in receiving ourselves as God's handiwork. Third, the Church walks with people patiently. Distress about one's sex is a heavy burden, not a sin to be scolded; the pastoral path is one of prayer, honesty, gentleness, and time, walked alongside a person rather than shouted at from a distance.

Christ is the friend of everyone the world pushes to the margin. To carry His name is to offer the same welcome — to love people enough both to embrace them and to tell them the truth, trusting that in Him, and not in the fashions of the age, the human heart finds the rest it is looking for.

This is a subject that touches real pain for many people. If you or someone you love is struggling, you are not alone and you are not beyond love. A trusted priest, pastor, or counsellor can walk with you; and the God who “formed your inward parts” has not forgotten you.

Voices of the Fathers

▶

The Fathers affirmed both the equal dignity of the sexes in the image of God and the goodness of the body God created. Their witness undercuts any idea that the Christian tradition either despised the body or reduced women and men to interchangeable ciphers.

St. Gregory of Nyssa
c. 335–395 AD
“God created man; in the image of God created He him… of the Divine, the rational and intelligent element, which does not admit the distinction of male and female; of the irrational, our bodily form and structure, divided into male and female: for each of these elements is certainly to be found in all that partakes of human life.” Gregory reads Genesis 1:27 as two clauses, not one. “The image” names what every human shares with God — reason and freedom, which know no distinction of sex. “Male and female” names the body's kinship with the rest of embodied creation. Neither clause cancels the other: the image is not diminished by being carried in a sexed body, and the sexed body is not a mistake imposed on a genderless soul.
St. Gregory of Nyssa, On the Making of Man, Ch. XVI.9 (NPNF 2nd Series, vol. 5)
St. Athanasius of Alexandria
c. 296–373 AD
“Unable to bear that death should have the mastery… He takes pity on our race, and had mercy on our infirmity, and condescended to our corruption… He takes unto Himself a body, and that of no different sort from ours.” The great Coptic patriarch's whole argument turns on this line: the Word did not merely appear to have flesh, or borrow some higher substitute for it — He took a real human body, the very kind He had made in the beginning. A body worth the Word's taking is not a prison to escape but a temple to inhabit; what God assumed, He honoured, and no Christian may call worthless what He was pleased to wear.
St. Athanasius, On the Incarnation, Ch. 8.2 (NPNF 2nd Series, vol. 4)
St. Irenaeus of Lyons
c. 130–202 AD
“Vain in every respect are they who despise the entire dispensation of God, and disallow the salvation of the flesh, and treat with contempt its regeneration, maintaining that it is not capable of incorruption.” Writing against Gnostics who scorned matter as a lower or evil substance, Irenaeus makes the flesh's destiny a test of orthodoxy itself: deny that the body can be saved, and the Eucharist, the Incarnation, and the resurrection all unravel with it. His answer to ancient body-hatred is the answer to its modern forms — the same flesh God made, God redeems.
St. Irenaeus, Against Heresies, Bk. V, Ch. 2.2 (ANF, vol. 1)
St. John Chrysostom
c. 347–407 AD
“Husbands, love your wives, even as Christ also loved the Church. You have seen the measure of obedience, hear also the measure of love… though you should suffer anything on her account, do not upbraid her; for neither did Christ do this.” Preaching to a culture that took a wife's obedience for granted, Chrysostom presses just as hard on the husband's side of the ledger, holding him to a standard of self-sacrificing love modelled on Christ and the Church — not the rule of a master over a servant, but the devotion of one who would rather suffer than shame his own wife. Equal honour and real distinction stand together, not against each other.
St. John Chrysostom, Homily 20 on Ephesians (NPNF 1st Series, vol. 13)

Frequently Asked Questions

▶

Does the Church say men and women are unequal?

No. The Church teaches that men and women are equal in dignity — both fully made in the image of God, both equally called to holiness and equally heirs of salvation (Galatians 3:28) — while also being really distinct. Equality is not sameness. To affirm the difference of the sexes is not to rank one above the other.

Isn't insisting on “biological sex” unloving toward people who struggle?

Love and truth are not enemies. The most loving thing is never to flatter a person with a falsehood but to walk with them in the truth, gently and patiently. The Church holds that our bodies are good gifts and that peace is found in receiving them — and she holds this alongside real compassion for anyone in distress (see A Word of Compassion).

Are traditional “gender roles” part of the faith?

The faith affirms that men and women are really male and female by God's design; it does not canonise every cultural custom about how each should dress, work, or behave. Those customs vary and can be questioned. The Church distinguishes the God-given reality of the sexes from the changeable expectations a given society layers on top of it.

What does the Church say about people who experience gender dysphoria?

That they are made in God's image, loved by Him, and deserving of compassion and patient pastoral care — not scorn. The Church does not treat the distress as a sin, but it also does not conclude that the body is false. It offers accompaniment, prayer, and the hope that identity is most surely founded on being God's own handiwork.

Why does any of this matter for the Gospel?

Because the Gospel is about the redemption of the whole person — body and soul — by a God who became flesh and rose bodily. A faith built on the Incarnation cannot treat the body as disposable. How we understand the body, and our being male and female, is finally a question about the Creator, the goodness of His work, and the resurrection He has promised.
