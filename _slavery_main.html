The Orthodox Lamp

Christianity & Slavery

The faith that was accused of upholding slavery is the faith that named it a sin and brought it to an end

Few charges are levelled at the Christian faith more often than this: that the Bible permits slavery, that the Church Fathers defended it, and that Popes themselves owned and traded human beings. The accusation is meant to discredit Christianity at its root. Yet the fuller history tells almost the opposite story. Every ancient civilisation — Israel's neighbours, Greece, Rome, the caliphates — was built on slavery long before the Gospel was preached, and each held it to be as natural as the weather. It was into that world that the Church introduced a scandalous new idea: that the slave and the master share one nature, one Baptism, one Father, and one Judgment. This page traces the whole arc — the Law of Moses, the Greco-Roman world, the witness of the Fathers (especially the Oriental Fathers of Egypt and Cappadocia), the objections honestly answered, and the long Christian road to abolition — set beside the slave systems the world has actually known, including the Islamic slave trade under which the Oriental Christians themselves suffered.

The Question

▶

The critic's case usually rests on three claims: that Scripture regulates slavery rather than condemning it; that revered Fathers accepted it; and that the medieval Papacy practised it. Each contains a grain of fact wrapped in a distortion. Slavery was the universal condition of the ancient world — historians estimate that a third or more of the population of classical Athens and Rome were slaves — and no book written into that world could pretend it did not exist. The question is not whether the Bible mentions slavery, but what it did to it: whether it left the institution as it found it, or planted principles that would one day destroy it.

What Christianity introduced
  • Master and slave share one human nature made in God’s image
  • Both are equally brothers in Christ, equal at the altar and the Judgment
  • Man-stealing (the slave trade) is condemned as a capital crime
  • Manumission — freeing slaves — is praised as a holy, God-pleasing act
  • Slavery is called a fruit of sin, not part of God’s created order
What the ancient world assumed
  • Some men are “slaves by nature,” fit only to be owned (Aristotle)
  • A slave was a “living tool,” property with no rights before the law
  • Masters could kill, brand, or maim slaves at will
  • Enslaving the conquered was the normal reward of war
  • Slavery was permanent, hereditary, and beyond question

The distortion in the popular charge is to read the Bible as if it invented or commanded slavery, when in fact it inherited a world drowning in it and began, quietly and then openly, to dissolve its foundations. The sections below follow that work from Sinai to abolition.

Scripture & the Ancient World

▶

The slavery of Israel's neighbours — Egypt, Assyria, Babylon — was chattel slavery: human beings owned as permanent property, bought and sold, with no claim on freedom. The Law of Moses did not create this; it constrained it, and for fellow Hebrews replaced it almost entirely with a system closer to indentured or contract labour, bounded by time and dignity.

A Hebrew who sold himself out of debt was to be released, debt-free, in the seventh year:

“When you buy a Hebrew slave, he shall serve six years, and in the seventh he shall go out free, for nothing.”Exodus 21:2 (ESV)
“And when you let him go free from you, you shall not let him go empty-handed. You shall furnish him liberally out of your flock, out of your threshing floor, and out of your winepress.”Deuteronomy 15:13–14 (ESV)

Every fiftieth year, the Jubilee, cancelled debts and returned people to their families and land (Leviticus 25). Most decisively, the Law struck at the very root of the slave trade — the stealing of persons to sell — making it a capital crime, the only property offence in the Torah punished by death:

“Whoever steals a man and sells him, and anyone found in possession of him, shall be put to death.”Exodus 21:16 (ESV)

This single command, taken seriously, makes the Atlantic slave trade impossible: every link in that chain — the raid, the sale, the possession — was a capital crime under Moses. The Law also forbade returning a runaway slave to his master, the reverse of every slave code in history including the American one:

“You shall not give up to his master a slave who has escaped from his master to you. He shall dwell with you… you shall not wrong him.”Deuteronomy 23:15–16 (ESV)

And beneath the statutes lay a conviction that would in time undo slavery altogether — that master and slave share one Maker who sees no rank between them. Job states it plainly:

“If I have rejected the cause of my manservant or my maidservant… what then shall I do when God rises up? … Did not he who made me in the womb make him? And did not one fashion us in the womb?”Job 31:13–15 (ESV)

The Law of Moses was not the last word — its provisions were a floor beneath a hard-hearted age, not the summit of God's will (cf. Matthew 19:8). But measured against Hammurabi, Egypt, and Assyria, it was a revolution in mercy, and it carried within it the seeds of slavery's undoing.

Greece, Rome & the New Testament

▶

The world of the Apostles was the Roman Empire, where slavery was total. Slaves may have numbered a third of Italy's population; they worked the mines and fields, staffed the households, and had no standing in law. Aristotle had taught that some people are “slaves by nature,” and Roman law treated the slave as a res, a thing. Christianity possessed no armies and no legislature; it could not overturn Rome's economy by decree. What it did instead was subvert the idea on which the whole system rested — that the slave was less than a man.

Saint Paul declares that in Christ the deepest human divisions, slavery among them, are abolished at the level where they matter most:

“There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus.”Galatians 3:28 (ESV)

He tells slaves who can gain their freedom to take it, and warns believers against selling themselves into bondage:

“Were you a slave when called? Do not be concerned about it. But if you can gain your freedom, avail yourself of the opportunity… you were bought with a price; do not become slaves of men.”1 Corinthians 7:21–23 (ESV)

He lists slave-traders — literally “men-stealers” — among the worst of sinners, alongside murderers, echoing the death penalty of Exodus:

“…the law is laid down … for enslavers, liars, perjurers, and whatever else is contrary to sound doctrine.”1 Timothy 1:9–10 (ESV)

The clearest picture is the little letter to Philemon. Paul sends back the runaway slave Onesimus — but commands the master to receive him no longer as a slave:

“…no longer as a slave but more than a slave, as a beloved brother … both in the flesh and in the Lord. So if you consider me your partner, receive him as you would receive me.”Philemon 16–17 (ESV)

A master who must greet his slave as he would greet the Apostle Paul — as a brother, an equal, a son — no longer really has a slave at all. This is how the Gospel worked on slavery: not by a slogan of revolt, which would have drowned the young Church in blood and left the institution intact, but by dissolving from within the belief that made slavery thinkable.

Voices of the Fathers

▶

Far from “defending” slavery, the greatest Fathers — and above all the Oriental Fathers of Egypt and Cappadocia — attacked it at the root. The most radical condemnation of slavery to survive from the ancient world came not from a philosopher but from an Egyptian-Cappadocian bishop.

Saint Gregory of Nyssa (d. c. 394), preaching in Cappadocia, is the first person in recorded history to condemn slavery itself as inherently sinful — not merely its abuses, but the ownership of any human being:

St Gregory of Nyssa (d. c. 394)
“‘I got me slaves and slave-girls,’ he says… You condemn man to slavery, when his nature is free and possesses free will, and you legislate in competition with God, overturning his law for the human species. … What did you find in existence worth as much as this human nature? … God said, Let us make man in our own image and likeness. … How many obols did you reckon the equivalent of the likeness of God?”
Homily 4 on Ecclesiastes (trans. Hall & Moriarty) Direct quote

He grounds the argument where Scripture does — in the image of God and the redemption of Christ, who freed the whole race from bondage and would never re-enslave it:

St Gregory of Nyssa (d. c. 394)
“God would not therefore reduce the human race to slavery, since he himself, when we had been enslaved to sin, spontaneously recalled us to freedom. But if God does not enslave what is free, who is he that sets his own power above God’s?”
Homily 4 on Ecclesiastes (trans. Hall & Moriarty) Direct quote

Saint John Chrysostom (d. 407), Archbishop of Constantinople and son of Antioch, taught that slavery is no part of God's order but a consequence of sin, and that in Christ it is emptied of meaning:

St John Chrysostom (d. 407)
“Slavery is the fruit of covetousness, of degradation, of savagery… for Noah, we know, had no servant, nor had Abel, nor Seth… It was sin which introduced servitude.”
Homily 22 on Ephesians (NPNF 1st ser., vol. 13) Direct quote
St John Chrysostom (d. 407)
“Such ought they to be who are near a king; and slaves they are no longer… For he is a brother. … He is the brother, not of his own master only, but also of the Son of God.”
Homily 22 on Ephesians (NPNF 1st ser., vol. 13) Direct quote

Saint Gregory the Great (d. 604), Pope of Rome, wrote into his official grant of freedom for two slaves a theology of manumission that treats liberty as man's original and proper state:

St Gregory the Great, Pope of Rome (d. 604)
“…it is a salutary deed if men, whom nature originally produced free, and whom the law of nations has subjected to the yoke of slavery, be restored by the benefit of manumission to the liberty in which they were born.”
Register of Letters VI.12 (trans. NPNF 2nd ser., vol. 12) Direct quote

These are not fringe voices. Gregory of Nyssa is a Father of the Second Ecumenical Council; Chrysostom is among the most beloved teachers of the whole Church, East and West; Gregory the Great is a Doctor of the Church. When the critic says “the Fathers supported slavery,” these are the very Fathers he must answer.

“But the Fathers and Popes owned slaves…”

▶

Honesty requires facing the hardest form of the charge, not the easiest. Some Christians did own slaves, some Fathers did counsel slaves to obey, and some medieval Popes did sanction enslavement. None of this is hidden here.

Didn’t Paul and the Fathers tell slaves to obey their masters?▶

Yes — and in a world where slave revolts (like Spartacus’) ended in mass crucifixion, telling powerless people to remain peaceful was pastoral realism, not approval of the system. In the same breath Paul tells masters to stop threatening and to remember they too have a Master in heaven who shows “no partiality” (Ephesians 6:9), tells slaves to take freedom if they can get it (1 Corinthians 7:21), and orders one master to receive his slave as a brother and an equal (Philemon 16). Counselling patience to the weak while dismantling the ideology of the strong is not an endorsement of slavery; it is the only strategy available to a movement with no political power.

Did the Church Fathers really accept slavery as normal?▶

Some, writing pastorally, addressed slaves and masters as they found them. But the Church's leading theologians went far beyond their era: Gregory of Nyssa condemned slave-owning as rebellion against God, Chrysostom called it the fruit of sin, and Gregory the Great treated freedom as man's birthright. No comparable voices exist in Greek, Roman, or any other ancient literature. The trajectory of patristic thought is unmistakably against slavery, not for it — which is precisely why, wherever the Church's mind prevailed, slavery receded.

Didn’t the Popes authorise slavery and the slave trade?▶

The record of the Renaissance Papacy is genuinely mixed and, at its worst, shameful. In Dum Diversas (1452) Pope Nicholas V granted Portugal the right to reduce “Saracens, pagans and other unbelievers” to “perpetual servitude” — a grant aimed at Muslim enemies but tragically extended to Africans. Yet the same office also produced the opposite: in Sublimis Deus (1537) Pope Paul III declared that the native peoples of the Americas “are by no means to be deprived of their liberty… even though they be outside the faith,” are “truly men,” and must not be enslaved. In 1839 Pope Gregory XVI, in In Supremo Apostolatus, condemned the slave trade outright and forbade any Catholic to defend it. The Papacy's failures are real; they are also the failures of one particular Western jurisdiction, entangled in the politics of Portugal, Spain, and the Papal States — and even there the Church's own conscience kept rising against the practice.

What does this have to do with the Oriental Orthodox?▶

Nothing — and that is the point. The Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, and Indian Churches were never part of the Papal apparatus that issued those bulls, held no colonial empires, and ran no Atlantic trade. For most of the second millennium the Oriental Christians were not slave-owners but slave-victims, living under Islamic rule and subject to the raids, tributes, and levies described below. To indict “the Church” for the Atlantic trade is to name a very specific Western chapter — and to pass over in silence the Eastern Christians who bore the lash rather than held it.

How Christianity Ended Slavery

▶

Slavery is as old as civilisation and was found on every continent. What has to be explained is not its existence but its abolition — a thing that happened only once in world history, and happened in Christendom, driven overwhelmingly by Christians appealing to Christian principles.

The pattern begins early. As the Church grew, freeing slaves became an act of piety; manumissions were performed in church before the bishop, and wealthy Christians freed slaves by the thousands as works of mercy. Chronic slavery in the medieval Christian West gradually gave way to serfdom and then to free labour, until by the High Middle Ages chattel slavery had largely vanished from Christian Europe — the first large civilisation in which this was true.

When slavery revived through the Atlantic trade, it was again Christians who led the fight to destroy it. The English abolition movement was founded and carried by devout believers — the Evangelical William Wilberforce, the former slave-trader John Newton who wrote “Amazing Grace,” the Quakers, and countless clergy — who argued from the image of God and the brotherhood of man that no Christian could hold another. Their campaign ended the British slave trade in 1807 and slavery across the Empire in 1833. In America the abolitionist cause was likewise saturated with Christian preaching and Scripture.

The abolitionists were doing consciously and politically what Gregory of Nyssa had done theologically fourteen centuries earlier: measuring slavery against the image of God in man and the freedom Christ purchased, and finding it condemned. No other civilisation produced an abolition movement from within. The idea that every human being bears God's image and is a brother for whom Christ died — the specifically Christian idea — is the ground on which slavery was finally judged and abolished.

The Islamic Slave Trade

▶

The Atlantic trade is rightly remembered with horror. Less remembered — though longer, larger in duration, and never producing an internal abolition movement — is the Islamic slave trade, under which the Oriental Christians themselves lived and suffered. Any honest history of slavery must include it.

Beginning in the seventh century and continuing into the twentieth, the trans-Saharan, Red Sea, and Indian Ocean slave routes carried an estimated ten to eighteen million Africans into the Muslim world over some thirteen centuries — a traffic that ran far longer than the roughly four centuries of the Atlantic trade. Europeans, too, were seized: the Barbary corsairs of North Africa enslaved on the order of a million Christians from Mediterranean coasts and ships between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries.

Two features stand out. First, male captives were frequently castrated to serve as eunuchs, a mutilation with a mortality so high that the surviving numbers represent a fraction of those taken. Second, under the Ottoman devshirme or “blood tax,” Christian families in the Balkans and Anatolia were forced to surrender their sons — boys taken from Orthodox homes, converted to Islam, and raised as Janissary soldiers or palace servants. For the Christians of the East, slavery was not a distant institution to be studied but a standing threat under which whole communities lived for a thousand years.

None of this excuses the sins of Western Christians in the Atlantic trade. It does correct a lopsided picture — one in which slavery is imagined as a peculiarly Christian or Western crime, when in truth Christian civilisation was the first to abolish it, and Christian peoples were among its most enduring victims.

The Oriental Christian Witness

▶

The Oriental Orthodox — the Copts of Egypt, the Ethiopians, the Syriacs, the Armenians, and the Malankara Christians of India — stand at a particular angle to this whole history, and it is a witness worth naming.

They gave the Church its most uncompromising opponent of slavery in Saint Gregory of Nyssa, and its great Egyptian and Antiochene teachers of the equal brotherhood of slave and free. They never held a colonial empire, ran no Atlantic trade, and issued no bulls of enslavement; the failures of the medieval Western Papacy are simply not theirs to answer for. And for the greater part of the last fourteen centuries they lived not as masters but as a subject people under Islamic rule, exposed to the raids, the child-levies, and the slave markets described above.

This is why the history matters for Oriental Christians in particular. The charge that “Christianity upheld slavery” collapses the whole Church into one Western chapter and then reads even that chapter selectively. The fuller truth is that the faith which the world accuses of chaining men is the faith that first called them all brothers — and the Oriental Churches, which bore the chains rather than forged them, are living proof of it.

Frequently Asked Questions

▶
If slavery is wrong, why didn’t God simply forbid it outright?▶

God’s way with a fallen world is often to plant a principle and let it grow, rather than to impose a rule an age cannot yet bear (cf. Matthew 19:8 on divorce). Scripture forbids the slave trade outright — man-stealing was a capital crime — and then sows the deeper seed, the image of God and the brotherhood of all in Christ, which in time uprooted the institution itself. A bare prohibition dropped into the ancient economy would have been ignored; a new vision of the human person reshaped civilisation.

Was biblical “slavery” the same as Atlantic slavery?▶

No. For Hebrews it was closer to indentured or contract labour: time-limited, debt-based, released with generous provision, and protected by law. It was not race-based, not permanent for fellow Israelites, and — crucially — could not be fed by kidnapping, since man-stealing carried the death penalty. The Atlantic system, built on the mass abduction of a targeted race for perpetual, hereditary bondage, is precisely what the Law of Moses made a capital crime.

Doesn’t the very existence of slave laws prove God endorsed it?▶

Regulation is not endorsement. Law meets people where they are in order to move them somewhere better: the Mosaic code took the brutal chattel slavery of the ancient Near East and, for Israel, hedged it with sabbaths, jubilees, release years, and protections unknown to any neighbouring nation — while forbidding the trade that fed it. The direction of the Law is toward freedom, not away from it.

Why bring up the Islamic slave trade — isn’t that changing the subject?▶

It is part of the same subject: the honest history of human bondage. The popular charge singles out Christianity while passing over the largest and longest slave trade in history, under which Eastern Christians were themselves victims. Naming it is not deflection; it is refusing a double standard and restoring the whole picture — in which Christian civilisation is the one that produced abolition.

Glossary

▶
Chattel slavery
The ownership of human beings as permanent, saleable property with no rights — the system of Egypt, Rome, and the Atlantic trade.
Indentured / bond service
Time-limited labour to repay a debt, ending in release — closer to the Hebrew servitude regulated by the Law of Moses.
Man-stealing
Kidnapping a person to enslave or sell. A capital crime in the Law (Exodus 21:16) and condemned by Paul (1 Timothy 1:10).
Manumission
The formal freeing of a slave. In the Church it became an act of piety, often performed before a bishop.
Jubilee
The fiftieth year in Israel (Leviticus 25) when debts were cancelled, servants freed, and land restored to families.
Devshirme
The Ottoman “blood tax”: the forced levy of Christian boys, converted to Islam and raised as soldiers or servants.
Barbary corsairs
North African raiders who enslaved on the order of a million European Christians from the 16th–18th centuries.
Abolitionism
The movement to end slavery, which arose only within Christendom, led by believers appealing to the image of God in man.
