Icons & Veneration

Why the Orthodox venerate holy images — and why this is not idolatry

From the catacombs to the Coptic sanctuary, Christians have always surrounded themselves with sacred images. The Orthodox honour icons of Christ, His Mother, and the saints — kissing them, bowing before them, lighting lamps and incense — yet they have always insisted that this honour is veneration, not the worship owed to God alone. Protestant Reformers charged that this is idolatry, a violation of the Second Commandment. The Orthodox answer that the very Incarnation of God, the witness of Scripture, and the unbroken practice of the ancient Church say otherwise: the One who could not be depicted has made Himself visible, and the honour shown the image passes to the One it represents.

The Heart of the Matter

▶

The dispute over icons is, at bottom, a dispute over two questions: Can the holy be depicted at all? And if an image is honoured, what is that honour — worship, or reverence? The Orthodox and the Protestant Reformers answer both differently.

Orthodox
  • Because God became visible in Christ, He can be depicted in His humanity
  • Honouring an icon is veneration (relative honour), not worship
  • The honour passes to the prototype — the person, not the wood and paint
  • God Himself commanded sacred images (the cherubim, the bronze serpent)
  • Icons have been venerated since the early Church, in East and West alike
Protestant / Iconoclast
  • The Second Commandment forbids making and bowing to images
  • Bowing, kissing, and incensing an image is in practice worship
  • God is spirit and cannot — or should not — be represented
  • Image-veneration is a later corruption, not apostolic practice
  • Honour and prayer belong to God alone, never through an object

The Orthodox case is not that images are harmless decorations to be tolerated, but that the rejection of holy images is itself a theological error — a failure to grasp what the Incarnation made possible. The argument that follows turns on four pillars: the Incarnation, the distinction between veneration and worship, the witness of Scripture, and the unbroken tradition of the Church.

The Incarnation Changes Everything

▶

The deepest Orthodox argument for icons is not about art at all — it is about the Incarnation. Under the Old Covenant, God forbade images of Himself for a clear reason: no one had seen Him, and any attempt to depict the invisible God would be a lie, reducing the Creator to a creaturely form. But in Christ, God did what He had never done before — He took flesh and became visible.

"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory…"John 1:14 (ESV)
"He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation."Colossians 1:15 (ESV)

This is the turning point. The God who could not be portrayed has portrayed Himself in human nature. To say Christ cannot be depicted is, in effect, to deny that He truly became man. St. John of Damascus — the great defender of icons during the eighth-century iconoclast crisis — made exactly this point, and turned the charge of materialism on its head:

"I do not worship matter, I worship the God of matter, who became matter for my sake, and deigned to inhabit matter, who worked out my salvation through matter."St. John of Damascus, On the Divine Images, First Apology §16

The logic is this: the same God who saved us through material means — a body, a cross of wood, water, bread and wine — is not dishonoured when His image is drawn in material pigment. The icon confesses the reality of the Incarnation. This is why the Orthodox regard iconoclasm (the breaking of icons) not merely as a liturgical disagreement but as a Christological one: to forbid the image of Christ is to flinch from the scandal that God truly took our flesh.

Veneration Is Not Worship

▶

The single most important distinction in this whole subject is one the English word "worship" tends to blur. The Church has always distinguished two utterly different acts:

Veneration — προσκύνησις (proskynesis)
  • Relative honour, respect, reverence
  • Shown to the holy: icons, the Cross, the Gospel book, the saints
  • Expressed by bowing, kissing, incense, lamps
  • The honour passes through the image to the person represented
Worship — λατρεία (latreia)
  • Adoration owed to God alone
  • The acknowledgement of Him as Creator and Lord of all
  • To give this to any creature or object is idolatry
  • Never offered to an icon, a saint, or any created thing

We honour many things without worshipping them. A man kisses a photograph of his late mother, or a soldier salutes a flag, without adoring paper or cloth — the honour is for the person or the country it represents. So with icons: the kiss and the bow are directed through the image to Christ or the saint. St. Basil the Great stated the governing principle, in the context of the Son as the image of the Father:

"…because the honour paid to the image passes on to the prototype."St. Basil the Great, On the Holy Spirit 18.45 (NPNF 2nd Series, Vol. 8)

Basil was defending the worship of the Son by showing that honour given to an image reaches its archetype. The Fathers who defended icons saw at once that the same principle applies: the reverence shown an icon is not arrested at the wood and paint but travels to the holy person depicted. The object is not divinised; it is a window. When the Orthodox bow before an icon of Christ, the bow terminates in Christ Himself — and to Christ alone, with the icon of His Mother or the saints, the honour terminates in them as friends of God, never as gods.

Images at God’s Own Command

▶

If all religious images were forbidden, God would be in contradiction with Himself — because the same God who gave the Second Commandment also commanded the making of sacred images, in the very heart of the place where He was worshipped.

"And you shall make two cherubim of gold; of hammered work shall you make them, on the two ends of the mercy seat… The cherubim shall spread out their wings above, overshadowing the mercy seat with their wings…"Exodus 25:18–20 (ESV)

The Ark of the Covenant — the holiest object in Israel, the place of God's presence — was crowned with golden images of angelic beings, made by divine command. Solomon's Temple went further still, filled with carved cherubim, palm trees, flowers, and oxen (1 Kings 6–7). The Israelites did not worship these images, but they were images nonetheless, sacred and God-ordained, in the holiest space of the Old Covenant.

Most striking is the bronze serpent. When Israel was dying of snakebite, God did not tell Moses to pray only; He commanded him to make an image — and those who looked upon it in faith were healed.

"So Moses made a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. And if a serpent bit anyone, he would look at the bronze serpent and live."Numbers 21:9 (ESV)

Christ Himself takes this very image as a type of His own saving death on the Cross: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up" (John 3:14). A material image, divinely commanded, became a means of grace and a figure of salvation. This is the scriptural pattern the Orthodox appeal to: God does not forbid images as such — He forbids idols.

The Second Commandment

▶

The Protestant objection rests almost entirely on one text, and it deserves to be read in full:

"You shall not make for yourself a carved image, or any likeness of anything that is in heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth. You shall not bow down to them or serve them, for I the LORD your God am a jealous God…"Exodus 20:4–5 (ESV)

Read alone, this sounds like a blanket prohibition of all images. But it cannot mean that — because four chapters later the same God commands the cherubim of gold, and Solomon fills the Temple with carved figures. Scripture does not contradict itself. The commandment must be read as a whole: "You shall not make… and bow down to them or serve them." The forbidden thing is the making of an image to be a god — a rival deity, a false object of worship. The opening verse names this directly: "You shall have no other gods before me" (Exodus 20:3). The Second Commandment is a prohibition of idolatry, not of religious art.

The decisive proof is the bronze serpent's later fate. The same image God had commanded was, centuries later, destroyed by the righteous King Hezekiah — precisely because the people had begun to worship it:

"He broke in pieces the bronze serpent that Moses had made, for until those days the people of Israel had made offerings to it (it was called Nehushtan)."2 Kings 18:4 (ESV)

Notice what changed. The image itself was good when made at God's command and used rightly. It became an abomination only when Israel offered it the worship (latreia) owed to God alone. The object did not change; the act did. This is exactly the line the Orthodox draw: the icon, venerated, is holy; the icon, worshipped as a god, would be an idol — and the Church has never permitted the latter.

The Fathers and the Councils

▶

The veneration of icons is not a medieval invention. The catacombs of Rome and the third-century house-church at Dura-Europos are covered with images of Christ, the Good Shepherd, the Virgin, and biblical scenes — painted by Christians under persecution, long before any emperor could have imposed them. The practice grew, came under fierce attack in the eighth century, and was vindicated by the Church.

c. 230–240 — Dura-Europos
The earliest known Christian house-church, in Syria, is decorated with wall paintings of Christ healing, the women at the tomb, and other scenes — evidence that Christians were depicting sacred subjects within two centuries of the Apostles.
726–787 — The Iconoclast Crisis
Byzantine emperors (beginning with Leo III) ordered the destruction of icons. St. John of Damascus — living safely under Muslim rule, beyond the emperor's reach — wrote his Three Treatises in their defence, grounding icons in the Incarnation.
787 — The Second Council of Nicaea
The Chalcedonian Church (Eastern Orthodox and Roman Catholic) condemned iconoclasm and defined the veneration of icons, carefully distinguishing it from the worship due to God alone.
843 — The Triumph of Orthodoxy
After a second wave of iconoclasm, the icons were finally restored, an event the Eastern Orthodox still commemorate on the first Sunday of Great Lent as the "Sunday of Orthodoxy."

The Council of Nicaea II articulated the distinction with great precision:

"…to these should be given due salutation and honourable reverence, not indeed that true worship of faith which pertains alone to the divine nature… For the honour which is paid to the image passes on to that which the image represents, and he who reveres the image reveres in it the subject represented."Definition of the Second Council of Nicaea, 787 (NPNF 2nd Series, Vol. 14)

An important note for the Oriental Orthodox. The Oriental Orthodox communion — the Coptic, Armenian, Syriac, Ethiopian, and Indian Churches — recognises only the first three Ecumenical Councils (Nicaea I in 325, Constantinople I in 381, and Ephesus in 431), having parted from the Chalcedonian Church after 451. The Second Council of Nicaea (787) therefore does not stand among the councils we count as Ecumenical. The Oriental Orthodox defence of icons does not rest on its authority as a council, but on the Incarnation, the witness of Scripture, and the Church's own unbroken practice — the very foundations Nicaea II itself appealed to. We esteem the teaching it set forth on icons, which simply confirmed what the whole ancient Church, ours included, had always done.

Icons in the Coptic Tradition

▶

The Coptic Orthodox Church has venerated holy images from antiquity. Egypt's dry climate has preserved some of the oldest Christian icons in the world — encaustic panels from the monasteries of the Egyptian and Sinai deserts, some dating to the sixth and seventh centuries. The Monastery of St. Catherine at Sinai, in the orbit of the Christian East, holds a treasury of these early images that escaped the iconoclast destruction further north.

Coptic iconography has its own unmistakable style — large, frontal eyes that gaze directly at the worshipper, simplified forms, and a deliberately otherworldly serenity meant to draw the beholder into prayer rather than to imitate nature. In the modern era this tradition was renewed by the iconographer Isaac Fanous, whose "neo-Coptic" school adorns Coptic churches around the world today.

In Coptic practice, as in all Orthodox practice, the faithful venerate icons by bowing, by kissing them, and by lighting lamps and offering incense before them — exactly the acts described at Nicaea II — while reserving worship for God alone. The icon is treated as a holy window: a meeting-place with the Lord and His saints, never an idol. When the Copts surround their sanctuaries with the images of Christ, the Theotokos, St. Mark, and the great martyrs and monastic fathers of Egypt, they are doing what their Church has done since the age of the catacombs.

This is why the iconoclast charge of idolatry lands no more heavily on the Copts than on the rest of the ancient Church: the practice is the same one the Fathers defended, grounded in the same Incarnation, and guarded by the same careful distinction between veneration and worship.

The Disputed Passages

▶

A few texts carry most of the weight in this debate. Here is how the Orthodox read the verses raised for and against the veneration of images.

Exodus 20:4–5 — "you shall not make… a carved image"▶

The Orthodox read the commandment as a unit: the prohibition is on making an image and bowing to it or serving it as a god. The context (v. 3, "no other gods before me") and the immediate sequel (God commanding the golden cherubim in Exodus 25) show that the target is idolatry — worshipping created things as deities — not the existence of religious art. A blanket reading would put God in contradiction with His own commands four chapters later.

Exodus 25:18–20; 1 Kings 6 — the cherubim and the Temple▶

God commands golden cherubim over the Ark, and Solomon fills the Temple with carved cherubim, palms, flowers, and oxen — all in the holiest space of Israel's worship. This is the decisive counter-text: sacred, God-ordained images stood at the centre of Old Covenant worship. The line was never "no images," but "no idols."

Numbers 21:8–9; John 3:14 — the bronze serpent▶

God commands Moses to make an image of a serpent, and looking on it in faith brings healing. Christ then takes it as a type of His own lifting up on the Cross. A material image, divinely commanded, becomes a means of grace and a figure of salvation — precisely the logic of the holy icon.

2 Kings 18:4 — Hezekiah destroys the bronze serpent▶

Far from undermining icons, this proves the Orthodox point. The same image God commanded was destroyed only when the people began to worship it. The fault was not in making the image but in giving it the latreia owed to God. The object was good; the idolatrous act was evil. The Church guards exactly this line.

John 1:14; Colossians 1:15 — the Word made visible▶

"The Word became flesh"; Christ is "the image (eikon) of the invisible God." The invisible God who could not be depicted has, in the Incarnation, made Himself visible and depictable in His humanity. These verses are the foundation of the entire Orthodox case: to depict Christ is to confess that He truly became man.

John 4:24 — "God is spirit"▶

Iconoclasts cite this to argue God cannot be portrayed. The Orthodox agree entirely that the divine nature is invisible and uncircumscribable, and so no icon ever attempts to depict the Father's essence or the Godhead as such. What the icon depicts is Christ in His human nature — the form God Himself assumed — and the saints who bear His likeness. "God is spirit" rules out depicting the invisible God in the abstract; it does not rule out depicting the God who became man.

FAQ

▶
Isn’t bowing to an icon the same as worshipping it?▶
No — the act of bowing takes its meaning from its object and intent. Scripture itself records the faithful bowing to people and holy things without idolatry: Abraham bowed to the Hittites (Genesis 23:7), and Joshua fell down before the Ark of the Lord (Joshua 7:6). Bowing can express reverence and respect, not only divine worship. The Orthodox bow before an icon as an act of honour directed to the person it represents, while reserving worship (latreia) for God alone.
Do the Orthodox pray to the icon?▶
No. No one prays to wood and paint. The icon is a focus and a window for prayer — the worshipper stands before the image of Christ and prays to Christ, or before the image of a saint and asks that saint's intercession. The honour and the prayer pass through the image to the living person in heaven; they never terminate in the object.
Why icons and not statues, like the Roman Catholics?▶
This is a matter of tradition and emphasis rather than a doctrinal divide over images as such. The Christian East developed the two-dimensional icon — painted, not sculpted — partly to keep a clear distance from the carved idols of the pagan world, and partly because the flat icon is understood as a "window" rather than a lifelike replica. The principle (veneration of images is lawful) is shared with Rome; the artistic form (icon rather than statue) is distinctively Eastern.
If God commanded the cherubim, why did He also forbid images?▶
Because the two commands address different things. The prohibition targets idols — images made to be worshipped as gods, in imitation of the surrounding pagan nations. The cherubim and the Temple carvings were sacred images within the worship of the true God, not rival deities. The same distinction runs through the whole Bible: images are condemned when worshipped as gods (the golden calf, Nehushtan) and commanded when they serve the worship of the Lord.
Should the Oriental Orthodox appeal to the Second Council of Nicaea?▶
The Oriental Orthodox accept only the first three Ecumenical Councils (Nicaea 325, Constantinople 381, Ephesus 431), and so do not count Nicaea II (787) among their Ecumenical Councils. The OO defence of icons therefore rests on the Incarnation, on Scripture, and on the Church's own ancient and continuous practice — which long predates 787. We can recognise that Nicaea II rightly articulated the veneration/worship distinction without making our doctrine depend on its conciliar authority.

Glossary

▶
Icon
From the Greek eikon, "image." A sacred representation of Christ, the Theotokos, the angels, or the saints, venerated as a window onto the holy person it depicts.
Veneration (proskynesis)
Relative honour and reverence shown to the holy — icons, the Cross, the saints — expressed by bowing, kissing, incense, and lamps. Distinct from worship.
Worship (latreia)
The adoration owed to God alone, acknowledging Him as Creator and Lord. To offer it to any creature or object is idolatry.
Prototype
The person or reality an image represents. The Fathers teach that the honour given an image "passes to the prototype" — to the one depicted, not the material.
Iconoclasm
Literally "image-breaking." The movement, peaking in eighth- and ninth-century Byzantium, that sought to destroy holy images and forbid their veneration.
Iconodule
A "servant" or venerator of icons — the defenders of images against the iconoclasts, such as St. John of Damascus and St. Theodore the Studite.
Idol
An image made to be a god, or worshipped as one. This — not religious art as such — is what the Second Commandment forbids.
Nehushtan
The name given the bronze serpent (2 Kings 18:4) once Israel began worshipping it; Hezekiah destroyed it. A biblical example of a good image turned into an idol by misuse.
Theotokos
"God-bearer," the title of the Virgin Mary defined at Ephesus (431). Her icon, holding the Christ-child, is among the most venerated in the Church.
Triumph of Orthodoxy
The final restoration of the icons in 843, commemorated by the Eastern Orthodox on the first Sunday of Great Lent.
