The Oriental Orthodox Family

Six ancient churches, one faith — Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, Ethiopian, Eritrean, and Malankara

Long before the great East–West schism of 1054, another family of churches — apostolic, ancient, and deeply rooted — had already taken its own path after the Council of Chalcedon in 451. Today the Oriental Orthodox number some 60 million faithful across Egypt, the Middle East, the Caucasus, the Horn of Africa, and India: six self-governing churches, worshipping in five ancient tongues, united in one Miaphysite faith.

One Communion, Six Churches

▶

The Oriental Orthodox are not a single centralized body but a communion of six autocephalous (self-governing) churches, each with its own patriarch or catholicos, its own liturgical language, and its own ancient culture — yet all confessing one faith, sharing one sacramental life, and recognizing one another’s mysteries fully. What unites them is the Christology of St. Cyril (see Miaphysite Christology →) and the first three ecumenical councils.

Coptic Orthodox
See of Alexandria, founded by St. Mark. Egypt. Liturgy in Coptic & Arabic. The cradle of Christian monasticism.
Syriac Orthodox
See of Antioch, where believers were first called Christians. Aramaic/Syriac liturgy. Home of St. Ephrem.
Armenian Apostolic
Founded by Sts. Thaddeus & Bartholomew. Armenia was the first nation to adopt Christianity as its state religion.
Ethiopian Tewahedo
Ancient Christianity in the Horn of Africa. Ge'ez liturgy. The broadest biblical canon in Christendom (81 books).
Eritrean Tewahedo
Sister church to the Ethiopian, granted autocephaly in 1994. Shares the Ge'ez liturgical tradition.
Malankara (India)
The St. Thomas Christians of Kerala, tracing their origin to the Apostle Thomas (A.D. 52). Syriac heritage.

The word Tewahedo (Ge’ez for “made one, unified”) in the Ethiopian and Eritrean names is itself a confession of the miaphysite faith: the one united nature of the incarnate Word.

The Coptic Orthodox Church

▶

The Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria traces its foundation to St. Mark the Evangelist, who according to tradition preached in Egypt and was martyred in Alexandria around A.D. 68. Its head bears the title Pope and Patriarch of Alexandria — a title older than the Roman use of “Pope.” From the See of Alexandria came giants of the whole Church: St. Athanasius, defender of the Nicene faith, and St. Cyril, champion of the Theotokos at Ephesus.

Egypt is the birthplace of Christian monasticism. St. Antony the Great went into the desert around A.D. 270; St. Pachomius founded communal monasticism; the deserts of Scetis and Nitria filled with the Desert Fathers whose sayings still guide Christians of every tradition. See Monasticism →.

The Coptic Church has survived under Islamic rule since the seventh century with remarkable endurance, keeping its own calendar (the Era of the Martyrs, dated from Diocletian’s persecution) as a permanent memorial of what it has cost to remain faithful.

The Armenian Apostolic Church

▶

Armenia holds a unique honour: it was the first nation in the world to adopt Christianity as its state religion, traditionally in A.D. 301, when St. Gregory the Illuminator baptized King Tiridates III. The church traces its apostolic roots to Sts. Thaddeus and Bartholomew, which is why it is called “Apostolic.” Its spiritual centre is Holy Etchmiadzin, and its head bears the title Catholicos of All Armenians.

In the early fifth century St. Mesrop Mashtots invented the Armenian alphabet expressly so that Scripture and the liturgy could be given to the people in their own tongue — one of the great acts of Christian literacy in history.

The Armenian Genocide (1915)
During the First World War, the Ottoman Empire deported and killed an estimated 1.5 million Armenian Christians. It was one of the first genocides of the modern age and remains central to Armenian identity. The Armenian Church has canonized the victims as martyrs of the faith. To be Armenian Orthodox is, in large part, to remember.

The Ethiopian & Eritrean Tewahedo Churches

▶

Christianity reached the Kingdom of Aksum (modern Ethiopia and Eritrea) in the fourth century, when St. Frumentius converted King Ezana — making Ethiopia one of the earliest Christian states on earth. But Ethiopian tradition reaches back further still, to the Ethiopian eunuch baptized by the Apostle Philip (Acts 8:26-39) and to ancient ties with Israel.

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church preserves the broadest biblical canon in all of Christendom — 81 books, including works treasured nowhere else as Scripture, such as 1 Enoch, Jubilees, and the books of Meqabyan. It keeps a strong sense of Old Testament continuity: a rich fasting discipline, distinctions once tied to Levitical practice, and in every church a tabot (a replica of the Ark of the Covenant) without which the building is not consecrated.

The Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church, sharing the same Ge’ez liturgical heritage, was granted autocephaly in 1994 following Eritrea’s national independence. The two churches remain sisters in faith and tradition.

The Syriac & Malankara Churches

▶

The Syriac Orthodox Church is heir to the ancient See of Antioch — the very city where, as the Book of Acts records, “the disciples were first called Christians” (Acts 11:26). Its liturgy is celebrated in Syriac, a dialect of Aramaic close to the language Christ himself spoke. From this tradition comes St. Ephrem the Syrian (d. 373), the “Harp of the Spirit,” whose hymns are among the treasures of the whole Church.

Far to the east, the Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church of Kerala, India, traces its origin to the Apostle St. Thomas, who according to firm local tradition landed on the Malabar coast in A.D. 52 and planted the faith among communities that endure to this day. Historically linked to the Syriac tradition of Antioch, the St. Thomas Christians are living proof that the Church of the East reached India long before it reached much of Europe.

A Church of the Martyrs

▶

More than any other Christian family, the Oriental Orthodox understand themselves through martyrdom. The Coptic calendar itself is dated from the Great Persecution under Diocletian. The Armenians remember 1915. And in our own time the witness has continued.

The 21 Martyrs of Libya (2015)
In February 2015, twenty-one Christian workers were beheaded on a Libyan beach by ISIS for refusing to deny Christ. Twenty were Coptic Egyptians; the twenty-first, a man from Ghana, watched their faith and reportedly declared, “Their God is my God” — and died with them. The Coptic Orthodox Church canonized all twenty-one and added them to its Synaxarium within weeks. Their images now stand in Coptic churches as icons of a faith worth dying for.
“Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness’ sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.”Matthew 5:10 — the beatitude the Oriental Orthodox have lived out for centuries

To love these churches is to love a faith that has been tested by fire and has not been consumed — the lamp still burning in Egypt, in Armenia, in Ethiopia, in Syria, and in India.

FAQ

▶
How many Oriental Orthodox churches are there?▶
Six autocephalous (self-governing) churches in full communion with one another: the Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria, the Syriac Orthodox Church of Antioch, the Armenian Apostolic Church, the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, the Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church, and the Malankara (Indian) Orthodox Syrian Church. Together they number roughly 60 million faithful.
Are the Oriental Orthodox the same as the Eastern Orthodox?▶
No, though the names are similar and both are ancient, apostolic, and non-papal. The Eastern Orthodox (Greek, Russian, Serbian, etc.) accept the Council of Chalcedon (451); the Oriental Orthodox accept only the first three ecumenical councils. The two families are not currently in sacramental communion, though modern dialogue has found their Christological faith to be substantially the same.
Why are they called Oriental Orthodox?▶
Oriental simply means eastern, and the term distinguishes this family of pre-Chalcedonian churches from the Eastern (Byzantine) Orthodox. They are also called the Non-Chalcedonian, Pre-Chalcedonian, or Miaphysite churches. Each church usually prefers its own name (Coptic, Armenian, and so on).
What holds these different churches together?▶
One shared faith. Despite worshipping in Coptic, Syriac, Armenian, Ge'ez, and Malayalam, and spanning Egypt, the Middle East, the Caucasus, the Horn of Africa, and India, all six confess the same Miaphysite Christology of St. Cyril, accept the same first three councils, share the same sacramental life, and recognise one another's orders and mysteries fully.
Is it true these are churches of martyrs?▶
Profoundly so, in both ancient and modern times. The Armenian Genocide of 1915 killed around 1.5 million; the Coptic Church has endured centuries of pressure and, in 2015, canonised 21 men beheaded in Libya. Persecution of Christians in Egypt, Syria, Ethiopia, and Armenia continues. The Oriental Orthodox often describe themselves as the Church of the martyrs.
