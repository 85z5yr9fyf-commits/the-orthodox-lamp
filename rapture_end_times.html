THE ORTHODOX LAMP

The Rapture & End Times — A 19th-Century Doctrine Tested

"But of that day and hour no one knows" — Matthew 24:36

✦ ✦ ✦

Introduction — A Doctrine Worth Examining

▼

Many sincere Christians today expect a secret “rapture” — a moment when believers will suddenly vanish from the earth, leaving the rest of humanity behind to face a seven-year tribulation, followed by a literal thousand-year earthly reign of Christ. Bestselling books, films, and entire prophecy ministries are built on this timeline. For many evangelicals it is simply assumed to be “what the Bible teaches.”

This page examines that system — known formally as dispensational premillennialism — with respect and care. The question is not whether Christ will return. Every Orthodox liturgy confesses that He “shall come again in His glory to judge the living and the dead.” The question is whether the specific rapture timeline taught in popular prophecy literature is what Scripture teaches and what the Church has historically believed — or whether it is a recent system read back into ancient texts.

The historical record on this point is striking: the secret-rapture system in its developed form is traceable to the 1830s. For the Church’s first eighteen centuries, Christians East and West awaited one visible Second Coming, one general resurrection, and one final judgment — which is what the Oriental Orthodox Church still confesses today. See also Sola Scriptura and Church History.

Where the Rapture Doctrine Came From

▼

Defenders of the rapture often assume it is ancient. Its documented history tells a different story.

1st–19th centuries

The historic creeds and the major Church Fathers speak of a single, visible, glorious Second Coming followed by the general resurrection and judgment. The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed (381) confesses one coming in glory “to judge the living and the dead,” whose “kingdom shall have no end” — with no two-stage return and no secret removal of the Church before a tribulation. Paraphrase

c. 1830

In Britain, John Nelson Darby, a former Church of Ireland clergyman and a leading figure among the Plymouth Brethren, begins developing and teaching a two-stage return of Christ: a secret coming for the saints (the rapture), then a later visible coming with the saints. This is the seed of modern dispensationalism. Paraphrase

1859–1870s

Darby makes repeated tours of North America, spreading dispensational teaching among evangelical pastors and conference speakers. Paraphrase

1909

The Scofield Reference Bible is published by Oxford University Press, printing Darby-style dispensational notes on the same page as the biblical text. It becomes enormously influential in American evangelicalism, and for many readers the notes and the text blend into one. Paraphrase

1970–1990s

Hal Lindsey’s The Late Great Planet Earth (1970) and later the Left Behind novels (from 1995) carry the rapture timeline into mass popular culture, selling tens of millions of copies. Paraphrase

None of this, by itself, proves the doctrine false — truth is not decided by a calendar. But it does reverse the burden of proof. A reading of Scripture that has no clear witness in the Fathers, councils, or historic liturgies across some eighteen centuries, and that emerged from one identifiable 19th-century movement, must demonstrate itself from the text against the older consensus. The next sections test its two key proof-texts.

1 Thessalonians 4 — Read in Context

▼

The central rapture text is 1 Thessalonians 4:16–17:

1 Thessalonians 4:16–17 Direct Quote
“For the Lord Himself will descend from heaven with a shout, with the voice of an archangel, and with the trumpet of God. And the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive and remain shall be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air. And thus we shall always be with the Lord.”
Paul is comforting believers grieving for their dead (v. 13): the departed will not miss Christ’s return. Note what the text actually describes — a shout, an archangel’s voice, the trumpet of God, and the resurrection of the dead. This is the loudest, most public event imaginable, not a secret disappearance.

Two observations from the text itself:

1. Nothing here is secret. The event Paul describes is announced by a shout and a trumpet and accompanied by the general resurrection. The historic Church read this passage as a description of the Second Coming — the same event as Matthew 24:30–31 and 1 Corinthians 15:51–52, where the “last trumpet” sounds and the dead are raised. The rapture system must split this one event into two comings separated by years, a division the text nowhere makes.

2. “To meet the Lord” suggests escort, not departure. The Greek word for “meeting” (apantēsis) is used in the New Testament for going out to meet an arriving figure and accompanying him in — the virgins going out to meet the bridegroom (Matthew 25:6) and the Roman brethren coming out to meet Paul on the Appian Way (Acts 28:15). Many scholars note that the word was used in Hellenistic contexts for citizens going out to welcome a visiting dignitary into their city. Paraphrase On this reading, believers rise to meet the descending Lord and accompany Him — they do not fly away with Him to heaven for seven years.

In other words, the strongest rapture proof-text, read in its own context, describes the public Second Coming the Creed confesses.

Matthew 24 — Who Is Actually “Left Behind”?

▼

The second pillar text is the saying about “one taken, one left”:

Matthew 24:37–39 Direct Quote
“But as the days of Noah were, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be. For as in the days before the flood, they were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day that Noah entered the ark, and did not know until the flood came and took them all away, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be.”
Read the comparison carefully: in the days of Noah, the ones “taken away” were the wicked — swept away by the flood — while Noah and his family were left safe. Jesus then immediately says “one will be taken and the other left” (v. 40–41).

In Jesus’ own analogy, being “taken” corresponds to being swept away in judgment, and being “left” to being preserved — close to the reverse of the Left Behind picture, where being taken is salvation and being left is doom. The parallel in Luke 17 reinforces this: when the disciples ask “Where, Lord?” concerning those taken, Jesus answers, “Wherever the body is, there the eagles will be gathered together” (Luke 17:37) — an image of judgment and carrion, not of heavenly rescue. Paraphrase

Matthew 24 also describes the coming of the Son of Man in unmistakably public terms:

Matthew 24:27 Direct Quote
“For as the lightning comes from the east and flashes to the west, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be.”
Lightning across the whole sky is the opposite of a secret event. Verses 30–31 add that “all the tribes of the earth” will see Him coming on the clouds, with a great trumpet — the same trumpet imagery as 1 Thessalonians 4. One coming, visible to all.

The Millennium — Revelation 20 and the Historic Reading

▼

The “thousand years” of Revelation 20:1–6 is the only passage in Scripture that mentions a millennium, and it sits inside the Bible’s most symbol-laden book — a book containing a seven-headed dragon, a woman clothed with the sun, and locusts with human faces. The question is not whether Revelation 20 is inspired but whether its numbers are meant as literal calendar arithmetic.

It is true that some early writers — Papias, Justin Martyr, and Irenaeus among them — held forms of millennial expectation (often called chiliasm). Justin himself, however, acknowledged that other faithful Christians of his day thought otherwise. Paraphrase Over time the Church, East and West, settled firmly on the non-literal reading: Augustine, in The City of God, interpreted the thousand years as the present age of the Church, in which Satan is bound from deceiving the nations and the saints reign with Christ — a reading that became the broad consensus of historic Christianity. Paraphrase

Two textual points support this:

The creed’s deliberate wording. The Council of Constantinople (381) confessed that Christ’s “kingdom shall have no end” — language drawn from Luke 1:33. An everlasting kingdom does not fit a system in which Christ’s reign is a bounded thousand-year earthly administration followed by a final rebellion. Paraphrase

The rest of the New Testament knows one resurrection and one judgment. Jesus says all who are in the graves — both “those who have done good” and “those who have done evil” — come forth in the same hour (John 5:28–29). Paul tells Felix there will be “a resurrection of the dead, both of the just and the unjust” (Acts 24:15) — one resurrection, two destinies. The dispensational system requires multiple resurrections separated by long intervals, a structure built from Revelation 20’s symbols and then imposed on these plainer texts.

John 5:28–29 Direct Quote
“Do not marvel at this; for the hour is coming in which all who are in the graves will hear His voice and come forth — those who have done good, to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil, to the resurrection of condemnation.”
One hour, one voice, all the graves — the righteous and the wicked raised together. This is the general resurrection the Creed confesses.

Objections Answered

▼

“The Church isn’t appointed to wrath — so it must be removed before the tribulation”

▼

The Claim

1 Thessalonians 5:9 (“God did not appoint us to wrath”) and Revelation 3:10 (“I will keep you from the hour of trial”) show that believers will be evacuated before end-times tribulation.

Response

Being spared God’s wrath is not the same as being spared tribulation. Jesus promised the opposite of exemption: “In the world you will have tribulation; but be of good cheer, I have overcome the world” (John 16:33). In John 17:15 He prays, “I do not pray that You should take them out of the world, but that You should keep them from the evil one” — preservation through, not removal from. The martyrs of every age, including the Coptic martyrs of the present day, were kept faithful through tribulation, not airlifted out of it. Revelation itself was written to churches already suffering, promising them endurance and victory, not escape.

“Daniel’s seventieth week requires a future seven-year tribulation”

▼

The Claim

Daniel 9:24–27 prophesies seventy “weeks” of years; the seventieth week is detached from the first sixty-nine and placed in our future as the seven-year tribulation, with a gap of two thousand years between week sixty-nine and week seventy.

Response

The multi-millennia “gap” between the sixty-ninth and seventieth weeks is supplied by the system, not stated by the text — Daniel 9 reads as a continuous sequence. The historic Christian reading saw the seventy weeks culminating in the work of Christ: Messiah is “cut off” (9:26) and confirms a covenant, bringing sacrifice to an end (9:27) — fulfilled in the Cross, after which the temple sacrifices were rendered obsolete and the temple itself destroyed in AD 70. Paraphrase A prophecy whose natural climax is Christ should not be re-engineered around a future Antichrist.

“The rapture must be true — Israel and the Church are separate programs”

▼

The Claim

Dispensationalism holds that God has two distinct peoples — earthly Israel and the heavenly Church — so the Church must be removed before God resumes His prophetic program with national Israel.

Response

This is the system’s real foundation, and it is the point at which the New Testament speaks most directly. Paul teaches one olive tree into which Gentiles are grafted among the natural branches (Romans 11:17–24), one “Israel of God” (Galatians 6:16), and one new man in whom the dividing wall is broken down (Ephesians 2:14–16). Believers in Christ are “Abraham’s seed, and heirs according to the promise” (Galatians 3:29). The Church is not a parenthesis in God’s plan while another program waits; she is the fulfillment of the promises, Jew and Gentile together. Remove the two-peoples premise and the need for a separate rapture evaporates.

“Date-setting aside, isn’t reading the headlines prophetically harmless?”

▼

The Claim

Even if specific predictions fail, matching current events to prophecy keeps Christians watchful and evangelistically urgent.

Response

Jesus’ own words counsel the opposite posture: “But of that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, but My Father only” (Matthew 24:36), and “It is not for you to know times or seasons” (Acts 1:7). Generations of confident identifications — of the Antichrist, of the ten nations, of the final generation — have each expired and been quietly replaced. Paraphrase Repeated failed expectation breeds cynicism in observers and disillusionment in believers. The watchfulness Christ commands is moral readiness — the faithful servant doing his master’s work whenever he returns (Matthew 24:45–46) — not newspaper decoding.

What Orthodoxy Teaches About the Last Things

▼

The Oriental Orthodox faith on the last things is short, ancient, and confessed in every Liturgy:

Christ will return — once, visibly, in glory. “This same Jesus, who was taken up from you into heaven, will so come in like manner as you saw Him go into heaven” (Acts 1:11). The Creed: He “shall come again in His glory to judge the living and the dead, whose kingdom shall have no end.”

There will be one general resurrection. All the dead, righteous and wicked, will rise (John 5:28–29; Acts 24:15). The body matters; salvation is not escape from creation but its renewal — “a new heaven and a new earth” (Revelation 21:1).

There will be one final judgment. Christ separates the sheep from the goats (Matthew 25:31–46), and the criteria He names are mercy shown to the hungry, the stranger, the sick, and the prisoner.

The Christian posture is watchful faithfulness, not timeline anxiety. The Church prays in every Liturgy for Christ’s coming and lives ready for it through repentance, the Eucharist, and works of mercy. The Lord’s words “Watch therefore, for you do not know what hour your Lord is coming” (Matthew 24:42) are fulfilled at every Liturgy, not at a prophecy conference.

This is the faith of the ancient Church, not a modern reaction. Long before dispensationalism, St. Cyril of Jerusalem opened his teaching on the last things with the plain shape of the hope: “We preach not one advent only of Christ, but a second also, far more glorious than the former.” Direct Quote (Catechetical Lecture 15.1, c. AD 350) — two comings, not three, with no secret one hidden between them. And St. Augustine, reading Revelation 20, identified the thousand-year reign with the present age of the Church rather than a future earthly kingdom: “the Church even now is the kingdom of Christ, and the kingdom of heaven. Accordingly, even now His saints reign with Him.” Direct Quote (City of God 20.9, c. AD 426). The framework that later produced the rapture had no place in the Church that gave us the Creed.

This is not a lesser hope than the rapture timeline — it is a greater one. Instead of a secret evacuation followed by a complicated sequence of dispensations, Orthodoxy awaits the one event the whole creation groans for (Romans 8:22–23): the appearing of her Lord, the resurrection of the dead, and the life of the world to come.

Common Questions

▼
Q: Does rejecting the rapture mean rejecting Christ’s return?▼

Not at all — the opposite. Orthodoxy rejects the secret, separate rapture precisely because it confesses the real thing: one visible, glorious Second Coming. Every Divine Liturgy proclaims His death, confesses His resurrection, and awaits His coming. The disagreement is over a 19th-century timeline, not over the blessed hope itself (Titus 2:13).

Q: Are Christians who believe in the rapture not real Christians?▼

That is not the claim of this page. Many who hold the rapture view love Christ, love Scripture, and long for His appearing — which is itself commended (2 Timothy 4:8). The argument here is that the system they have inherited is historically recent and exegetically strained, and that the older, simpler confession of the Church is both more biblical and more steadying. Disagreement about prophecy charts is not a verdict on anyone’s sincerity.

Q: What about the Antichrist and the tribulation — does Orthodoxy deny them?▼

No. Scripture speaks of a final deceiver (2 Thessalonians 2:3–10; 1 John 2:18) and of tribulation before the end (Matthew 24:21–22), and the Fathers took both seriously. What Orthodoxy declines to do is construct a detailed advance timetable — identifying the man, the nations, and the dates — from symbols given for the Church’s vigilance rather than her curiosity. The Church expects to endure whatever comes, kept by her Lord, as she has endured persecution in every century.

Q: Doesn’t 1 Corinthians 15 say we will be “changed in the twinkling of an eye”? Isn’t that the rapture?▼

1 Corinthians 15:51–52 describes the instantaneous transformation of believers’ bodies “at the last trumpet,” when “the dead will be raised incorruptible.” Note when this happens: at the last trumpet, together with the resurrection of the dead — the same public event as 1 Thessalonians 4:16 and Matthew 24:31. The verse teaches the speed of the transformation at the Second Coming, not a separate secret event seven years earlier.
