I. Introduction

▼

"Religion and science have always been at war, and science keeps winning." The sentence functions, for many people, as the unexamined background of unbelief: Galileo in chains, flat-earth bishops, the steady retreat of God before the telescope. A Christian should know that this story has a name in the academic literature — the "conflict thesis" or "warfare thesis" — and a reputation: historians of science have spent decades dismantling it. The relationship between Christianity and science has included real episodes of tension, but as a master narrative, "perpetual warfare" is not history. It is a nineteenth-century polemic that outlived its evidence.

This page traces where the story came from, what the record actually shows — including an honest look at Galileo — and what science can and cannot pronounce on. The aim is not to claim science for the Church as a trophy, but to clear a myth off the road between a seeker and God. "The heavens declare the glory of God; and the firmament shows His handiwork" (Psalm 19:1).

II. Where the "Warfare" Story Came From

▼

The warfare narrative was crystallized by two Victorian books: John William Draper's History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom (1896). Both were openly polemical works written amid the era's battles over Darwin and church authority, and both popularized "examples" that historians have since traced to legend — most famously the claim that the medieval Church taught a flat earth.

The verdict of the field today is not a Christian talking point. The historians of science who dismantled the thesis include unbelievers; a standard collection — Galileo Goes to Jail and Other Myths about Science and Religion (Harvard, 2009), edited by Ronald Numbers — assembles specialists, many of them agnostics and atheists, to debunk the warfare legends one by one Paraphrase. The medieval educated world knew the earth was a sphere — it is in Bede, in Aquinas, in the standard university textbooks of the era Paraphrase; the tale that Columbus had to overcome flat-earth churchmen descends from a romanticized 1828 biography by Washington Irving, not from the sources. When the foundational anecdotes of a narrative turn out to be manufactured, the narrative itself deserves suspicion.

III. Christianity and the Birth of Science

▼

The deeper irony of the warfare story is that modern experimental science, as a sustained institutional enterprise, took root in medieval and early-modern Christian Europe — and the Christian doctrine of creation is a serious candidate for part of the reason. The argument, advanced by historians and philosophers of science, runs Paraphrase: a world created by a rational God is intelligible — it runs on discoverable laws, not on the whims of warring deities; and because God created freely, the world's actual laws cannot be deduced from an armchair — you must go and look. Rationality makes science possible; contingency makes it empirical. Both assumptions are theological before they are scientific, and the pioneers said so themselves, writing of laws given by a lawgiver and of studying the creation to honor its Creator.

The institutions tell the same story. The university itself grew out of the cathedral schools under church charters. The medieval Church was, for centuries, the chief patron of astronomy and natural philosophy in Europe. Copernicus was a canon of a cathedral chapter; Gregor Mendel, the founder of genetics, an Augustinian friar and abbot; Georges Lemaître, who first proposed what became the Big Bang theory, a Catholic priest — a detail with its own irony, since some scientists initially resisted his expanding universe partly because it sounded too much like "In the beginning" Paraphrase. A Church at perpetual war with science makes a poor explanation of a civilization whose clergy kept founding and funding it.

IV. The Galileo Case — and Other Myths

▼

Every warfare narrative leads with Galileo, so take it honestly. The affair was real: in 1633 the Roman Inquisition tried Galileo, forced him to abjure Copernicanism, and confined him under house arrest for the rest of his life. The Church was wrong, and said so itself centuries later. Christians gain nothing by airbrushing this.

But the popular version misleads at almost every other point Paraphrase. Galileo was never tortured and never imprisoned in a dungeon — his "arrest" was spent in villas and his own home, where he continued to work and published his most important book on physics. The dispute was not a simple clash of evidence versus dogma: in Galileo's day the decisive proof of the earth's motion did not yet exist (stellar parallax — the direct evidence — was not observed until 1838), and the reigning alternative system could account for the existing observations; some of the era's resistance was scientific, not just theological. Nor was it faith versus unbelief: Galileo was a Catholic who argued from Augustine that Scripture's purpose is salvation, not astronomy. The affair was a tangle of real theology, real science-in-progress, personal feuds, and Counter-Reformation politics — a cautionary tale about authority overreaching, not a parable of religion's incompatibility with science.

And it is, tellingly, the example always reached for — a single trial, four centuries ago, endlessly retold because the supply of comparable cases is thin. One genuine conflict does not make a war; against it stand the centuries of church-sponsored astronomy before Galileo and the believing scientists after him (next section).

V. The Believing Scientists

▼

If science and Christian faith were at war, the casualty list should include the people who actually built science. Instead, the founders of field after field were believers — many of them devout, some clergy:

Copernicus (heliocentric astronomy; cathedral canon) · Kepler (planetary motion; intended for Lutheran ministry, wrote of astronomy as reading God's design) · Galileo (a Catholic to his death) · Boyle (chemistry; funded Christian apologetics and Scripture translation) · Newton (mechanics and optics; wrote more on theology than physics, though his views were heterodox) · Faraday (electromagnetism; devout member of a small Protestant church) · Maxwell (electromagnetic theory; committed evangelical Christian) · Mendel (genetics; friar) · Pasteur (microbiology; a believing Catholic) · Kelvin (thermodynamics; professing Christian) · Lemaître (Big Bang cosmology; priest) Paraphrase — and in our own day, believers lead at the highest levels of science, such as Francis Collins, director of the Human Genome Project and later of the U.S. National Institutes of Health, an evangelical Christian who wrote The Language of God.

The point is not that science endorses Christianity — science is done by Christians, Muslims, Jews, Hindus, and atheists, because the laws are there for anyone to find. The point is the reverse claim fails: a faith allegedly at war with science cannot explain why so many of science's architects practiced both, finding in the rationality of nature not a rival to God but His signature.

VI. What Science Can and Cannot Say

▼

The surviving form of the conflict thesis is usually not historical but philosophical: "science explains the world without God, so God is redundant." Two confusions hide inside the sentence.

Method is not metaphysics. Science methodologically studies natural causes — that is its tool, not its discovery. A metal detector finds no seashells; it does not follow that the beach has none. Whether anything exists beyond nature is a question science's method brackets at the outset, and therefore cannot adjudicate at the end. The claim "only scientifically provable things are real" is itself not scientifically provable — scientism refutes itself in one sentence.

Explaining mechanism is not explaining away. Discovering how gravity shapes galaxies no more eliminates a Creator than understanding internal combustion eliminates the engineer. Christianity never taught that God is one cause among others inside the system, filling gaps where mechanisms run out — that "God of the gaps" is a modern caricature the Church should be first to reject. God is the reason there is a system: why there is something rather than nothing, why it obeys elegant mathematics, why it produced minds that can know it. Those questions get sharper, not weaker, as science advances — see Explanations for God's Existence and The Moral Argument.

VII. Orthodoxy and Science

▼

The Oriental Orthodox tradition has its own long acquaintance with learning — Alexandria, the intellectual capital of the ancient world, was the cradle of the Coptic Church, and the Catechetical School of Alexandria stood among the era's great centers of Christian scholarship. The Fathers did not read Genesis as a science textbook: St. Basil's Hexaemeron and other patristic commentary treat creation with theological purpose, and Christian interpreters from antiquity onward allowed that the "days" and figures of Genesis may carry meaning beyond a bare chronicle Paraphrase (the question of origins and the age of the earth is treated honestly on its own page).

Orthodoxy's confidence here is theological: truth cannot contradict truth, because the God who authored Scripture is the God whose rationality the cosmos reflects. The Church does not anxiously await each discovery to see whether the faith survives; she expects the heavens to declare His glory in whatever language the telescopes learn next. The faith rests not on gaps in physics but on events in history — above all the Resurrection (see the evidence) — and on the encounter with the living God to whom the whole creation points.

VIII. FAQ

▼
Q: Aren't most leading scientists atheists?▼

Surveys consistently show scientists less religious than the general public, with belief varying widely by country, field, and how questions are framed — and a substantial fraction of scientists believing in God or a higher power in essentially every major survey Paraphrase. Two cautions: minority status is not falsity (truth is not decided by polling any profession), and the causation is murky — the data are at least as consistent with self-selection (who enters academia) as with science eroding faith. The existence of first-rank believing scientists, from Maxwell to Collins, shows there is no incompatibility in principle — which is the actual question.

Q: Religion says "believe"; science says "prove." Aren't those opposite methods?▼

The contrast caricatures both sides. Faith, biblically, is trust grounded in evidence and experience — the apostles argued from eyewitness testimony, fulfilled prophecy, and public events ("we did not follow cunningly devised fables" — 2 Peter 1:16), and this site exists because Christianity invites examination. Science, meanwhile, runs on trust too — in instruments, in peer testimony, in the unprovable premises that nature is uniform and reason reliable. Both enterprises mix evidence and trust; they differ in subject matter, not in one being gullible and the other rigorous.

Q: Don't miracles contradict the laws of nature science depends on?▼

Only if the laws are prescriptions God is subject to, rather than descriptions of how creation ordinarily runs at its Creator's word. A miracle is not nature malfunctioning; it is the Lawgiver acting directly. The full argument — including Hume's famous objection — has its own page: Miracles — Answering Hume.

Q: What about evolution and the age of the earth?▼

Treated fully on Creation, Evolution & the Age of the Earth, including the range of views faithful Orthodox Christians hold and what the Church actually requires: that God is the Creator of all things, visible and invisible — a confession no scientific finding addresses, let alone refutes.
