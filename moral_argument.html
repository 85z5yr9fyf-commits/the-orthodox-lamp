The Moral Argument

Right and wrong are real — and that fact points to God

"Human beings, all over the earth, have this curious idea that they ought to behave in a certain way, and cannot really get rid of it" (C.S. Lewis, Mere Christianity, 1952). This page takes the famous argument of Mere Christianity, strips away the literary style, and lays it out step by step in plain words — with Scripture and the Church Fathers at every stage.

The Objection Stated — At Its Strongest

▶

The atheist objection runs like this: "You do not need God to be good. Atheists love their children, give to charity, and keep their promises. Morality comes from evolution, which gave us empathy, and from society, which teaches us rules. And if morality did come from God, that would make it worse, not better — the old Euthyphro dilemma shows that divine commands are either arbitrary or unnecessary."

Taken at its strongest, the objection makes three claims: that moral behavior does not require belief in God; that moral feelings can be explained by natural causes; and that grounding morality in God creates more problems than it solves. The first claim is true — and Scripture itself teaches it (Romans 2:14–15). The argument on this page never denies it. The question is not whether an atheist can behave morally. The question is whether, if atheism is true, morality is real — whether "cruelty is wrong" is a fact about the world, or just a feeling some primates happen to have.

"They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, while their conscience also bears witness, and their conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse them."Romans 2:14–15 — Scripture predicts moral atheists: the law is written on every heart, believing and unbelieving alike

The Argument in Plain Words

▶

In one sentence: if there is no God, nothing is really right or wrong — but some things are really wrong — therefore God exists.

Set out formally, the argument has just two premises and a conclusion:

Premise 1. If God does not exist, objective moral facts do not exist — only feelings, customs, and survival strategies.
Premise 2. Objective moral facts do exist — some things (torturing children for fun, genocide, betrayal of the innocent) are really wrong, wrong even if every human being approved of them.
Conclusion. Therefore God exists.

The logic is airtight; the only question is whether the premises are true. Premise 2 is the one nobody can live without — even the people who deny it in the seminar room affirm it the moment they are wronged. And Premise 1, remarkably, is defended most loudly not by Christians but by the most serious atheist philosophers themselves (Step 5). The Christian and the honest nihilist agree about the conditional; they disagree only about which end of it to keep.

Notice what the argument does not claim. It does not claim atheists are bad people. It does not claim you must read the Bible to know stealing is wrong. It claims that the moral law every person already knows — believer and unbeliever alike — cannot be what it plainly is (universal, binding, above all opinion) unless there is a Lawgiver above all opinion.

Step 1 — There Is a Real Moral Law

▶

In one sentence: every human being appeals to a standard of fair and unfair, right and wrong — and expects everyone else to know it too.

Listen to people quarrel. Nobody says merely "I dislike what you did." They say: "That's not fair." "You promised." "How would you like it if someone did that to you?" "He was there first." Each of these appeals to a standard — and expects the other person to recognize it. And the striking thing is that the other person almost never rejects the standard. He makes excuses: "I didn't mean it," "things were different," "I had no choice." Excusing yourself is admitting the law — you only excuse what you know was wrong.

C.S. Lewis
"Quarrelling means trying to show that the other man is in the wrong."
Mere Christianity (1952), Book I — a quarrel is impossible unless both sides already accept a shared standard of right
St. Paul
"For when Gentiles, who do not have the law, by nature do what the law requires, they are a law to themselves… They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts."
Romans 2:14–15 — the moral law is universal because God wrote it into human nature, not because Moses published it

The standard is also remarkably consistent across cultures. Details differ — how many wives, which foods, what counts as polite — but no culture in history has admired cowardice in battle, praised betraying your friends, or honored the man who takes everything and gives nothing. Selfishness has never been admired anywhere. Cultures disagree about applications of the moral law the way they disagree about applications of arithmetic; nobody concludes from a disputed bill that numbers are a matter of taste.

"He gave them the law of life as a heritage… He set His eye upon their hearts, to show them the majesty of His works."Sirach 17:11, 8 — the Wisdom literature of the wider canon: the law of life given to mankind as such, before and beneath the law of Sinai

Step 2 — The Moral Law Is Not Mere Instinct

▶

In one sentence: the moral law judges between our instincts, so it cannot itself be one of them.

The first natural reply is: "That standard is just herd instinct — an inherited urge to help the group." But watch what actually happens inside you. A man hears someone drowning. Two instincts fire at once: the urge to help (herd instinct) and the urge to stay safe (self-preservation). And then a third thing speaks — not an urge, but something that judges between the urges and says the weaker one (helping, usually) is the one you ought to obey. A referee between two players cannot be one of the players. The thing that ranks instincts cannot be an instinct.

Here is the simple test: no instinct is right all the time, and none is wrong all the time. Mother-love is good — until it becomes spoiling favoritism. Fighting spirit is good in defense — and evil in cruelty. The moral law is the thing that tells you when each impulse is right. It is not one of the notes on the piano; it is the sheet music that says which note to play and when. The notes are given by nature; the music is not.

"The spirit of man is the lamp of the LORD, searching all his innermost parts."Proverbs 20:27 — conscience as a lamp God placed inside the man: it illuminates the impulses, it is not one of them

Step 3 — The Moral Law Is Not Mere Custom

▶

In one sentence: we measure cultures against each other and call reformers "better" — which is impossible unless a standard stands above every culture.

The second natural reply: "We learned morality from parents and society — it is just convention, like driving on the right." It is true we learned it from parents, but that proves nothing about where it comes from. We learned the multiplication table from parents too, and arithmetic is not a human invention. The question is whether morality is like the times tables (taught, but true) or like traffic rules (taught, and merely agreed).

Two facts settle it. First: we compare moralities. Everyone — including every atheist — believes Nazi morality was worse than the morality that opposed it, not just different from it. But "worse" requires a measuring stick outside both. If each culture's morality is its own final standard, then the sentence "that culture's values were evil" is meaningless — and nobody believes it is meaningless. Second: we praise moral reformers. The abolitionist who condemned slavery was condemning the settled custom of his own society. If morality is the custom of society, the reformer is by definition immoral. Yet we know he was right and his society was wrong — which means both were answerable to something above both.

"Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness!"Isaiah 5:20 — the prophets assume a fixed standard that whole societies can get wrong; relativism cannot even state this verse
"For three transgressions of Moab, and for four, I will not revoke the punishment, because he burned to lime the bones of the king of Edom."Amos 2:1 — God judges a pagan nation for a crime against another pagan nation: the moral law binds nations that never received Sinai

Step 4 — Evolution Cannot Ground It

▶

In one sentence: evolution may explain why we feel moral impulses, but a feeling produced for survival is not an obligation — and was never aimed at truth.

The strongest modern version of the objection says: "Kin altruism and reciprocity evolved because cooperative apes out-reproduced selfish ones. Morality needs no further explanation." Grant every word of the biology. It explains the existence of moral feelings. It does not — cannot — explain moral facts. "This impulse helped my ancestors survive" and "therefore I am bound to obey it" are two different kinds of statement, and no amount of the first adds up to the second. Philosophers call this the is–ought gap: from what is (descriptions of nature), you can never derive what ought to be (obligations). Hunger evolved too; that does not make eating your neighbor's food right or wrong. The biology is silent exactly where morality speaks.

Worse for the objector: if our moral convictions were produced by selection pressure — aimed at reproduction, not at truth — why trust them at all? The evolutionary story does not support morality; it debunks it. The most candid evolutionary philosophers say exactly this:

Michael Ruse, atheist philosopher of biology
"Morality is a biological adaptation no less than are hands and feet and teeth. Considered as a rationally justifiable set of claims about an objective something, ethics is illusory."
"Evolutionary Theory and Christian Ethics," in The Darwinian Paradigm (1989) — the evolutionary account, followed honestly, ends in illusion, not in ethics
Richard Dawkins
"The universe that we observe has precisely the properties we should expect if there is, at bottom, no design, no purpose, no evil, no good, nothing but blind, pitiless indifference."
River Out of Eden (1995) — consistent naturalism stated plainly: no evil, no good. Yet its author condemns religion as evil — using the standard his own book dissolved

So the choice is stark. Either moral obligation is real — in which case it needs a ground that survival stories cannot supply — or it is an adaptive illusion, in which case "genocide is wrong" is on the same shelf as a peacock's tail. Nobody believes the second. Not even the people who write it.

Step 5 — Honest Atheists Concede the Point

▶

In one sentence: the claim "no God, no objective morality" is not Christian propaganda — it is the published verdict of the most serious atheist thinkers.

Premise 1 of the argument (above) is often called question-begging. It is not. It has been asserted, in print, by the hardest-headed atheists of the last two centuries — men with no motive to help the Christian case:

Friedrich Nietzsche
"When one gives up the Christian faith, one pulls the right to Christian morality out from under one's feet. This morality is by no means self-evident… Christianity is a system, a whole view of things thought out together. By breaking one main concept out of it, the faith in God, one breaks the whole."
Twilight of the Idols (1889), "Expeditions of an Untimely Man" §5 — the most consistent atheist in history despised those who kept Christian ethics after dropping Christian faith
J.L. Mackie, atheist philosopher
"There are no objective values."
Ethics: Inventing Right and Wrong (1977), opening sentence of chapter 1 — the title says it: on atheism, right and wrong must be invented, not discovered
J.L. Mackie, again
"Moral properties constitute so odd a cluster of properties and relations that they are most unlikely to have arisen in the ordinary course of events without an all-powerful god to create them."
The Miracle of Theism (1982), p. 115 — the 20th century's leading atheist philosopher concedes: if objective moral values DO exist, they point to God
Jean-Paul Sartre
"The existentialist finds it extremely embarrassing that God does not exist, for there disappears with Him all possibility of finding values in an intelligible heaven."
Existentialism Is a Humanism (1946) — Sartre, endorsing the point he credits to Dostoevsky's Ivan Karamazov: without God, everything is permitted

Hold these together with Mackie's two statements and the logic closes like a trap. Mackie the atheist says: objective values would point to God; therefore (being an atheist) he denies objective values exist. The Christian runs the same conditional the other way: objective values would point to God; objective values plainly exist (Step 1); therefore God. Same premise — the only question left is whether the torture of innocents is really wrong. That question answers itself.

Step 6 — From Law to Lawgiver

▶

In one sentence: a command requires a commander, and an obligation is always owed to a person — an impersonal universe of atoms cannot oblige anyone to do anything.

Run the eliminations together. The moral law is real (Step 1). It is not instinct (Step 2), not custom (Step 3), not a survival trick (Step 4) — and the best atheists agree it cannot float free in a godless universe (Step 5). Then what is it? Notice its peculiar character: it comes to us as a demand. It does not describe what we do (we break it constantly); it prescribes what we ought to do. It presses on us from outside our desires, often against them. It behaves, in short, exactly like a command.

But commands come from minds. Rocks do not issue demands; equations do not hold you responsible; the Big Bang does not care whom you betrayed. And obligations are owed to someone — break a promise and you wrong a person, not a principle. If the moral law stands above every human person and every human society (Steps 1–3 showed it does), then the Person to whom we are answerable stands above humanity itself. That is the Lawgiver: a Mind behind the universe that is intensely interested in fair play, honesty, courage, and love — interested enough to write the law into every heart He made.

"The law of the LORD is perfect, reviving the soul; the testimony of the LORD is sure, making wise the simple."Psalm 19:7 — the same psalm that hears the heavens declare God's glory hears the law declare His character: two witnesses, sky and conscience

This is as far as the bare argument carries: not yet the Trinity, not yet the Incarnation — but already a personal, moral God, which is to say, already the end of atheism. Who this Lawgiver is, He has shown us Himself (below; and see The Evidence for God for the nine other converging lines).

The Euthyphro Dilemma Answered

▶

In one sentence: goodness is neither a standard above God nor an arbitrary choice of God — it is God's own unchanging nature, and His commands express what He is.

The objection, from Plato's Euthyphro, modernized: "Is something good because God commands it? Then morality is arbitrary — had God commanded cruelty, cruelty would be good. Or does God command it because it is already good? Then goodness exists above God, and you don't need Him for morality after all." Either horn is supposed to wound: arbitrary tyranny or unnecessary middleman.

The dilemma only works if those are the only two options. They are not. The classical Christian answer — held by the Fathers long before the objection was revived — is a third option: the standard of goodness is God's own eternal nature. God does not consult a rulebook above Himself (so the second horn fails), and He does not invent values by whim (so the first horn fails). He commands love, faithfulness, and justice because He is love, faithfulness, and justice. Asking "could God have commanded cruelty?" is asking "could God be other than God?" The question is not deep; it is malformed.

"Jesus said to him, 'Why do you call me good? No one is good except God alone.'"Mark 10:18 — goodness is not a quality God happens to have; it is what God is. Christ's question presses the young man toward exactly this
"Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of lights, with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change."James 1:17 — the standard cannot shift, because the Nature it expresses cannot change

Notice the quiet irony: the Euthyphro objection was Plato's argument against the quarrelsome, changeable gods of paganism — gods who were themselves under fate and given to whim. Against those gods it succeeds. Against the eternal, simple, unchangeable God of Christian confession, it never connects: there is nothing above Him to measure Him, and nothing changeable in Him to be arbitrary.

The Image of God & Human Dignity

▶

In one sentence: the equal and immeasurable worth of every human being rests on the image of God — and atheism, having removed the image, has nothing to put under the worth.

Here the moral argument touches the ground. Modern people — atheists most loudly — believe every human being has equal dignity and inviolable rights: the disabled infant and the genius, the stranger and the kinsman. But look at human beings as nature presents them: unequal in every measurable way — strength, intelligence, usefulness, beauty. If value tracks any measurable property, equality is false. Equal dignity is only true if every person bears something non-measurable and identical — and that is precisely the Christian doctrine of the image of God. Secular human rights are a branch cut from this tree; the leaves have not yet withered, but nothing feeds them.

"So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them."Genesis 1:27 — the foundation: worth bestowed by creation, not achieved by capacity
"Whoever sheds the blood of man, by man shall his blood be shed, for God made man in his own image."Genesis 9:6 — the reason murder is absolutely wrong is the image: to strike the icon is to insult the One it images

This is why the Church's moral witness has always been concrete: the early Christians rescued exposed infants, honored slaves as brothers, and built the first hospitals — not because evolution whispered it, but because every face is an icon of God. The same conviction grounds the Oriental Orthodox witness today, often under persecution: the martyr refuses to deny Christ for the same reason the Church refuses to abandon the poor — the image of God is not negotiable.

Conscience in Scripture

▶

In one sentence: Scripture teaches everything the moral argument observes — a universal law written within, a conscience that bears witness, and a God who judges by it.

"For when Gentiles, who do not have the law, by nature do what the law requires, they are a law to themselves, even though they do not have the law."Romans 2:14 — the charter text: the moral law is in human nature itself, prior to all revelation
"He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"Micah 6:8 — the law's content in one verse: justice, mercy, humility — addressed to "man" as man
"So whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them, for this is the Law and the Prophets."Matthew 7:12 — the Golden Rule: Christ states the law every conscience already recognizes, and claims it as the sum of revelation
"Owe no one anything, except to love each other, for the one who loves another has fulfilled the law… love is the fulfilling of the law."Romans 13:8, 10 — the moral law is not finally a list but a Person's character: love

Scripture is also realistic about conscience: it can be trained or damaged. Paul speaks of consciences that are "weak" (1 Corinthians 8) and "seared" (1 Timothy 4:2) — which is exactly why cultures and individuals can go badly wrong about applications while the underlying law remains. A bent lamp is still a lamp; the possibility of a seared conscience presupposes a true standard it deviates from.

The Fathers on the Law Written Within

▶

The argument of this page is not a modern invention polished by C.S. Lewis. The Fathers of the undivided Church taught the natural moral law and the witness of conscience from the beginning:

St. John Chrysostom
"When God formed man, he implanted within him from the beginning a natural law… He gave utterance to conscience within us; and made the knowledge of good things, and of those which are the contrary, to be self-taught."
Homilies on the Statues 12 (AD 387) — Abel, Noah, and Abraham knew right from wrong with no written law: conscience taught them
Tertullian
"O testimony of the soul, by nature Christian!"
Apology 17 (c. AD 197) — even pagans cry out "God sees" and "God will judge": the soul testifies to its Maker before any catechism reaches it
St. Justin Martyr
"Every race of men knows that adultery, and fornication, and homicide, and such like, are sinful."
Dialogue with Trypho 93 (c. AD 160) — written a century before Constantine: the universality of the moral law was already a Christian argument
St. Irenaeus of Lyons
The righteous patriarchs before Moses "had the meaning of the Decalogue written in their hearts and souls."
Against Heresies 4.16.3 (c. AD 180) — the Ten Commandments publish what creation had already inscribed
St. Augustine
"Theft is punished by Thy law, O Lord, and the law written in the hearts of men, which iniquity itself effaces not."
Confessions 2.4 (c. AD 397) — reflecting on his boyhood theft of pears: he knew it was wrong while doing it, by a law sin cannot erase
St. Anthony the Great, father of monks
"Virtue is not far from us, nor is it without ourselves, but it is within us, and is easy if only we are willing."
St. Athanasius, Life of Anthony 20 (c. AD 360) — the Coptic desert father: the moral law is native to the soul, planted there by its Creator
St. Basil the Great
"The bread which you hold back belongs to the hungry; the coat, which you guard in your locked storage-chests, belongs to the naked."
Homily on "I Will Tear Down My Barns" (c. AD 368) — the moral law in action: not a philosophy but a demand with names and faces

The Law Fulfilled in Christ

▶

In one sentence: the moral law is a signpost, not a destination — it shows the standard, proves that every one of us breaks it, and so drives us to the One who kept it and restores us.

This is where Lewis's argument was always heading, and where this page ends. The moral law gives two pieces of information: there is a standard of behavior pressing on every human heart, and not one of us meets it. Honest self-examination confirms the second as firmly as quarreling confirmed the first. "None is righteous, no, not one" (Romans 3:10) is not pessimism; it is the plain reading of anyone's own record against the law written in his own heart.

So the law, taken seriously, produces a need it cannot satisfy. It diagnoses; it does not heal. That is its God-given function: "through the law comes knowledge of sin" (Romans 3:20). The answer to the diagnosis is not a better philosophy of ethics but a Person — the Lawgiver Himself entering His creation, fulfilling the law He wrote, and then doing what no law could: rewriting it from the inside.

"Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."Matthew 5:17 — the Sermon on the Mount does not relax the moral law; it deepens it from acts to the heart, and the Preacher embodies it
"I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts. And I will be their God, and they shall be my people."Jeremiah 31:33 — the new covenant: the law that was written on hearts at creation, and smudged by sin, is rewritten by the Spirit in Christ

In the Oriental Orthodox understanding, salvation is exactly this restoration: the image of God (above), darkened by the fall (see Original Sin), is renewed in Christ, and the believer grows into His likeness. The moral argument begins with a quarrel on the street and ends in the liturgy: the law that condemned us is fulfilled in the One we receive at the altar.

"But Atheists Are Good People" — The Boomerang

▶

In one sentence: moral atheists do not weaken this argument — they are its evidence, and every moral protest the atheist makes borrows the law he denies.

The most common reply to everything above is: "I'm an atheist and I'm a decent person — so morality clearly doesn't need God." But re-read the argument: it never claimed belief in God is required for moral behavior. It claimed God is required for morality to be real. Romans 2 predicts moral atheists — the law is written on every heart, and no one's unbelief erases the handwriting. The kindness of the atheist is not a problem for the argument; it is Exhibit A.

And the objection boomerangs, just as the problem of evil does (see The Problem of Evil). When the atheist condemns the Crusades, religious hypocrisy, or the God of the Old Testament as wicked, he is not expressing a preference like disliking olives — he means it is really, objectively wrong. But "really, objectively wrong" is exactly what his own worldview cannot supply (Steps 4–5). Every moral accusation against God is launched from a platform only God can hold up.

C.S. Lewis
"My argument against God was that the universe seemed so cruel and unjust. But how had I got this idea of just and unjust? A man does not call a line crooked unless he has some idea of a straight line."
Mere Christianity (1952), Book II — Lewis's own path out of atheism: his strongest objection to God presupposed God

Frequently Asked Questions

▶
Objection: "Cultures disagree about morality, so it can't be objective."▶
Disagreement does not disprove truth — cultures disagreed about the shape of the earth, and the earth had a shape the whole time. Moreover the disagreement is shallower than advertised: it concerns applications (whom the rules cover, how they apply), not the core. No culture has prized cowardice, treachery, or ingratitude. And the objection refutes itself the moment its user condemns another culture's practice — slavery, say — as wrong rather than merely different.
Objection: "Morality is just empathy."▶
Empathy is a feeling — selective, partial, and easily switched off, strongest for the near and the similar. Obligation binds precisely where empathy fails: toward the stranger, the enemy, the unborn, the unappealing. If morality were empathy, you would owe nothing to anyone you don't happen to feel for — and the man with weak empathy would have weak duties. Empathy is one of the instincts the moral law judges (Step 2); it cannot be the judge.
Objection: "Religious people do terrible things too."▶
True, and Scripture says so more fiercely than any atheist — read the prophets, or Romans 2:21–24. But notice: the objection is a moral accusation. Calling the Church's sins "evil" invokes the objective standard this whole page is about. The argument is not "Christians are good, therefore God"; it is "the law both Christians and atheists break and appeal to is real, therefore the Lawgiver is real." Hypocrisy proves the standard; it does not abolish it.
Objection: "Can't we be good for goodness' sake, without God?"▶
You can certainly behave well without believing — Romans 2 again. But "for goodness' sake" assumes goodness is a real thing with a claim on you. That is the whole question. On consistent naturalism (Mackie, Ruse, Step 5), "goodness" is an invented label for evolved preferences — and nobody owes obedience to a label. The phrase "for goodness' sake" is a fossil of theism: it treats Goodness as something almost personal, with a sake. Christians simply know its name.
Objection: "Divine-command morality is just obedience out of fear."▶
This misreads the conclusion. The standard is not God's arbitrary decree backed by threats (the Euthyphro section answers that); it is God's own nature, and we are made in His image. So the moral life is not a slave placating a master — it is an icon growing into its likeness. "Perfect love casts out fear" (1 John 4:18). Fear of judgment is a beginning the Fathers acknowledge, but the goal is love: the law fulfilled, not merely obeyed (Romans 13:10).
Objection: "The Old Testament itself contains commands I find immoral."▶
Two things are true at once. First: the difficulty is real and the Church reads those passages within the whole canon, completed in Christ — who is the full revelation of God's character and the key to everything before Him. Second: notice the shape of the objection. "I find this immoral" appeals to an objective standard above human opinion — which is the very thing atheism cannot supply and this argument establishes. The objection, whatever its force against an interpretation of Scripture, is itself evidence for the moral law, and so for the Lawgiver. You cannot borrow God's measuring stick to beat God with.
Objection: "Sam Harris showed science can determine morality."▶
Harris's argument (The Moral Landscape, 2010) defines goodness as "the well-being of conscious creatures" and then lets science measure well-being. But the definition is the whole trick: science can measure well-being once you've decided well-being is what's good — it cannot tell you that well-being is good, or why anyone is obliged to maximize the stranger's well-being at cost to himself. The is–ought gap (Step 4) is assumed away on page one, not bridged. Even sympathetic philosophers made this point in reviews.

Glossary

▶
Moral Argument
The argument that objective moral facts exist and require God as their ground; classically stated for modern readers in Book I of C.S. Lewis's Mere Christianity.
Objective vs. Subjective
Objective: true independently of anyone's opinion (the earth is round). Subjective: dependent on the subject's feeling (olives taste good). The argument claims basic moral truths are objective.
Natural Law
The moral law God implanted in human nature at creation, knowable by conscience without special revelation (Romans 2:14–15; Chrysostom, Statues 12).
Conscience
The God-given inner witness that applies the moral law to one's own acts — "the lamp of the LORD" (Proverbs 20:27). Can be trained, weakened, or seared, which explains moral variation.
Euthyphro Dilemma
Plato's question: is the good good because God wills it, or willed because it is good? Answered by a third option — goodness is God's own unchanging nature.
Is–Ought Gap
The logical point (associated with David Hume) that no description of what IS can by itself yield what OUGHT to be. Evolution gives only "is"; morality speaks in "ought."
Moral Relativism
The view that moral truth is relative to cultures or individuals. Self-defeating in practice: it cannot condemn any atrocity, rank any reform as progress, or even state "tolerance is good" as more than local custom.
Image of God
The doctrine (Genesis 1:27) that every human bears God's image — the only available ground for equal, immeasurable human dignity, and the reason murder is absolutely forbidden (Genesis 9:6).
