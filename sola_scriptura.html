THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Scripture, Sacred Tradition, and the Authority of the Historic Church

"Stand firm and hold to the traditions that you were taught by us, either by our spoken word or by our letter." — 2 Thessalonians 2:15

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

Sola Scriptura — "Scripture alone" — is the formal principle of the Protestant Reformation. Articulated by Martin Luther at the Diet of Worms (1521) — "my conscience is captive to the Word of God" — it holds that Scripture alone is the supreme and sufficient authority for Christian doctrine and practice. No tradition, council, pope, or bishop may bind the Christian conscience unless what they teach can be verified by Scripture.

The Oriental Orthodox tradition, rooted in the Alexandrian, Antiochene, and Armenian theological schools and representing the faith of the ancient Eastern churches that were never part of Western Christendom, rejects sola scriptura — not because it denigrates Scripture, but because it believes sola scriptura misunderstands the relationship between Scripture, Tradition, and the Church.

For the Oriental Orthodox, Scripture is not opposed to Tradition — Scripture is part of Tradition. The biblical texts were written within the Church, for the Church, and were recognized by the Church. The same Spirit who inspired the authors of Scripture also guides the Church in interpreting it. Scripture and Tradition are not two separate sources of authority competing for supremacy; they are two expressions of the single apostolic deposit of faith.

II. What Is Sola Scriptura? — The Protestant Claim

▼

It is important to distinguish the strong and weak forms of sola scriptura as articulated within Protestantism itself:

Strong Sola Scriptura: Scripture is the only authority for Christian doctrine. Tradition, councils, and creeds have no independent authority — they are useful only insofar as they accurately reflect Scripture, and every Christian may judge for themselves whether they do. This is the position of many Baptist and evangelical traditions.

Weak Sola Scriptura (Prima Scriptura): Scripture is the supreme authority, but tradition, councils, and creeds have derivative authority as witnesses to the correct interpretation of Scripture. This is the position of Reformed and Lutheran confessional Protestantism. The Westminster Confession, the Augsburg Confession, etc. have real authority — but always subject to correction by Scripture.

Both forms, the Oriental Orthodox argue, face fatal problems — the "canon problem" and the "interpretation problem" — which ultimately undermine themselves.

III. The Oriental Orthodox View — Scripture Within Tradition

▼

The Oriental Orthodox do not place Tradition alongside or above Scripture as a separate, competing authority. Rather, they understand Scripture as the written core of the one apostolic Tradition. The Tradition encompasses: the Scriptures themselves, the Creeds and councils, the liturgical life of the Church, the writings of the Fathers, and the ongoing life of the Spirit in the Body of Christ.

St. Basil the Great (c. AD 330–379) articulated the standard Eastern position: "Of the beliefs and practices preserved in the Church, some we possess from written teaching, and others we have received in mystery from the tradition of the apostles. Both have the same force for piety." (On the Holy Spirit, ch. 27)

The apostolic Tradition was transmitted in two modes from the beginning: written (the letters of Paul, the Gospels, etc.) and oral (the preaching, the liturgy, the sacramental practice). These were not two separate streams but two aspects of the one apostolic deposit. The Church — guided by the Holy Spirit — recognized the written texts as Scripture precisely because they recognized them as faithful expressions of the same apostolic faith they already held and practiced.

IV. Scripture Commands Tradition

▼

Remarkably, the Bible itself explicitly commands Christians to hold to tradition — not Scripture alone. This is rarely noted in Protestant discussions of sola scriptura.

2 Thessalonians 2:15
OO
"So then, brothers, stand firm and hold to the traditions that you were taught by us, either by our spoken word or by our letter."
Paul explicitly commands holding to traditions transmitted both in writing ("our letter") AND orally ("our spoken word"). The two modes of transmission — written and oral — are placed on equal footing. Paul does not say "hold only to our letters"; he says hold to everything we taught you, by whatever means. This directly contradicts sola scriptura: Paul himself insists that his oral tradition carries the same authority as his written letters.
2 Timothy 2:2
OO
"And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
The apostolic teaching is transmitted through a chain of persons — from Paul to Timothy to faithful men to others. This is the living oral tradition of the Church. Paul does not say "write it all down and give everyone a Bible." He describes a human, ecclesial chain of transmission that is distinct from (though consistent with) the written texts.
1 Corinthians 11:2
OO
"Now I commend you because you remember me in everything and maintain the traditions even as I delivered them to you."
Paul commends the Corinthians for maintaining the traditions as he delivered them — this is the language of formal transmission (paradosis/paralambanō), the same technical language used throughout the Eastern tradition for the handing-on of the apostolic deposit. The traditions Paul delivered were both oral and sacramental, not merely written.
John 21:25
CTX
"Now there are also many other things that Jesus did. Were every one of them to be written, I suppose that the world itself could not contain the books that would be written."
John explicitly acknowledges that the written Gospels do not capture the totality of Jesus' teaching and actions. The Church possesses more than what was written down — and this "more" is the living tradition: the liturgy, the sacramental practices, the oral teachings preserved in the episcopal succession. Scripture itself points beyond itself to a living, unwritten tradition.
2 John 1:12
CTX
"Though I have much to write to you, I would rather not use paper and ink. Instead I hope to come to you and talk face to face, so that our joy may be complete."
The Apostle John deliberately withholds teaching from the written letter, preferring to transmit it face-to-face. This is not laziness — it reflects the apostolic conviction that the full deposit of faith is communicated through personal presence and oral instruction, not only through writing. The preference for personal, living transmission over the written text is an implicit critique of sola scriptura's exclusive focus on the written word.

V. The Canon Problem — Scripture Cannot Define Itself

▼

The most fundamental problem with sola scriptura is the canon problem: sola scriptura requires knowing which books are Scripture — but Scripture itself does not contain a list of its own books. The 27-book New Testament canon was determined not by the New Testament but by the Church, through a gradual process of discernment, conciliar discussion, and episcopal consensus that culminated in the late 4th century.

The key councils that defined the New Testament canon were: the Council of Laodicea (c. AD 360), the Synod of Hippo (AD 393), and the Third Council of Carthage (AD 397). The bishops who made these determinations were the same bishops who practiced and defended the full sacramental, episcopal, creedal tradition of the Church — the tradition that Protestants reject as non-binding.

This creates an irresolvable inconsistency for sola scriptura: the Protestant accepts the canon on the authority of the Church's tradition, then rejects the Church's tradition as non-binding because "Scripture alone" is the authority. But which Scripture? The Scripture the Church defined. The Protestant accepts the product of the tradition while rejecting the tradition that produced it.

Furthermore, different Christian traditions have different canons: the Protestant canon (66 books), the Catholic canon (73 books), the Eastern Orthodox canon (varies, generally larger), and the Oriental Orthodox canon (the Ethiopian Church has 81 books — the largest canon in Christendom). If Scripture alone were sufficient, we would expect agreement on which books constitute Scripture. Instead we find centuries of disagreement resolved only by — the Church's tradition.

VI. The Interpretation Problem — Scripture Cannot Interpret Itself

▼

Even granting agreement on the canon, sola scriptura faces a second fatal problem: Scripture requires interpretation, and interpreters disagree. If Scripture alone is the supreme and sufficient authority, and individual Christians read it and reach different conclusions, who adjudicates? The answer within Protestantism is: everyone interprets for themselves, guided by the Holy Spirit.

The result is visible in history: from the single principle of sola scriptura in 1521, Protestantism has produced over 30,000 denominations — each claiming to follow Scripture, each disagreeing with the others on the nature of Baptism, the Eucharist, church government, predestination, and dozens of other issues. This is not a caricature; it is the documented historical result of the application of sola scriptura over five centuries.

The Oriental Orthodox approach is different: the authoritative interpretation of Scripture belongs to the Church — specifically to the bishops in council as the successors of the Apostles. The Ecumenical Councils (Nicaea, Ephesus, etc.) did not impose meanings on Scripture from outside; they articulated the Church's consistent, received understanding of Scripture against heretical novelties. The Council of Nicaea did not invent the Trinity; it defended the apostolic understanding of Christ against Arius's novel interpretation.

VII. The Church Fathers on Scripture and Tradition

▼
St. Basil the Great (c. AD 330–379)
On the Holy Spirit, Chapter 27 — c. AD 375
"Of the beliefs and practices whether generally accepted or publicly enjoined which are preserved in the Church, some we possess derived from written teaching; others we have received delivered to us in a mystery by the tradition of the apostles; and both of these in relation to true religion have the same force."
Significance: Basil is the defining Eastern statement on Scripture and Tradition. "Both have the same force" — written and oral apostolic tradition carry equal authority for piety. Basil then gives specific examples of apostolic traditions not found in Scripture: signing with the cross, facing east in prayer, the epiclesis over the Eucharist. These practices, he insists, are apostolic tradition with full authority even without written documentation.
St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202)
Against Heresies, Book III, Chapter 4 — c. AD 180
"What if the apostles had not left us the Scriptures? Would it not be necessary to follow the course of the tradition which they handed down to those to whom they did commit the Churches? To which course many nations of those barbarians who believe in Christ do assent, having salvation written in their hearts by the Spirit, without paper and ink, and, carefully preserving the ancient tradition."
Significance: Irenaeus imagines a Church without written Scripture and asks: would such a Church be without authority? His answer is no — the apostolic tradition in the Church would be sufficient. He points to actual barbarian peoples who received the faith orally without written Scripture and held it faithfully. Scripture is the written deposit of the apostolic tradition; it is not the only form of that deposit.
St. Vincent of Lérins (died c. AD 445)
Commonitory, Chapter 2 — c. AD 434
"Since the canon of Scripture is complete, and sufficient of itself for everything, and more than sufficient, what need is there to join with it the authority of the Church's interpretation? For this reason — because, owing to the depth of Holy Scripture, all do not accept it in one and the same sense, but one understands its words in one way, another in another."
Significance: Vincent articulates precisely the "interpretation problem" of sola scriptura 1,100 years before the Reformation. He acknowledges Scripture's sufficiency in principle — but insists that the Church's interpretive tradition is necessary in practice precisely because individuals read Scripture differently. His famous "Vincentian canon" — what has been believed "everywhere, always, and by all" — is the antidote to individualistic interpretation.
St. John Chrysostom (c. AD 347–407)
Homily 4 on 2 Thessalonians — c. AD 400
"Hence it is manifest that they did not deliver all things by letter, but there is much also that was not written. Like that also, the unwritten tradition is worthy of belief. So that we consider the tradition of the Church also to be worthy of belief. It is a tradition — seek no further."
Significance: Chrysostom — commenting on 2 Thessalonians 2:15, Paul's command to hold traditions delivered orally — explicitly states that unwritten tradition is worthy of belief. His conclusion, "It is a tradition — seek no further," is a direct affirmation of the authority of oral apostolic tradition independent of its written documentation. This is the universal Eastern patristic position.

VIII. What Is Sacred Tradition? — A Clarification

▼

A common Protestant misunderstanding is that "Tradition" in the Oriental Orthodox sense means human customs, clerical traditions, or later additions to the original faith. This misunderstands what the Eastern tradition means by Paradosis (the Tradition).

Sacred Tradition in the Oriental Orthodox sense encompasses:

  • The Holy Scriptures themselves — The written texts are part of the Tradition, not separate from it.
  • The Ecumenical Councils — The definitions of Nicaea (325), Constantinople (381), Ephesus (431), and the Oriental Orthodox councils, which articulate the apostolic faith.
  • The Creeds — The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed as the summary of apostolic faith, prayed by the whole Church at every Eucharist.
  • The Liturgy — The Divine Liturgy of St. Basil, St. Cyril (St. Mark), and the other ancient Eucharistic prayers, which embody and transmit the faith in worship.
  • The Church Fathers — The theological writings of the Fathers, who represent the accumulated wisdom of the apostolic succession in interpreting Scripture and defending the faith.
  • The ascetic and sacramental life — The lived practice of prayer, fasting, the sacraments, and the pursuit of theosis that has been handed down through generations.

Sacred Tradition is not a static set of texts alongside the Bible but the living, breathing life of the Church guided by the Holy Spirit — the same Spirit who inspired the Scriptures in the first place. To place Scripture against Tradition is to place the Spirit against himself.

IX. Protestant Objections

▼

Objection: Jesus condemned "the traditions of men" in Mark 7 — tradition corrupts Scripture

▼

The Claim

Jesus explicitly rebuked the Pharisees for "teaching as doctrines the commandments of men" (Mark 7:7) and for "making void the word of God by your tradition" (Mark 7:13). This shows that human tradition is dangerous and should not be given authority alongside Scripture.

Response

The Oriental Orthodox fully agree that human tradition can corrupt the word of God — and this is precisely why the Oriental Orthodox insist on apostolic tradition, not arbitrary human customs. The Pharisaic traditions Jesus condemned were post-biblical oral rulings that contradicted the plain sense of Scripture. The apostolic tradition that the Oriental Orthodox affirm — the liturgy, the Creeds, the councils — does not contradict Scripture; it is the context in which Scripture was received, preserved, and correctly understood. Furthermore, Paul in the same breath condemns Gnostic/pagan "philosophy and empty deceit, according to human tradition" (Colossians 2:8) while commanding fidelity to apostolic tradition (2 Thess 2:15). The distinction Jesus and Paul both draw is between apostolic/divine tradition and human tradition — not between Scripture and all tradition.

Objection: 2 Timothy 3:16–17 says Scripture makes us "complete, equipped for every good work" — so it is sufficient

▼

The Claim

"All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work" (2 Tim 3:16–17). Scripture is sufficient — it makes us "complete."

Response

This passage establishes the divine inspiration and profitability of Scripture — which the Oriental Orthodox fully affirm. But "complete, equipped for every good work" does not mean "Scripture alone is the only authority." A well-equipped soldier is equipped for every battle — but this doesn't mean a compass, a map, and fellow soldiers are useless. Paul tells Timothy that Scripture equips him for good works; he also tells Timothy to hold to the traditions (2 Tim 1:13–14), to guard the deposit entrusted to him (2 Tim 1:14), to entrust what he heard to faithful men (2 Tim 2:2), and to follow the pattern of sound words (2 Tim 1:13). The same letter that contains 3:16–17 commands traditional transmission of the apostolic deposit — the very thing sola scriptura denies. Furthermore, "the Scriptures" Timothy knew from childhood (3:15) were primarily the Old Testament — not the full New Testament canon, which was not yet determined.

Objection: The Church Fathers also held to Scripture's supremacy — they are not evidence for Tradition as an independent authority

▼

The Claim

When Irenaeus, Basil, or Chrysostom appeal to tradition, they mean Scripture — they always tested tradition by Scripture, and never placed tradition as an authority equal to or above Scripture. They were proto-Protestants who would have agreed with sola scriptura.

Response

This claim does not survive a reading of the Fathers. Basil explicitly states that written and oral apostolic tradition "have the same force" — not that oral tradition derives its authority from Scripture. Chrysostom says "it is a tradition — seek no further" without appealing to a scriptural warrant. Irenaeus appeals to the succession of bishops as his primary argument against Gnostic heresy — not to Scripture alone, because the Gnostics also claimed to follow Scripture (their own versions of it). The Fathers did not test tradition by Scripture; they used Scripture and tradition together as the two modes of the single apostolic deposit. To claim the Fathers for sola scriptura requires either ignoring their actual statements or drastically reinterpreting them.

X. FAQ

▼
Q: Does the Oriental Orthodox Church add to Scripture?▼

No. The Oriental Orthodox Church adds nothing to Scripture — it receives and transmits the apostolic deposit of which Scripture is the written center. The Creeds, councils, and patristic writings do not supplement Scripture with new revelation; they articulate and defend the meaning of Scripture against error. The Nicene Creed does not add to John 1:1 ("the Word was God") — it clarifies what "God" means in that context against the Arian denial of Christ's full divinity. This is not addition; it is faithful interpretation.

Q: How does the Oriental Orthodox Church handle apparent contradictions between Scripture and Tradition?▼

The Oriental Orthodox do not experience Scripture and Tradition as competing authorities that can contradict each other — they are two modes of the one apostolic deposit. An apparent contradiction between Scripture and a patristic teaching signals either a misinterpretation of Scripture, a misinterpretation of the Father, or a later addition to "tradition" that is not genuinely apostolic. The criterion for authentic tradition is precisely the Vincentian canon: what has been believed everywhere, always, and by all. A practice or teaching that emerges in the 5th century without earlier attestation does not qualify as apostolic tradition simply because a Church claims it.

Q: If tradition is authoritative, how do you avoid traditionalism — repeating errors just because they are old?▼

This is a genuine concern and the Oriental Orthodox take it seriously. The safeguard is the breadth and universality of the criterion: genuine apostolic tradition is attested across multiple independent churches, in multiple languages, from the earliest centuries. A local practice or a single Father's opinion does not constitute Tradition in the binding sense. What is held "everywhere, always, and by all" (Vincent of Lérins) — the Nicene faith, the seven sacraments, the apostolic succession — is binding. What is held locally or recently has proportionally less authority. This is why the Oriental Orthodox appeal to Ecumenical Councils, not merely to their own patriarchal decisions.
