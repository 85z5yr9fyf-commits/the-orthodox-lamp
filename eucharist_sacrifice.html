I. Introduction

▼

For the Oriental Orthodox — as for the whole ancient Church — the Eucharist is not a bare symbol or a mere memory meal. It is the true Body and Blood of Christ, and the Church's offering of it is a real, though unbloody, sacrifice: the one sacrifice of Calvary made present on the altar. This is the faith of the Divine Liturgy of St. Mark, the Anaphora of St. Basil, and every ancient rite East and West.

Most Protestant traditions deny this, holding that the Lord's Supper is a memorial or a symbol, and that to call it a "sacrifice" contradicts the letter to the Hebrews, which teaches that Christ died "once for all." This page sets out the biblical foundation for the Eucharistic sacrifice, shows how it is reconciled with Hebrews, and marshals the witness of the earliest Church — including writers within living memory of the apostles.

II. The Memorialist Claim

▼

The objection to the Eucharistic sacrifice usually takes two forms. First, the symbolic claim: when Christ said "this is my body," he spoke figuratively, as he did when he called himself a door or a vine; the Supper is a memorial that stirs our faith, nothing more. Second, the Hebrews claim: the epistle insists Christ offered himself "once for all" (Heb 10:10), so any ongoing sacrifice would deny the sufficiency of the Cross and amount to "re-crucifying" Christ.

The historic Church answers both. The words of institution are not treated figuratively by the apostolic Church; Paul warns that to eat unworthily is to be "guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord" (1 Cor 11:27) — language impossible of a mere symbol. And the Eucharist does not repeat or add to Calvary; it re-presents the one eternal sacrifice, making the faithful partakers of it. The Mass adds nothing to the Cross; it applies it.

III. The Institution — A New Passover

▼

Christ instituted the Eucharist within the Passover — the great sacrificial meal of Israel — and gave it in explicitly sacrificial and covenantal terms.

Matthew 26:26–28
Words of institution
"This is my body… this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins."
"Poured out… for the forgiveness of sins" is sacrificial language drawn from the temple. The cup is his blood "of the covenant" — echoing Exodus 24, where covenant blood was sprinkled on the people.
Luke 22:19 / 1 Corinthians 11:24–25
"Do this in remembrance"
"This is my body, which is given for you. Do this in remembrance of me."
The Greek anamnésis ("remembrance") is not a bare recollection but a liturgical making-present, the same word used for the memorial offerings of the temple (Lev 24:7; Num 10:10). "Do this" — touto poieite — can bear the sense "offer this."
1 Corinthians 11:26–27
Guilt of the body and blood
"Whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord."
One cannot be "guilty of the body and blood" of a mere symbol. Paul presumes a real presence, received worthily or unworthily.

Christ is "our Passover lamb" who "has been sacrificed" (1 Cor 5:7). As Israel did not merely remember the Exodus at Passover but entered into it anew each year, so the Church does not merely recall Calvary — she is made a partaker of the one sacrifice of the true Lamb.

IV. The Eucharist as Sacrifice in Scripture

▼

Beyond the institution, Scripture speaks of the Christian assembly as having an altar, a participation in Christ's body and blood, and a pure offering foretold by the prophets.

Malachi 1:11
The pure offering foretold
"From the rising of the sun to its setting my name will be great among the nations, and in every place incense will be offered to my name, and a pure offering."
A single, universal, "pure offering" among all the Gentile nations — fulfilled, the Fathers say, in the Eucharist offered in every place. The temple sacrifices were confined to Jerusalem; this offering is worldwide.
1 Corinthians 10:16–21
Participation and the altar
"The cup of blessing that we bless, is it not a participation in the blood of Christ?… You cannot partake of the table of the Lord and the table of demons."
Paul sets the Lord's "table" (an altar term) against pagan sacrificial tables. The Eucharist is the koinônia — real participation — in Christ's body and blood, parallel in structure to sacrificial eating.
Hebrews 13:10
"We have an altar"
"We have an altar from which those who serve the tent have no right to eat."
The Christian community has an altar — and an altar exists for sacrifice. The verse ties Christian worship to a sacrificial reality from which the old priesthood is excluded.
John 6:53–55
True food and drink
"Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you… For my flesh is true food, and my blood is true drink."
When the crowd recoiled, Christ did not soften the saying as metaphor; he intensified it. The eucharistic realism of the ancient Church rests here.

V. The One Sacrifice, Re-Presented — Answering Hebrews

▼

The strongest objection is drawn from Hebrews: Christ "offered for all time a single sacrifice for sins" (Heb 10:12); "we have been sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all" (Heb 10:10). If the sacrifice is finished, how can the Church offer it?

The historic answer distinguishes between repetition and re-presentation. The Church does not slay Christ again or add a new sacrifice to Calvary. There is one sacrifice, offered once in time on the Cross — but that one sacrifice is eternal in its power, and Christ is described in heaven as "a Lamb standing, as though it had been slain" (Rev 5:6): the sacrificed Lamb perpetually before the Father. In the Eucharist, that single, all-sufficient offering is made present to the Church across time, so that we who were not at Golgotha may nonetheless partake of its fruit.

This is precisely what Hebrews itself implies: Christ is a priest "forever after the order of Melchizedek" (Heb 7:17) — and Melchizedek's priestly act was to bring out bread and wine (Gen 14:18). A priest forever must have a continuing offering. The Eucharist is not a second Calvary but the one Calvary, entered into. Far from denying Hebrews, the Eucharistic sacrifice depends on it: because the offering is once-for-all and eternal, it can be re-presented without ever being repeated or diminished.

VI. The Witness of the Early Church

▼

If the sacrificial understanding of the Eucharist were a later corruption, the earliest Christian writers would speak of the Supper only as a symbol. Instead, from the first century onward, they call it a sacrifice, an offering, and the true Body and Blood of Christ.

The Didache (c. A.D. 90–110)
"On the Lord's own day gather together and break bread and give thanks, having first confessed your sins so that your sacrifice may be pure… for this is that which was spoken by the Lord: In every place and time offer to me a pure sacrifice; for I am a great King, says the Lord, and my name is wonderful among the nations."
— Didache 14. Direct quote (ANF). The oldest church order calls the Eucharist a "sacrifice" (thusia) and applies Malachi 1:11 to it.
St. Ignatius of Antioch (c. A.D. 107)
"Take ye heed, then, to have but one Eucharist. For there is one flesh of our Lord Jesus Christ, and one cup to [show forth] the unity of His blood; one altar; as there is one bishop, along with the presbytery and deacons."
— Epistle to the Philadelphians 4. Direct quote (ANF). A hearer of the apostles speaks of "one altar" and "one Eucharist" — the flesh and blood of the Lord.
St. Justin Martyr (c. A.D. 155)
"[The bread and the cup of the Eucharist are] the sacrifices which we Gentiles offer to Him in every place, that is, the bread of the Eucharist, and also the cup of the Eucharist… God speaks by the mouth of Malachi… 'in every place incense is offered to My name, and a pure offering.'"
— Dialogue with Trypho 41. Direct quote (ANF). Justin identifies the Eucharist as the "pure offering" of Malachi, offered by the Gentiles "in every place."
St. Irenaeus of Lyons (c. A.D. 180)
"The oblation of the Church, therefore, which the Lord gave instructions to be offered throughout all the world, is accounted with God a pure sacrifice, and is acceptable to Him… For as the bread, which is produced from the earth, when it receives the invocation of God, is no longer common bread, but the Eucharist, consisting of two realities, earthly and heavenly."
— Against Heresies IV.18. Direct quote (ANF). Irenaeus calls the Church's oblation "a pure sacrifice" and denies it is "common bread."

These witnesses — from Syria, Rome, and Gaul, within decades of the apostles — are unanimous. The Eucharist was understood as a true sacrifice and the real Body and Blood of Christ from the very beginning, not as an innovation of a later age.

VII. Objections & Answers

▼
"Hebrews says Christ died once for all — the Mass re-crucifies him."▼

The Claim

Since Christ's sacrifice was "once for all" (Heb 10:10), any continuing Eucharistic sacrifice contradicts Scripture and denies the sufficiency of the Cross.

Response

The Church does not repeat Calvary; she re-presents it. There is one sacrifice, offered once in time, eternal in power — the Lamb "standing, as though it had been slain" before the Father (Rev 5:6). The Eucharist makes that one offering present so we may partake of it; it adds nothing and repeats nothing. A priest "forever after the order of Melchizedek" (Heb 7:17), whose sign was bread and wine, must have a continuing offering. The Eucharistic sacrifice rests on the once-for-all Cross, it does not compete with it.

"'This is my body' is figurative, like 'I am the vine.'"▼

The Claim

Christ spoke symbolically. Just as he called himself a door and a vine, "this is my body" means "this represents my body."

Response

When the crowd took John 6 literally and were scandalized, Christ did not correct them toward metaphor — he intensified the saying and let disciples leave (John 6:60–66). Paul warns that unworthy reception makes one "guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord" (1 Cor 11:27) and brings judgment for "not discerning the body" (11:29) — impossible of a bare symbol. And the entire ancient Church, including writers who knew the apostles, received the words as real. "I am the vine" was never the subject of such warnings or such unanimous realism.

"The sacrificial view was invented in the Middle Ages."▼

The Claim

The idea of the Eucharist as sacrifice, and transubstantiation-like realism, developed centuries later and is absent from the early Church.

Response

The Didache (c. 90–110) already calls the Eucharist a "sacrifice" and cites Malachi 1:11; Ignatius (c. 107) speaks of "one altar"; Justin (c. 155) names it the "pure offering" of the Gentiles; Irenaeus (c. 180) calls it "a pure sacrifice" and "no longer common bread." This is the first- and second-century witness, a millennium before the medieval scholastics. The precise terminology developed, but the reality was confessed from the start (see How the Church Knows on development versus corruption).

"Malachi 1:11 refers to prayer or good works, not the Eucharist."▼

The Claim

The "pure offering" in Malachi is a metaphor for the spiritual sacrifices of prayer and praise, not a literal liturgical offering.

Response

The earliest interpreters — the Didache and Justin Martyr, closest in time to the apostles — apply Malachi's "pure offering… in every place" directly to the Eucharist, precisely because it is the one universal offering fulfilling what the localized temple sacrifices foreshadowed. Prayer and praise are indeed spiritual sacrifices, but Malachi speaks of a single, definite offering among the nations, which the ancient Church unhesitatingly identified as the Eucharist.

VIII. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Does "sacrifice" mean Christ suffers again?▼

No. The Eucharistic sacrifice is unbloody. Christ does not die again or suffer again. The one bloody sacrifice of the Cross is made sacramentally present, so that its fruit is applied to the faithful. The Church's classic phrase is that the Liturgy "re-presents" — makes present again — the single sacrifice, without ever repeating it.

Is this the same as the Roman Catholic Mass?▼

The Oriental Orthodox share with Rome the ancient faith that the Eucharist is a true sacrifice and the real Body and Blood of Christ. The Orthodox generally avoid the specific philosophical term "transubstantiation" and the scholastic categories that came with it, preferring to confess the mystery (the change is real; the manner is God's) without over-defining it. The shared reality is older and deeper than the later Western terminology.

What is the "epiclesis"?▼

The epiclesis is the invocation of the Holy Spirit upon the gifts, asking the Father to send the Spirit to make the bread and wine the Body and Blood of Christ. Irenaeus already speaks of the bread receiving "the invocation of God" and becoming "no longer common bread." It is a hallmark of the ancient Eastern liturgies and expresses that the change is the work of God, not of man.
