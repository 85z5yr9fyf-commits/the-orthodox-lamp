THE ORTHODOX LAMP

Infant Baptism — A Biblical and Historical Defense

"Let the children come to me… for to such belongs the kingdom of heaven." — Matthew 19:14

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

The baptism of infants and young children is one of the most ancient and universally attested practices of the Christian Church. Yet it is also one of the most contested — rejected by Baptist and Anabaptist traditions, questioned by many evangelical Protestants, and sometimes misunderstood even by those who practice it. This site examines the biblical, historical, and patristic evidence for infant baptism from the perspective of the Oriental Orthodox tradition.

The Oriental Orthodox churches (Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara) have baptized infants from time immemorial — not as a cultural holdover or clerical convenience, but as a theological conviction rooted in Scripture, in the nature of the New Covenant, and in the unanimous witness of the early Church. Infant baptism is not something "added" to Christianity; it is the original and universal practice that some later Protestant movements abandoned.

Moreover, in the Oriental Orthodox tradition, baptism is administered together with Chrismation (Confirmation) and the Eucharist in a single rite — infants are fully initiated into the Body of Christ and receive communion from their very first day as baptized Christians. This ancient and apostolic practice stands in sharp contrast to the delayed confirmation and "first communion" practices of later Western Christianity.

II. What Is Baptism? — The Sacramental Foundation

▼

Before asking whether infants should be baptized, the Oriental Orthodox tradition begins with the prior question: What is Baptism? The answer to this question determines everything. If Baptism is primarily a public declaration of personal faith — a testimony to an experience already received — then clearly infants who cannot articulate faith should not be baptized. But if Baptism is what the New Testament actually says it is, the question looks entirely different.

What the New Testament says Baptism is

John 3:5
OO
"Unless one is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God."
Christ's own words: birth of water and Spirit is the condition for entering God's kingdom. This is not a declaration of an already-existing birth; it is the means by which the new birth occurs. Baptism is new birth — a divine action upon the recipient, not a human declaration of an internal experience.
Romans 6:3–4
OO
"Do you not know that all of us who have been baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death? We were buried therefore with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead… we too might walk in newness of life."
Baptism is death and resurrection with Christ — a participation in the Paschal mystery. This is not symbolic language in Paul; it is ontological. Something real happens in Baptism: union with Christ's death and resurrection. This divine act is performed by God upon the one baptized; it does not require the recipient's cognitive articulation.
Colossians 2:11–12
OO
"In him also you were circumcised with a circumcision made without hands… having been buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him through faith in the powerful working of God."
Paul explicitly compares Baptism to circumcision — the Old Covenant rite of initiation that was administered to infants on the eighth day. Just as circumcision initiated infants into the covenant community of Israel, Baptism initiates into the New Covenant community of Christ. This parallel is the apostolic argument for infant baptism.
Titus 3:5
OO
"He saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy, by the washing of regeneration and renewing of the Holy Spirit."
"The washing of regeneration" — Baptism is explicitly called a washing that regenerates (produces new birth). Regeneration is not a human achievement but a divine mercy. The parallel with infant baptism is natural: infants cannot contribute "works of righteousness," which is precisely why their baptism is the purest expression of grace — entirely God's act, not a response to human faith or effort.
Acts 2:38–39
OO
"Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. For the promise is for you and for your children and for all who are far off."
Peter's Pentecost sermon makes the promise of Baptism explicitly multigenerational: "for you and for your children." The word "children" (Greek: teknois) covers offspring including infants. The promise of forgiveness and the Holy Spirit is extended to children — not as a future adult decision, but as part of the immediate apostolic offer to every household.

III. Old Testament Types — Circumcision and the Covenant

▼

The most important Old Testament foundation for infant baptism is the theology of covenant initiation. God has always welcomed children into the covenant community — not on the basis of their personal decision or articulated faith, but through the covenant of their parents and the gracious initiative of God himself.

Genesis 17:9–12
CTX
"This is my covenant, which you shall keep, between me and you and your offspring after you: Every male among you shall be circumcised… He who is eight days old among you shall be circumcised."
God's covenant with Abraham explicitly includes infants — "he who is eight days old." The covenant sign is placed on infants who have no capacity for faith or decision. This establishes the fundamental principle: God's covenant grace is extended to children within the covenant family. The New Covenant does not contract this expansion of grace — it intensifies it.
Colossians 2:11–12
OO
"In him also you were circumcised with a circumcision made without hands… having been buried with him in baptism."
Paul explicitly identifies Baptism as the New Covenant circumcision — "the circumcision of Christ." This identification is the apostolic warrant for infant baptism: just as circumcision was the covenant sign for infants under the Old Covenant, Baptism is the covenant sign for infants under the New Covenant. To refuse baptism to infants is to give them less than the Old Covenant gave to Abraham's children.
Joel 2:16
CTX
"Gather the people. Consecrate the congregation; assemble the elders; gather the children, even nursing infants."
In the prophetic vision of the great assembly before God, nursing infants are explicitly included — gathered and consecrated with the whole people. This inclusive vision of God's covenant community encompassing even the youngest children undergirds the New Testament practice of bringing children into the Church through Baptism.
Psalm 22:9–10
CTX
"You are he who took me from the womb; you made me trust you at my mother's breasts. On you was I cast from my birth, and from my mother's womb you have been my God."
The Psalmist describes a faith-relationship with God that began at birth — not at some point of conscious adult decision. This Psalm (quoted extensively in the New Testament as Messianic) affirms that the God of Israel relates to human beings from the womb. The new birth of Baptism is the New Covenant fulfillment of this womb-to-tomb covenantal relationship.

IV. New Testament Evidence

▼
Matthew 19:13–15
OO
"Then children were brought to him that he might lay his hands on them and pray. The disciples rebuked the people, but Jesus said, 'Let the little children come to me and do not hinder them, for to such belongs the kingdom of heaven.'"
Jesus places his hands on children and blesses them — the same gesture of blessing/ordination used throughout Scripture. His declaration "to such belongs the kingdom of heaven" is not merely poetic; it is a statement of covenantal belonging. "Do not hinder them" became the classic baptismal formula in the early Church — used in the context of baptismal instruction to ensure that baptism was not withheld from children (cf. Acts 8:36).
Mark 10:13–16
OO
"And he took them in his arms and blessed them, laying his hands on them."
Mark adds that Jesus "took them in his arms" — an intimate, enfolding gesture of welcome and belonging. The children did not walk to Jesus by their own volition; they were brought by their parents. This pattern — children brought to Christ by faith-filled parents — is the New Testament model for infant baptism. The children's own faith is not the operative factor; Christ's welcome is.
1 Corinthians 7:14
OO
"For the unbelieving husband is made holy because of his wife, and the unbelieving wife is made holy because of her husband. Otherwise your children would be unclean, but as it is, they are holy."
Paul declares children of at least one believing parent to be "holy" — a liturgical, covenantal term denoting belonging to the sacred sphere of God's people. The children are not holy because of their own faith but because of their parents' covenant standing. This covenantal holiness is exactly what infant baptism seals and confers — it is not invented in baptism but confirmed and ratified by it.
Luke 18:15–17
OO
"Now they were bringing even infants to him that he might touch them… Jesus called them to him, saying, 'Let the children come to me, and do not hinder them, for to such belongs the kingdom of God.'"
Luke specifies "infants" (Greek: brephē — nursing babies, not merely older children). These are pre-cognitive, pre-decisional infants being brought to Jesus — and Jesus receives them and declares the kingdom theirs. The kingdom belongs to these infants not because of their decision but because of Christ's sovereign welcome. Infant baptism is the sacramental extension of this welcome.
Acts 16:15
OO
"After she was baptized, and her household as well, she urged us, saying, 'If you have judged me to be faithful to the Lord, come to my house and stay.'"
Lydia's entire household is baptized on the basis of her faith — not on the basis of each member's individual profession. Ancient households included children, infants, and servants. The household baptism pattern throughout Acts (see also Cornelius in Acts 10:48, the Philippian jailer in Acts 16:33, Crispus in Acts 18:8, Stephanas in 1 Cor 1:16) strongly implies that infants and young children were included in these communal baptisms.
Acts 16:33
OO
"And he took them the same hour of the night and washed their wounds; and he was baptized at once, he and all his family."
The Philippian jailer is baptized "at once" in the middle of the night — with his entire family. There is no separate faith-examination of each household member. The solidarity of the household with the head's conversion is the operating principle — a thoroughly covenantal pattern consistent with the entire sweep of biblical covenant theology from Abraham onward.

V. Household Baptisms — The New Covenant Pattern

▼

The household baptism pattern in Acts is one of the strongest arguments for infant baptism. In every case, an entire household is baptized on the basis of the head of household's faith — with no record of individual faith examinations of each member. Given that ancient Mediterranean households routinely included infants, young children, and servants, the silence about excluding children is deafening.

The five household baptisms recorded in the New Testament are: Cornelius's household (Acts 10:24, 48), Lydia's household (Acts 16:15), the Philippian jailer's household (Acts 16:33), Crispus's household (Acts 18:8), and the household of Stephanas (1 Corinthians 1:16). In each case, the pattern is: one person believes → the whole household is baptized.

This pattern is not accidental. It reflects the covenantal theology running throughout Scripture: when the head of a household enters into covenant with God, the household enters with them. Abraham's household was circumcised with him (Genesis 17:23, 27). Rahab's household was saved with her (Joshua 6:25). The Passover lamb protected each household as a unit (Exodus 12:3–4). The New Covenant does not eliminate this household-inclusive principle; it fulfills it in Baptism.

VI. Witness of the Church Fathers

▼
Origen of Alexandria (c. AD 184–253)
Commentary on Romans, 5:9 — c. AD 244
"The Church has received from the apostles the tradition of giving baptism also to the little children. For those who have been entrusted with the secrets of the divine mysteries knew very well that all have the natural stains of sin which must be washed away through water and the Spirit."
Significance: Origen — the greatest biblical scholar of the pre-Nicene period — explicitly states that infant baptism was received from the Apostles as a tradition. He does not present it as a local custom or his personal opinion but as apostolic tradition universally practiced in the Church. This is the clearest early patristic statement attributing infant baptism to apostolic institution.
St. Hippolytus of Rome (c. AD 170–235)
Apostolic Tradition, Chapter 21 — c. AD 215
"First baptize the little ones. All those who are able to speak for themselves shall speak. But for those who cannot speak for themselves, their parents or someone from their family shall speak."
Significance: The Apostolic Tradition — one of the oldest liturgical documents in Christianity — specifies that children who cannot speak are baptized first, with parents speaking for them. This is not a controversial innovation being introduced; it is presented as the established liturgical order. By AD 215 at the latest, infant baptism with proxy faith-profession by parents was the universal Roman (and presumably universal) practice.
St. Cyprian of Carthage (c. AD 200–258)
Epistle 58, To Fidus — c. AD 253
"But in respect of the case of the infants, which you say ought not to be baptized within the second or third day after their birth… we all thought very differently in our council. For in this course no one of us thought that there was anything to be followed but the mercy and grace of God… we ought not to hinder any person from baptism and the grace of God. This is to be observed and maintained for all, but especially for infants and newly-born persons."
Significance: A bishop named Fidus had written to Cyprian asking whether baptism should be delayed until the eighth day (like circumcision). Cyprian convened a council of 66 bishops, who unanimously agreed that baptism should not be delayed — infants should be baptized as soon as possible after birth. This synodal letter reveals: (1) infant baptism was universal and unquestioned in mid-3rd-century North Africa; (2) the only question was when, not whether. No bishop argued against infant baptism.
St. Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430)
On Forgiveness of Sins and Baptism, Book I — c. AD 411
"This is the firm tradition of the universal Church in the matter of baptizing infants, who certainly are not yet able to believe with the heart or confess with the mouth, but receive the sacrament of salvation. If any one seeks for divine authority in this matter, though what is held by the whole Church, and that not as instituted by Councils, but as a matter of invariable custom, is rightly held to have been handed down by apostolical authority, still we can form a true conjecture of the value of the sacrament of baptism in the case of infants from the parallel of circumcision."
Significance: Augustine — the most influential theologian in Western church history — explicitly declares infant baptism to be: (1) the universal and invariable custom of the whole Church; (2) not instituted by councils (i.e., not a later development) but received from apostolic authority; (3) theologically grounded in the parallel with circumcision. Augustine's testimony confirms that by the late 4th/early 5th century, infant baptism was so universal that no one seriously disputed it.
St. John Chrysostom (c. AD 347–407)
Baptismal Catecheses — c. AD 388
"We baptize infants, although they are not guilty of any sins, in order that there may be given to them holiness, righteousness, adoption, inheritance, brotherhood with Christ, and that they may be his members." PARAPHRASE
Significance: Chrysostom identifies the purpose of infant baptism not primarily as forgiveness of original sin (Augustine's emphasis) but as the positive bestowal of grace — holiness, righteousness, adoption, inheritance, brotherhood with Christ, membership in his Body. This Eastern theological emphasis — Baptism as positive initiation into the divine life, not merely removal of guilt — characterizes the Oriental Orthodox theology of infant baptism to this day.

VII. Historical Timeline

▼
c. AD 50–100
New Testament Age — Five household baptisms recorded in Acts and Paul's letters, with no age restrictions mentioned. Jesus explicitly welcomes nursing infants (Luke 18:15) and declares the kingdom belongs to them.
c. AD 215
Hippolytus — Apostolic Tradition — Full baptismal rite specifying that children who cannot speak are baptized first, with parents speaking for them. No hint of controversy or novelty.
c. AD 244
Origen — Explicitly states infant baptism was "received from the Apostles" as tradition. Attributes it to apostolic institution, not gradual development.
c. AD 253
Cyprian's Council of 66 Bishops — Unanimously affirms infant baptism and rules it should not be delayed. The entire North African episcopate agrees without dissent.
c. AD 390–430
Augustine vs. Pelagianism — Pelagius denied original sin; Augustine defended it, with infant baptism as a key piece of evidence: why would the Church universally baptize infants if there were nothing to cleanse? Augustine's defense established infant baptism in Western theology for over a millennium.
AD 519–526
First appearance of adult-only baptism as a distinctive view — Some fringe groups began questioning infant baptism. These were universally condemned as heretical by all branches of Christendom — Catholic, Orthodox, and eventually Protestant magisterial Reformers (Luther, Calvin, Zwingli all maintained infant baptism).
AD 1525
Anabaptists reject infant baptism — The Swiss Brethren (Felix Manz, Conrad Grebel) rebaptize adults, coining the term "Anabaptist" (re-baptizer). This is the first sustained movement in Church history to reject infant baptism — appearing 1,500 years after Christ, with no patristic support.

VIII. Protestant Objections

▼

Objection: There is no direct command to baptize infants in the New Testament

▼

The Claim

Every clear baptism in the New Testament involves personal faith and profession first. The Great Commission (Matthew 28:19) says "make disciples, then baptize" — discipleship precedes baptism. There is no explicit "baptize your infants" command.

Response

The same "no explicit command" argument would eliminate circumcision of infants under the Old Covenant — yet God explicitly commanded it at eight days. The New Testament also nowhere says "do not baptize infants." More importantly, Paul explicitly identifies Baptism as the New Covenant circumcision (Colossians 2:11–12) — and since circumcision was explicitly for infants, the logical conclusion is that Baptism includes infants too. The household baptisms in Acts (including Lydia's, the jailer's, Stephanas's) presuppose infant inclusion since no member of the household is excluded. Origen (AD 244) — within 150 years of the Apostles — says it was received from the Apostles. If it was not apostolic, where did it come from, and why did no one object?

Objection: Faith must come before baptism — Mark 16:16 says "whoever believes and is baptized will be saved"

▼

The Claim

The consistent New Testament pattern is: hear the gospel → believe → be baptized (Acts 2:41; 8:12; 8:36–37; 16:31–33). Faith must precede baptism. Infants cannot believe, therefore they cannot be baptized.

Response

This argument applies to adult converts — the normal evangelistic situation described in Acts. Adults hearing the gospel for the first time are called to personal faith before baptism. But this pattern does not apply to children born within the covenant community. No one suggests that the infants circumcised on the eighth day in Israel first had to hear and respond to a gospel presentation. The covenant logic is different for those born into the covenant family. Furthermore, Luke 1:15 describes John the Baptist as "filled with the Holy Spirit, even from his mother's womb" — an infant experiencing divine grace before birth. Luke 18:15–17 describes Jesus welcoming nursing infants as belonging to the kingdom. The assumption that infants cannot have any relationship with God is not biblical; it is a philosophical presupposition imported into the text.

Objection: Infant baptism is a Catholic/Orthodox tradition, not the original practice

▼

The Claim

Infant baptism was a gradual development that became universal only in the Constantinian era, when the Church compromised with Roman culture. The "original" Church was a free gathering of committed adult believers who chose baptism as a personal act of faith.

Response

This "original free church" model is entirely a modern myth with no historical support. The earliest descriptions of Church life (Didache c. AD 100, Ignatius c. AD 107, Justin Martyr c. AD 155) already show a structured, liturgical, episcopal community — not a free assembly of independent believers. Infant baptism is attested in the Apostolic Tradition by AD 215, attributed to apostolic tradition by Origen by AD 244, and confirmed by unanimous episcopal council by AD 253 — all well before Constantine (AD 313). If infant baptism were a Constantinian compromise, it would appear after AD 313. It appears centuries before. The Anabaptists who rejected it in 1525 were the innovators; the Oriental Orthodox who maintained it are the conservatives.

IX. Frequently Asked Questions

▼
Q: What happens to infants who die before baptism?▼

The Oriental Orthodox tradition entrusts unbaptized infants to God's mercy. We do not teach that unbaptized infants are condemned to hell or to "limbo" (a medieval Western concept never taught in the East). God is the God of all, who desires all to be saved. The consistent Eastern answer is: we know where God promises to be; we do not presume to know all the ways God may act in mercy beyond those promises. Parents are encouraged to bring children to baptism as soon as possible — precisely because of the gravity of what Baptism confers — but the fate of those who die before baptism is commended to God's infinite love.

Q: Do Oriental Orthodox baptize by immersion or sprinkling?▼

The Oriental Orthodox tradition baptizes by triple immersion — three full immersions in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. This is the ancient and apostolic form attested in the Didache (c. AD 100), Justin Martyr (c. AD 155), and Hippolytus (c. AD 215). For infants, the immersion is typically done in a baptismal font specifically designed for this purpose. In cases of necessity (serious illness, risk of death), pouring (affusion) is permitted — the Didache itself allows this in cases where sufficient water is unavailable. Sprinkling is not the normal or preferred form.

Q: Do infants receive communion in the Oriental Orthodox Church?▼

Yes. In the Oriental Orthodox tradition, Baptism, Chrismation, and the Eucharist are administered together in a single rite — even to infants. Newly baptized infants receive a small amount of the consecrated wine on a spoon immediately after their baptism and chrismation. This ancient practice is grounded in John 6:53: "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you." If this verse is taken seriously, withholding the Eucharist from baptized infants would be withholding life from them. The Eastern Church has maintained this practice consistently; it was the Western Church that gradually abandoned infant communion from the 9th–13th centuries.

Q: What role do godparents play in infant baptism?▼

Godparents (sponsors) play a crucial theological and pastoral role in infant baptism. They speak the baptismal renunciations and affirmations on behalf of the infant, undertaking a solemn commitment to assist in raising the child in the Christian faith. The use of godparents reflects the communal, ecclesial nature of Baptism: the infant is received not only into a family but into the Body of Christ, the Church, who collectively takes responsibility for the child's formation. The institution of godparents is ancient — attested in the Apostolic Tradition of Hippolytus (c. AD 215) and presupposed in earlier texts. Godparents also establish a spiritual kinship (kumbareia) with the baptized child and their family, a bond considered sacred in Oriental Orthodox culture.

Q: If a person baptized as an infant later rejects the faith, were they really saved?▼

Baptism confers genuine grace — new birth, forgiveness, the indwelling of the Holy Spirit, incorporation into Christ's Body. This is real and objective. However, the Oriental Orthodox tradition also holds that human beings have genuine freedom and can turn away from the grace they have received. The grace of Baptism is not irresistible or permanent regardless of the recipient's subsequent choices. An adult who was baptized as an infant but later apostasizes has not shown that their infant baptism was invalid; they have shown that they have rejected and abandoned the grace they were given. The responsibility for maintaining and growing in the baptismal grace lies with the individual (and their parents and godparents during childhood). This is why the Christian life is a continuous effort — not a one-time event, whether that event is infant baptism or adult conversion.
