THE ORTHODOX LAMP

The Filioque — The Holy Spirit and the Great Schism

A Biblical, Historical, and Theological Examination

"When the Helper comes, whom I will send to you from the Father, the Spirit of truth, who proceeds from the Father, he will bear witness about me." — John 15:26

✦ ✦ ✦

I. Introduction

▼

The filioque controversy is one of the most important and least understood theological disputes in Christian history. The Latin word filioque means "and from the Son" — and the dispute concerns whether these three words should be added to the Nicene Creed's description of the Holy Spirit's procession. The original Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed (AD 381) states that the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." The Western (Latin) Church gradually added "and from the Son" — the filioque — so that the Creed read that the Spirit "proceeds from the Father and the Son."

This addition was the primary theological cause of the Great Schism of AD 1054, which divided Western (Roman Catholic) and Eastern (Orthodox) Christianity — a division that persists to this day. The Oriental Orthodox churches (Coptic, Ethiopian, Syriac, Armenian, Malankara) never accepted the filioque, maintaining the original Nicene Creed and the Eastern theology of the Spirit's procession from the Father alone.

The issue is not merely a matter of a few words. At stake is the eternal nature of the Holy Trinity — specifically, the eternal relationships between the three Persons of the Godhead. The question "from whom does the Holy Spirit eternally proceed?" touches the deepest mysteries of the divine life and has profound implications for prayer, worship, and the entire experience of God in the Christian life.

II. The Nicene Creed — Original and Altered

▼
Original Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed (AD 381)

"And in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the Giver of life, who proceeds from the Father, who together with the Father and the Son is worshipped and glorified, who spoke through the prophets."

Used by: Oriental Orthodox, Eastern Orthodox, and originally all of Christendom. Never altered by any Ecumenical Council.

Western Version with Filioque (from c. AD 589)

"And in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the Giver of life, who proceeds from the Father and the Son, who together with the Father and the Son is worshipped and glorified, who spoke through the prophets."

Used by: Roman Catholic Church, most Protestant denominations (who inherited it from the Latin West).

The Council of Nicaea (AD 325) and the Council of Constantinople (AD 381) established the Creed. The Council of Ephesus (AD 431) — one of the councils the Oriental Orthodox accept — explicitly forbade any addition to the Nicene Creed: "It is not permitted to bring forward, or to write, or to compose a different faith as a rival to that established by the holy Fathers assembled with the Holy Ghost at Nicaea." The filioque addition violates this explicit conciliar prohibition.

III. What Is the Filioque? — The Theological Question

▼

The filioque concerns the eternal procession of the Holy Spirit — not his temporal mission into the world (which both East and West affirm involves the Son), but his eternal relationship to the Father and Son within the immanent Trinity.

The Eastern tradition, following the Cappadocian Fathers and Cyril of Alexandria, holds that the Father alone is the mone archē (sole principle/source) of the Trinity — the one from whom the Son is eternally begotten and the Spirit eternally proceeds. The Father is the font of divinity; the Son and Spirit derive their personal existence from the Father (while being fully equal in divine nature). This preserves the monarchy (single-source unity) of the Father.

The Western tradition, beginning especially with Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430), emphasizes the unity of the divine essence shared by Father and Son — and concludes from this that the Spirit proceeds from the divine essence (which both Father and Son equally possess) or from both Father and Son as a single principle. This "double procession" is what the filioque expresses.

The Oriental Orthodox reject the filioque primarily for three reasons: (1) it contradicts the explicit text of John 15:26; (2) it was added unilaterally by one part of the Church without an Ecumenical Council; and (3) it distorts the Eastern understanding of the Trinity by introducing a "double principle" for the Spirit's existence.

IV. Biblical Evidence

▼
John 15:26
OO
"When the Helper comes, whom I will send to you from the Father, the Spirit of truth, who proceeds from the Father, he will bear witness about me."
The key text in the entire filioque debate. Christ himself says the Spirit "proceeds from the Father" (Greek: para tou Patros ekporeuetai). He does NOT say "from the Father and the Son." He says he will "send" the Spirit — but the procession is from the Father. The Oriental Orthodox distinguish: temporal mission (Christ sends the Spirit into the world) from eternal procession (the Spirit's eternal origin from the Father alone). The filioque conflates these two distinct relationships.
John 14:16–17
OO
"And I will ask the Father, and he will give you another Helper, to be with you forever, even the Spirit of truth."
Christ asks the Father to give the Spirit — the Father is the source of the Spirit's coming. Christ is the one who asks; the Father gives. This distinction of roles within the Trinity — Father as source, Son as mediator — does not support the Western view that the Son is a co-equal source of the Spirit's procession alongside the Father.
John 20:22
CTX
"And when he had said this, he breathed on them and said to them, 'Receive the Holy Spirit.'"
Christ breathes the Spirit on the Apostles after the Resurrection — this is the temporal mission of the Spirit by the Son. Western theologians cite this as evidence that the Spirit proceeds "from the Son." The Eastern response: the temporal mission (Christ breathing the Spirit in time) does not establish the eternal procession (the Spirit's origin within the immanent Trinity). The risen Christ can send the Spirit in time without being the eternal source of the Spirit's hypostatic existence.
Romans 8:9
CTX
"You, however, are not in the flesh but in the Spirit, if in fact the Spirit of God dwells in you. Anyone who does not have the Spirit of Christ does not belong to him."
The Spirit is called both "Spirit of God" (the Father) and "Spirit of Christ" — suggesting a close relation to both Father and Son. The West uses this language to support the filioque. The East responds: the Spirit is the Spirit of Christ in the sense of belonging to Christ and being sent by him — not in the sense of eternally proceeding from him. The Spirit belongs to both Father and Son; this does not mean the Spirit originates from both.
Galatians 4:6
CTX
"And because you are sons, God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying, 'Abba! Father!'"
The Father sends "the Spirit of his Son" — another close association between Spirit and Son. The East: "Spirit of his Son" denotes the Spirit's mission and relationship to Christ in the economy of salvation, not the Spirit's eternal origin. In the temporal economy, the Spirit is sent by the Father through the Son; in the eternal immanent Trinity, the Spirit proceeds from the Father alone.

V. Historical Origins of the Filioque

▼
c. AD 400–430
Augustine of Hippo — In his treatise On the Trinity, Augustine develops a Western Trinitarian theology emphasizing the unity of the divine essence. He concludes that the Spirit proceeds "principally from the Father but also from the Son," laying the theological groundwork for the filioque. This is a genuine theological development, not apostolic tradition.
AD 447
Pope Leo the Great — Uses filioque language in a letter, though not in the Creed itself. The Western theological tradition is moving toward filioque as a received teaching.
AD 589
Third Council of Toledo (Spain) — The filioque is formally inserted into the Nicene Creed for the first time in the Visigothic Spanish Church, to combat Arianism. This is the first official addition of filioque to the Creed — a local, regional decision, not an Ecumenical Council.
c. AD 800
Charlemagne and the Frankish Church — The Frankish church adopts the filioque and begins pressuring Rome to include it. When Eastern monks in Jerusalem complain that Frankish monks are singing the filioque, the dispute becomes international. Charlemagne commissions theologians to defend the filioque against Eastern critics.
AD 809
Pope Leo III refuses the filioque — Pope Leo III explicitly refuses to add the filioque to the Creed, stating that while the doctrine may be orthodox, the addition is inappropriate. He has the Creed without filioque engraved on silver shields in St. Peter's Basilica. This is the last time Rome officially opposed the filioque.
AD 867–880
The Photian Schism — Patriarch Photius of Constantinople writes his famous encyclical condemning the filioque on theological grounds, leading to a major schism between Rome and Constantinople. Though temporarily healed, this establishes the filioque as the central theological point of East-West division.
AD 1014
Rome formally adopts the filioque — Pope Benedict VIII, under pressure from Holy Roman Emperor Henry II, officially inserts the filioque into the Creed at Rome. This is the final step — over 400 years after its first insertion in Spain, Rome officially adopts what will become the primary theological cause of the Great Schism of 1054.
AD 1054
The Great Schism — Cardinal Humbert and Patriarch Michael Cerularius exchange mutual excommunications, formally dividing Eastern and Western Christendom. The filioque is among the central theological disputes. The Oriental Orthodox — already separated after Chalcedon in 451 — observe the schism as confirmation of the Eastern churches' theological position.

VI. Eastern Theological Objections

▼
St. Photius of Constantinople (c. AD 820–893)
Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit — c. AD 886
"If the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, then either the Father is not the sole principle of divinity, or there are two principles in the Trinity — two causes, two sources — which is worse than polytheism. How can one preserve the monarchy of the Father if the Son is equally a source of the Spirit? And if the Spirit proceeds from two, why not say the Son also proceeds from the Father and the Spirit? The filioque introduces a principle of confusion into the very being of God." PARAPHRASE
Significance: Photius's theological argument against the filioque is the classic Eastern critique: the filioque undermines the monarchy of the Father (the Father as the sole source and principle of the Trinity) and introduces two principles into the Spirit's eternal existence. If both Father and Son are sources of the Spirit, the unity of the Trinity is threatened. This argument has never been satisfactorily answered by Western theologians.
St. Gregory Palamas (c. AD 1296–1359)
Apodictic Treatises on the Procession of the Holy Spirit — c. AD 1335
"We do not say that the Spirit is from the Son or through the Son in terms of his eternal procession, his hypostatic existence. But we say that the Spirit is given through the Son, manifested through the Son, shines forth through the Son — in the economy of salvation. These are different things: eternal procession and temporal mission. The filioque confuses what must be kept distinct." PARAPHRASE
Significance: Palamas draws the crucial distinction that underlies the entire Eastern position: the Spirit's eternal hypostatic procession (from the Father alone) vs. the Spirit's temporal manifestation and mission (through the Son). The Eastern tradition consistently accepts that Christ sends the Spirit in the economy of salvation — this is not disputed. What is disputed is whether this temporal mission implies an eternal double procession.

VII. The Council Prohibition — Ephesus 431

▼

Beyond the theological objections, the Oriental Orthodox have a canonical/ecclesiological objection to the filioque: it was added to the Creed in violation of an explicit prohibition by an Ecumenical Council that the Roman Catholic Church itself accepts.

The Council of Ephesus (AD 431) — accepted by Rome and by the Oriental Orthodox — declared in its Seventh Canon: "The holy synod decreed: It is not permitted to anyone to introduce, to write, or to compose a faith other than that defined by the Holy Fathers assembled in Nicaea with the Holy Spirit. Those who dare to compose, to produce, or to present another Creed… shall be deposed if they are bishops or clerics, and shall be anathematized if they are laymen."

The filioque is an addition to the Creed defined by the Fathers at Nicaea and Constantinople. It was added unilaterally — first by a regional Spanish council (589), then gradually by the Frankish church, and finally by Rome (1014) — without an Ecumenical Council. Even if the filioque theology were correct (which the Oriental Orthodox deny), its insertion into the Creed is a canonical violation of the Ephesian prohibition.

The Oriental Orthodox point to Pope Leo III (c. AD 809) as evidence: even the Bishop of Rome refused to add the filioque to the Creed, acknowledging that the Ephesian prohibition applied. The later reversal by his successors does not change the canonical fact.

VIII. Theological Implications

▼

The filioque dispute is not merely an abstract scholastic debate. The Oriental Orthodox theologians have consistently argued that it has profound practical implications for the understanding of God, salvation, and the Church.

Trinitarian Structure: The Eastern understanding — Father as sole source, Son eternally begotten, Spirit eternally proceeding from the Father — maintains a clear monarchical structure within the Trinity without compromising the equality of the three Persons. The filioque introduces a structural asymmetry: the Spirit's existence depends on two Persons (Father and Son together) while the Son's existence depends only on one (the Father). The Spirit is thus relationally subordinate to both, while the Son is subordinate only to the Father. This asymmetry troubles Eastern theologians.

The Spirit's Role in Salvation: In the Eastern tradition, theosis (the deification and transformation of the human person) is accomplished by the Holy Spirit who proceeds from the Father and rests on the Son. The Spirit is the Gift of the Father given through the Son — arriving directly from the Father's "paternal bosom" to the believer. In the Western filioque tradition, the Spirit proceeds from the Father-Son unity and is essentially the Love that binds Father and Son — which, critics argue, subordinates the Spirit to the Father-Son relationship rather than allowing the Spirit to be distinctly the "Gift of the Father" (as Jesus calls him in Luke 24:49 and John 14:26).

Ecclesiological Implications: St. Photius argued that the filioque was not merely a theological error but a symptom of the papal ecclesiology — the claim that one bishop (Rome) can unilaterally change the universal faith without conciliar agreement. The same spirit of unilateral authority that allowed Rome to add the filioque to the Creed is the same spirit that claims papal infallibility and universal jurisdiction. The two issues are connected at their root.

IX. Western Arguments for the Filioque

▼

Western Argument: Augustine's theology demonstrates the double procession from Scripture

▼

The Western Claim

Augustine in On the Trinity demonstrates from multiple Scripture passages that the Spirit proceeds from the Son as well as the Father. John 20:22 (Christ breathing the Spirit), Romans 8:9 ("Spirit of Christ"), and Galatians 4:6 ("Spirit of his Son") all show the Spirit's intimate relation to the Son that amounts to procession.

Eastern Response

The Oriental Orthodox accept Augustine as a theologian of the Western tradition worthy of respect but not as a universal Father binding on Eastern theology. Augustine's Trinitarian theology, shaped by Neo-Platonic categories, represents a distinctly Western development not shared by the earlier Alexandrian, Antiochene, or Cappadocian schools. The scriptural passages cited describe the Spirit's temporal economy of salvation (the Spirit as sent by Christ, belonging to Christ, working through Christ) — not the Spirit's eternal hypostatic procession within the immanent Trinity. The distinction between the immanent Trinity (God as God is in himself) and the economic Trinity (God as he reveals himself in salvation history) is crucial and is consistently maintained by the Eastern Fathers.

Western Argument: The Fathers of the West support the filioque theology

▼

The Western Claim

Ambrose of Milan, Hilary of Poitiers, and other Western Fathers use language implying the double procession before it was formally inserted into the Creed. The filioque is not an innovation but the articulation of a theology already present in Western Christianity.

Eastern Response

Even granting that some Western Fathers used filioque-adjacent language (which Eastern theologians dispute or interpret differently), this is a regional Western development, not the universal apostolic tradition. The Eastern Fathers — Athanasius, the Cappadocians, Cyril of Alexandria, John Chrysostom — all speak of the Spirit proceeding from the Father, with the Son as the mediator through whom the Spirit is sent to the world. The universal tradition (East and West through Nicaea and Constantinople) confesses procession from the Father. The specifically Western development of filioque theology represents a divergence from this universal tradition, not its clarification.

X. The Oriental Orthodox Position — A Summary

▼

The Oriental Orthodox position on the filioque can be summarized in four points:

1. Scriptural: John 15:26 says the Spirit "proceeds from the Father" — not "from the Father and the Son." Christ's own words are the supreme authority, and they do not support the filioque. The passages cited in favor of it (John 20:22; Romans 8:9; Galatians 4:6) describe the Spirit's temporal mission and relationship to Christ in the economy of salvation — not the Spirit's eternal procession within the immanent Trinity.

2. Patristic: The Eastern Fathers — Athanasius, the Cappadocians, Cyril of Alexandria, and all the major Alexandrian and Antiochene theologians — speak of the Spirit proceeding from the Father, often adding that the Spirit is sent through the Son or manifested through the Son. None of them affirm a double procession in the eternal Trinity. The filioque is a Western theological development, not the universal apostolic tradition.

3. Conciliar: The Council of Ephesus (AD 431) explicitly forbade additions to the Nicene Creed. The filioque was added in violation of this prohibition — first regionally (Spain, AD 589), then universally by Rome (AD 1014) — without an Ecumenical Council. Even Pope Leo III (c. AD 809) refused to add it, acknowledging the Ephesian prohibition. The insertion is canonically irregular regardless of its theological content.

4. Theological: The filioque threatens the monarchy of the Father (his role as the sole source of the Trinity) and introduces a "double principle" for the Spirit's existence — a problem that Western theologians have never satisfactorily resolved. The Eastern theology — Father as sole source, Son as eternally begotten, Spirit as eternally proceeding from the Father through the Son in the economy — maintains the unity and monarchy of the Trinity without subordinating any Person.

XI. FAQ

▼
Q: Do most Protestant churches use the filioque?▼

Yes. Most Protestant denominations inherited the Nicene Creed from the Western (Latin) Catholic tradition, including the filioque. When Protestants recite the Nicene Creed, they typically use the Western version with "and the Son." This is not a conscious theological decision for most Protestants but a historical inheritance from the Latin Church they came from. The filioque is rarely a point of active theological reflection in Protestant churches — it is simply received as part of the traditional Creed. Some ecumenically minded Protestant theologians have expressed openness to removing the filioque in the interest of East-West Christian unity.

Q: What do modern Catholic-Orthodox dialogues say about the filioque?▼

Modern ecumenical dialogues have made significant progress on the filioque. The Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity issued a clarification in 1995 ("The Greek and Latin Traditions Regarding the Procession of the Holy Spirit") acknowledging that the East and West may be expressing the same truth in different ways — and that the filioque was an unauthorized addition to the Creed. Pope John Paul II recited the Nicene Creed without filioque in joint celebrations with Eastern Orthodox patriarchs on several occasions. However, Rome has not officially removed the filioque from the Creed for Latin Rite Catholics, and the Eastern Orthodox and Oriental Orthodox consider its removal a prerequisite for any meaningful reunion.

Q: Is the filioque the only reason for the East-West Schism?▼

No — the Great Schism of 1054 had multiple causes: the filioque was the primary theological dispute; papal primacy and jurisdiction was the primary ecclesiological dispute; and various cultural, political, and liturgical differences (use of unleavened bread in the Latin Eucharist, clerical celibacy rules, etc.) contributed. The 1054 schism was also the culmination of centuries of growing estrangement — the Photian schism of 867–880 being an earlier rupture that was healed but left scars. The filioque, however, is consistently identified by Eastern theologians as the most fundamental theological issue — because it touches the eternal being of God himself.
